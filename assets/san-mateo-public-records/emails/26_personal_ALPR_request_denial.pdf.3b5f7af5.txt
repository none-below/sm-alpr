Hi Kelly,

Thank you for the prompt response.
I want to clarify the nature of my request, because I believe it may
have been categorized in a way that doesn’t fully apply.

§ 1798.90.55(b), which you cited, restricts agency-to-agency and
agency-to-third-party transfers. Whether it applies when the requestor
is the data subject is a different question — and one the ACLU case
did not address. That case involved the ACLU and EFF requesting bulk
ALPR scan data for millions of vehicles from LAPD and LASD. The
court’s privacy balancing under § 6255(a) was based on the risk of
exposing the movements of millions of uninvolved people to a third
party. My request is for records about my own vehicle only. The
privacy interest the court found dispositive — protecting strangers’
location data from third-party disclosure — does not apply when the
requestor is the data subject. The court emphasized that the § 6255(a)
balancing test is case-specific, and the facts here are materially
different.

Additionally, I note that SMPD’s own ALPR policy (Policy 463, Section
463.8) states that requests from non-Law-enforcement agencies “will be
processed as provided in the Records Maintenance and Release Policy
(Civil Code § 1798.90.55).” This language implies a processing pathway
exists for such requests — not an automatic denial.

I’d also note that my request referenced “all ALPR systems,” but SMPD
also operates Flock Condor cameras and other video surveillance
equipment that may have captured images of my vehicle. Civil Code §
1798.90.55(b) applies specifically to “ALPR information” as defined in
§ 1798.90.5. Video surveillance footage that is not collected through
an ALPR system would not fall under that restriction. To the extent
any non-ALPR cameras captured footage of my vehicle, I’d like to
understand what process applies to accessing that footage.

Hello Brian,

Your request has been denied pursuant to Government Code 7927.705, as
records prohibited from disclosure under federal or state law. In this
case, Civil Code section 1798.90.55, subdivision (b) prohibits a
public agency from selling, sharing, or transferring ALPR information,
except to another public agency, and only as otherwise permitted by
law.

In addition, your request been denied under the public interest
exemption, as the public interest in releasing information on private
vehicles is outweighed by the public interest in maintaining
individual privacy. (Gov. Code 7922.000; California Constitution
Article 1, Section 1; American Civil Liberties Union Foundation v.
Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 1032, 1044).

Thank you,
