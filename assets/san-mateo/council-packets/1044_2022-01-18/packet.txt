SUBJECT:
Remote Teleconferenced City Council and Commission Meetings вЂ“ Reauthorization 
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to reauthorize remote teleconferenced City Council and Board and Commission meetings for 30 days, 
through February 17, 2022, as allowed by Assembly Bill 361 amending the Brown Act, based upon the GovernorвЂ™s 
emergency proclamation and findings that meeting in person would present imminent risks to the health or safety of 
attendees. 
BACKGROUND:
At its October 18, 2021, City Council meeting, the Council adopted Resolution No. 100 (2021) authorizing continued 
remote meetings of the Council and its subordinate legislative bodies due to the imminent risk to the health and safety of 
attendees due to possible transmission of COVID-19 and the recent spread of variants. The resolution became effective 
October 18, 2021 through November 17, 2021, or until the City Council adopts a subsequent resolution. AB 361 requires 
confirming the emergency circumstances 30 days after the first teleconference meeting and every 30 days thereafter. At 
the October 18 meeting, the Council accepted staffвЂ™s recommendation to pass a resolution at every meeting to ensure 
there is no gap in the effectiveness of authorizing resolutions. The action being requested at this January 18th meeting is to 
adopt a resolution reauthorizing remote teleconferenced Council and Commission meetings per the requirements of 
Assembly Bill 361. 
It was noted in the original resolution that current health orders require masking in all indoor facilities due to health and 
safety concerns. In addition, San Mateo County Health Order No. c19-12 provides that the Delta variant is highly 
transmissible in indoor settings and requires multi-component prevention strategies to reduce its spread. The California 
Department of Public Health is currently investigating the length of vaccine protection. The Center for Disease Control and 
Prevention recommends avoiding indoor spaces that do not offer fresh air from the outdoors as much as possible. 
Returning to meeting in the Council Chambers or smaller conference rooms means being in an enclosed space for 
meetings that commonly last for one to five hours and would seem to create additional exposure for participants to a 
possible transmission of the virus. These health directives and advice are still in place.
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for taking this action as all software and hardware needs to meet remotely have already been 
purchased and are in use.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
Adoption of this resolution authorizing remote teleconferenced meetings of the City Council and City Boards and 
Commission is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative activity that will not result in 
direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(5).)

WHEREAS, the City of San Mateo is committed to preserving and nurturing public access and participation in 
meetings of the City Council and its Boards and Commissions; and
 
WHEREAS, all meetings of City of San MateoвЂ™s legislative bodies are open and public, as required by the 
Ralph M. Brown Act (Cal. Gov. Code 54950 вЂ“ 54963), so that any member of the public may attend, participate, and 
watch the DistrictвЂ™s legislative bodies conduct their business; and
 
WHEREAS, the Brown Act, Government Code section 54953(e), makes provisions for remote 
teleconferencing participation in meetings by members of a legislative body, without compliance with the 
requirements of Government Code section 54953(b)(3), subject to the existence of certain conditions; and
 
WHEREAS, a required condition is that a state of emergency is declared by the Governor pursuant to 
Government Code section 8625, proclaiming the existence of conditions of disaster or of extreme peril to the safety 
of persons and property within the state caused by conditions as described in Government Code section 8558; and
 
WHEREAS, a proclamation is made when there is an actual incident, threat of disaster, or extreme peril to 
the safety of persons and property within the jurisdictions that are within the CityвЂ™s boundaries, caused by natural, 
technological, or human-caused disasters; and
 
WHEREAS, it is further required that state or local officials have imposed or recommended measures to 
promote social distancing, or, the legislative body meeting in person would present imminent risks to the health and 
safety of attendees; and
 
WHEREAS, such conditions now exist in the City, specifically, the Governor has proclaimed a state of 
emergency exists for the state of California due to the conditions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic and the impact 
on the health and safety of its residents; and
 
WHEREAS, the Delta and Omicron variants are highly transmissible in indoor spaces, the California 
Department of Public Health is currently investigating how long vaccine protection lasts, and the Center for Disease 
Control and Prevention recommends avoiding indoor spaces that do not offer fresh air from the outdoors; and  
WHEREAS, in accordance with Assembly Bill 361, the City Council does hereby find that as a result of the 
emergency, meeting in person would present imminent risks to the health or safety of attendees and the Council 
desires to authorize continued remote teleconferenced meetings of its legislative bodies; and
WHEREAS, as a consequence of the local emergency, the City Council does hereby find that the legislative 
bodies of City of San Mateo shall conduct their meetings without compliance with paragraph (3) of subdivision (b) of 
Government Code section 54953, as authorized by subdivision (e) of section 54953, and that such legislative bodies

Approve the request for travel out of state and lodging for Council Member Joe Goethals and City Clerk Patrice Olds, 
subject to the City Council Guidelines, as the City's official representatives at the 101st Airborne 1st Brigade Ball on January 
28, 2022, to represent the recognition of the 50th Anniversary of the 1972 welcome home parade San Mateo held for its 
adopted unit (1/327 A Company) returning from the Vietnam War. 
BACKGROUND:
In 1968 the City adopted Company A of the first brigade, 327th regiment of the 101st Airborne Air Assault Division of the 
Army (known as 'ABU') and proceeded to write letters and send care packages to the unit in Vietnam, developing a strong 
relationship between the residents of San Mateo and the soldiers. In January 1972 the unit was finally coming home, and 
the City secured permission to host the unit here in the City of San Mateo prior to their returning to their stateside base. 
To the best of our knowledge, the City of San Mateo was the only city in the country to have a welcome home parade for 
returning Vietnam service members. For three days the unit was feted, stayed in resident homes, had a gala, and was 
treated to a parade and festival in Central Park - solidifying the strong relationship between ABU and San Mateo. 
The City has three times replicated that original welcome home event with our current soldiers of our adopted unit - the 
last time in 2018 to celebrate the 50th anniversary of the actual adoption. 
As this is the 50th anniversary of the original welcome home parade, the Army has arranged to celebrate this occasion and 
recognize San Mateo for the continuous relationship with ABU through every deployment since 1968 at their Bastogne 
Brigade Ball on January 28, 2022. The Army has requested representatives of the City be present to receive its appreciation 
for our commitment to the unit. Council Member Goethals has expressed an interest in representing the City at this event, 
and City Clerk Olds, as the manager of this special relationship, also wishes to attend on behalf of the City. Projected costs 
for air travel, lodging, a rental car and a gift commemorating the occasion are expected to be under $3,000.

RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to accept grant funds in the amount of $710,114 from the State of California Department of Justice, 
Tobacco Grant Program; authorize the Chief of Police to execute the Memorandum of Understanding with the California 
Department of Justice and all other necessary grant documents; authorize the addition of 1.00 Full-Time Equivalent Police 
Officer position for the three-year duration of the grant period; and amend the adopted fiscal year 2021-22 budget.
BACKGROUND:
The San Mateo Police Department has been awarded a grant from the California Department of Justice (DOJ) for the 
Proposition 56 Tobacco Grant Program.  The intent of this grant is to fund a Police Officer dedicated to reducing and 
preventing the use of tobacco products among youth, teens and our community at large through education, outreach and 
enforcement activities. The total grant award is $710,114 and is to be expended over the course of three years. 
The grant funding will provide salary and benefits for 1.00 Full-Time Equivalent (FTE) Youth Services Police Officer (YSO) 
who will be dedicated to anti-vaping/tobacco outreach, education and enforcement.  The responsibilities of the YSO will 
include working in collaboration with SMPD Youth Services Unit, School Districts, and the City Parks and Recreation 
Department to conduct outreach, education and enforcement. 
The YSO will develop and implement tobacco awareness and prevention curriculum classes, work to increase awareness to 
reduce tobacco usage, and work with our Community Engagement Office to implement a public education and outreach 
campaign leveraging social media.  The YSO will also build relationships with local tobacco retailers to ensure regulations 
on tobacco product sales are being followed, and conduct enforcement operations targeting problematic retailers to gain 
compliance with related City ordinances.
The assignment of an Officer to perform the tasks funded by the grant will create a vacancy in the complement of police 
officers assigned to the Field Operations Patrol Division.  Authorization for an additional 1.00 FTE Police Officer position is 
requested for the three-year grant period to ensure there is no loss of service to the community.  The addition of this 
position will have a net-zero budget impact as the cost will be fully offset by the grant.
The Police Department plans to staff the YSO position in the spring, with a proposed expenditure timeline commencing in 
April 2022 and concluding in March 2025.  Therefore, the requested action includes authorizing the addition of 1.00 FTE 
Police Officer position and amending the adopted 2021-22 budget in the amount of $59,826.  This reflects costs that will be 
incurred during the remainder of the current fiscal year, including $55,088 for the additional Officer position, $2,000 in 
operating expenses for training and marketing materials for students, and $2,818 for administrative costs for staff time

BACKGROUND:
Regular, fossil-based diesel is currently used to power vehicles in the CityвЂ™s fleet, including dump trucks, street sweepers, 
vactor trucks, and light duty maintenance trucks. As part of the August 15, 2021 presentation to Council on the CityвЂ™s 
Green Fleet 2030 strategic objective, staff recommended the use of renewable diesel to replace the fossil-based fuel. 
Renewable diesel is derived from biomass sources such as vegetable oils, grasses, and woody material and can be used 
with no modifications to the vehicles or fueling system as it behaves identically to fossil-based diesel. It is cleaner burning, 
and its use will decrease greenhouse gas and CO2 lifecycle emissions significantly. Staff recommends authorizing the Public 
Works Director to exercise extensions per the contract agreement.
City of San Mateo Municipal Code Section 3.60.030 requires, as a general rule, that contracts valued at $100,000 or more 
be competitively bid.  However, Municipal Code Section 3.60.050 contains a number of exceptions to the CityвЂ™s 
competitive bidding requirement.  Section 3.60.050(b), which provides that competitive bidding is not required when the 
City Council finds that it would be вЂњimpracticable or unavailing,вЂќ applies here.  City staff has confirmed that the price 
quoted by Valley Oil is the lowest available and therefore competitive bidding would be вЂњunavailing.вЂќ  City staff solicitated 
quotes for renewable diesel from three providers.  Pricing for renewable diesel was requested as a proposed markup on 
the Oil Price Information Service (OPIS) daily rack average costs. OPIS reports the average costs of fuels in different areas 
of the country several times each day, providing a tool for both oil companies and awarding bodies. Using OPIS data 
removes the fluctuating cost of fuel from the quoted differentials and instead puts the focus on the individual vendor 
delivery markups, which improves transparency and efficiency in the bid evaluation process.

RISK MANAGEMENT AND DIVERSIFICATION 
 
Mitigating Credit Risk in the Portfolio  
Credit risk is the risk that a security or a portfolio will lose some or all its value due to a real or 
perceived change in the ability of the issuer to repay its debt. The City will mitigate credit risk by 
adopting the following strategies: 
 
вЂў 
The diversification requirements included in the вЂњAuthorized InvestmentsвЂќ section of this 
policy are designed to mitigate credit risk in the portfolio. 
 
вЂў 
No more than 5 percent of the total portfolio may be deposited with or invested in securities 
issued by any single issuer unless otherwise specified in this policy. 
 
вЂў 
The City may elect to sell a security prior to its maturity and record a capital gain or loss in 
order to manage the quality, liquidity or yield of the portfolio in response to market 
conditions or CityвЂ™s risk preferences. 
 
вЂў 
If the credit ratings of any security owned by the City are downgraded to a level below the 
quality required by this investment policy, it will be the CityвЂ™s policy to review the credit 
situation and make a determination as to whether to sell or retain such securities in the 
portfolio. 
 
o If a security is downgraded, the Finance Director/City Treasurer will use discretion 
in determining whether to sell or hold the security based on its current maturity, the 
economic outlook for the issuer, and other relevant factors. 
 
o If a decision is made to retain a downgraded security in the portfolio, its presence in 
the portfolio will be monitored and reported monthly to the City Council. 
 
Mitigating Market Risk in the Portfolio  
Market risk is the risk that the portfolio value will fluctuate due to changes in the general level of 
interest rates. The City recognizes that, over time, longer-term portfolios have the potential to 
achieve higher returns. On the other hand, longer-term portfolios have higher volatility of return. 
The City will mitigate market risk by providing adequate liquidity for short-term cash needs, and 
by making longer-term investments only with funds that are not needed for current cash flow 
purposes. 
 
The City further recognizes that certain types of securities, including variable rate securities, 
securities with principal paydowns prior to maturity, and securities with embedded options, will 
affect the market risk profile of the portfolio differently in different interest rate environments. The 
City, therefore, adopts the following strategies to control and mitigate its exposure to market risk: 
 
вЂў 
The City will maintain a minimum of six months of budgeted operating expenditures in 
short term investments to provide sufficient liquidity for expected disbursements. 
вЂў 
 
вЂў 
The maximum stated final maturity of individual securities in the portfolio will be five (5) 
years, except as otherwise stated in this policy. 
вЂў 
 
вЂў 
The duration of the portfolio will generally be approximately equal to the duration 
(typically, plus or minus 20 percent) of a Market Benchmark, an index selected by the City 
based on the CityвЂ™s investment objectives, constraints, and risk tolerances.

Excess Educational Revenue Augmentation Fund (ERAF) 
During the time that the budget assumptions for the fiscal year 2021-22 were being developed, the County ControllerвЂ™s 
Office was awaiting guidance from the State ControllerвЂ™s Office (SCO) regarding the calculation of Excess ERAF. The County 
of San Mateo is one of a handful of counties in the State of California where the majority of the school districts are basic 
aid, and the few non-basic aid school districts that remain are able to meet their minimum funding requirements through 
ERAF. The remainder of the ERAF (that was funded by property tax taken from local government beginning in 1994) is then 
returned back to the local county and cities. In anticipation of a potentially adverse outcome, the adopted 2021-22 budget 
assumed a $1.8 million reduction in Excess ERAF as a result of the dispute between the state and the limited number of 
counties that generate excess ERAF (San Francisco, San Mateo, Santa Clara, Napa, and Marin). Fortunately, excess ERAF 
revenues, after incorporating the SCOвЂ™s guidance, are projected to be $7.9 million. 
Former Redevelopment Agency (RDA) Property Tax
Upon dissolution of the former RDA in 2012 via Assembly Bill X 1 26, the property tax increment that had previously been 
allocated to the RDA serves as a funding source for its recognized obligations (primarily tax allocation bonds) and 
passthrough agreements with the taxing entities. The remaining property tax is allocated between the taxing entities, 
which includes the City. This residual property tax is recognized in the CityвЂ™s General Fund. 
A 2020 court of appeal decision, Chula Vista v. Drager (Sandoval), resulted in the equalization of pro-rata property tax 
allocation between taxing entities that have favorable passthrough agreements and those that do not. The end result for 
the City is an unfavorable budget variance of $2.0 million. This case law will prospectively change the CityвЂ™s property tax 
assumption for the its share of the residual former RDA property tax revenues.

CalPERS ALM 
As a part of its quadrennial Asset Liability Management (ALM) cycle, the CalPERS Board recently selected a 6.8% discount 
rate. The discount rate is the projected return on investment (ROI) over the next thirty years, as well as the level of risk 
that CalPERS is willing to take in order to achieve that ROI. As a precursor to the ALM cycle, CalPERS issued a press release 
touting its вЂњToward a 7% SolutionвЂќ strategy that espoused вЂњMore and better assetsвЂќ, which signaled its intent to increase 
the asset allocation of private debt and private equity through the ALM cycle and introduce leverage as a strategic element 
of asset allocation.
In July 2021, CalPERS reported a preliminary ROI of 21.3% for fiscal year 2020-21. At first blush, higher interest returns may 
seem favorable, but interest is a measure of risk, and the deviation from the discount rate means that CalPERSвЂ™ investment 
portfolio is taking on too much risk. As such, the Funding Risk Mitigation Policy (RMP) was triggered, which reduced the 
discount rate from 7.0% to 6.8%. The investment gain вЂ“ the difference between the 7.0% discount rate and the 21.3% ROI, 
will be amortized over 20 years, with a ramp up over the first five years (1/5th or 20% of the annual amortization amount 
recognized each year), beginning in the actuarial valuations with a measurement date of June 30, 2021, which applies to 
the pension contribution rates for 2023-24. The reduction of the discount rate will be amortized over 20 years with no 
ramping, as ramping for changes in actuarial assumptions was eliminated beginning in measurement periods of June 30, 
2019. 
With the above in context, the CalPERS Board considered a number of candidate portfolios that incorporated a variety of 
asset allocations and degrees of risk tolerance. It came as no surprise that the asset allocation for every candidate portfolio 
increased the allocation for private equity and most introduced leverage as a part of the allocation strategy:
