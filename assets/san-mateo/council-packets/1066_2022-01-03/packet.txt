SUBJECT:
Remote Teleconferenced City Council and Commission Meetings – Reauthorization 
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to reauthorize remote teleconferenced City Council and Board and Commission meetings for 30 days, 
through February 2, 2022, as allowed by Assembly Bill 361 amending the Brown Act, based upon the Governor’s 
emergency proclamation and findings that meeting in person would present imminent risks to the health or safety of 
attendees. 
BACKGROUND:
At its October 18, 2021, City Council meeting, the Council adopted Resolution No. 100 (2021) authorizing continued 
remote meetings of the Council and its subordinate legislative bodies due to the imminent risk to the health and safety of 
attendees due to possible transmission of COVID-19 and the recent spread of variants. The resolution became effective 
October 18, 2021 through November 17, 2021, or until the City Council adopts a subsequent resolution. AB 361 requires 
confirming the emergency circumstances 30 days after the first teleconference meeting and every 30 days thereafter.  At 
the October 18 meeting, the Council accepted staff’s recommendation to pass a resolution at every meeting to ensure 
there is no gap in the effectiveness of authorizing resolutions. The action being requested at this January 3rd meeting is to 
adopt a resolution reauthorizing remote teleconferenced Council and Commission meetings per the requirements of 
Assembly Bill 361. 
It was noted in the original resolution that current health orders require masking in all indoor facilities due to health and 
safety concerns. In addition, San Mateo County Health Order No. c19-12 provides that the Delta variant is highly 
transmissible in indoor settings and requires multi-component prevention strategies to reduce its spread. The California 
Department of Public Health is currently investigating the length of vaccine protection. The Center for Disease Control and 
Prevention recommends avoiding indoor spaces that do not offer fresh air from the outdoors as much as possible. 
Returning to meeting in the Council Chambers or smaller conference rooms means being in an enclosed space for 
meetings that commonly last for one to five hours and would seem to create additional exposure for participants to a 
possible transmission of the virus. These health directives and advice are still in place.
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for taking this action as all software and hardware needs to meet remotely have already been 
purchased and are in use.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
Adoption of this resolution authorizing remote teleconferenced meetings of the City Council and City Boards and 
Commission is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative activity that will not result in 
direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(5).)

RESOLUTION NO. ___ (2022)
REAUTHORIZING REMOTE TELECONFERENCE MEETINGS OF THE LEGISLATIVE BODIES OF CITY OF SAN MATEO 
EFFECTIVE JANUARY 3, 2022 UNTIL THE EARLIER OF FEBRUARY 2, 2022 OR SUCH TIME THE CITY COUNCIL ADOPTS 
A SUBSEQUENT RESOLUTION, PURSUANT TO THE BROWN ACT 
WHEREAS, the City of San Mateo is committed to preserving and nurturing public access and participation in 
meetings of the City Council and its Boards and Commissions; and
 
WHEREAS, all meetings of City of San Mateo’s legislative bodies are open and public, as required by the 
Ralph M. Brown Act (Cal. Gov. Code 54950 – 54963), so that any member of the public may attend, participate, and 
watch the District’s legislative bodies conduct their business; and
 
WHEREAS, the Brown Act, Government Code section 54953(e), makes provisions for remote 
teleconferencing participation in meetings by members of a legislative body, without compliance with the 
requirements of Government Code section 54953(b)(3), subject to the existence of certain conditions; and
 
WHEREAS, a required condition is that a state of emergency is declared by the Governor pursuant to 
Government Code section 8625, proclaiming the existence of conditions of disaster or of extreme peril to the safety 
of persons and property within the state caused by conditions as described in Government Code section 8558; and
 
WHEREAS, a proclamation is made when there is an actual incident, threat of disaster, or extreme peril to 
the safety of persons and property within the jurisdictions that are within the City’s boundaries, caused by natural, 
technological, or human-caused disasters; and
 
WHEREAS, it is further required that state or local officials have imposed or recommended measures to 
promote social distancing, or, the legislative body meeting in person would present imminent risks to the health and 
safety of attendees; and
 
WHEREAS, such conditions now exist in the City, specifically, the Governor has proclaimed a state of 
emergency exists for the state of California due to the conditions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic and the impact 
on the health and safety of its residents; and
 
WHEREAS, the Delta and Omicron variants are highly transmissible in indoor spaces, the California 
Department of Public Health is currently investigating how long vaccine protection lasts, and the Center for Disease 
Control and Prevention recommends avoiding indoor spaces that do not offer fresh air from the outdoors; and  
WHEREAS, in accordance with Assembly Bill 361, the City Council does hereby find that as a result of the 
emergency, meeting in person would present imminent risks to the health or safety of attendees and the Council 
desires to authorize continued remote teleconferenced meetings of its legislative bodies; and
WHEREAS, as a consequence of the local emergency, the City Council does hereby find that the legislative 
bodies of City of San Mateo shall conduct their meetings without compliance with paragraph (3) of subdivision (b) of 
Government Code section 54953, as authorized by subdivision (e) of section 54953, and that such legislative bodies

SUBJECT:
Fiscal Year 2021-22 Comprehensive Fee Schedule – Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to amend the fiscal year 2021-22 Comprehensive Fee Schedule to include changes previously made to 
the Art in Public Places In Lieu Fee, Childcare Development Impact Fee, and Transportation Improvement Fee, as well as 
the establishment of the Wastewater Capacity Charge.   
BACKGROUND:
In fiscal year 2019-20, the San Mateo City Council included a review of the City’s development impact fees as a priority in 
its strategic plan. Over the last few years, City staff have worked with two teams of consultants to analyze and update the 
City’s impact and in-lieu fees, which culminated in the presentation of a series of recommendations to City Council made 
on November 1, 2021. 
At that meeting on November 1, 2021, City Council adopted Resolution No. 111 (2021), which established a Wastewater 
Capacity Charge and updated the Transportation Improvement Fee, the Childcare Development Impact Fee, and the Art in 
Public Places In Lieu Fee. After adoption of the accompanying Ordinance No. 2021-24 on November 15, 2021, the new fee 
changes are scheduled to go into effect once the 60-day waiting period expires on January 14, 2022.
The proposed resolution (Attachment 1) updates the City’s Comprehensive Fee Schedule to account for those changes, 
making them more accessible to staff and developers.  The fees were previously established and will take effect as 
scheduled.  This action is to incorporate the previously-established fees into the City’s fee schedule.  The affected pages of 
the City’s comprehensive fee schedule are attached (Attachment 2).  The City’s complete Comprehensive Fee Schedule is 
available at cityofsanmateo.org/1030/Comprehensive-Fee-Schedule.
BUDGET IMPACT:
Future revenue for the impact and in-lieu fees in question will depend on the amount of future construction in San Mateo. 
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
The Comprehensive Fee Schedule amendment for fiscal year 2021-22 is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is a 
government fiscal activity which does not involve any commitment to any specific project which may result in a potentially 
significant physical impact on the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(4).)

BACKGROUND:
Development Impact Fees
The City of San Mateo assesses four development impact fees – Wastewater Treatment Plant Phase II Expansion Fee, 
Transportation Improvement Fee, South Trunk Area Sewer Improvement Fee, and Park Impact Fee.
Local governments often charge fees as a condition of approval for development projects to fund public improvements in 
order to compensate for the demands that the developments have on public resources. These fees are commonly known 
as development impact fees. In 1989, the State Legislature passed Assembly Bill 1600 (AB 1600), which added Sections 
66000 et seq. to the California Government Code, commonly known as the Mitigation Fee Act. Government Code Section 
66006 requires that the City make available to the public the following information regarding development impact fees 
within 180 days after the end of each fiscal year:
1.
A brief description of the type of fee.
2.
The amount of the fee.
3.
The fee’s beginning and ending balances for the fiscal year.
4.
Total fees collected and the interest earned, if applicable.
5.
Identification of each public improvement on which impact fees were expended and the amount of expenditure 
on each improvement, including the total percentage of the cost of the public improvement that was funded with 
impact fees.
6.
Identification and timeline of public improvements any unexpended fees will be used for.
a.
This information is listed in Exhibit A to the Resolution. Exhibit A shows the five-year plan for public 
improvement projects and their anticipated expenditures related to the Mitigation Fee Act and Quimby 
Act. Specific details on each of the projects listed in Exhibit A can be found in the five-year capital 
improvement program section in the City’s 2021-22 Budget, which was adopted by City Council on June 
21, 2021.
7.
Description and uses for transfers or loans of fees, if applicable.
8.
Amounts of any refunds and allocations.
Further, the Mitigation Fee Act requires that findings describing the continuing need for impact fees be made every five 
years specifying the intended use of any unexpended impact fees, regardless of whether the fees are committed or

In-Lieu Fees
In-lieu fees are fees that are collected by the City instead of requiring developers to provide or construct specified 
improvements or amenities with their projects. The City of San Mateo assesses five in-lieu fees – Parking In-Lieu Fee, Art in 
Public Places Fee, Tree Replacement Fee, Below Market Rate Housing Fee, and Park In-Lieu Fee.
Park In-Lieu Fees are assessed pursuant to the Quimby Act under Government Code Section 66477. The other in-lieu fees 
do not have statutory reporting requirements but are included in this report for informational purposes. Under the 
Quimby Act, local governments may require the dedication of land for parks or the payment of fees in-lieu of land 
dedication as a condition of subdivision map approval. While park in-lieu fees are exempt from the reporting requirements 
included in the Mitigation Fee Act, these fees have separate Quimby Act reporting requirements, specifically:
1.
The City must develop a schedule specifying the use of land or fees to develop park or recreational facilities to 
serve the residents of the subdivision. 
2.
Any Quimby fees collected must be committed within five years after the payment of the fees or the issuance of 
building permits on one-half of the lots created by the subdivision, whichever occurs later. 
3.
If not committed, the fees shall be distributed and paid to the record owners of the subdivision in the same 
proportion that the size of the lot bears to the total area of lots within the subdivision (Gov. Code section 
66477(a)(6)).
Linkage Fees
Linkage fees are exactions for social needs indirectly related to development projects. The City of San Mateo assesses two 
linkage fees – Child Care Development Fee and Affordable Housing Commercial Linkage Fee. While these are linkage fees, 
they are included in this report with reporting requirements under the Mitigation Fee Act for informational purposes and 
to make sure that information is available in the event that a court rules in the future that linkage fees are subject to the 
reporting requirements of the Mitigation Fee Act.
This report complies with requirements of the Mitigation Fee Act and the Quimby Act outlined above.

Development Impact Fees
Wastewater Treatment Plant Phase II Expansion Fee
In April 2005, construction began to increase capacity of the wastewater treatment plant, and construction was 
substantially completed in the spring of 2009. Funds from the Sewer Enterprise Fund were used to pay for the expansion 
project. Under a Joint Powers Agreement (JPA), the treatment plant is partially owned by the Estero Municipal 
Improvement District (Foster City). Based on San Mateo’s share of the treatment plant and based on the total projected 
cost attributable to development based on the 2005 expansion study, the amount that is to be recovered through 
development fees is $3.52 million in total.
The Wastewater Treatment Plant Phase II Expansion Fee is imposed on all development that results in a change of use and 
that adds more sewage to the City’s sewage treatment system. The fee took effect on July 1, 2006 and will continue to be 
charged until $3.52 million is collected. Funds collected are transferred annually to the fund balance of the Sewer 
Enterprise Fund as a repayment to the Fund for the expansion cost. As shown in the 2020-21 Comprehensive Fee 
Schedule, the fee is $2.34 per gallon of average daily flow, which is determined by reference to site sewer study or 
engineering standards.

Tree Replacement Fee
San Mateo Municipal Code Chapter 27.71 establishes a fee on all development requiring approval of a planning 
application, except for single-family dwelling design review applications, for the removal of existing trees on development 
sites. The fee is imposed if the trees to be removed are not made up with replacement trees on-site. As shown in the 2020-
21 Comprehensive Fee Schedule, the fee is charged $475 per landscape unit, which is a unit of measurement that indicates 
the value of each tree that is to be removed relative to the tree that is to be replaced. 
Under San Mateo Municipal Code Chapter 13.35, the City may also impose a fee on any person or entity for removing 
trees that encroach on City streets. The fee is imposed if the tree to be removed is not replanted with a like tree satisfying 
City requirements. The fee is to be used by the City for plantings on public land. As shown in the 2020-21 Comprehensive 
Fee Schedule, the tree replacement cost is $475 per tree.

SUBJECT:
Classification Updates - Department of Public Works
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to approve the 1) job specification and salary range for the new classification of Solid Waste 
Technician, 2) reallocation of the vacant Traffic Engineering Technician position to Solid Waste Technician, 3) 
reinstatement of the Downtown Coordinator classification, 4) job specification and salary range for the classification of 
Downtown Coordinator, and 5) the addition of one Full Time Equivalent Merit Downtown Coordinator position in the 
Department of Public Works Solid Waste Section.
BACKGROUND:
Per San Mateo Municipal Code Section 2.57.030(d), classification updates become effective upon approval by the City 
Council.
Create a Solid Waste Technician classification in the Department of Public Works
Historically, the Solid Waste Division of Public Works utilized a full-time merit Traffic Engineering Technician classification 
to perform a variety of operational responsibilities in support of solid waste, public nuisance, and public right-of-way 
programs.  The Traffic Engineering Technician position was left vacant in November 2020 and has been filled with a per-
diem Engineering Technician I, who performs solid waste program tasks.  The Solid Waste Division’s utilization of the 
Traffic Engineering Technician position is not consistent with the classification’s defined roles and responsibilities and 
there is a need to create a new classification for the specialized technical work required.
 
The proposed new Solid Waste Technician position will replace the Traffic Engineering Technician position and will be 
responsible for the implementation of the illegal dumping program, anti-littering efforts, and solid waste tasks related to 
SB 1383 and the Municipal Regional Stormwater Permit.  The salary range for Solid Waste Technician will match the 
monthly salary range for Traffic Engineering Technician at $6,439.21 – $7,678.52, which includes a 1% salary increase per 
the San Mateo City Employees Association (SMCEA) Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) effective January 9, 2022.
Reinstate, and update the job specification for, the classification of Downtown Coordinator and add one (1) Full Time 
Equivalent (FTE) Merit Downtown Coordinator position in the Department of Public Works
The Downtown Coordinator classification was a full-time merit position staffed out of the City Manager’s Office (CMO).  
Due to changing responsibilities of the position and budget constraints, the position was abolished in the CMO and was 
filled with a per-diem in the Department of Public Works.  The Downtown Coordinator performs a variety of tasks to 
implement the City’s efforts to support businesses, support of Public Works operations, and improve or maintain a positive 
customer experience in downtown San Mateo and other business districts.
The Department of Public Works is requesting to reinstate the Downtown Coordinator classification, update its job 
specification, and add one (1) FTE Merit Downtown Coordinator position to better align with the current and future needs 
40 of 171

CITY OF SAN MATEO
of the Department.  Adding a merit Downtown Coordinator position with a dedicated liaison will reaffirm the City’s 
commitment to the downtown area and provide careful oversight of the downtown cleaning contractor, as well as provide 
research into options for enhanced cleaning and improved downtown customer experience. The monthly salary range for 
the full-time merit Downtown Coordinator is $6,720.52 – $8,012.53 which includes a 1% salary increase per the SMCEA 
MOU effective January 9, 2022.
BUDGET IMPACT:
The current Traffic Engineering Technician is fully funded by the Solid Waste Fund. If the reallocation of the Traffic 
Engineering Technician to a Solid Waste Technician is approved by Council, the position will remain fully funded by the 
Solid Waste Fund and will require no supplemental budget appropriation.
The current Downtown Coordinator per-diem position is funded by the Solid Waste Fund.  If approved by Council, 
reinstating the Downtown Coordinator to a merit position would result in an estimated $27,000 increase for the remainder 
of the fiscal year.  However, no appropriation is needed, as these costs can be absorbed by the Solid Waste Fund. The 
associated salary and benefits for the Downtown Coordinator will be incorporated into the 2022-24 Business Plan.
There is no budget impact associated with updating the job specification for the classification of Downtown Coordinator.

SUBJECT:
Citywide Street Reconstruction – Agreement 
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with Nichols Consulting Engineers, Chtd. for design services for the Citywide Street Reconstruction, 
Phases 4 and 5, in an amount not to exceed $1,460,000; establish a contingency reserve of $146,000; and authorize the 
Public Works Director to execute the agreement in substantially the form presented and approve amendments within the 
contingency amount. 
BACKGROUND:
After San Mateo voters passed Measure S in November 2015, City Council approved the Smooth Streets Program 
(Program) to repair streets with a Pavement Condition Index under 40 using this funding source. Of the twenty 
(20) estimated miles of roadway included in the Program, approximately six (6) more miles remain to be incorporated into 
a capital improvement project. The remaining streets are planned for completion as part of two final construction 
packages– Citywide Street Reconstruction Phase 4 (construction starting in 2023) and Citywide Street Reconstruction 
Phase 5 (construction starting in 2024). 
The remaining street segments in the Program are the most complex and will require a significant amount of geotechnical 
investigation and engineering. Therefore, staff issued a request for proposals for engineering design and geotechnical 
services on September 17, 2021 for the two final phases (Phases 4 and 5) of the Program. Six proposals were received, 
three of the firms were interviewed, and Nichols Consulting Engineers, Chtd. (NCE) was selected as best qualified for this 
effort. NCE has a very strong background in roadway reconstruction, including having geotechnical and pavement 
specialists on its staff. They provided examples of previous projects of similar scope and complexity and also presented a 
thorough public outreach plan that includes public meetings to address questions and concerns from the public. 
Given the condition of the remaining street segments, the complexity of design, and variety of potential reconstruction 
techniques that could be used, staff proposes to design both phases concurrently to ensure the sequence of 
implementation appropriately aligns with method of reconstruction needed for each street segment.  The proposed fee of 
$1,460,000 is approximately 8% of the estimated $18.34 million construction cost for the final two phases in the Program 
and covers both phases of design as well as engineering services during bidding and construction. Staff finds the proposed 
fee reasonable and recommends entering into an agreement with NCE in a not-to-exceed amount of $1,460,000 and 
establishing a contingency of $146,000 (10% of the contract budget) to cover any unforeseen changes during the design 
process.  
BUDGET IMPACT:
Sufficient funds are available in the Citywide Street Reconstruction Package 1 Project to award this agreement and fund 
the contingency reserve.

SUBJECT:
Mechanical and Automated Parking Facilities – Updated Standards and Specifications 
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to update the City’s “Standard Drawings and Specifications” for off-street parking to include standards 
and specifications for mechanical and automated off-street parking facilities. 
BACKGROUND:
Mechanical and automated parking systems allow for the parking and storage of vehicles with less area and/or volume 
than is required for traditional surface parking and can offer a means to reduce construction costs that often constrain a 
project’s feasibility.  The City has adopted “Standard Drawings and Specifications” for off-street parking (see Attachment 2) 
that are intended for parking garages and parking lots and were not developed with these types of equipment in mind. 
Adherence to these existing standards when reviewing mechanical and automated facilities precludes a majority of 
alternative parking systems and/or requires costly customizations, which can significantly impact the cost reduction 
benefits of these systems.  
The City initiated a Zoning Code amendment in 2018 to establish standards for alternative parking systems.  Draft 
development standards were reviewed by the Planning Commission in 2018 and 2019; however, this effort has been on 
hold since 2019 due to staffing limitations and a need to focus on other Planning Division initiatives and priorities. 
DISCUSSION:
Due to new projects seeking mechanical parking lift systems, staff is proposing that the City’s “Standard Drawings and 
Specifications” for off-street parking be updated via resolution based on the immediate need to establish objective 
standards for mechanical and automated parking facilities in multi-family and mixed-use residential projects.  These 
standards are intended to be interim in nature and establish basic requirements with which to assess these alternative 
parking systems.  It is anticipated that comprehensive development standards for mechanical and automated parking 
facilities will be brought back to the Planning Commission and City Council for consideration in mid-to-late 2022. 
Planning Commission Recommendation
On December 14, 2021, the Planning Commission held a public hearing to consider the draft standards for mechanical and 
automated parking facilities.  After discussing the item, the Commission voted 4-1 to recommended approval of an 
amended resolution (based on staff’s suggestions) to the City Council.  Amendments recommended for approval included: 

Changing all references of stall and/or platform dimensions to free and clear vehicle bay dimensions. 


PARKING STANDARDS 
MECHANICAL AND AUTOMATED PARKING SYSTEMS 
All mechanical and automated parking systems must comply with the specific standards and 
requirements set forth below, in addition to all applicable provisions of the City of San Mateo Municipal 
Code.  
 
DEFINITIONS 
1. MECHANICAL PARKING is a parking facility that uses mechanical devices or systems to retrieve 
or store one or more vehicle(s) by means of a lifting mechanism that vertically and/or 
horizontally stacks and stores the vehicle(s).   
 
2. AUTOMATED PARKING is a parking facility where vehicular storage and retrieval is accomplished 
entirely through a mechanical conveyance system that does not require an attendant to park a 
vehicle(s). 
APPLICABLITY  
 
1. LOCATIONS PERMITTED.  A mechanical and/or automated parking facility(ies) may be used to 
satisfy off-street parking requirements for residential uses in multi-family or mixed-use 
developments located outside of R1 (single-family) zoned areas.  Such facilities are not 
permitted on surface lots and must be fully enclosed within a building or undergrounded. 
 
2. COMPACT PARKING STALLS.  Required parking provided in mechanical and/or automated 
parking facility(ies) are not considered compact stalls and are not subject to compact stall 
limitations outlined in Municipal Code Section 27.64.265. 
VEHICLE BAY DIMENSIONS 
All required off-street parking provided in a mechanical and/or automated parking facility(ies) shall be 
designed to conform with the following: 
1. Width.  Vehicle bays shall have a minimum unobstructed width of 6-feet 9-inches. 
2. Length.  Vehicle bays shall have a minimum unobstructed length of 17-feet 5-inches. 
3. Vertical Clearance.  Any vehicle loading bay or area where persons exit and/or enter a vehicle 
shall provide a minimum vertical clearance height of 6-feet 8-inches.  All other vehicles bays 
shall have a minimum clearance height of 5-feet.   
4. Backup Space.  A minimum of 24-feet of backup space shall be provided for all vehicle bays that 
backout into a drive aisle.  
PERFORMANCE STANDARDS  
Mechanical and/or automated parking systems shall be subject to the following:  
1. Mechanical and/or automated parking facilities may not be used to provide off-street visitor, 
loading, or accessible parking spaces.

SUBJECT:
Updated Standards and Specifications for Mechanical and Automated Parking Facilities
RECOMMENDATION:
Recommend to the City Council adoption of a Resolution to update the City’s “Standard Drawings and Specifications” to 
include standards and specifications for mechanical and automated off-street parking facilities. 
BACKGROUND:
As a way to provide required parking within constrained urban areas, the use and adoption of automated and/or 
mechanical parking facilities has been on the rise throughout California and the Bay Area.  As development costs for 
projects continue to increase, implementation of these alternative parking systems can offer a way to reduce development 
costs that constrain a project’s feasibility.  Currently, automated and/or mechanical parking facilities are not explicitly 
addressed by the Zoning Code but have, on occasion, been permitted within the City of San Mateo.  The most recent 
example of a project with a mechanical parking facility is the multi-family residential building at 1919 O’Farrell Street that 
was approved by the Planning Commission on October 26, 2021.  In addition, staff has had discussions with multiple other 
developers and applicants over the past few years who have expressed interest in utilizing mechanical and/or automated 
parking systems to meet off-street parking requirements.  
Existing Parking Standards
The City of San Mateo’s current off-street parking standards are contained in Article 1 of Chapter 27.64 of the Zoning Code 
and establish parking requirements for various uses, minimum parking stall dimensional requirements and access 
standards, among other things.  Section 27.64.120 (Stall Dimensions) stipulates that “all required off-street parking stalls 
shall have a vertical clearance, length and width in conformity with the requirements of the City “Standard Drawings and 
Specifications,” in addition to the requirements contained in the Zoning Code.  The current “Standard Drawings and 
Specifications” for off-site parking, which were previously adopted via resolution, are included as Attachment 2. These 
were developed for parking stalls on paved streets, surface parking lots, or in garages, and did not anticipate new parking 
system technologies.  Currently, automated and/or mechanical parking systems would have to meet the requirements 
specified in the attachment or request a concession or waiver.  Such requirements preclude a majority of alternative 
parking systems, which contain vehicle storage compartments and function differently than traditional parking stalls,  
and/or require costly customizations which can significantly reduce the cost reduction benefits of these systems.  
2018-2019 Code Amendment Initiative
Prompted by the increasing use and adoption of mechanical and automated parking facilities to provide required parking, 
the City initiated a Zoning Code amendment in 2018 to establish standards for these alternative parking systems.  The City 
retained the services of Walker Consultants, a parking design and consulting firm, to assist staff with the development of 
the mechanical and automated parking standards.  On November 13, 2018, the Planning Commission held a study session, 
to review draft development standards for mechanical and automated parking facilities and receive public input.  Based on

the feedback received at the study session, revised development standards were brought back to the Planning Commission 
on March 12, 2019 for review and a City Council recommendation.  Following public comments and discussion, the 
Commission voted to continue the proposed code amendments to a date uncertain with a request for more information 
on system requirements for residential developments.  The minutes from both Commission meetings are included as 
Attachment 3.  This initiative has been on hold since the March 2019 Planning Commission meeting due to staffing 
limitations and a need to focus on other Planning initiatives and priorities. 
DISCUSSION: 
Under Municipal Code Section 27.64.010, City “Standard Drawings and Specifications” are adopted by resolution of the 
City Council.  Consistent with the Code, and based on the immediate need to establish objective standards for mechanical 
and automated parking facilities in multi-family residential projects, staff is proposing that the “Standard Drawings and 
Specifications” referenced in Section 12.76.120 be updated via resolution.  The proposed mechanical and automated 
parking facilities standards, which are included in Attachment 1, are based on the draft code language that was reviewed 
by the Commission in 2018-2019.  These standards are intended to be interim and establish basic dimensional and 
performance requirements that can be applied to multi-family residential projects.  It is anticipated that comprehensive 
development standards for mechanical and automated parking facilities will be drafted and brought back to the Planning 
Commission for consideration in mid-2022. 
For reference, automated and mechanical parking facilities are defined as follows:

An automated parking facility is a parking facility where vehicular storage and retrieval is accomplished entirely 
through a mechanical conveyance system that does not require an attendant to park a vehicle. 

A mechanical parking facility is a parking facility that uses mechanical devices or systems to retrieve or store one or 
more vehicle(s) by means of a lifting mechanism that vertically and/or horizontally stacks and stores the vehicle(s).  
Interim Development Standards
As further detailed in Attachment 1, it is recommended that all mechanical and/or automated parking facilities conform 
with the following: 
Stall Dimensions

Width.  Platform widths shall have a minimum unobstructed width of 6-feet 9-inches.

Length.  Platform lengths shall have a minimum unobstructed length of 17-feet 5-inches.

Vertical Clearance.  A minimum vertical clearance height of 7-feet for any vehicle loading bay or area 
where persons exit and/or enter a vehicle, unless otherwise approved by the Building Official.  All other 
stalls shall have a minimum clearance height of 5-feet.

Backup Space.  A minimum of 24-feet of backup space for all stalls that backout into a drive aisle.
Performance Standards 

Mechanical and/or automated parking facilities may satisfy off-street parking requirements for residential 
uses in multi-family or mixed-use-developments located outside of R1 (single-family) zoned areas and 
must be fully enclosed within a building or undergrounded.

Onsite signage to identifying maximum vehicle heights permitted within the mechanical and/or automated 
parking facility. 

On-call maintenance service agreement for the life of the use on the property. 

Annual maintenance inspection reports for the lifetime of the facility.

Submittal of an operations plan to include information on retrieval efficiency, operational details, 
emergency backup power, and schematic/technical drawings upon application.

SUBJECT:
Pedestrian Mall – Streetscape Design Agreement and Appropriation
RECOMMENDATION:
Award an agreement for design services to Community Design + Architecture for the pedestrian mall streetscape design in 
the amount of $145,485 and authorize the City Manager to execute the contract in substantially the form presented; 
adopt a resolution to appropriate $160,485 from the General Fund available fund balance; and provide feedback on the 
proposed placement of a racial equity art project within the pedestrian mall.
BACKGROUND:
During the COVID-19 pandemic, the City piloted street closures on portions of B Street using temporary barricades to allow 
for outdoor dining and expanded public open space. Based on the success of the pilot, the City committed to permanently 
closing two adjacent segments of B Street between 1st and 2nd Avenues and 2nd and 3rd Avenues through the Pedestrian 
Mall Act process. The ordinance closing the street segments to establish the pedestrian malls was adopted at the October 
4, 2021 City Council meeting.
The pedestrian malls currently have temporary water-filled barricades closing off the streets, which has made deliveries to 
the businesses and garbage service challenging in addition to not being visually appealing. During the establishment 
process, staff recommended to Council that the City complete the capital improvements needed for the permanent 
operation of the pedestrian malls in two separate phases. The first phase, to be funded directly by the City in the short-
term, will include the installation of bollards, traffic signal modification, pavement striping and painting, ADA access 
enhancements, removable planters and other lower-cost improvements to replace the temporary barricades and beautify 
the street segments. 
The second phase would include much more extensive improvements, including a complete reconfiguration of the two 
street segments likely raising the street to the level of the sidewalk, reconfiguring storm water drainage, permanent 
planters, and other more costly improvements. Given the high projected cost of this phase, which is estimated to be 
several million dollars,  it is anticipated to occur some time out in the future and may require the formation of an 
assessment district to help support the expense. 
Staff issued an RFP to streetscape design firms to develop construction design documents for the first phase of 
improvements. Staff received 11 proposals from interested firms. A panel from the City Manager’s Office and Public Works 
reviewed the proposals and selected Community Design + Architecture for this project. Their proposal stood out in two key 
ways: their experience in creating low-cost but visually striking street closure installations in other communities and the 
experience of the sub-consultants on the team in handling the necessary traffic signal and intersection adjustments 
needed to ensure that pedestrian and bicycle safety and access is maintained.
127 of 171

--- page 128 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
The agreement with Community Design + Architecture is for the production of construction design drawings and 
engineering estimates that will establish the project budget and be used to solicit construction bids. During the design 
process, the City will be working closely with local stakeholders, including the businesses and property owners along the 
pedestrian malls and the Downtown San Mateo Association, to identify design preferences and key logistical issues that 
need to be resolved. The intention is to bring a conceptual design to City Council for review and comment before moving 
forward with the final construction design. It is likely that there will be several design options for consideration at the 
conceptual phase that will inform the final design and overall construction budget.
The City did not anticipate this project in the current adopted Capital Improvement Program (CIP) budget. As a result, a 
new CIP project, Pedestrian Mall Improvements, has been created for this effort. The design phase of this project requires 
an appropriation of $160,485 to cover the cost of the agreement as well as the necessary Public Works staff time to review 
and comment on the plans. Staff recommends appropriating these funds from the available fund balance from the General 
Fund. An additional future appropriation will be required for the construction of this project. Staff will request this 
appropriation once the construction budget has been identified following the completion of the design work.
Racial Equity Community Art Project
The City Council Strategic Plan includes a racial equity community art project. To date, work on this effort has been stalled 
due to the difficulty in identifying a site for the art installation. The new pedestrian malls create a unique opportunity to 
locate this art installation in the form of a mural painted directly onto the street, possibly in the center pedestrian area on 
one or both blocks. Staff presented this concept to the Civic Arts Committee at their November meeting and they 
enthusiastically supported the idea. If supported by the City Council, the concept will be incorporated into the design plans 
for the pedestrian malls. The identification of the artist acting on behalf of the City and artwork would occur through a 
separate process with feedback from both the Civic Arts Committee and the soon to be established Diversity and Equity 
Taskforce.  Placement of the mural on the street will be by the City rather than a private party and as such will be 
“government speech.” The street will not be opened up as a public forum for private speech through this action, which 
therefore will not subject to a related analysis under the First Amendment. 
BUDGET IMPACT:

RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on operational impacts and other City efforts related to COVID-19, confirm rules and regulations issued 
by the City Manager acting as the Director of Emergency Services in response to declared emergency, and confirm the 
need for a continued local declaration of emergency. 
BACKGROUND:
On March 4, 2020, the Governor proclaimed a state of emergency in response to the outbreak of respiratory illness due to 
a novel coronavirus (COVID-19). As a consequence of this proclamation the City Manager, acting as the City’s Director of 
Emergency Services, was authorized to make and issue rules and regulations on matters reasonably related to the 
protection of life and property as affected by such emergency. In accordance with Government Code Section 8558(c), the 
City Council declared a local health emergency on March 16, 2020. Per Government Code Section 8630(c), the City Council 
is required to review the need for continuing the local emergency at least once every 60 days until the City Council 
terminates the declaration of local emergency. 
In addition to positioning the City for potential mutual aid and cost recovery opportunities, the emergency proclamation 
also provides the City Manager broader authority to be able to expeditiously address issues related to the emergency. 
With that said, actions taken by the City Manager outside of the general authority of the duties provided for in the City 
Charter are subject to confirmation by Council as soon as practicable. 
State and local emergency proclamations are still in effect as we continue to be impacted by the pandemic.  With cases 
rising as a result of the latest variant and local health orders remaining in place, City operations will continue to need to 
adjust to local health conditions. As such, it is recommended that Council approve another 60-day extension of the local 
emergency proclamation, which would keep it in effect in January and February.

There is no budget impact for receiving this informational report.
