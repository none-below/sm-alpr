SUBJECT:
Consideration of Fine Elimination at City Libraries 
RECOMMENDATION:
Review the elimination of fines within the San Mateo Public Library system and provide direction as to whether fines 
should be permanently eliminated.
BACKGROUND:
Historically, libraries have used nominal fines as an incentive for patrons to return library materials on time. As a matter of 
principle, public libraries have never sought to use fines to punish patrons or to generate significant revenue to support 
operations.  Over the last decade, the San Mateo Public Library’s (SMPL) fine revenue has been trending down 
dramatically as our peer libraries in the Peninsula Library System (PLS) have enacted steps to increase access for patrons, 
especially those facing financial limitations.  In fact, over the past ten years, practically every major public library system in 
the nation, including PLS, has eliminated fines for senior, teen, and children’s materials. Several years ago, PLS instituted 
automated renewal of items that had no holds on them, which greatly reduced the chances of patrons accumulating fines 
because they no longer had to remember exact due dates. 
Furthermore, many public libraries have also decided that the process of collecting fines was one of “spending dollars to 
collect pennies” when viewed from the lens of the amount staff time spent on the task. SMPL staff believe they can be 
more efficient and effective in overall service provision without having to pursue small fine sums from patrons.   Staff 
research indicates that fine elimination over the past several years has not resulted in a significant change in patron 
behavior.  This is supported by the monitoring of behaviors during the pandemic when SMPL and all PLS members paused 
fines.  The vast majority of patrons who usually returned their items on time have continued to do so. The patrons who 
were prone to not returning items on time continue to do so, but that results in them losing borrowing privileges as 
opposed to accumulating fines.  Currently, SMPL is one of just two PLS libraries still collecting fines. The other one, Daly 
City, is also in the process of a fine elimination review. 
The Library Board considered this policy change in late 2021 and was supportive of eliminating fines for library materials.
BUDGET IMPACT:
In fiscal year 2018-19, the last full year of library operations, San Mateo Public Library collected $55,640 in late fines, which 
is down from just seven years ago, when SMPL collected $127,000.  This downward trend is expected to continue due to 
related developments in library services by all Peninsula Library System members, most notably is that fines have been 
eliminated by nearly all PLS members.  Further, since fines are still on pause during the pandemic, the fiscal impact of fine 
elimination has already effectively been incorporated into the operating budget.

SUBJECT:
Consideration of Support for Single-Payer Healthcare 
RECOMMENDATION:
Discuss and consider directing staff to return to Council with a resolution in support of single-payer healthcare.
BACKGROUND:
At the November 15, 2021 Council meeting during reports and announcements, Council Member Lee brought up the 
concept of adopting a resolution of support for single-payer healthcare. A majority of Council agreed to discuss this at its 
January 3, 2022 meeting. 
Per Physicians for a National Health Program (PNHP), single-payer national health insurance, also referred to as Medicare 
for All, is a system in which a single public or quasi-public agency organizes healthcare financing but the delivery of care 
still largely remains in the private sector. Under this system, all residents of the United States would be covered for 
medically necessary services. In addition to all residents being covered, proponents of a single-payer system also cite other 
benefits including a healthier population overall due to access to preventive care and decoupling healthcare from your 
employment status. Critics of a single-payer system cite the cost of such a program, which would necessitate additional 
taxes and the potential for reduced government spending in other areas, as well as longer wait times to see specialists. 
In 2021, the City of South San Francisco adopted a resolution in support of single-payer healthcare. That resolution is 
included as Attachment 1 for reference.
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for considering a resolution in support of single-payer healthcare.

Resolution in support of Single-Payer Healthcare by South San Francisco City Council.
WHEREAS, every person in South San Francisco deserves high quality healthcare; and
WHEREAS, the number of Americans without health insurance before the COVID-19 pandemic was
still nearly 30 million, with more than 40 million Americans underinsured, despite important gains made since
the implementation of the Affordable Care Act; and
WHEREAS, the current COVID-19 pandemic has led to record levels of unemployment, loss of
employer-sponsored health insurance, a severely strained healthcare system, widespread illness, and taken a
profound toll on our community’s mental health, all of which is placing significant demands on our healthcare
system, and
WHEREAS, many people delay seeking needed healthcare due to an inability to pay, leading to a sicker
and poorer population in the long run; and
WHEREAS, such a population is significantly more likely to develop serious illness if exposed to
COVID-19 and will subsequently face higher mortality rates from the disease; and
WHEREAS, the ever-increasing costs of healthcare, which are further elevated due to the pandemic,
may challenge our already strapped state and municipal budgets; and
WHEREAS,COVID-19 related treatment could cost uninsured patients tens of thousands of dollars; and
WHEREAS, in order to equitably and effectively address the healthcare burden of the COVID-19 crisis,
we must urge the United States Congress and California State Legislature to expand health insurance coverage
to provide comprehensive coverage to every resident without any cost sharing; and
WHEREAS, Single-Payer Healthcare would provide health insurance for every person for all necessary
medical care including prescription drugs; hospital, surgical and outpatient services; primary and preventive
care; emergency services; reproductive care; dental and vision care; and long-term care; and
WHEREAS, Single-Payer Healthcare would provide coverage without copays, deductibles or other out-
of-pocket costs, and would slash bureaucracy, protect the doctor-patient relationship and assure patients a free
choice of doctors; and
WHEREAS, recent polls show that a majority of Americans support Medicare for All; and
WHEREAS, Single-Payer Healthcare will guarantee that all residents of South San Francisco will be
