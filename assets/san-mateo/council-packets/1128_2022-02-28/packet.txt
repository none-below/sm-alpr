5.   OLD BUSINESS
A.  Vote on Public Art Gift Proposal:  At the August 2021 Library Board meeting, community members 
Jackie and Vince Siminitus presented their proposal for adding a library-themed sculpture of their 
choosing to the grounds of the Main Library.  The Library Board formed a committee to review the 
proposal.  The committee shared its findings and recommendations with the rest of the Library Board at 
the September 2021 meeting.  The Board of Trustees voted on the proposal to accept the artwork 
donation and the vote failed 1-4.

Ayes:  Ms. Diaz Nash

Noes:  Ms. De Winter, Mr. Linhares, Ms. Wheeler, and Mr. Volz
Ms. Diaz Nash suggested that the Library Board review the Library’s Artwork Donation Policy & 
Guidelines in the future to see if any changes are needed, and to see how the Library’s policy integrates 
with the City’s policy - The City of San Mateo Civic Arts Committee: Guidelines for The Art in Public 
Places.
B.  Review of Library Board Charter: Assistant City Attorney Bahareh Abdollahi provided historical and 
legal overview of the Library Board Charter to enhance the Library Board of Trustees’ understanding of 
their advisory roles in shaping, guiding and supporting the Library’s policy decision-making process.  
The San Mateo City Charter was passed by voters in 1922, and the Library Board’s powers and duties 
are covered in Section 6.01 through 6.03.  Ms. Abdollahi explained that once a charter has been 
adopted, any future changes or amendments need to be voted on.  The City Council or the voters can 
make an initiative to put a charter amendment on the ballot.  In addition, Ms. Abdollahi shared that 
Chapter 2.15 of the San Mateo Municipal Code also has information on the organization, powers and 
duties of the Library Board.  In general, any decision-making authority lies with the executive team 
including the City Manager and the City Librarian, and the City Council has authority on legislative 
actions.  It is the intended role for all the City’s Boards and Commissions, including the Library Board, to 
serve in an advisory capacity by making recommendations to the executive team and to the elected 
officials. Ms. Abdollahi clarified that although the language in some parts of the Charter seems 
contradictory, it is necessary to follow the spirit of the document, and to look at the one hundred years 
of practice where the City’s Boards and Commissions have served in an advisory capacity.   
6.

Volz introduced themselves and shared a little about their professional backgrounds in education.  They 
both expressed how important the library is to the community, and that they are grateful to be able to 
serve as trustees.  Ms. De Winter, Mr. Linhares and Ms. Diaz Nash welcomed Ms. Wheeler and Mr. Volz, 
and advised them to not hesitate to contact any of them if they had any questions.
B. Appoint New Library Board Vice President:  President De Winter explained that based on the rotation 
schedule, it is Mr. Linhares’ turn to rotate into the Vice President position.  Ms. De Winter added that a 
vote is not needed for the rotation, and that the rotation will occur again in July when Mr. Linhares will 
rotate into the President position.  Mr. Linhares rotated into the Vice President position.  
C. Fiscal Year 2022-2023 Fee Schedule:  Senior Management Analyst Marcia Mendonca presented the 
revisions to the Library’s Fee Schedule for Fiscal Year 2022-2023 to the Library Board for their review 
and answered questions from the Trustees.  Mr. Moore advised that he has completed his presentation 
to the City Council regarding the elimination of overdue fines, but he does not have a date yet on when 
the Council will vote on it.  Mr. Moore advised the Board that if they wish to advocate for a vote to 
eliminate overdue fines, they can follow up with the City Council.  Mr. Moore added that the Library has

Report by City Librarian James Moore:

Mr. Moore thinks there is a strong chance that overdue fines will be eliminated.  San Mateo 
Public Library remains one of just two Peninsula Library System libraries that still officially 
charge overdue fines.

Like the Delta variant, the Omicron variant has slowed down library activity over the past 
several weeks.  The current hours at the Main Library seem to be meeting the public needs very 
well.  Feedback has been received from some patrons that opening earlier at the Hillsdale and 
Marina branches would be welcome.  Expanding branch hours is a priority, but approximately 
seventeen positions are currently unfilled.  Recruiting for part-time positions is especially 
challenging right now.  Options are being weighed to possibly combine some per diem positions 
to add hours and appeal to more potential candidates.

This past November preliminary documentation was submitted to be eligible for a Building 
Forward: Library Infrastructure Grant Program.  We have been advised the official grant 
application won’t be available until some time in February.  This is a matching grant program, 
and the funds would be used to correct the structural problems at the Marina Library.

Each month, the Children’s Department offers a service-oriented program for 1st through 3rd 
graders, Maker Wednesdays: Pay It Forward.  This program explores different ways kids can 
uplift and give back to their environment and community.  For January, Children’s Librarian 
Jordan Bumber organized a project where children created hand drawn mini portraits of 
African-American heroes and bundled them with an exciting fact about the hero’s life.  These 
artistic creations were placed inside the pockets of a DIY advent calendar that was donated to 
the San Mateo Senior Center.  Seniors will be able to uncover a different African-American hero 
each day during Black History Month in February.  Ms. Bumber is also evaluating the feasibility 
of starting a pen pal program between children and seniors.  This program also meets one of 
the Library’s Work Plan goals of Diversity, Equity, & Inclusion, not only culturally but also from 
an age standpoint.

Senior Management Analyst Marcia Mendonca will present the Annual Impact Report at the 
February meeting.

In 2020-2021, the San Mateo Public Library continued thoughtfully adapting to
the evolving circumstances created by COVID-19. In the process of adjusting our
services and seeking fresh ways to continue providing superb service, we
maintained our principles of engaging, educating, and empowering our community
by providing vital access, services, and resources. We strove to be a steady, reliable
presence during a period of lingering uncertainty. We have also been steadily
restoring hours and services with an eye towards a full return to pre-pandemic
operations as soon as circumstances permit. This colorful report provides a
summary of our impact from the past year. I hope you find it interesting, insightful

This pilot proposal will increase public access by 31 hours over current reduced Covid hours. Overall, it 
would result in the additional two additional hours of service over pre-covid service hours. I am 
recommending closing the main library one hour earlier M-Th, at 8p instead of 9pm, in exchange for 
providing two additional hours of public access on Sundays at Main and four more hours of service at 
the branches (two per branch, respectively). Combing several years of observation of patron usage 
patterns along with reviewing feedback from patron and staff surveys conducted in late 2021 lead me  
to conclude:
1.
The 8p-9p hour at the main library is often so slow that there as are many staff in the building as 
patrons. Conversely, many patrons have long expressed a desire for increased access on 
Sundays, where the main library currently open only 4 hours. Specific library-related programs 
can still occur after 8p with staff present.
2.
During the 8p-9p hour at main I have observed or found evidence of concerning and illegal 
activities in the underground parking lot, emergency stairwells, and public bathrooms that leave 
me thinking it is better to close at 8p M-Th. The darkness in the evening, unavoidably light 
staffing, and the many places to go unseen seems to draw some people seeking to commit 
inappropriate acts during that late hour.
3.
The lines outside the main library on Sundays and patron feedback strongly suggest that more 
families and patrons would like to visit the library earlier than 1p on Sundays. So, I propose we 
open two hours earlier at 11a on Sundays.
4.
Staff report it being dead time after 7p at the branches as well. So, instead of being open one 
evening per week until 8p, I am proposing that both branches are open two evenings per week 
until 6p and 7p respectively. This will increase access for school kids, families, and working 
people.
5.
To create more equitable access to SMPL resources, increasing hours at the branches by adding 
additional evenings and opening at 10a instead of noon Fridays is a good step. 
6.
Staffing vacancies and shortages still present a challenge and managers will require 2-3 weeks to 
analyze how to make these adjustments work. Scheduling is very complex for a large staff, and 
any adjustments in area will impact all service areas.
7.
Our MOU agreement with SEIU requires that staff receive a 30-notice before any changes to 
their schedules, so realistic it could now be well into before we can add or adjust service hours.
