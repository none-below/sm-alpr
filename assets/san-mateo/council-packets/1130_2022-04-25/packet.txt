Reports by Board Members:

Ms. De Winter attended the Foundation Board meeting.  Ms. De Winter shared that the May 
23rd Library Board meeting will likely be the first in-person meeting since February 2020.

Mr. Linhares did a couple of walk-throughs at the three Library locations and was impressed 
with how clean and inviting they are.  

Ms. Diaz Nash shared that she received inquiries from Library patrons who wanted to know if 
the Friends of the Library (FOL) book sale will be held this year.  Ms. Mendonca advised that 
due to the fluidity of the health issues related to the pandemic, the Library will not have a large 
sale this year.  Instead, there will be smaller sales throughout the year.  In addition, donations 
are currently accepted by appointment only, but in mid-April drop-in lobby donations will 
resume.  Lastly, in addition to books being sold in the FOL sale area at the Main Library, books 
are also sold on Amazon.

Mr. Volz has volunteered to be a judge for the youth poetry contest during National Library 
Week.

Ms. Wheeler shared that she is looking forward to attending the reception during National 
Library Week on April 6th.
Future agenda topic requests included inviting the winners of the youth poetry contest to a future 
Board meeting, and holding future Board meetings at the Hillsdale and Marina branches.  
Report by Deputy City Librarian Rukshana Singh:

Ms. Singh submitted a grant for 1.75 million dollars to the State Library for the structural 
repairs at Marina.  The City would be required to match funds for this project, and the entire 
project would cost just under 3.5 million dollars.  Ms. Singh worked collaboratively with Public 
Works to complete the application for this grant.  Funding for this grant is made possible by the 
SB-129 Budget Act of 2021, which allocated 4.39 million dollars in one-time funds to the CA 
State Library to address critical maintenance and life-safety needs at library facilities 
throughout the state.

A Mandarin language teacher from the Nueva School contacted the Library to arrange for a 
tour for her 11th and 12th grade students.  Library Assistant Anna Cao led the tour and informed 
the students about the Chinese language collection and how the Library makes selections.  The 
students also searched for books on their project on literature and social justice.

Effective April 11th, the Main Library will be open until 8:00 p.m. Monday through Thursday, 
and there will be extended evening and morning hours at both Hillsdale and Marina.  The pilot 
will run for six months to allow time to assess if the hours will fit community needs.  Service 
hours are not being added to the Sunday schedule at this time due to staffing and scheduling 
logistics that need to be resolved.

Over 100 employees throughout Pacific Library Partnership (PLP) member libraries (including 
20 employees from San Mateo Public Library) participated in a virtual homelessness training 
with Ryan Dowd, who is a national expert on working with people experiencing homelessness.  
The training was funded through a grant from PLP.

There were events held at the Main Library to commemorate 80 years since the signing of 
Executive Order 9066 on February 19, 1942.  The order led to the incarceration of 120,000 
Japanese Americans during World War II.  Children’s Services featured authors Cynthia 
Kadohata and Amy Lee Thai, and there was a presentation with Steve Okamoto who talked 
about his family’s experience at the Tanforan Assembly Center.

Some recent Library events included the Music at Kohl Mansion family concert featuring Sound

History
• In 2004 the Foundation Board envisioned a Biotech Center. 
•$2 million was procured from the Genentech Foundation, as a one-time donation to the capital 
campaign. 
 
•An advisory committee made of community members, educators, local doctors, and two 
Genentech librarians developed the original vision for the Center. 
•The Genentech librarians compiled a list for the original core collection and money was put into 
a trust fund for future collection development.

History
• A former librarian from biotech company Gilead was hired a few months before the ribbon was 
cut at the new library. 
•In the first few years, the trust fund paid for collections, programs, and part of the librarian’s 
salary. 
• 4-5 yearly programs included talks by local physicians, advisory committee members, and 
individuals linked to committee members. 
• After a few years, the advisory committee dwindled, and programming decreased.

Moving Forward
• The current librarian overseeing the biotech center is Michelle Baute. 
•A pared down version of the advisory committee was restarted with Michelle’s hire.  
• The committee and Michelle meet a couple of times a year to brainstorm ideas.
• Michelle’s first 2 programs offered were on CRISPR and genetic genealogy. 
• Biotechnology resources: 
• Over 700 book titles
• Scientific and medical titles not usually found in a public library, include New Scientist, New England 
Journal of Medicine, Journal of the American Medical Association, and Science.
• Electronic resources.

Process
• Michelle keeps up to date on and looks for topics that she thinks will be of interest and 
relevance to the community.
• Once potential topics are identified, Michelle conducts research to find scientists who are doing 
groundbreaking or interesting work. 
• Next comes time to make contact.
• It works best to plan programs a few months ahead to allow enough time for potential speakers 
to plan around their busy schedules.

• Where does Michelle find her speakers?

Goals
What are the goals of the Biotechnology Learning Center? 
• Spark curiosity and interest in science through diverse programming, regardless of attendees’ 
levels of science background. 
• Offer programs that offer a forum to learn and ask questions of experts.  
• Provide access to a unique collection of biotechnology-related books, journals, and reference 
materials, from popular titles to technical manuals.  
• Support local teachers and foster science interest among the next generation.

Review of Artwork Donation to SMPL
What is it: A bronze replica of the Frederic Remington "Coming Through The Rye" Bronze Sculpture, 
29"H x 30"L x 27"W. Weight: 150 lbs. Donated by San Mateo resident Steve McKay.
What is it worth: This has not yet been firmly established. The piece was insured for $8,000 when 
shipped and then San Mateo Public Library Foundation (SMPLF) Executive Director, Lynne Esselstein saw 
that online auctions range from $5K to over $20K+ (for this size).  Lynne initially suggested we use the 
insured value of $8K and found the same piece on Ebay priced at $7.8K. I did my own online search and 
found that replicas of this dimension are, indeed, around $5k and above. However, before her recent 
departure she said there is no firm way to value it because there are many replicas out there, and it is 
hard to tell which ones are considered of high enough quality to fetch a sale price in the thousands. The 
Library Foundation (LF) declined to formally value it when they accepted it. 
The proposal: The sculpture was donated to the Library Foundation in September 2020, then handed to 
San Mateo Public Library (SMPL). It’s been stored at the main library since then, but it has not been 
officially accepted as a gift, mostly due to the rush of events since the pandemic began. Mr. Mckay 
hoped the statue could be displayed at the library, but he agreed in an MOU that it will be solely up to 
us whether to do so or simply sell it for funds to support SMPL’s collection.
What to do next. Our options:
1.
Display sculpture permanently perhaps in the Western section
2.
Display it temporarily for several weeks or months, then sell it to raise money for our collection
3.
Do not display it all, the imagery struck a couple staffers who I asked about it as potentially 
controversial or offensive in a time of reckoning with racial prejudice and the highly unbalanced 
socio-economic landscape it has contributed to.
4.
Despite his signing an MOU that agrees to SMPL never displaying the sculpture, as a courtesy, I 
am willing to considerable returning the sculpture to him if we choose to not display it all. 
Key questions:
1.
How we firmly assign a value of this sculpture?
2.
Should we display it: temporarily or long-term?
3.
Should seek to sell it or offer to return it?
Key Lessons:  I learned from this experience (and the recent review of the art policy apropos the 
recently proposed public art gift by two highly respected longtime library supporters) to be more 
mindful of greenlighting physical gifts to the LF on behalf SMPL. Wanting to be cooperative with LF’s 
efforts fundraise for SMPL, I thought at the time, “Sure, we’ll take it. If we don’t display it, we can just 
sell it.” Clearly, it is a more complicated process than that.
