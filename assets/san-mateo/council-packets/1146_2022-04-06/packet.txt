1. 2022 Community Funding Recommendations 
 
That the Community Relations Commission provide recommendations for organizations to receive Community 
Development Block Grant funding for the 2022-2023 and 2023-2024 Program Years. The Commission recommended 
an even distribution of funds for all community funding applicants. (Crowell/Boles; 5-0) 
 
Service providers were allowed 3 minutes each to do a short presentation of their services to the commission. The 
organizations present made the following comments: 
 
• 
Project Sentinel (Fair Housing Services): During the pandemic it has become more important and challenging 
than ever to provide fair housing services. Communicating with people and getting them together is more 
difficult with COVID restrictions and the barrier of obtaining and preserving affordable housing remains high. 
Project sentinel provides services that help people obtain and maintain housing. It is done through outreach, 
education, investigation and advocacy. They hope to find solutions with housing providers but will also 
enforce fair housing through administrative complaints and litigation at no cost to the tenants. They hope to 
create opportunities for housing for people regardless of protected class status. The vast majority of people 
their assist are in the extremely low income category. 
• 
Rebuilding Together Peninsula (Minor Home Repair): They have proposed receiving less money for the year-
round minor home repair programs in the next coming year while requesting an increase in funding for the 
special event for a total of the same of last year’s request. The reason is because as the housing stock 
continues to age, an increase in the need for larger repairs with larger volunteer groups will be the trend. 
The people they serve continue to struggle to stay in the area due to the high cost of living, they are 
longtime residents who make well below the average median income. More multigenerational living 
arrangements have been adopted. Being able to provide these repairs alleviates some the financial burden 
these families face. To continue adapting to COVID they have changed size of volunteer groups and use of 
contractors. 
• 
Rape Trauma Services (Advocacy – Victims of Sexual Violence): The pandemic has been devasting to families 
served by this program, particularly for underserved youth who rely on the adults at their school to disclose 
sexual abuse. Healing and ending the cycles of violence are tied by the emotional safety that stable housing 
brings. Most families they interacted with needed rehousing to create a healthy living environment. Without 
funds from San Mateo they would struggle to do long term healing work. They provide support through 
counseling, finding affordable housing, immigration assistance.  
• 
Legal Aid Society (Advocacy – Eviction Defense): They continue to try to navigate the changing laws related 
to housing during COVID. The current state of the housing market is greatly difficult for low to moderate 
income residents, given the high rents combined with the pandemic. Many evictions for nonpayment were 
filed immediately when the mortarium ended earlier this year. While rental assistance has been able to

BACKGROUND:
The Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) Program provides flexible funding for community development projects 
and programs to assist individuals and families from extremely low- to moderate-income households as defined by the 
Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). The City receives an entitlement grant based on a national 
formula and has participated in this program since 1977. The City also received an entitlement amount of HOME funds 
from 1994 to 2015 that was used to assist housing programs and projects for very low-income households. In 2016, the 
City joined a consortium with the County of San Mateo and the City of South San Francisco to pool our HOME funds to 
provide more efficiency and timeliness in expending those funds. The City still collects loan payments from loans made 
prior to 2015 and must re- program those funds into HOME-eligible projects. 2022-23 Annual Action Plan The Annual 
Action Plan outlines the City’s budget for the coming program year with details of the programs and projects that will be 
undertaken to address the needs identified in the Consolidated Plan. The City has not yet received an award letter from 
HUD detailing the exact amount of funding it will receive for the coming year. However, the CDBG grant is estimated to be 
$732,000 for the 2022-23 program year using past year averages. The following budget was drawn based upon this 
estimate and will be adjusted slightly. The remaining funds for the budget will come from program income and prior year 
resources. The annual plan also includes programs to address affordable housing and other community needs. The major 
CDBG activities are: • Administration – a maximum of 20% of the CDBG grant is eligible for administrative costs. The fair 
housing grant of $25,000 is included in the administration budget. • Community Funding – the cap for public services is 
15% of the grant and prior year program income. The Community Resources Commission identified nine non-profit 
agencies for provision of public services to meet priority needs at the previous CRC meeting, providing agencies with two 
year agreements. • Economic Development – the City will fund one grant for $25,000 for microenterprises assistance. • 
Minor Home Repair – the City will fund three providers for home repair, energy upgrades, and accessibility modifications 
to eligible households. • Street Improvement Project – the Department of Public Works will manage this project, 
prioritizing bicycle and pedestrian improvements located in the CDBG Priority Target Area consisting of the North 
Shoreview and North Central neighborhoods. • Public Facilities – the City will fund one grant of $100,000 for renovations 
to the San Mateo Free Clinic.

Executive Summary 
AP-05 Executive Summary - 91.200(c), 91.220(b)
1.
Introduction
The Annual Action Plan (Annual Plan) is required of the City of San Mateo by the U.S. Department of 
Housing and Urban Development (HUD) in order for the City to receive federal Community Development 
Block Grant (CDBG) funds. The City's Annual Plan for fiscal year 2022-23 is the fifth year of the multi-year 
2018-22 Consolidated Plan. The Annual Plan outlines needs and develops strategies to address priority 
needs of low- and moderate-income populations in San Mateo.
2.
Summarize the objectives and outcomes identified in the Plan  
This could be a restatement of items or a table listed elsewhere in the plan or a reference to 
another location. It may also contain any essential items from the housing and homeless needs 
assessment, the housing market analysis or the strategic plan.
The City's Consolidated Plan identifies the following needs and has developed goals and activities that 
further HUD's program goals. 
Need: Affordable Housing
Goal: Increase, maintain, and improve the supply of affordable housing for low- and moderate-income 
families and individuals. 
Activities: The City will use CDBG and local funds for development of housing, acquisition and/or 
rehabilitation of housing, minor home repairs, weatherization, and accessibility modifications. 
Additionally, the City will continue to administer its existing below-market rate housing rental and 
ownership units. 
Need: Public Facilities Improvements
Goal: Preserve and improve public facilities that serve a high percentage of low- and moderate-income 
families as well as those with special needs. 
Activities: The City will use CDBG funds, when feasible, to make accessibility modification to public 
facilities and to provide funds for rehabilitation of facilities with urgent needs. 
Need: Public Services

3.
Evaluation of past performance 
This is an evaluation of past performance that helped lead the grantee to choose its goals or 
projects.
The City recognizes the ongoing needs of the San Mateo community and continues to fund core service 
needs. All of the Community Funding programs have met or exceeded their goals for service, showing an 
ongoing need for the services they are providing the community. The cost of housing continues to be 
extremely high in San Mateo and as a result many of the activities focus on services related to housing.
The City's Minor Home Repair programs have been slower to meet their goals, but all three 
organizations and City staff agree that there is a great need for their services. One concern from the sub 
grantees is that the rising costs of doing projects paired with new logistical difficulties from the COVID-
19 pandemic have made it difficult to find and complete enough home repairs to stay on track of 
meeting goals.

The City of San Mateo does not receive Emergency Solutions Grant (ESG) funding, as the San Mateo 
County Department of Housing (DOH) is the sole recipient in the county. However, the City does 
participate in COC meetings as part of its ongoing responsibility in receiving HUD funding. DOH 
coordinates closely with the COC in regards to the County's ESG, COC, and CDBG funding and is 
represented on the COC Steering Committee. DOH has also delegated responsibility for management of 
all ESG funds, plus the portion of the County CDBG funds used for homeless activities, to the COC Lead

issuing requests for proposals for ESG funding, awarding contracts, gathering community input, data 
analysis, and contract monitoring. The COC Lead Agency is also the Homeless Management Information

Explain how federal funds will leverage those additional resources (private, state and local funds), including a description of how 
matching requirements will be satisfied
The City receives direct entitlement funds from Federal CDBG Program that are used to address identified needs. The City is also part of the San 
Mateo County HOME Consortium; HOME funds formerly granted directly to the City are now combined with the County of San Mateo and the 
City of South San Francisco in order to use the funds more efficiently. The City also receives HOME loan repayments from projects assisted with 
HOME funds provided prior to joining the consortium in 2015.
The City manages a varied portfolio of housing loans from several programs including Housing Rehabilitation and First Time Homebuyer 
programs as well as developer loans for new construction or acquisition/rehabilitation projects. Program income from this portfolio augments 
the CDBG and HOME funds as well as now defunct programs such as Redevelopment and former CalHOME owner rehab loan program. Loan 
repayments from loans funded with former Redevelopment funds are deposited in the Low Mod Housing Asset Fund to be used for future

affordable housing programs.  The City also sets aside the equivalent of 20% of the property tax increment attributed to the former 
Redevelopment areas for housing, known as “boomerang funds”. The City collects subordination processing fees and fractional unit fees 
associated with its Below Market Rate Program in its City Housing Fund, which is typically used to assist regional housing initiatives that are 
outside of the San Mateo’s city limits. 
The City Council adopted a commercial linkage fee ordinance which became effective on 11/5/16. New commercial projects with square footage 
in excess of 5,000 square feet pay a fee to mitigate the impact of new jobs on local housing supply.
The City also receives an annual allocation of the State Permanent Local Housing Allocation (PHLA) as provided by the Building Homes and Jobs 
Act to increase the supply of affordable housing.  It is estimated that the City will receive approximately $531,000 for 2021.  There are a number 
of new State programs that City will watch and apply for as they become available including Local Early Action Planning grants to support 
planning efforts to increase the supply of housing, as well as the Local Housing Trust Fund Program.  The City also tracks and supports legislation 
that promotes and increases resources for affordable housing.
Section 8 funds are provided to San Mateo residents through the San Mateo County Housing Authority. McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance 
Act funds are managed by the San Mateo County Human Services Agency as part of the Continuum of Care, in which San Mateo participates. The 
County of San Mateo also supports housing projects countywide with its HOME and CDBG funds which often are used to leverage City resources. 
The Housing Endowment and Regional Trust (HEART) of San Mateo County is a public/private partnership whose goal is to raise funds to create 
affordable housing opportunities in San Mateo County. 
Low-Income Housing Tax Credits are offered through the State on a competitive process and with the dissolution of Redevelopment, 
applications for these funds are expected to become even more in demand and competitive. The State also has also set aside a portion of the 
Cap and Trade funds for housing located close to transit in its Affordable Housing Sustainable Communities program. The City will support 
developer applications for tax credits and other State Housing programs as they become available for appropriate and eligible projects.

Housing Preferences and Distribution 
In light of the huge response, usually resulting in hundreds of applications for affordable housing 
projects, San Mateo adopted its Marketing Policies for Affordable Housing to establish a fair and 
effective process to lease rental units, and sell ownership units to new eligible buyers. The policy 
establishes household size priorities for bedroom sizes and a local preference for people who either live 
or work in San Mateo.  The locality preference is a result of the City’s desire to prioritize local funds for 
local residents and to reduce commutes and resulting traffic congestion for those who work in the 
City.  The City has analyzed the racial/ethnic make-up of San Mateo using census data and compared it 
to the local region, defined as the County of San Mateo, and found no material differences in 
racial/ethnic percentages, therefore determined that this policy does not have a disparate impact on 
any minority group. This preference does not apply if a particular funding source prohibits a local 
preference.
The City has provided subsidies for a variety of housing types including rental, ownership, transitional 
and supportive housing units in a variety of locations and for a range of income levels, in an effort to

Annual Action Plan
2022
OMB Control No: 2506-0117 (exp. 09/30/2021)
Discussion
The City utilizes 100% of its CDBG funds for eligible activities that assist low- and moderate-income 
households through direct services as well as improvements to the CDBG Priority Neighborhoods.
The CDBG grant funds plus annual program income from loan repayments are consistently allocated and 
spent in a timely manner to maximize assistance to the community. However, it is expected that there 
will be delays in delivering services, completing projects, and drawing down funds due to the COVID-19 
pandemic.
The three-year period for calculating CDBG benefit is 2021, 2022, and 2023.
