2. Sustainability-Related Amendments to the 2022 Building Code 
Andrea Chow, Sustainability Analyst, provided a presentation to the Commission asking for feedback on 
potential amendments to the 2022 Building Code for new construction and existing buildings related to building 
electrification and electric vehicle readiness. 
Commissioners asked questions of staff. 
 
Public Comment.  Following members of the public spoke to the item:  Robert Whitehair and Megan Strain on 
behalf of the San Mateo Climate Action Team; Drew, Blake Herrschaft (Peninsula Clean Energy), and Carol 
Steinfeld. 
 
Comments from commissioners included: develop a new construction building electrification reach code that 
establishes a process for exceptions for commercial buildings that is fair and responsive to stakeholders; develop 
creative public outreach and education around the benefits of electrification and specific outreach to renters; need 
forcing mechanism to promote change; address dangers of natural gas inside homes; the need for consistency of 
4 of 31

--- page 5 ---
reach codes across the region for the development community; consider other policy options beyond reach codes 
including requiring electrification measures at time of sale similar to the Sewer Lateral Program, requiring an 
energy audit at time of sale; electrification measures at time of remodel; initiate a cost sharing program for 
disadvantaged when switching out gas water heaters for heat pump heaters, similar to Sewer Lateral Program; 
and “end of flow” policy. 
 
No motion needed for this item, but commissioners gave following recommendations. 
1. New Construction Building Electrification: Adopt a reach code requiring all new construction be all-
electric, with a defined process to evaluate and grant exceptions, as proposed by the Bay Area Reach 
Codes group. Recommendation supported by Loraine, Narita, Robbins, and Rowinski. 
 
2. New Construction Electric Vehicle Readiness: Adopt of the Bay Area Reach Codes Model Code for all 
building types. This recommendation was supported by Loraine and Narita. Commissioner Robbins and 
Commissioner Rowinski expressed concern about providing Level 1 EV Charging instead of Level 2 for 
multifamily buildings.  
 
3. Existing Buildings: All commissioners supported requiring panel capacity for future electrification of gas 
equipment at time of electric panel upgrade or panel installation and requiring outlet installation during 
kitchen or laundry renovations. Commissioners Narita and Loraine supported exploring the measures 
requiring heat pump air conditioning and heat pump pool water heater equipment at time of replacement 
and be sized appropriately. Commissioners Robbins and Rowinski did not support requirements around 
heat pump equipment due to cost and availability. All commissioners expressed concerns about the cost 
burden and other constraints of requiring heat pump water heaters at time of replacement. 
  
Commissioners asked to have this item brought back before this item goes to City Council.

BACKGROUND:
The 2020 Climate Action Plan (CAP) includes strategies and measures to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The 2020 
CAP contains implementation measures in the following categories: building electrification, renewable energy, energy 
efficiency, municipal energy efficiency, clean transportation fuels, sustainable transportation, solid waste, water and 
wastewater, and off-road equipment. 
To track CAP implementation, staff provides annual updates to the Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission and City 
Council on the status of each implementation measure and key metrics. Attachment 1 to this report provides both 
qualitative and quantitative data to show how progress has been made on each of the CAP measures and Attachment 2 
summarizes available metrics for each CAP measure. 
Some key highlights of implementation on the 2020 CAP are as follows:

Electric Vehicle Infrastructure: The City completed installation of 13 Level 2 Electric Vehicle charging ports at the 
Main Street Garage in Downtown San Mateo in April 2022. The project includes the installation of Powerflex 
equipment utilizing CALeVIP funding, greatly reducing project capital costs. CALeVIP funding was secured in 2020 
with the assistance of Peninsula Clean Energy. Staff also applied for funding to install chargers at the 2nd and El 
Camino Garage and will potentially receive funding in the CALeVIP’s next funding allocation.  

Electric Leaf Blowers: The City launched the electric leaf blower rebate program in July 2021. To date, 30 rebates 
have been distributed, including five rebates to commercial landscapers. The City Council identified evaluating leaf 
blower regulations as a Council priority for FY 2022-23. Staff will present policy options and best practices to the 
Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission for review later this year.

Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance: The City Council adopted the Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance 
that will require food facilities and restaurants to use compostable products instead of plastic disposables. This 
ordinance goes into effect October 1, 2022. County and City staff will assist food facilities in the transition to 
compostable products that meet the ordinance requirements.

Composting Organics: The City continues to take action in compliance with SB 1383 (Lara) “California’s Short-Lived 
Climate Pollutants Reduction Act” to address statewide methane gas emission reduction targets. In November

enforceable requirements for organic waste generators, haulers, and other entities subject to City authority. In

Management Authority (SBWMA) for technical support in meeting SB 1383 requirements including education and

compliance with the City’s reach code requirements. 
Staff continues to implement measures in all Climate Action Plan categories. For FY 2022-23 City Council identified a few 
specific sustainability measures to prioritize including the development of a plan to decarbonize existing buildings, the 
development and adoption of reach codes for the 2022 building code cycle, and the exploration of policies to address gas 
leaf blower use.

ENERGY EFFICIENCY
EE 1 RESIDENTIAL ENERGY EFFICIENCY RETROFITS

The BayREN Home + program provides rebates for energy efficiency upgrades including building 
air sealing, insulation, duct replacement and sealing, and more. In 2020, 16 single family home 
upgrades were completed, and 41 individual measure rebates issued. In 2021, 21 single family 
home upgrades were completed, and 107 individual measure rebates issued. This amounts to 
37 home upgrades and 148 individual measure rebates issued since 2020.

In 2020, 44 new Energy Advisor leads were generated and in 2021 there were a total of 72 leads, 
amounting to 116 leads since 2020. The Energy Advisors are a resource to residents interested 
in making an energy upgrade or in the BayREN Home + program.

The Bay Area Multifamily Building Enhancements (BAMBE) program provides rebates for saving 
15% of the building’s energy usage for project measures that include heating and cooling 
systems, water heating systems, lighting and appliances, building sealing and insulation and 
more. In 2020, 7 units participated in the program and in 2021, 18 units participated in the 
program, amounting to 25 units since 2020.

The Home Energy Score program provides a score of home energy use on a scale of 1 to 10. It 
estimates home energy use, associated costs, and provides energy solutions to cost-effectively 
improve home efficiency. In 2020, there were 77 home energy score rebates and in 2021, there 
were 44 home energy score rebates in San Mateo, amounting to 121 home energy score rebates 
since 2020.


There are 21 certified businesses in San Mateo certified through the San Mateo County Green

1. The Problem Addressed by the Decarbonization Objective  
 
Zooming out for a moment,  the singular reason that we are deeply interested in the effective 
implementation of the Decarbonization Objective in San Mateo is this: the Intergovernmental Panel on 
Climate Change (the “IPCC”) has made it clear, with hard scientific evidence, that the  world has a mere 
seven (7) years to emit Greenhouse Gas Emissions at the current rate  before the earth’s temperature 
increase exceeds the point of no return of 1.5 degrees Celsius. Every fuel gas installation made from this 
day forward is taking the planet in the wrong direction, toward climate catastrophe. For those 
unfamiliar with why 1.5 degrees Celsius of warming is so problematic, please see the NY Times article of 
April 4, 2022, link below in footnotes. 1 
 
In addition, most tellingly, the IPCC also said in its March 2022 Report: 
 
“The technologies and policies necessary to adequately address climate change exist, and the only 
real obstacles are politics and fossil fuel interests.” 
Local government action is possibly the best hope we have for averting the climate catastrophe that will 
come for all of us if we keep conducting “business as usual”. Rather than waiting for Federal

In summary, the two-pronged ordinance would be adopted this summer, and would provide that:  
 
a) New Construction: All installations (e.g., water heaters, furnaces, and kitchen ranges) must be 
electric, effective January 1, 2023; and  
 
b)  Existing Buildings: when gas-powered installations reach the end of their useful life, the 
replacement installations must be electric, effective January 1, 2026.

electric installations in existing buildings, but declined to adopt, or delayed the adoption of, such an 
ordinance. We feel that San Mateo can achieve success, where other cities have not, by delaying the 
implementation of an ordinance for existing buildings, until January 1, 2026. This will allow San Mateo to 
do things right by following the roadmap to success, described further below.

g) Uncertainty with respect to decarbonization date.  There was a lack of clarity about the intent 
of the SIC with respect to the decarbonization date: Is it 2030 or 2045 or some other date?  
  
First, we note that the City Council has already adopted a goal to eliminate methane gas use in the 
City by 2030. Therefore, we are confused by the SIC’s discussion around a decarbonization date. 
Does the SIC wish to move the goal posts that City Council established just recently?  
  
Second, we emphasize that waiting until 2045 to decarbonize buildings, as in Half Moon Bay, is just 
too far into the future in terms of the impact on climate, and the impact on residents. With respect 
to the impact on climate, please see the problem overview in Paragraph 1, above. With respect to 
the impact on residents, we assert that “kicking the can” so far down the road is unfair to residents 
and businesses trying to budget and plan for the future. Everyone would benefit from more 
certainty in response to the climate crisis. 
  
Data helps illustrate this. Every year, around 7% of all water heaters fail given that newer models 
can last 12 years, and older models may last longer.  Furnaces last considerably longer – up to 40 
years or more, but some fail sooner than that and those that last a long time show signs of aging and 
emit more noxious gases, the older they get. Each year in San Mateo, at least 2,000 gas water 
heaters fail and are replaced.  
  
The point is, extending the decarbonization date to 2045 makes a purchase decision in the near 
future (e.g., within the next several years) quite difficult and confusing.  For example, in the near 
term, within a single household, this could allow for the purchase of a new gas water heater (with a 
12-year lifespan), but require a new electric furnace (with a lifespan of 40 years).  What benefit 
would this bring to a household? We see none. A 2045 date would lead to confusion, lost 
decarbonization opportunities, and “stranded assets.” 
  
For the foregoing reasons, we urge the SIC to respect the 2030 target that City Council has recently 
set for the elimination of methane gas in the City.

h) Health dangers of gas stoves.  Commissioner Narita raised alarm bells over health dangers of
