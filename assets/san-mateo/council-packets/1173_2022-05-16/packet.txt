SUBJECT:
Citywide Street Rehabilitation Package 1-C Project (Phase 2) – Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve Amendment No. 1 to the agreement with Andreini Brothers, Inc. for the Citywide Street Rehabilitation Package 1-
C (Phase 2) Project to increase the total contract amount by $260,000, for a total of $2,119,555, and authorize the Public 
Works Director to execute the amendment in substantially the form presented and issue change orders in accordance with 
the contract documents and up to the contingency amount.  
BACKGROUND:
The Citywide Street Rehabilitation Package 1-C Phase 2 Project (Project) is part of Public Works’ Pavement Management 
Program. The Project will repair approximately 0.8 miles of streets across four locations. Council approved a construction 
agreement for the Project to Andreini Brothers, Inc. on August 16, 2021 in the amount of $1,859,555 and authorized a 
contingency of $190,000. 
The contractor has completed paving of most of the street segments in the Project; however, unforeseen conditions were 
encountered on 2nd Avenue. Upon removal of the existing roadway section, the contractor found extensive soft soil in the 
subgrade throughout the majority of the roadway that was not identified during the design investigations. The contractor 
was directed to over-excavate the soft soil areas until firm ground was reached and place and compact aggregate base 
with geotextiles in the over-excavated section. While the contract included an allowance bid item to mitigate unforeseen 
subgrade conditions, due to the extensive soft soil conditions on this street segment, the City used the entire $200,000 
unstable subgrade allowance and incurred a total unforeseen additional cost of $250,000. 
Also, early in the Project construction, additional work was performed under contract change orders in the amount of 
$115,000. The additional work was performed to take advantage of favorable pricing, address problematic deficiencies in 
existing drainage that was causing potentially dangerous ponding conditions, and avoid premature excavation of a newly 
paved roadway. This additional work included:

Installation of streetlight conduit on 5th Avenue in anticipation of future high-voltage street lighting conversion;

Concrete work on Rand Street to address deteriorated sidewalk conditions; and

Installation of a catch basin and storm drain at the intersection of Fairfax Avenue and Franklin Street to alleviate 
ponding issues. 
It is estimated that $320,000 is needed to complete paving of the remaining street segments. The Project has 
approximately $160,000 remaining in the contract budget and contingency amount. Staff also recommends increasing Bid 
Item 45, “Unstable Subgrade Allowance” by $100,000 to account for potential poor subgrade conditions in the remaining 
streets. This information is summarized in the following table.

SUBJECT:
Bermuda Drive Bridge Replacement Project – Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve Amendment No. 1 with Biggs Cardosa Associates, Inc. for professional design services for the Bermuda Drive 
Bridge Replacement Project in an amount not to exceed $216,874 for a total agreement amount of $651,874; increase the 
contingency reserve by $20,000 for a total contingency balance of $65,000; and authorize the Public Works Director to 
execute the agreement in substantially the form presented and issue amendments within the contingency amount.
BACKGROUND:
In 2008, the City applied for preliminary engineering funds through the Highway Bridge Replacement and Rehabilitation 
Program (HBRRP) to evaluate the condition of the Bermuda Drive Bridge over Belmont Creek and address the structural 
deficiencies identified in a bridge inspection report prepared by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). In 
2010, Caltrans approved the HBRRP funds and City Council approved a professional services agreement with Biggs Cardosa 
Associates, Inc. (BCA) to perform the preliminary engineering.
Structural assessments and a detailed seismic analysis done in 2011 and 2012 by BCA recommended a full replacement of 
the bridge rather than a structural retrofit. In 2017, Caltrans provided official approval to proceed with a revised scope of 
work that includes full bridge replacement. On May 21, 2018, City Council approved the award of a professional services 
agreement with BCA for design and environmental documentation services for the Bermuda Drive Bridge Replacement 
Project (Project). The design work also includes: (1) an expanded alternatives analysis evaluating different construction 
phasing and construction methods to reduce public impacts from the construction and (2) public outreach assistance 
during the design phase. The design is currently anticipated to be completed during 2024.
Amendment No. 1 provides additional budget for BCA to conduct the alternatives analysis, coordinate with utilities, and 
provide public outreach support. Staff reviewed and negotiated a proposal with BCA for Amendment No. 1 with a not-to-
exceed amount of $216,874. Staff believes the proposal to be reasonable for the scope of work and expertise required and 
recommends approving this amendment, as well as increasing the contingency reserve by $20,000. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Sufficient funds are available in the Bermuda Drive Bridge Replacement Project to fund this amendment and increase to 
the contingency reserve. 
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This action of approving Amendment No. 1 with Biggs Cardosa Associates, Inc. to conduct an alternatives analysis, 
coordinate with utilities, and provide public outreach is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or 
administrative activity that will not result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines

Chlorides: The Consultant will provide recommendations for protection from chlorides in the

field testing is proposed at this time and results are assumed to be conservative. If there is a 
cost impact associated with chloride levels, testing at a lab can occur as an additional service. 
Migration of salinity up the channel will be a function of nuisance flows associated with 
irrigation and time since previous rainfall. Highest values are likely to occur during late 
summer.
G. 
Temporary Dewatering During Construction: The Consultant will prepare a design and plans 
and specifications for temporary dewatering or creek diversion during construction. 
Analyses of drainage improvements for storm events other than the 10 and 100-year storm events are excluded

The Consultant will prepare the required environmental documents to comply with the California 
Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) and the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) for the proposed 
Bermuda Drive Bridge Replacement project, to be undertaken by the City of San Mateo (City) and Biggs 
Cardosa Associates.  Since the project is an HBP project, the California Department of Transportation 
(Caltrans) will be the lead agency for NEPA.  The Consultant proposes to prepare a Categorical Exclusion 
with the appropriate technical studies, which will meet the requirements of NEPA, and a Categorical 
Exemption, which will meet the requirements of CEQA.
A.
Review of Background Information and Project Initiation: Completed
B.
NEPA Documentation:
1.
Field Review Meeting: In order to minimize the extent of the environmental review 
process, it will be crucial to obtain agreement from Caltrans Local Assistance and 
Professionally Qualified Staff (PQS) Staff, regarding the existing conditions at the 
project site and the necessary environmental documentation for this project.  With the 
change in the project, it is anticipated that an updated Preliminary Environmental 
Survey (PES) Form and a new Field Review meeting with Caltrans will be required for 
the project.  The Consultant will prepare the Caltrans PES Form and assist the team

2.
NEPA Technical Studies: Based on the Consultant's understanding of the project and 
the site conditions, it is anticipated that a Categorical Exclusion (CE) with technical 
studies will be the appropriate document for the project under NEPA.  The NEPA 
technical studies will be prepared per the Caltrans’ Supplemental Environmental 
Review (SER) formats and will specifically comply with NEPA-related environmental 
processes, including the following: Endangered Species Act, Executive Order 11990 
(Protection of Wetlands), Executive Order 11988 (Floodplain Management) and 
Section 4(f) evaluation.
Following preparation and receipt of these studies, the Consultant will compile and 
package the draft NEPA technical environmental studies for submittal to the team for 
review and comment.  The scope assumes one review cycle for the reports.  Upon 
approval by the City and the project team, these technical studies will be submitted to 
Caltrans for review and comment.  The technical studies will then be reviewed by the 
appropriate Caltrans technical staff (biologist, hazardous materials, etc.).  The 
Consultant will respond to Caltrans comments and will revise the technical materials, 
if necessary.  Once the reports are approved, Caltrans will attach them to the one page 
CE form that they prepare, and the entire packet will be considered to be the CE 
document.

CONSTRUCTION DOCUMENTS
Based on the approved Bridge Type Selection, Construction Documents will be prepared by the design team.  
The Construction Documents package will include specifications and construction plans and will conform to 
City's and Caltrans' Standards with Federal requirements.
Final design calculations will be prepared for the bridge structure and submitted for City staff review.  An 
opinion of probable construction cost of the project will also be provided.  The Consultant will coordinate 
and incorporate any utility agency's future needs, if any, in the bridge.  The Consultant will also design all 
street transitions to the bridge.
A.
Design Criteria: for Bridge Design: Final bridge design will be performed in accordance with 
Caltrans Bridge Design Specifications and other Caltrans Design Manuals.  Design will be 
based on the "Load and Resistance Factor Design" method, with HL93 and permit truck 
design live loads. Seismic design will be performed in accordance with current Caltrans Seismic 
Design Criteria.

Plan Sheets and Details: The plan sheets will be prepared in AutoCAD.  Plans will be prepared

submittals will be made during the preparation of the Construction Documents as follows: 
When the documents are 65% complete; when the documents are 95% complete; when the 
final documents are complete.  Each submittal will incorporate the review comments from the 
previous submittal by the City staff as well as those of all other reviewing agencies.

SUBJECT:
2022 Citywide Sanitary Sewer Cleaning and Inspection Large Diameter Pipes Project – Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with Pipe and Plant Solutions, Inc. for the 2022 Citywide Sanitary Sewer Cleaning and Inspection – 
Large Diameter Pipes Project in an amount not to exceed $368,631; establish a contingency in the amount of $55,000; and 
authorize the Public Works Director to execute the agreement in substantially the form presented and issue change orders 
within the contingency amount. 
BACKGROUND:
The City regularly performs cleaning and video inspections of all its sanitary sewer mains to meet the collection system 
management goals of the Sanitary Sewer Management Plan. The objectives are to maintain the sewer mains and manholes 
by removing debris that may cause backups and to perform video inspections of the pipes and manholes for condition 
assessment. 
The 2022 Citywide Sanitary Sewer Cleaning and Inspection – Large Diameter Pipes Project (Project) encompasses cleaning 
and video inspection of approximately 22,670 linear feet of sewer mains larger than 12-inches, and 51 sewer manholes 
located primarily along Highway 101 Northbound, Highway 101 Northbound frontage roads, 16th Avenue between the 
Caltrain tracks and Highway 101, and alongside the Caltrain tracks between 21st Avenue and 16th Avenue. 
The Project was advertised on March 28, 2022. Two bids were received and opened on April 21, 2022, and the results are 
as follows: Pipe and Plant Solutions, Inc. ($368,631) and Express Plumbing, Inc. ($459,624). The engineer’s estimate for this 
project is $369,000. The complete bid results are shown in Attachment 2 - Bid Summary. 
After review of the bid documents, the Pipe and Plant Solutions, Inc.’s bid package was found responsive, the contractor 
was found responsible, and possessed the appropriate licenses.  Therefore, staff recommends the Project be awarded to 
Pipe and Plant Solutions, Inc. as the lowest responsive and responsible bidder in the amount of $368,631. Staff also 
recommends establishing a contingency reserve in the amount of $55,000 to address any unanticipated items that may 
arise during construction. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Sufficient funding is available in the Large Diameter Cleaning and Condition Assessment Project to award this agreement. 
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
The City prepared a Final Programmatic Environmental Impact Report (Final PEIR) in accordance with the California 
Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) (SCH No. 2015032006). Council on June 6, 2016, certified the PEIR. In accordance with 
CEQA, the Final PEIR serves as the primary environmental compliance document for the implementation of the Clean

SUBJECT:
Ordinance Introduction – Access to Reproductive Health Care Facilities
RECOMMENDATION:
Introduce an Ordinance to prohibit individuals from harassing, intimidating, or obstructing persons seeking access to a 
reproductive health care facility within 100 feet of an entrance to such a facility.
BACKGROUND:
Since at least 2014, individuals seeking services at the Planned Parenthood health care facility in San Mateo have 
experienced protestors aggressively approaching them and engaging in harassing behavior, as explained in more detail 
below.  In addition, there is a distinct possibility that the U.S. Supreme Court may overturn its decision in Roe v. Wade 
(1973) 410 U.S. 113 that established a constitutional right to an abortion.  The Court is considering whether to do so in 
Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization, and a decision is expected in June 2022.  If this occurs, more patients may 
seek services at Planned Parenthood facilities in California, including in San Mateo, which could lead to more

DISCUSSION
Legal Background
The City has constitutional authority to establish and enforce ordinances that regulate conduct in order to provide for the 
public health, safety, and welfare. However, the City’s authority is limited by the competing constitutionally protected 
rights of individuals to use public sidewalks to exercise their right of free expression. The U.S. Supreme Court has balanced 
these two competing sets of rights and concluded that a city may impose reasonable restrictions on the time, place and 
manner of protected speech, provided the restrictions are justified without reference to the content of the regulated 
speech, that they are narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest, and that they leave open ample 
alternative channels for communication of the information.
The question of the constitutionality of buffer zone laws has reached the U.S. Supreme Court several times in the last three 
decades, in Madsen v. Women’s Health Center Inc. (1994), Schenck v. Pro-Choice Network of W. New York (1997), Hill v. 
Colorado (2000), and McCullen v. Coakley (2014). Generally, a government may impose a buffer zone in the area around a 
reproductive healthcare facility, so long as the restrictions are content-neutral time, place, or manner regulations of 
speech, and narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest.
The recommended ordinance complies with the legal criteria established by the U.S. Supreme Court. The ordinance seeks 
to address the significant governmental interests of providing safe patient access to healthcare and unobstructed use of 
sidewalks by the general public. Due to the history of complaints related to harassing and obstructive conduct on a 
sidewalk near a reproductive health care facility, the ordinance is focused on regulating that conduct at the location where 
the problematic conduct has occurred, within a 100-foot “buffer zone” around the Planned Parenthood facility.
What the Ordinance Would Do
The recommended ordinance complies with the legal criteria established by the U.S. Supreme Court. The ordinance seeks 
to address the significant governmental interests of providing patient access to reproductive health care services. Due to 
the history of complaints related to harassing, intimidating, and obstructive conduct on a sidewalk, driveway and street 
near a reproductive health care facility, the ordinance is focused on regulating that conduct at the location where the 
problematic conduct has occurred, within one-hundred (100) feet of the entrance of a reproductive health care facility. 
Within one-hundred (100) feet of the entrance of a reproductive facility, the ordinance prohibits approaching within eight 
(8) feet of any person seeking to enter or exit the reproductive health care facility, or any occupied motor vehicle seeking 
to enter or exit the parking lot of such a facility, without the consent of such a person or vehicle occupant, for the purpose 
of harassing or intimidating such person or vehicle occupant. The ordinance also prohibits obstructing access to or 
departure from any entrance to a building or driveway of a reproductive health care facility.
These restrictions are content-neutral and are narrowly tailored restrictions that serve a significant governmental interest, 
but still provide alternative avenues of communication. The reasonable time, place and manner restrictions in the 
proposed ordinance avoid the negative effects of harassing, intimidating and obstructive conduct, and prohibits excessive 
noise within 100 feet of an entrance to a reproductive health care facility, without targeting the message or topic of 
speech.
The restrictions are narrowly tailored and allow ample alternative means of communicating in the areas in front of 
reproductive health care facilities through consensual conversations that are not harassing or intimidating or through non-
consensual conversations where there is an 8-foot distance between speaker and target audience to reduce or eliminate 
the harassing, intimidating or obstructing conduct accompanying any such speech.

Appeal:
The Property Owner has appealed the tenant relocation payments asserting he did not convert or lease the space, did not 
receive rent from the current occupants, and has never met the current occupants. 
Analysis: 
In accordance with SMMC 7.50.030, the tenant shall be eligible for relocation payments from a property owner if the
tenant household is displaced from its dwelling or room due to the City's code enforcement activities. Specifically, SMMC
7.50.030(a)(2) states that the tenant household shall vacate the dwelling or room (whether or not the Property Owner
requires vacation) after the City has issued a notice to vacate, notice to abate life-threatening condition, or declaration of
substandard condition covering that dwelling or room. Pursuant to SMMC 7.50.030(b)(2), the tenant household is not 
deemed displaced, and therefore not eligible for relocation payments, if the property owner or the property owner’s agent 
can demonstrate that they did not create the noncomplying condition(s) and the tenant household or their guests created 
the noncomplying condition(s), or the tenant household moved into the dwelling unit after the creation of the

During staff’s investigation, Gabriel and his spouse confirmed to City staff that he is employed by Mr. Simon as a 
handyman and property manager for the subject property, resides in the upstairs residential unit, and was instructed by 
Mr. Simon to find tenants to occupy the commercial ground floor unit. The tenants of the commercial ground floor unit 
(Sandra and Gladys), as well as Gabriel, told staff that Mr. Simon had been to commercial ground floor unit due to the

other occasions and had a bathtub installed in the unit.  Gabriel’s spouse told staff that the property owner instructed

Based on staff’s investigation and conversations with involved parties, staff has determined that Mr. Simon was aware of

management duties including leasing and collecting associated rent(s) for the ground level unit. Therefore, the property 
owner does not qualify for relief under SMMC 7.50.30(b)(2). Therefore, staff recommends the appeal be denied and for 
tenant relocation benefits to be provided to the tenants according to City requirements.

SUBJECT:
Downtown Use of Right-of-Way – Update 
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on policies and plans related to the public right-of-way in downtown San Mateo and provide direction 
to staff for prioritization of uses on B Street.
BACKGROUND:
As part of private development planning application reviews, Public Works staff evaluates proposed modifications to the 
public right-of-way, including changes to sidewalks, roadway configuration, parking, parklets, loading zones, and site 
access. When reviewing these proposed modifications, staff confirms conformance with the San Mateo Municipal Code 
(Municipal Code), policies, adopted plans (e.g., General Plan, Pedestrian Master Plan, and Bicycle Master Plan), and laws 
and regulations (e.g., the Mitigation Fee Act). However, building infrastructure to support private development and all the 
uses of the public right-of-way is often not possible given the limited amount of space and roadway widths available, 
requiring the need to prioritize specific uses. These challenges are becoming more apparent in the downtown, specifically 
on B Street, due to proposed redevelopment of older buildings and the establishment of the pedestrian mall. This study 
session will provide an overview of the potential uses of the public right-of-way on B Street, and staff will seek Council 
direction on their prioritization.
Guiding Documents
As mentioned above, private development planning applications are reviewed based on the requirements in the Municipal 
Code (i.e., Title 26 Subdivisions and Title 27 Zoning) and City Council adopted master plans. The planning documents 
include the following: 
•
General Plan – The General Plan, which is currently being updated, includes goals to maintain a street and 
highway system that accommodates future growth while supporting a multimodal circulation network that 
provides an alternative to automobile travel. 
•
Downtown Area Plan – Adopted in 2009, this plan establishes a framework for specific policies that pertain to 
new downtown development as well as the preservation of existing downtown resources. This plan and 
Municipal Zoning Code Section 27.38.100 require that new developments in downtown be built to the property 
line (with some exceptions). This requirement is rooted in urban design principals and is intended to create a 
uniform building frontage in downtown. However, projects are required to meet specific open space 
requirements and are permitted to be set back from the property line to meet the requirement. 
•
Pedestrian Master Plan – Adopted in 2012, this plan includes various types of pedestrian improvements to 
improve connectivity, safety, and the overall pedestrian experience. It also establishes standards for the 
99 of 130

--- page 100 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
construction of new or updated sidewalks based on the type of use. The Pedestrian Master Plan provides 
standard details for sidewalks ranging from 7 to 26 feet (Attachment 1).
•
Bicycle Master Plan – Updated in 2020, this plan includes a designated citywide bicycle infrastructure to improve 
safety and use. The Bicycle Master Plan includes a Class IV separated bike lane on B Street between 1st and 5th 
Avenues, connecting to existing and planned facilities north and south of the downtown. The Class IV facility is 
recommended for B Street based on industry standards for the vehicle speeds and volumes on this roadway to 
provide safety and comfort for bicyclists of all ages and abilities (Attachment 2).
Conflicts and Tradeoffs
Due to limited public right-of-way widths, building / property lot line requirements in downtown, and the establishment of 
the pedestrian mall on B Street, development planning applications are proposing uses that are favored over others. Some 
of the conflicts and tradeoffs include the following. 

Pedestrian Mall and Bicycle Facilities – The pedestrian mall on B Street extends between 1st and 2nd Avenues and 
2nd and 3rd Avenues, while the 2020 Bicycle Master Plan proposes a separated bicycle lane on B street from 1st to 
5th Avenues through the pedestrian mall. City Council direction will be needed on whether pedestrian and 
bicycle uses can both be facilitated in space while maintaining safety for pedestrians and other users. The 
implementation of bicycle improvements in downtown may also result in trade-offs with pedestrian facilities, 
on-street parking, loading zones, or roadway configuration. 

Construction to the Property Line – Municipal Code Section 27.38.100 requires new developments to build to 
the property line, with exceptions allowed to meet ground floor open space requirements or if setback buffers 
are required. However, the Pedestrian Master Plan calls for widening sidewalks to enhance the pedestrian 
realm. In an area like downtown, a development project could accommodate this by either (1) designing 
buildings that provide a ground floor setback from the property line to accommodate wider sidewalks and/or 
utilize this area to help meet the project open space requirements, (2) request modifications to the public right-
of-way configuration (e.g. elimination of on-street parking, bicycle lanes, or a reduction in the number of vehicle 
lanes) for the wider sidewalk, or (3) request to design a sidewalk of typical width.

On-Street Parking and Parklets – In some instances, developers request to include on-street parking to allow for 
the opportunity for future flex zone usage like parklets. In other cases, parklets utilize existing on-street parking 
spaces that result in a reduction in available parking. Provision of on-street parking on project frontages may 
provide some additional parking to users or parklets for businesses but may limit the availability of right-of-way. 
It is important to note that downtown includes several off-street parking facilities within a two to five minute 
walk from B Street that have capacity for additional parking.  

Land Use and Business Operations - There are various ways in which the curb is utilized to facilitate businesses. 
These uses include public loading zones for business supply delivery and parking (including short-term parking 
spaces for customer or delivery service pick-ups and designated spaces for disabled users). New developments in 
downtown have been more frequently utilizing concessions under the State Density Bonus Law to waive on-site 
loading and parking requirements. This may effectively increase reliance on the public right-of-way for loading 
and/or parking, which may limit the availability of right-of-way for other uses. In some cases, bicycle 
infrastructure and loading zones may be accommodated on project frontages with short-term, disabled parking 
provided on sides streets, but would require sidewalks of typical widths.  
Future Steps / Longer-term Initiatives
Staff recognizes that due to limited public right-of-way, zoning requirements, and multiple master plans, a comprehensive 
assessment is needed that sets a clearer framework for the preferred uses in downtown. While the City is beginning to 
work on a Complete Streets Plan to reconcile approved master plans, the focus of this document is to address safety 
concerns for all modes of transportation and does not address prioritizing different modes of transportation or land uses in 
the downtown. The downtown core is a discrete area of the City with more complex needs and concerns.  Given these

challenges, prioritization of different transportation modes may vary by street or area of the downtown in a way that is 
different from the rest of the City. 
In addition to overlap between the Municipal Code and City-adopted plans, differing project-specific interests often arise 
regarding the use of the public right-of-way. One project might prioritize wide sidewalks while another project might 
prioritize on-street loading over other uses within the public right-of-way (e.g., bike lanes, parking). Project-driven 
prioritization of the right-of-way can lead to inconsistent implementation of improvements along important corridors and 
is not an effective way of ensuring the Council’s vision is implemented. 
A comprehensive plan addressing multi-modal transportation and priorities for different corridors in the downtown is an 
option to avoid these inconsistent, project-specific decisions or discrepancies in adopted plans during the review process. 
However, significant additional discussion and community engagement would be needed to develop this type of plan. 
Given the current schedule of the General Plan update, which is scheduled for completion by the end of 2023, an update 
to the Downtown Area Plan is likely several years away from completion.
Prioritization of the Public Right-of-Way
In the meantime, to help address the different priorities observed between current and future projects, staff is requesting 
direction from Council on its priorities for the various uses of the public right-of-way so that staff can appropriately review 
and comment on proposed development applications. In this first discussion, staff is seeking Council feedback specifically 
along B Street in downtown because of the implementation of the pedestrian mall and due to several development 
projects that are currently seeking entitlements. Specifically, staff will be asking Council for direction on the following 
questions.

Pedestrian Mall: Is Council’s vision for these street segments pedestrian-only or multi-modal (e.g., include bicycle 
facilities)?

Pedestrian: Should staff prioritize the pedestrian experience (e.g., wider sidewalks, outdoor dining) along B Street 
over competing priorities (e.g., bicycles, on-street parking)?

Parking: Given the relatively easy access to off-street parking lots near B Street, is preserving on-street parking to 
support business functions and parklets (wider sidewalks also allow for outdoor dining) along B Street a priority for 
this corridor? 

Bicycles: Should bicycle movement be prioritized along B Street when compared to other modes or on-street 
parking?
Staff anticipates returning to Council at a later date to introduce legislation for adoption that formalizes the direction 
provided in the study session. Additional study sessions will likely be necessary to obtain additional direction from Council 
regarding other matters related to development and right-of-way uses in the downtown until a more complete plan can be 
developed.

A.1. Sidewalk Standards - Introduction 
Discussion  
Sidewalks form the backbone of the pedestrian transportation network. Good street and sidewalk design can foster healthier 
communities by improving public safety, enhancing mobility, reducing environmental impacts, and building community 
character.  
Sidewalks consist of one or several zones.  The zones are named for the primary activity that occurs in the zone. Section A.2 
describes the recommended sidewalk zones for San Mateo, which include through, planter/furniture, frontage, and flex-use 
zones.  The presence and width of each zone along a given sidewalk depends on the adjoining roadway type and 
transportation needs, surrounding land uses, and community needs and desires. 
The widths of sidewalks determine the types of pedestrian elements that can be installed and affect the pedestrian activities 
that occur there. In residential areas, sidewalks four to six feet wide are likely appropriate. In commercial settings with a mix of 
uses, wider sidewalks are sometimes essential for high pedestrian traffic and/or to accommodate amenities such as street 
furniture or newspaper stands. Streetscape elements can vary from a simple landscape strip in a residential setting to many 
elements such as street trees, pedestrian lighting with banners, and benches in areas with larger pedestrian traffic. These 
Pedestrian Design Guidelines establish eight sidewalk types with varying zones and widths.  By standardizing streetscape 
design by land use, the guidelines ensure that future development of public rights-of-way in San Mateo’s residential, 
commercial, and mixed use areas meet the City’s vision for vibrant, healthy pedestrian environments. These guidelines seek to 
create places that are sensitive to the land use context, distinctive, attractive, and rich in amenities and that provide more 
convenience and choice for pedestrians. Table A-1 lists the sidewalk types for residential, commercial, and mixed use land uses. 
Sections A.3 through A.10 present the different sidewalk types.

BIKE BOX

•	Improves visibility and motorists’ awareness of bicyclists at intersections
SEPARATED BIKE LANE (CLASS 4)
•	A bicycle-only facility that is physically separated from motor vehicle traffic and 
distinct from the sidewalk 
•	Appropriate on streets with medium or high traffic volumes and/or speeds
WAYFINDING 
•	Typically includes signage and pavement markings
•	Helps bicyclists identify the best routes to destinations and to connecting routes
•	May include distances or travel times to destinations
BIKE LANE (CLASS 2)
•	Provides exclusive space for bicyclists in the roadway; designated by pavement 
markings and signage
•	Buffered bike lanes increase comfort by adding a painted buffer to a standard 
bike lane
•	Appropriate on streets with low or medium traffic volumes
BIKE DETECTION/PUSH BUTTON
•	 Bicycle detectors are located in the street at intersections and trigger a green light 
for bicyclists who wait above the detector marking
•	Bicycle pushbuttons are located on signal poles within reach of a bicyclist waiting 
in the roadway; the buttons trigger a green light for bicyclists once pushed (similar 
to pedestrian pushbuttons)
BICYCLE BOULEVARD (CLASS 3)
•	Prioritize bicycle through-travel 
•	Use traffic calming to slow vehicle traffic and maintain low motor vehicle volumes
•	Often applied on quiet streets, in neighborhoods
PAINTED CONFLICT MARKINGS
•	Painted pavement markings can improve visibility of bike lanes at intersections 
•	Alerts all roadway users of expected behaviors to reduce conflicts with turning 
vehicles and at driveways
RURAL ROUTE (CLASS 3)
•	Provide shoulders to accommodate bicyclists
•	Supplement with warning signage and wayfinding 
•	Located in rural areas where dedicated bicycle facilities will not fit, or would be 
inappropriate given the surrounding context 
RECTANGULAR RAPID FLASHING BEACONS (RRFB)
•	 RRFBs combine a crossing warning sign with a bright flashing beacon that is

We write to thank you for making two blocks of B Street into a pedestrian mall. This mall has

B Street is one of the main focal points of downtown San Mateo, and pedestrians and bicyclists

should also work to implement the capital improvements on the existing mall.

The City routinely issues refunding bonds to reduce debt service required for General Fund and Sewer Enterprise 
debt. But for restrictions in the special tax formula, the City would lower the special tax levy for property 
taxpayers within the CFD to correspond to the lower debt service achieved by the refunding. However, the tax 
formula stipulates that unless the City takes action to forego the issuance of any authorized but unissued debt, 
the City may not lower special taxes in the CFD. City staff believes it would be prudent to keep this additional 
financing capacity for at least the short-term to pay for unanticipated public infrastructure needs in the CFD. At 
any time in the future, the City Council could by resolution relinquish this additional bonding capacity, at which 
time the City would be authorized to lower special taxes and pass debt service savings to property taxpayers 
within the CFD.
In the meantime, special tax revenues not needed to pay administrative expenses and debt service on the 
refunding bonds would be retained in two separate accounts, one designated for services and the other for 
capital improvements that were authorized when the CFD was formed. Expenditure of amounts on services or 
capital improvements not approved at that time (for example, a new library or fire station) would require 
approval by 2/3 of the registered voters residing in the CFD. 
The financing schedule has the sale of refunding bonds the week of May 23 and a closing on June 7. Given that 
this is a refunding and the goal is to maximize savings, the sale may be delayed depending upon conditions in 
the bond market.

BUDGET IMPACT:
If bonds are not sold, there will be certain non-contingent fees. These include the rating fee of $59,000 and the 
fees of the municipal advisors, estimated in the aggregate at approximately $60,000. The CFD would pay these 
amounts and has sufficient funds to do so. If bonds are sold, all costs of issuance would be paid from bond 
proceeds.
The principal and interest on the bonds (along with all administrative costs) are payable only from special taxes 
levied in the CFD. The City is not guaranteeing the repayment of the bonds and is not pledging any collateral to 
secure the bonds. The special taxes are not a personal obligation of the property owners in the CFD, but a 
property owner’s obligation to pay special taxes is secured by a lien of the owner’s property. In the event that 
property owners do not pay their special taxes, the City’s only obligation is to direct the fiscal agent to advance 
funds from the debt service reserve fund (which will be funded with bond proceeds and/or a surety bond when 
bonds are sold) and to initiate judicial foreclosure proceedings on the delinquent property owners.
