SUBJECT:
Potential Ballot Measures – Voter Opinion Survey Results 
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an informational report about the results of a voter opinion survey on the feasibility of two potential measures to 
either increase the City's sales tax rate or make changes to the City's existing real property transfer tax, and provide 
direction on whether to proceed with a measure on the November 2022 ballot.
BACKGROUND:
On October 4, 2021 and April 18, 2022, the City Council directed staff to proceed with a voter opinion survey on potential 
revenue enhancement measures that could be placed on a future ballot to support a range of City services and programs. 
The City hired the independent firm True North Research to conduct a statistically-significant survey on two potential 
general tax measures, the results of which will be presented during this meeting. A full report on the results is provided in 
Attachment 1.
The Council expressed an interest in understanding and comparing the feasibility of two different measures, therefore a 
split poll was conducted whereby half of the participants considered one option and the other half were presented with a 
second option. Staff from the Finance Department and City Manager’s Office worked closely with True North Research 
President Dr. Timothy McLarney in drafting the voter opinion survey. 
Participants were asked about one of two potential options:
1)
A ¼ (quarter) cent sales tax increase 
2)
Changing the City’s existing 0.5% real property transfer tax (RPTT) rate to a tiered system that included two 
options for properties sold or transferred for the following amounts:
a.
No increase for properties under $2.5 million, a 0.25% increase for properties between $2.5 million to $5 
million, a 0.5% percent increase for properties between $5 million to $10 million, and a 1% increase for 
properties over $10 million dollars. 
b.
No increase for properties under $3 million, a 0.25% percent increase for properties between $3 million to 
$5 million, a 0.5% percent increase for properties between $5 million to $10 million, and a 1% increase for 
properties over $10 million dollars. 
Split Survey Results
The survey was conducted between April 25 and May 1, 2022, in English and Spanish to a random sampling of 678 
registered voters who are likely to participate in the November 2022 election. The survey polled 339 of participants on the 
sales tax increase, and 339 participants on the RPTT. The statistical margin of error is +/- 3.7% for questions answered by 
all participants, and +/- 5.3% for questions specific to either the sales tax or real property transfer tax measure. The survey

was designed to poll voters’ initial reaction to the two options, then provide information about the measure followed by 
an interim test, then present both negative and positive arguments, followed by a final test of the options. 
Respondents’ initial reaction when only presented with the ballot language was 59% support for a 1/2% sales tax increase, 
and 51% support for switching RPTT to a tiered rate that includes the lowest tier set at $2.5 million. After learning more 
about the measures and being presented with both positive and negative arguments, support decreased for both 
measures. The final test indicates 54% support for a sales tax increase, and 49% support for switching RPTT to a tiered rate 
that includes the lowest tier set at $2.5 million. For those who did not support the RPTT, they were then asked if their 
opinion would change if the lowest tier was set at $3 million and support increased to 61%; however, this should be 
interpreted cautiously and most of the increase was soft support (meaning most of the responses were “probably yes” 
instead of “definitely yes.”) 
Survey participants also provided input on what types of services and projects they felt most strongly about funding. The 
services and programs that participants felt most strongly about funding included: maintaining parks, open spaces and 
recreation facilities including courts, fields and playgrounds; maintaining local streets and infrastructure; providing quick 
responses to 911 emergencies; providing fire protection and paramedic services; and making improvements to roads, 
intersections, sidewalks and bike lanes to better manage congestion on city streets. 
General Tax Revenue Enhancement Options
General tax measures require a simple majority of voter approval and must be placed on a ballot during a regular election,
meaning the next opportunities are either November 2022 or November 2024. As a general tax, the revenue would not be
tied to specific projects, however, it would go into the City’s General Fund, which is used to provide a range of services and
improvements.  
Note that a proposed statewide ballot measure entitled the “Taxpayer Protection and Government Accountability Act” 
would retroactively apply to taxes adopted after January 1, 2022 but before its effective date.  Among other things, the 
measure would require specified ballot title and summary language for general taxes, and would prohibit an advisory 
measure indicating that the revenue from the general tax will, could, or should be used for a specific purpose from 
appearing on the same ballot.  The measure would also prohibit Charter amendments that impose taxes.
Changes to Real Property Transfer Tax
As a charter city, San Mateo has an existing voter-approved real property transfer tax (RPTT) rate of 0.5% that applies to
residential and commercial properties. This tax is only paid upon transfer or sale of a property, and in residential sales is
customarily split between a buyer and seller.
Other charter cities in the Bay Area, including San Francisco, Berkeley, Oakland, Richmond, and San Jose have recently 
shifted from a flat rate to a tiered structure. If the City were to shift to the tiered structure, it could generate between 
approximately $5.6 million to $6.8 million in additional revenues per fiscal year based on the transactions that were 
subject to the City’s RPTT in 2020-21.
It is important to note that for properties at the lower tier, which constitute the majority of total sales, a new tiered
system would not affect RPTT rates. According to the San Mateo County Association of Realtors, in 2021 in the City of San
Mateo, the median sale price of a single-family home and of a condo was $1.84 million and $880,000, respectively.

Budget Picture & Funding Needs
The City has taken significant steps to respond to the economic impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic and implemented a 
fiscal sustainability plan to reduce expenditures. The City also received $19.27 million in one-time federal stimulus funds 
from the American Rescue Plan Act, the remainder of which was included in the current fiscal year budget.
As more fully described in the mid-year budget update on April 18, 2022, the City’s current 2021-22 fiscal year General 
Fund is expected to end with $160.2 million in revenues and $146.6 million in expenditures. While the City’s General Fund 
for the current fiscal year is expected to end with a surplus, the Long-term Financial Plan indicates the City will need to 
draw down on Rainy Day Reserves over the next 4 years. Over the next 10 years, the General Fund is projected to have 
enough resources to maintain core services at existing levels and for the Rainy Day Reserves to be restored to pre-
pandemic levels by 2032.
Although the General Fund is expected to be able to maintain existing service levels, there are a number of capital 
improvements and priorities that will require significant additional resources. Below are preliminary estimates (not 
accounting for inflation) for several capital and priority projects that have been identified and for which there aren’t 
sufficient resources:

Recreation Facilities Strategic Plan – $90 million


The following Council objectives will also require significant additional resources:


Meet the 100% trash reduction mandate





Implement bicycle and pedestrian projects and programs that address safety and align with adopted City plans

I N T R O D U C T I O N
Encompassing 15.9 square miles in the San Francisco Bay Area, the City of San Mateo was incor-
porated in 1894 and is currently home to an estimated 105,661 residents.1 One of only two
charter cities in San Mateo County, the City is governed by a five-member City Council, while the
City’s daily operations are managed by a dedicated team of employees that provide a full suite of
services to residents and the local business community.
Over the past decade, the City of San Mateo’s revenues have not kept pace with the growing
costs associated with providing municipal services and facilities. Although the City has been pro-
active in responding to this challenge by reducing costs where feasible and through effective
financial management practices, the practical reality is that existing revenues simply do not sup-
port the high quality services and facilities that residents have come to expect. The economic
fallout of the pandemic’s arrival in 2020 served to deepen this disparity, with forecasts at the
time anticipating a 90% reduction in the City’s transient occupancy tax (TOT) revenues, a 10%
reduction in sales tax revenues, and an annual budget deficit of $7 million. Although the City’s
Fiscal Sustainability Plan to reduce the cost of General Fund operations and one-time stimulus
funds from the American Rescue Plan Act have the City on course to maintain existing service
levels with a limited draw on the City’s Rainy Day Reserve over the next four years, this balance
does not include funding for capital improvements and other projects of interest to residents
and the City Council—such as improved road maintenance, maintenance of infrastructure, bicy-
cle and pedestrian safety projects, and projects outlined in the Recreation Facilities Strategic
Plan.
MOTIVATION FOR RESEARCH    The primary purpose of this survey was to produce an
unbiased, statistically reliable evaluation of voters' interest in supporting a local revenue mea-
sure to help close the funding gap noted above. Additionally, should the City decide to move for-
ward with a measure, the data can guide how best to structure a measure so it is consistent with
the community's priorities and expressed needs. Specifically, the study was designed to:
•
Gauge current support for enacting a local revenue measure to provide funding for general 
municipal services and facilities;
•
Identify the types of services and projects that voters are most interested in funding, should 
the measure pass;
•
Expose voters to arguments in favor of, and against, the proposed measure to assess how 
information affects support for the measure; and
•
Estimate support for the measure once voters are presented with the types of information
they will likely be exposed to during an election cycle.
It is important to note at the outset that voters’ opinions about tax measures are often some-
what fluid, especially when the amount of information they initially have about a measure is lim-
ited. How voters think and feel about a measure today may not be the same way they think and
feel once they have had a chance to hear more information about the measure during the elec-
tion cycle. Accordingly, to accurately assess the feasibility of establishing a local revenue mea-
sure to fund municipal services and facilities, it was important that in addition to measuring

design that employed multiple recruiting methods (email, text, and telephone) and multiple data

J U S T  T H E  F A C T S
The following section is an outline of the main factual findings from the survey. For the reader’s
convenience, we have organized the findings according to the section titles used in the body of
this report. Thus, if you would like to learn more about a particular finding, simply turn to the
appropriate report section.
IMPORTANCE OF ISSUES   
•
When asked in an open-ended manner to identify the most important issue facing San Mateo
that they would like the City to address, 13% of respondents could not think of an important
issue or indicated that there are currently no issues facing San Mateo—that everything is
fine. Among those who mentioned a specific issue, the top responses were affordability of
housing (25%), growth and development (21%), traffic congestion (16%), and crime/gangs/
drugs (11%).
INITIAL BALLOT TEST   
•
With only the information contained in the ballot statement, 59% of likely November 2022
voters indicated they would support a 1/4 cent sales tax to fund general city services in the
City of San Mateo, such as fire protection, paramedic, and 911 emergency response, police
patrols and crime prevention, maintenance of streets, parks, open space, and infrastructure,
traffic management, addressing homelessness, and other city services. Approximately one-
third of voters (32%) indicated they would oppose the sales tax, and 9% were unsure or
unwilling to share their opinion.
•
Although it would provide the same level of funding for the same types of general city ser-
vices, initial support for the proposed real property transfer tax increase was considerably
lower than that found for the sales tax. At the Initial Ballot Test, 51% of likely November
2022 voters stated that they would support the real property transfer tax, 41% were
opposed, whereas 9% were unsure or unwilling to share their opinion.
•
Among those who were unsure or opposed to a measure at the Initial Ballot Test, the most
common reasons for not supporting a measure were the perception that taxes are already
too high (46%), a concern that money has been or will be misspent (28%), and a belief that
the City already has enough money (12%) or that there are other ways municipal services can
be funded (11%).
PROJECTS & SERVICES   
•
Of the services tested that could be funded by a general tax, voters prioritized using the
funds to maintain parks, open spaces, and recreation facilities including courts, fields and
playgrounds (86% strongly or somewhat favor), maintain local streets and infrastructure
(85%), provide quick responses to 911 emergencies (84%), provide fire protection and para-
medic services (82%), and make improvements to roads, intersections, sidewalks, and bike
lanes to better manage traffic congestion on city streets (82%).

C O N C L U S I O N S
The bulk of this report is devoted to conveying the details of the study findings. In this section,
however, we attempt to ‘see the forest through the trees’ and note how the collective results of
the survey answer the key questions that motivated the research. The following conclusions are
based on True North’s interpretations of the survey results and the firm’s collective experience
conducting revenue measure studies for public agencies throughout the State.
Is a revenue measure 
feasible for the Novem-
ber 2022 ballot?
Voters value the services they receive from the City of San Mateo,
although they see opportunities for the City to better address issues like
housing affordability, traffic congestion, public safety, and maintenance
of streets, infrastructure, parks, and public spaces. How these senti-
ments translate into support for a local revenue measure to fund city ser-
vices—and feasibility of the measure if placed on a future ballot—
depends on the type of measure being considered.
Voters exhibited solid initial support for a 1/4 cent sales tax measure
that would fund general services in San Mateo, such as fire protection,
paramedic, and 911 emergency response, police patrols and crime pre-
vention, maintenance of streets, parks, open space, and infrastructure,
traffic management, addressing homelessness, and other city services.
With just the information conveyed in the 75-word ballot statement, 59%
of voters indicated they would support the sales tax. Although support
for the measure waned somewhat in response to negative arguments,
throughout the survey support remained at least 4 points above the sim-
ple majority (50% +1) required for passage of a general sales tax.
The results of this survey indicate that a sales tax measure is feasible for
the November 2022 ballot provided that it is accompanied by robust
community/opinion leader engagement, education, and communication
to raise awareness of the City’s service and facility challenges and build
community consensus around a proposal (more on this below).
The survey results were less promising with respect to the feasibility of a
real property transfer tax measure when applied to properties that sell
or transfer for $2,500,000 or more. Although it would provide the same
level of funding for the same types of general city services, initial sup-
port for the proposed real property transfer tax increase (51%) was con-
siderably lower than that found for the sales tax, and just 1% above that
required for passage of a general tax (50%+1). Support for the real prop-
erty transfer tax also waned over the course of the survey in response to
negative messaging, landing at 49% at the Final Ballot Test. That said,
raising the sale/transfer amount threshold from $2,500,000 to
$3,000,000 resulted in a 12% increase in support for the proposed mea-
sure (61% total support) near the end of the interview, although most of
the increase was soft support (probably yes).

The feasibility of a real property transfer tax increase for the November

not apply to properties below $3,000,000 and community education/

Which projects and ser-
vices do San Mateo vot-
ers view as priorities?
A general tax is “any tax imposed for general governmental purposes”2
and is distinguished from a special tax in that the funds raised by a gen-
eral tax are not earmarked for a specific purpose(s). Thus, a general tax
provides a municipality with a great deal of flexibility with respect to
what is funded by the measure on a year-to-year basis.
Although the San Mateo City Council would have the discretion to decide
how to spend the revenues, the survey results indicate that voters are
most interested in using the proceeds to fund public works and public
safety. Specifically, voters most strongly favored using measure pro-
ceeds to maintain parks, open spaces, and recreation facilities including
courts, fields and playgrounds, maintain local streets and infrastructure,
provide quick responses to 911 emergencies, provide fire protection and
paramedic services, and make improvements to roads, intersections,
sidewalks, and bike lanes to better manage traffic congestion on city
streets.
How might public infor-
mation/education affect 
support for the pro-
posed measure?
As noted in the body of this report, individuals’ opinions about revenue
measures are often not rigid, especially when the amount of information
presented to the public on a measure has been limited. Thus, in addition
to measuring current support for a measure, one of the goals of this
study was to explore how the introduction of additional information
about a measure may affect voters’ opinions about the proposal.
It is clear from the survey results that voters’ opinions about the pro-
posed measures are somewhat sensitive to the nature, and amount, of
information they have about the measure. Information about the specific
services that could be funded by the measure, as well as arguments in
favor of the measure, were found by many voters to be compelling rea-
sons to support the measure. However, voters also exhibited some sensi-
tivity to opposition arguments, and there is a risk they could be swayed
by divisive and hyper-partisan campaigning during the November elec-
tion cycle. Accordingly, one of the keys to building and sustaining sup-
port for a measure will be the presence of an effective, well-organized
public outreach effort and a separate, independent campaign that
focuses on the need for a measure as well as the many benefits that it
will bring.

How might changes to 
the economic or politi-

A survey is a snapshot in time—which means the results of this study
and the conclusions noted above must be viewed in light of the current
economic and political climates. On the one hand, this should provide
some reassurances to the City that a local revenue measure is feasible.
Even with concerns regarding the pandemic, inflation, high gas prices,
and the trajectory of the economy, voters were generally supportive of
the proposed sales tax measure on the natural.
On the other hand, the months leading up to the November 2022 elec-
tion are likely to be punctuated with significant events on the public
health, economic, and political fronts. Exactly how these events unfold
and may shape voters’ opinions remains to be seen. Should the economy
and/or political climate improve, support for the measure could
increase. Conversely, negative economic and/or political developments
(including devolving into a hyper-partisan environment), could dampen
support for the measure below what was recorded in this study.

I N I T I A L  B A L L O T  T E S T
As noted in the Introduction, the primary objective of this study was to estimate voter support
for two potential ballot measures—a 1/4 cent sales tax to provide funding for general city ser-
vices in the City of San Mateo, such as fire protection, paramedic, and 911 emergency response,
police patrols and crime prevention, maintenance of streets, parks, open space, and infrastruc-
ture, traffic management, addressing homelessness, and other city services, and a real property
transfer tax increase on properties that sell/transfer for more than $2,500,000 that would fund
the same list of general city services. To this end, Questions 3 and 5 were designed to take an
early assessment of support for the proposed measures.
The motivation for placing Questions 3 and 5 up-front in the survey is twofold. First, voter sup-
port for a measure can often depend on the amount of information they have about a measure.
At this point in the survey, the respondent has not been provided information about the pro-
posed measure beyond what is presented in the ballot language. This situation is analogous to a
voter casting a ballot with limited knowledge about the measure, such as what might occur in
the absence of an effective education campaign. Question 3 (and 5), also known as the Initial Bal-
lot Test, is thus a good estimate of voter support for the proposed measure as it is today, on the
natural. Because the Initial Ballot Test provides a gauge of natural support for a measure, it also
serves a second purpose in that it provides a useful baseline from which to judge the impact of
various information items conveyed later in the survey on voter support for a measure.
SUPPORT BY MEASURE TYPE   Figure 2 on the next page presents the results of the Initial
Ballot Test for both the sales tax and real property transfer tax measures. Overall, 59% of likely
November 2022 voters indicated they would support a 1/4 cent sales tax to fund general city
services in the City of San Mateo, such as fire protection, paramedic, and 911 emergency
response, police patrols and crime prevention, maintenance of streets, parks, open space, and
infrastructure, traffic management, addressing homelessness, and other city services. Approxi-
mately one-third of voters (32%) indicated they would oppose the sales tax, and 9% were unsure
or unwilling to share their opinion. For general taxes in California, the level of support recorded
at the Initial Ballot Test for the sales tax measure is approximately 9 percentage points above the
simple majority (50%+1) required for passage.
Although it would provide the same level of funding for the same types of general city services,
initial support for the proposed real property transfer tax increase was considerably lower than
that found for the sales tax. At the Initial Ballot Test, 51% of likely November 2022 voters stated
that they would support the real property transfer tax, 41% were opposed, whereas 9% were
unsure or unwilling to share their opinion.

P O S I T I V E  A R G U M E N T S
If the City chooses to place a measure on an upcoming ballot, voters will be exposed to various
arguments about the proposal in the ensuing months. Proponents of the measure will present
arguments to try to persuade voters to support the measure, just as opponents may present
arguments to achieve the opposite goal. For this study to be a reliable gauge of voter support for
the proposed sales tax or real property transfer tax measure, it is important that the survey sim-
ulate the type of discussion and debate that will occur prior to the vote taking place and identify
how this information ultimately shapes voters’ opinions about the measure.
The objective of Question 8 was thus to present respondents with arguments in favor of the pro-
posed measure and identify whether they felt the arguments were convincing reasons to support
it. Arguments in opposition to the measure were also presented and are discussed later in this
report (see Negative Arguments on page 24). Within each series, specific arguments were admin-
istered in random order to avoid a systematic position bias. For the reader’s convenience, we
show the results of arguments presented for the sales tax in Figure 5, and those for the real
property transfer tax in Figure 6.

M E T H O D O L O G Y
The following sections outline the methodology used in the study, as well as the motivation for
using certain techniques.
QUESTIONNAIRE DEVELOPMENT   Dr. McLarney of True North Research worked closely
with the City of San Mateo to develop a questionnaire that covered the topics of interest and
avoided possible sources of systematic measurement error, including position-order effects,
wording effects, response-category effects, scaling effects, and priming. Several questions
included multiple individual items. Because asking items in a set order can lead to a systematic
position bias in responses, items were asked in random order for each respondent.
Some of the questions asked in this study were presented only to a subset of respondents. For
example, only individuals who did not support the sales tax or were unsure at the Initial Ballot
Test (Question 3) were asked if there was a particular reason they did not support or were unsure
about the measure (Question 4). The questionnaire included with this report (see Questionnaire
& Toplines on page 36) identifies the skip patterns that were used during the interview to ensure
that each respondent received the appropriate questions.
PROGRAMMING, PRE-TEST & TRANSLATION   Prior to fielding the survey, the ques-
tionnaire was CATI (Computer Assisted Telephone Interviewing) programmed to assist interview-
ers when conducting telephone interviews. The CATI program automatically navigates skip
patterns, randomizes the appropriate question items, and alerts the interviewer to certain types
of keypunching mistakes should they occur. The survey was also programmed into a passcode-
protected online survey application to allow online participation for sampled voters. The integ-
rity of the questionnaire was pre-tested internally by True North and by dialing into voter house-
holds in the City prior to formally beginning the survey. Once finalized, the questionnaire was
professionally translated into Spanish to allow for data collection in English and Spanish.
SAMPLE   The survey was administered to a stratified and clustered random sample of regis-
tered voters in the City of San Mateo who are likely to participate in the November 2022 election,
with a subset who are also likely to participate in the lower turnout June 2022 primary election.
Consistent with the profile of this universe, the sample was stratified into clusters, each repre-
senting a combination of age, gender, and household party type. Individuals were then randomly
selected based on their profile into an appropriate cluster. This method ensures that if a person
of a particular profile refuses to participate in the study, they are replaced by an individual who
shares their same profile.
To reliably estimate support for a sales tax measure as well as a real property transfer tax mea-
sure, a split-sample methodology was employed such that 339 voters were administered a sur-
vey that focused on a sales tax, whereas a separate 339 voters were asked questions regarding a
real property transfer tax measure. All respondents received generic questions that applied to
both types of measures.
The split-sample approach is used because it is the most reliable method of estimating voter
support for alternative tax measures. Prior research (and actual election results) have consis-
tently shown that attempting to estimate support for multiple tax measures (e.g., sales tax and

STATISTICAL MARGIN OF ERROR   By using the probability-based sampling design
noted above, True North ensured that the final sample was representative of voters in the City
who are likely to participate in the November 2022 election. The results of the sample can thus
be used to estimate the opinions of all voters likely to participate in the November 2022 elec-
tion. Because not all voters participated in the survey, however, the results have what is known
as a statistical margin of error due to sampling. The margin of error refers to the difference
between what was found in the survey of 678 voters for a particular question and what would
have been found if all estimated 42,956 likely November 2022 voters in the City had been sur-
veyed.
Figure 13 provides a graphic plot of the maximum margin of error in this study. The maximum
margin of error for a dichotomous percentage result occurs when the answers are evenly split
such that 50% provide one response and 50% provide the alternative response. For this survey,
the maximum margin of error is ± 3.7% for questions answered by all 678 respondents, ± 5.3%
for questions specific to a particular measure (sales tax or real property transfer tax).

RECRUITING & DATA COLLECTION   The survey followed a mixed-method design that
employed multiple recruiting methods (email, text, and telephone) and multiple data collection
methods (telephone and online). Telephone interviews averaged 17 minutes in length and were
conducted during weekday evenings (5:30PM to 9PM) and on weekends (10AM to 5PM). It is stan-
dard practice not to call during the day on weekdays because most working adults are unavail-
able and thus calling during those hours would likely bias the sample.
Voters recruited via email and text were assigned a unique passcode to ensure that only voters
who received an invitation could access the online survey site, and that each voter could com-
plete the survey only one time. During the data collection period, an email reminder notice was
also sent to encourage participation among those who had yet to take the survey. A total of 678
surveys were completed in English and Spanish between April 25 and May 1, 2022.
DATA PROCESSING   Data processing consisted of checking the data for errors or inconsis-
tencies, coding and recoding responses, weighting, and preparing frequency analyses and cross-
tabulations.
ROUNDING    Numbers that end in 0.5 or higher are rounded up to the nearest whole num-
ber, whereas numbers that end in 0.4 or lower are rounded down to the nearest whole number.
These same rounding rules are also applied, when needed, to arrive at numbers that include a
decimal place in constructing figures and tables. Occasionally, these rounding rules lead to small
discrepancies in the first decimal place when comparing tables and charts for a given question.

SUBJECT:
OneShoreline – Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update from OneShoreline on efforts addressing sea level rise in the area and provide feedback.
BACKGROUND:
In 2018, the City was part of a Staff Advisory Team (SAT) organized by the City/County Association of Government’s 
Countywide Water Coordination Committee tasked with developing a Countywide agency focused on sea level rise. The 
effort led to the creation of the Flood and Sea Level Rise Resiliency Agency / OneShoreline in 2019. OneShoreline’s mission 
is to address sea level rise, flooding, coastal erosion, and large-scale stormwater improvements through integrated 
regional planning, design, permitting, project implementation, and long-term operations and maintenance. When created, 
OneShoreline was tasked to develop an Integrated Flood and Sea Level Rise Resiliency Investment Plan for the Bayshore 
and the Coastside to address short-term (2050) and long-term (2100) sea level rise, flooding, and coastal erosion. On April 
1, 2019, the City committed to provide funding in the amount of $55,000 per year to OneShoreline for the first three years.
OneShoreline will provide an update and receive Council feedback and questions. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact associated with receiving this update.
