SUBJECT:
Park Development Fee Credits - Policy Considerations 
RECOMMENDATION:
Review findings from Economic and Planning Systems (EPS) study on park development fee credits and provide comment 
and feedback for policy direction.   
BACKGROUND:
In July 2021 the Commission received a report prepared by Matrix Consulting Group with their findings and 
recommendations regarding the City’s park related development fees, i.e. Park In-Lieu and Park Impact.  The Park In-Lieu 
Fee is governed by the Quimby Act and applies to residential projects that require the subdivision of land.  The revenue 
generated from this fee allows the City to purchase land to maintain the City’s standard of 2 acres per 1,000 residents.  The 
Park Impact Fee is levied on new residential units that do not require the subdivision of land and can be used for all capital 
related costs, including both land acquisition and facility improvements.  The calculation formula is the same for both  
fees. 
The Matrix study recommended the elimination of the In-Lieu (Quimby) Fee and to incorporate the acreage standard into 
the City’s Park Impact Fee.  By eliminating the In-Lieu Fee and incorporating the parks standard in the Impact Fee, the City 
can account for the broader impact future development will have on parks resources and eliminate the need for a 
standalone fee utilized only on subdivided land.  
Currently, the Park In Lieu and Park Impact Fees are assessed only on residential developments.  However, because future 
employees would also utilize the City’s parks, the Matrix study also established a nexus between future commercial 
development and park maintenance and improvement costs, albeit at a lesser impact than residents.  
The Commission feedback was favorable and supported the direction provided by the City Council to eliminate use of the 
Quimby Act and establish only one Park Impact Fee; to broaden applicability to non-residential projects; and to set fees at 
a level that will allow for the City to recover as much of its costs as possible, while considering possible exceptions for all 
affordable housing projects.  The Commission also requested a review of the current framework by which facility credits 
are granted (for on-site recreation amenities provided by the developer) and whether any changes to those are warranted.  
Staff entered into a contract with EPS to perform that study and they are prepared to outline their findings and possible 
policy directions. 
The Commission is being asked to provide comment and feedback on the general policy directions that will be outlined by 
EPS in their presentation.  The feedback will then inform staff and EPS on the development of more specific guidelines as 
well as possible calculation formulas.

POLICY CONSIDERATIONS
1.
Should the City provide credit for privately-owned recreation spaces?
–
No – City collects fees, buys land, and develops traditional City parks and recreation facilities.
–
Yes – Private developers create open space and recreation facilities as part of their projects.
2.
Should the City provide credit for exclusive-access recreation facilities?
–
No – Parks and recreation facilities should be open to the public.
–
Yes – Exclusive facilities satisfy demand that would otherwise be met by City parks.
3.
Should the City reduce credit for privately-owned recreation spaces?
–
No – Privately-owned recreation spaces are as good as public sites.
–
Yes – The City prefers publicly-owned facilities.
4.
Should the City reduce credit for exclusive-use recreation spaces?
–
No – Exclusive facilities are similarly desirable to publicly-accessible facilities.
–
Yes – The City prefers publicly-accessible facilities.

SUBJECT:
Proposed Five Year (2022-2027) Parks and Recreation Capital Improvement Plan
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and comment on the Proposed Five Year Capital Improvement Plan for FY 2022-2027. 
BACKGROUND:
The Parks and Recreation Department develops an updated five-year capital improvement plan every year. Staff reviews 
the current list of identified park improvements as well as estimates of expected revenues over the same period and 
recommends changes based on priorities and anticipated income. The Proposed FY 2022-2027 CIP plan is included as 
Attachment 1. Budget estimates for new and existing projects are provided for each of the next five years. As the largest 
source of revenue for park and recreation capital improvements are park in-lieu and park impact fees which rely on 
residential development activity, the proposed expenditure plan is subject to change, especially for years 3-5. Parks and 
Recreation staff perform due diligence by speaking with Planning Division staff as well as making direct contact with 
developers in order to ascertain the likelihood of a project becoming “shovel ready” within the time frame.
On this front it is important to note that after consulting with the Planning Division the Department had forecast 
$3,407,000 in In Lieu fees for 2021-22 and year to date we have received $54,344 and no other large projects are 
projected to pull building permits by the end of the FY.  In addition,  our current study of development related fees (once 
completed) will impact our receipts from 2023-24 onwards. The unknown impact of the Fee study in conjunction with 
uncertainty of receipts demonstrates the difficulty in budgeting with confidence for a 5-year period and need to reprofile 
the Capital Improvement Plan.
In the proposed five-year plan, the primary focus for improvement projects are upgrades to playgrounds and surrounding 
areas given their age and current condition. Priorities continue to be East Hillsdale, Washington, Los Prados, West Hillsdale 
and Skate Plaza upgrades. All these projects have exceeded (or will shortly exceed) their 20-year life cycle, whilst the Skate 
Plaza funds will be directed towards correction of safety hazards. Further out and as funding allows, staff has tentatively 
programmed funds for the conversion of a third sports field to synthetic turf as well as identified some funds to support 
the implementation of the Recreation Facilities Strategic Plan. As is evident in the Proposed FY 2022-2027 CIP plan, the 
Department has reprofiled expenditures reflecting the lack of certainty of funding receipts and potential for large scale 
projects to be further delayed. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Revenue projections are considered estimates given that development projects may be delayed or may never be 
implemented.
