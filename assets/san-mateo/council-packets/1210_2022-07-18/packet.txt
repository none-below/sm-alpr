SUBJECT:
Pedestrian Malls – Draft Design
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and comment on the draft schematic design proposal for short-term improvements to the pedestrian malls on B 
Street between 1st and 3rd Avenues.
BACKGROUND:
The City Council approved permanent street closures and creation of two pedestrian malls on B Street between 1st and 
3rd Avenues in October 2021. During the pedestrian mall establishment process, staff recommended that the City 
complete the capital improvements needed for the operation of the pedestrian malls in two separate phases. The first 
phase, to be completed in the short-term, includes the installation of bollards, required traffic signal modifications, and 
street furnishings and other improvements to replace the temporary barricades and beautify the street segments. These 
short-term improvements will allow the City to continue to pilot the pedestrian malls outside of the pandemic and 
provides flexibility to change the block configuration in the future (perhaps by adding an additional block as has been 
discussed or potentially removing a block).
The second phase or long-term phase would include more extensive improvements, including a complete reconfiguration 
of the two street segments, likely raising the street to the level to be flush with the sidewalk, reconfiguring storm water 
drainage, and other more costly and non-reversible improvements. Given the high projected cost of this phase, which is 
estimated to be several million dollars per block,  it is anticipated to occur at a  future date  and may require the formation 
of an assessment district to help support the expense. 
In January 2022, the City entered into an agreement for design services with Community Design + Architecture (CD+A) to 
develop a streetscape design for short-term improvements to the pedestrian malls. Since that date, the design team has 
been working closely with staff and the business stakeholders on the pedestrian mall to develop the draft design plan that 
is included as Attachment 1 to this report. 
Existing Conditions and Design Challenges
The initial project work included extensive discussions with the businesses located along the pedestrian malls to 
understand their logistical needs in terms of loading, garbage, and other business operations. Additionally, the design 
team completed an engineering survey of the street to identify the cross slopes across different segments of the street and 
a curb use analysis to identify ADA parking space and loading zone locations on the surrounding streets.  
The business logistics survey indicated that business deliveries come at all hours of the day. Service vehicles are allowed to 
access the street prior to 10:00 am each day, which is authorized by Council resolution. While this will assist with garbage

service and a portion of deliveries, additional loading zones need to be created to facilitate deliveries during other hours to 
prevent trucks from parking in front of the pedestrian mall entrances, which impedes traffic flow. Additionally, the 
restaurant businesses all stated a need for increased short-term parking to allow for take-out, pick-up, and delivery 
services. 
The cross-slope analysis found that the slope varies significantly depending on the location within the two blocks. In some 
locations nearest to the curb there is an over 10% slope, although other portions of the former parking space areas have 
only a 3-4% slope. The slope within the center Emergency Vehicle Access (EVA) lane at the top of the crown of the street is 
significantly flatter (approximately 2%). In addition, the crown of the street is several inches above the height of the 
sidewalk curb. These means that in the future, in order to create a flush street, certain portions of the street will first need 
to be ground down and this might require utility relocations. As part of this design contract, the CD+A team will pothole to 
identify the location of underground utilities, which will assist with the planning for the long-term design.
Staff worked with the design team to identify short-term solutions to address the extreme cross slopes in the street. 
Unfortunately, we were not able to come up with any design solutions that did not involve significant repaving of the 
street, which would be at great expense and be more of a long-term, irreversible improvement. As a result, staff is 
allowing businesses to put in platforms, provided that they are designed to be easily removable for special events. Given 
the variation of the slope on different portions of the street, not all businesses will need platforms in order to have a 
comfortable dining experience. In addition, staff is recommending to all of the businesses that they put at least some 
tables and chairs along the sidewalk to have tables that provide additional ADA accessibility.
Operational Design Improvements
The draft design includes a number of critical operations-related improvements to transition the pedestrian mall away 
from its current temporary state and make it more functional for the downtown. First and foremost, the water-filled 
barricades will be removed and bollards and planters will be used to block off the street. In order to allow for vehicle 
access to the EVA lane, the center bollard will fold down.
The current EVA lane is 12-feet wide. After discussions with Recology and SMC Fire, it was determined that this width is 
too narrow to allow for reasonable access by trucks. As a result, the draft design expands the center EVA line to 16 feet. 
The design team developed a curb allocation plan that provides for loading zones at each intersection in both the east and 
west direction, additional short-term parking, and on-street ADA spaces to replace the two that were on the pedestrian 
mall blocks.
Other operational improvements that are covered in the draft design include removing the current lane and parking 
markings, removing parking meters and related signage, and adjusting the traffic signals to account for the street closures 
(both removing physical signal heads and adjusting signal timing). In addition, the draft design includes adding removable 
ADA ramps to allow for mid-block access from the sidewalk to the pedestrian malls, bike racks, and additional garbage 
cans on each block.
Aesthetic and Public Use Enhancements
The draft design also includes features intended to enhance the looks and feel of the pedestrian malls and to provide a 
greater ability for the public to use the spaces outside of patronizing the businesses on the blocks. The draft design 
designates two areas on each block to serve as public use zones. These zones are located in front of non-restaurant 
businesses in order to not interfere with any restaurant’s ability to provide outdoor dining. On each block, one of the 
zones will have tables and chairs that can be used for the public to eat food purchased at businesses located outside of the 
pedestrian mall. The other zone will include creative non-table seating options. Activities that could occur in these public 
zones, outside of planned special events, include street musicians and mobile parks and recreation activities. Planter pots 
will be used to further enhance the look at feel of these public areas.
The most expensive and impactful aesthetic improvement that is proposed is painting a pattern on the street. The paint 
that would be used has an estimated duration of between 5 to 10 years before it would need to be refreshed. The 
durability will depend on how much the EVA lane is used by vehicles and also on the frequency of power-washing. As a

result, this design option may require a significant amount of maintenance to ensure it remains attractive. 
Picking a paint pattern for the street is perhaps the most challenging design decision that would need to be made as part 
of this process. The design team developed 6 preliminary options for consideration. Please note that the on-the-street 
experience of these options will be very different than what one sees viewing them from a birds-eye view in plan layout. 
The first 2 options (A and A-A) are intended to be an organic shape in a simple pattern. The proposed color can be easily 
changed out, if this option is preferred. Options B and B-B are inspired by the red bricks in the existing downtown and the 
diamond pattern in the cinema plaza. The last 2 options, C and D, are geometric patterns in color palettes that borrow 
from the City of San Mateo seal and create a bold color experience. If Council supports the concept of painting the streets, 
staff recommends that the Council narrow down the options to roughly 3 to 4 and then putting out a community survey 
and letting the community provide input on its preferred option. 
All of the street paint design options leave a space on each block that can be used for the Social Equity Community art 
project that the City Council has directed staff to move forward with. The selection of the art that would go there would be 
through a separate process, the parameters of which have not yet been determined. The art portion would be funded by 
the Art in Public Places Fund and could potentially include rotating murals that are replaced every few years.  The 
remainder of the street painting treatment would not be considered public art and would need to be maintained through 
the capital improvement program.
The street painting concepts also include painting the intersection of 2nd and B Street. Including paint here would provide 
a visual tie to connects the two pedestrian mall blocks. However, the intersection will continue to see cross-traffic, which 
may mean that the paint in this location does not last quite as long and may get marred by tire tracks. City Council 
direction is needed on whether painting the intersection is a priority.
Business Feedback
Staff held two meetings with the business community to present the draft design concept. The first meeting was with the 
Downtown San Mateo Association (DSMA) board on June 14th, and the second meeting, held on June 21st, was with the 
business and property owners on the pedestrian malls. At these meetings, the business community expressed appreciation 
that the City was taking their logistical needs into consideration. There were no major concerns stated with the draft 
design, but they did want to make sure that the City takes into consideration long-term maintenance, cleanliness, and 
upkeep. When presented with the paint options for the street, there was no one option that was preferred. Some 
participants appreciated the options that echoed the existing pavement pattern in the cinema plaza (Options B and B-B), 
while others preferred the two geometric options (Options C and D).
Proposed Costs and Project Phasing
The design is still in the schematic phase, so the final cost to implement has yet to be determined. The design team 
prepared a Rough Order of Magnitude cost estimate. Allowing for high contingencies given the preliminary phase of the 
design, the cost of implementing these proposed improvements will be between $600,000 and $1,000,000. Of this cost, 
roughly 40% is for the operational improvements (bollards, traffic signals, parking meter removals, curb allocation 
adjustments), and the remainder is for the aesthetic and public use enhancements. Currently, there is $600,000 allocated 
in the fiscal year 2022-23 Capital Improvement Program to implement this project.
Staff is strategizing on the possible phasing of this project. Given the unique and diverse nature of the tasks that are 
covered under the design, it may make sense to do some of the work, such as the sign removals,  parking meter removals, 
traffic signal adjustments, and bollard installations, through existing on-call contractors and/or the City’s maintenance 
crews, and then limit the bid package or packages to the aesthetic improvements, such as the paint and planters. Some of 
this work can proceed on a more expediated schedule depending on contractor availability.

Hello,  
 
Regarding the agenda item on the B St pedestrian mall design: 
 
Crosswalk safety at B St/2nd Ave: The planned paint scheme at the crosswalk is a good improvement that signals to 
pedestrians they can use the entire junction to cross.  
I have continued concerns that drivers at that intersection often run the red light when they do not see pedestrians 
(pedestrians are often still present near the white barriers; I have been nearly struck multiple times crossing while cars 
had a red light). 
 
Lack of shade and wind protection continues: Since the new rules around canopies and walls on sidewalk dining 
facilities, the quality of the outdoor space has markedly declined. Windy days feel much colder. Sunny days feel much 
hotter, and the umbrellas allowed are much poorer than the full canopies that were previously allowed.  
Please reconsider the rules to permit better shade and wind protection both on B Street and other downtown streets.
