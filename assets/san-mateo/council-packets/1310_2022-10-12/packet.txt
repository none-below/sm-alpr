SUBJECT:
Transit-Oriented Development Pedestrian Access Plan – Draft Plan Review
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive and review the draft Transit-Oriented Development Pedestrian Access Plan and make a recommendation to City 
Council for adoption.
BACKGROUND:
The Transit-Oriented Development Pedestrian Access Plan (Plan) began in April 2021. The City was awarded a 
Transportation Development Act (TDA) Article 3 grant in the fiscal year 2019-20 for approximately $75,000 to fund the 
Plan. Matching funds from the City were provided to fully fund the $143,000 project. The Plan assesses capital 
improvement needs along walking routes to transit that are within a one-half-mile radius of the City’s three Caltrain 
stations and other high-quality transit stops to increase safety and comfort for pedestrians of all ages and abilities. Based 
on the assessment, infrastructure and programmatic improvements are recommended in the Plan to complete the 
pedestrian realm and improve access to transit within the study area. Following a competitive process, the City entered 
into an agreement with Fehr & Peers to assist with developing the Plan. 
Staff and the consultant team presented the findings from community outreach, existing conditions review, and project 
prioritization effort at the February 9, 2022 Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission meeting. At this meeting, staff 
will present the contents of the Draft Plan. 
Draft Transit-Oriented Development Pedestrian Access Plan
Staff is requesting feedback from the Commission on the Draft Plan, and ultimately a recommendation to City Council for 
adoption. Staff will provide a brief overview of the Plan components; the link to the full Draft Plan is included in 
Attachment 1. 
Community Outreach and Engagement
A variety of platforms, tools, and venues were used for the outreach effort to maximize participation and achieve the goals 
in the Community Engagement Plan of informing, educating, and listening to all voices in the community. Chapter 2 of the 
Draft Plan provides an overview of the extensive community engagement and outreach process which included six pop-up 
events, three virtual focus meetings with local community groups, an online survey, interactive online mapping activity, 
and sidewalk decals with a QR code to the project webpage. Overall, the team spoke with more than 100 people at pop-up 
events and focus group meetings and received over 300 comments at in-person events and on the online interactive 
mapping activity. 
Needs Assessment

Findings from the community outreach effort and existing conditions review were used to assess the current pedestrian 
realm and identify areas of concern within the study area. In the Downtown study area, streets leading directly to the 
Caltrain Station, including First Avenue, North Railroad Avenue, and Transit Center Way, were found to be lacking 
pedestrian facilities that prioritize pedestrians and create a pedestrian-friendly environment. For example, improving the 
crossing at Transit Center Way and adding wayfinding and placemaking in the surrounding area would increase pedestrian 
comfort and safety and enhance the connection to the downtown area. For the Hayward Park and Hillsdale study areas, 
the two major challenges identified were station access streets not meeting City standards and State Route 92 and the 
Caltrain tracks as physical barriers limiting pedestrian access to station entrances. Pedestrian infrastructure needs along El 
Camino Real were found to be consistent for the majority of the corridor in the City. Adding pedestrian facilities and 
amenities such as safer crossings, clear walking paths, and pedestrian-scale street lighting would greatly enhance the 
pedestrian experience on El Camino Real. 
Priority Projects
City staff and the consultant team presented the draft priority project list to the City Council at the June 20th meeting. 
Project areas within the study area were identified through the prioritization process and assigned a name that correlates 
to the general location of the project. In the Downtown study area, for example, the project areas are named El Camino 
Real - Downtown, Downtown Gateway, and North Station Access. Within each project area are individual improvements 
that can be implemented separately from one another, which allows for greater flexibility with implementation while still 
achieving the same goals for the overall project area. 
Chapter 4 of the Draft Plan provides graphical displays, or ‘cut sheets’ for each project area. Each cut sheet displays a map 
showing improvements and provides a summary of issues identified in the needs assessment and how improvements will 
address those issues. The icons on the cut sheets correspond to the countermeasure toolbox in Chapter 4. The toolbox 
presents typical pedestrian safety improvements that are also applicable to other City streetscape and redevelopment 
projects. Countermeasures include high-visibility and mid-block crosswalks, pedestrian-scale lighting, ADA-compliant curb 
ramps, and wayfinding signage. 
The improvements included in the Draft Plan aim to enhance pedestrian access, safety, and comfort near transit. For 
example, in Downtown one major recommended improvement is the construction of a new station entrance on the 
northeast side of the station at Cypress and Railroad Avenues. This entrance would provide transit users coming from the 
northeast direct access to the northbound platform and would allow traveling across the tracks to the pedestrian path on 
the west side, connecting pedestrians to the southbound platform and North B Street. At Hayward Park, new high-visibility 
crosswalks are proposed along Leslie Street at 17th and 19th Avenues as well as pedestrian scale lighting under the State 
Route 92 overpass. At Hillsdale, additional wayfinding is proposed around the station, and a new sidewalk on the west side 
of Delaware Street to close the existing sidewalk gap between Bay Meadows and the station entrance on 25th Avenue.
Implementation and Funding Strategy
Chapter 5 provides funding and implementation strategies, a near-term implementation action plan, and planning-level 
cost estimates for improvements in the Plan. Implementation strategies were identified that could support funding and 
streamline the construction of improvements. Those strategies are incorporating pedestrian improvements with other 
ongoing City and regional projects or routine maintenance, utilizing local resources earmarked for transportation 
improvements, updating zoning ordinances and evaluating private developer contributions to fund and construct 
improvements, and applying for grant funding. 
The implementation action plan provides the next steps for each implementation strategy. Near term implementation 
actions include conducting a fee study to determine the appropriate amount of private developer contributions to fund 
improvements in the Plan and identifying whether any upcoming resurfacing, maintenance, or other streetscape projects 
overlap with improvements in the Plan. Planning-level cost estimates for improvements in the Plan are provided in 
Chapter 5 to support the implementation strategies and action plan.
The following are policy considerations needed to move forward with the proposed implementation strategies in the Draft 
Plan:

• Ability to upgrade pedestrian facilities to meet City standards – Limited public right-of-way and existing City 
ordinances limit the ability of development projects to fully upgrade their project frontage to meet City standards. Once 
the Plan is adopted, the implementation action plan proposes that the City consider options including reviewing and 
updating zoning ordinances, reducing vehicle lanes, requiring easements, changing circulation, or a combination of 
strategies, to ensure pedestrian facilities can be upgraded to be fully compliant with City standards. 
• Identifying funding sources for off-site pedestrian improvements – Being located close to transit, new 
development projects within TOD areas add extra capacity to pedestrian facilities leading to transit. Following Plan 
adoption, the implementation action plan proposes that the City conduct a fee study to calculate and determine the 
appropriate amount of private developer contributions to fund the construction of off-site improvements on paths leading 
transit and evaluate a VMT impact fee option for projects outside the TOD area as an option to meet their mitigation 
requirements by funding projects that reduce VMT. 
Next Steps
Following this meeting, Commission and public feedback will be incorporated into the Draft Plan. The Draft Plan is 
expected to be presented to the City Council for adoption in November 2022.
