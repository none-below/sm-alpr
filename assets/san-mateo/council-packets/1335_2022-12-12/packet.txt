SUBJECT:
General Municipal Election November 8, 2022 - Certification of Results 
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a resolution to declare the results of the General Municipal Election held on November 8, 2022 as presented in the 
Certificate and Statement of Results of Votes Cast for the City of San Mateo. 
BACKGROUND:
The City of San Mateo held its first candidate by-district election for Districts 1, 3, and 5 on November 8, 2022; and for 
submitting to the voters Measure CC - to Increase Existing Real Property Transfer Tax Rate for Properties Sold for $10 
Million or More.
The number of ballots cast for a candidate in each district were District 1 – 8,960; District 3 – 6,700; and District 5 – 8,417. 
The number of ballots cast citywide for the ballot measure were 34,630 with 23,454 voting yes and 9,227 voting no. The 
ordinance approving Measure CC was therefore adopted by the voters on November 8, 2022 and will go into effect ten 
days after certification of election results on December 22, 2022. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
The City Clerk has budget of $213,500 to cover costs of this election. It is not anticipated that additional funding will be 
necessary. 
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This resolution of adoption of the election results is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or 
administrative activity that will not result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines 
Section 15378(b)(5).)

SUBJECT:
City Council Vacancy – Applicant Interviews and Presentation
RECOMMENDATION:
Determine the Council appointee to the vacant seat for a term effective December 12, 2022 through the end of the term 
of former Deputy Mayor Diane Papan.
BACKGROUND:
On November 17, 2022 Deputy Mayor Diane Papan submitted her resignation letter to the City Clerk with an effective date 
of December 4, 2022, creating a vacancy on the City Council for her at-large seat that is set to end on December 2, 2024 
(per City Charter Section 2.01 (c) 1, the term of office begins on the first regular council meeting in December immediately 
following the general election).
Council met on November 21, 2022 to approve the timing, advertising, and application process for the vacancy. The 
application period opened on November 22, 2022 and closed on December 7, 2022 at 5 pm following a robust outreach 
and advertising campaign. Nine applications were received and qualified to be considered for the appointment, one 
applicant, Maureen Freschet withdrew on Friday December 9, 2022. 
The City Council established a special meeting for Monday, December 12, 2022 at 6 pm in the Council Chambers to receive 
a ten-minute presentation from each applicant. Council may ask follow-up questions of each applicant. The City Attorney 
and City Clerk drew the order by lots and the order of the presentation will be:

Following the applicant presentations, public comment will be taken, and then Council may have some discussion, and 
then they will conduct a vote via paper ballot. The City Attorney will collect the ballots and tally the vote and hand to the 
City Clerk who will announce the results. The Council may elect to conduct as many votes as they desire until a consensus 
is reached, and Council also may amend the selection process with a majority vote to do so.  
City Charter Section 2.08 – Vacancies in Office states:

1. Climate disruption. Every public safety building, the new sewage plant, large swaths of the city
from the bay to the El Camino, major highways and our power grid are in extremely high rick
hazardous areas. We need to take inventory of our most important facilities, acknowledge the
possibility of displacement for neighborhoods and have plans in place for redundant power
sources to make us a more resilient city.

The first thing | would do is aggressively educate the public to the dangers we face, set

priorities to address the most pressing ones and apply for grants to the state and federal

agencies to be matched with local funding sources.

2. Housing and transportation. These two issues can and both can be solved which in turn
will help us reduce our GHG emissions. The city went through a downtown specific plan

recently and its key finding was that to be financially viable 20,000 people needed to live

downtown. With a combination of TOD along the rail corridor line, increasing the
number of residences in downtown and along the El Camino Real coupled with a highly
efficient Accessory Dwelling Unit program we can address our housing needs. Locating
most of the new housing in the above-mentioned areas of the city would remove some
of the pressure on the existing neighborhoods of going through radical changes yet still
being able to help reduce the overall housing crisis through the ADU program.

Concentrating housing in high density, walkable areas with services and jobs is how cities
have been developed for centuries. With the advent of the automobile suburbs were
developed and that substantially changed the way societies functioned. We are in a time
that is demanding us to again change how we live our daily lives. It is time to meld the
suburbs with metropolitan features to form a new way of using our resources and planning.

To expand housing downtown will be a boom to the small business through not only more
shoppers yet also in attracting and keeping the work force it cannot get today because of
the lack of affordable housing. Affordable housing works as an economic engine and will
also reduce GHG by reducing long distance commutes for low paying jobs. The expansion of
office buildings in recent years has turned us into an employment center and now supplying
housing for those high paid workers and the low wage workers who come to serve them is
an urgent issue. ,

Another component of the housing issue is the rapidly changing demographics of our
region. We have an aging population with its own special needs in housing. Housing near
services is attractive to both the millennials and aging baby boomers. What we are building
today for the work force has a strong possibility of being occupied by retired folks who will
then need to sell their single-family homes. Building enough walkable neighborhoods and
keeping our economy strong enough to make the single-family homes an option for as
many workers to purchase will be highly important as we move forward.

COVID-19 Recovery and Quality of Life

We have to help San Mateo residents and businesses get back on their feet during
these times of pandemic recovery hindered by sky high inflation. The inflation we have
seen over the past year alone has drastically caused hardship to all San Mateans
causing housing evictions which contribute to homelessness issues, our youth not
getting the nutrition they deserve due to choices between rent and groceries, and more.
The consequences of the pandemic also still need to be addressed such as the
education gaps of our children resulting from learning loss, small businesses closures
unable to pay back their PPP loans, and more.

One of the actions | would execute to address the quality of life and pandemic recovery
priorities is to partner with my colleagues to ensure that the budget report for FY 22-23
includes a survey from the community of their priorities and the quality of life issues
most affecting them. This data would help to inform how the Council amends the
proposed budget and reallocates funding towards these priorities.

For example, if the community needs more rental assistance for low-income tenants,
then the City could reallocate how it spends its HUD Home Grant dollars to reallocate
funding temporarily towards Tenant Based Rental Assistance (TBRA). If the community
wants to see more traffic enforcement officers as traffic picks up during the pandemic
recovery with companies requiring return to in-person work, then the rehiring of frozen
vacant positions could be prioritized towards traffic enforcement officers versus other
units to ensure safe routes to school and 0 pedestrian and bicyclist fatalities and
collisions.

As a former Councilmember, | have experienced acting on this priority first-hand by
voting to uphold the State and County’s temporary eviction moratoriums, sponsoring the
Sunnyvale Cares program which provided grant funding to local small businesses
struggling to keep their doors open, and more.

Personal Statement Supplemental
1. Why are you interested in serving on the San Mateo City Council?
San Mateo is a community with diverse groups of people, fantastic public spaces and
unmatched food and culture. However, for too long we have lived in a city with
insufficient infrastructure in transportation, housing and climate change resiliency.
Investing in the city’s infrastructure presents one of our greatest levers to improve our
most vulnerable citizens’ lives and to unlock opportunities for all our families. For
instance, our public transport system can be greener and more accessible. We can
double-down on our housing initiatives to make housing more affordable, all while
preserving elements of the City that people hold most dear. We should forge ahead to
complete the construction and expansion of the Wastewater Treatment Plant. I want to
serve on the Council because I have experience across the non-profit, business and civic
sectors, positioning me to make an immediate contribution on the San Mateo City
Council.

I’m a parent and homeowner in San Mateo. I work full-time, as does my spouse. Neither

situation isn’t representative of every family, our perspective is an important one to be
represented on the Council. If appointed to the vacancy on the Council, I can commit to
excellent preparation for all of the Council’s business, and spend time getting to know
ALL councilmembers well. In addition, I could devote 2 hours per week, 8 hours per
month to serve on external committees. I can commit to holding 2x/month office hours
to hear from the public and other stakeholders.

I appreciate how important it is to invest in a good working relationship with fellow
council members and the city manager to support the City’s organizational performance
and reputation. My experience working in a group is extensive. My approach to
decision-making is as follows:
1. What Is Going On? Understand the historical context and the status of an
initiative or proposal. Seek to understand the various perspectives in support or
in opposition to the initiative. If possible, speak to the stakeholders on multiple
sides and to members of the public. Having gathered information from a broad
set of stakeholders, form an initial point of view with an articulation of pros (+)
and of cons (-). Who does this initiative benefit and hurt? What might be some of
the unintended consequences of the initiative?
2. Articulate the Options. On paper, flesh out the various options, including
doing nothing. Discuss the options with Council members and together highlight
any associated trade offs (the things you won’t do if you support this initiative).
3. Be an Honest Broker. If there’s no consensus on which option is best, consult
stakeholders and the public again. Incorporate the feedback and come back to
discuss this as a Council. If there’s disagreement, reframe the initiative to help
surface shared interests. Build off shared value and help any Council members
shy away from digging in their heels. Acknowledge there are no perfect solutions
but build a sense of shared pride around the decision, even if the decision doesn’t
go my way. Communicate the outcome to the public.

When | moved to San Mateo (from San Francisco) 24 years ago | soon got involved with my

5. Describe your experience working in a group, receiving information, and arriving at consensus in
decision making.

There are many, many such experiences in my years as a commissioner.

One notable example surrounds an issue that might not have seemed so significant in political circles at
the time but was surprisingly contentious. For many years dog owners ran their pets off-leash in a
number of City parks (in contravention of the established rules) mostly on the playing fields. At one
point, a dog got away and attacked a pet that was being walked nearby. Residents on all sides, pet
owners and non-pet owning park users, particularly athletic groups who were unhappy with droppings
on the fields, complained to Council. Council quickly passed the matter down to the Parks Department.
| was chair of the commission at that time, and in coordination with staff we set hearings on the issue.

The crowds that came out were huge — larger than any we had known before on any Parks and
Recreation issues -- and very vocal and emphatic on all sides. We gave all involved, the dog supporters,
athletic groups, and other park users, multiple opportunities to voice their concerns and, correctly, be
and feel ‘heard.’ Then, in coordination with staff (and after a little municipal code research) we
determined that the Parks and Recreation director actually had authority, without need to go up to
Council, to put in place a pilot program to address the situation. With outreach to all stakeholders, we
managed to establish a pilot program that included time and location limits on off-leash park use, some
modest infrastructure upgrades to increase safety and access to resources (like doggy bags),
organization of citizen groups to self-monitor behavior, and increased City clean-up opportunities. The
result was a true win-win.

6. What do you see as the City’s top 3 challenges and how would you tackle them?

The City’s top three challenges in my view (and experience) are housing, circulation (traffic and
infrastructure), and services and support for lower income residents.

The housing shortfall requires support for truly smart development and respect for the needs of all in
our community. We have to recognize the reality that we are a largely built-out City and with few
opportunities and resources for City sponsored or funded development. That means developing in in-fill
and upgrade opportunities and recognizing the need for developers to build, within our mandates and
guidelines, but projects that make economic sense. Continuing, and | believe more aggressively,
encouraging mixed use development and higher density development along transit is the course we
should follow. Transit should be broadly viewed to include major roads as well as public rail transit to
maximize reasonable opportunities. Mixed use, higher density residential and commercial together
where possible, more efficiently utilizes space and affords residents with living spaces and available
nearby services. The updated general plan, while still in process, should provide a reasonable roadmap
to help the City in this endeavor.

Circulation — traffic and infrastructure — issues also need to be addressed. These matters are an
important ‘ends’ in and of themselves. Smooth streets, bicycle lanes, better pedestrian access, and
safety upgrades are all required by our residents and visitors as they move about the City for work and
access services, shopping, and entertainment. But these matters have increased importance as we work
to meet our housing goals as new residences mean more people and more strain on our infrastructure.
We owe it to our existing residents and those we hope to accommodate with increased housing to
prioritize traffic and infrastructure, both with focus and resources to keep this a livable and enjoyable
City for all.

For my part, going through (and chairing) the general plan process has shown the many opportunities to
better meet housing needs while maintaining and improving much of the character of our near suburb
community.

The childcare facility shortage in our area is an issue that draws less attention than some, but I believe it
needs to be focused upon in a greater and a more urgent way. One of the impediments to our City’s
ability to support our hugely important workers and households, particularly at the lower income range,
is the lack of accessible and affordable childcare that would allow folks to enter and stay in the
workforce, better afford to stay and live in our City, and more quickly move up the income ladder as
well.

The recently developed childcare fund to support private day care is a step in the right direction. |
believe greater, and more urgent efforts and resources should be directed this way. In addition, |
believe there is a place for City funded day care as well, particularly in some of the lesser served parts of
our community. We need to look at how a number of the City’s facilities could be upgraded (necessary
to obtain licenses to provide full day and week care) and developed as possible to meet this need.

Subject: The appointment of a fifth councilmember 
  
  
 
Dear San Mateo City Councilmembers and Staff, 
  
I am writing today regarding the selection of a person to fill the currently vacant fifth 
council seat. 
  
Under normal times, I would write extolling the virtues of this or that person and 
perhaps pointing at important policy work that needs to be addressed. However, we are 
sadly starting from a much lower bar at this stage of the process. 
  
So, in selecting a fifth person, I would emphasize several overriding principles: 
  
1.       Firstly, a fifth councilmember must be solemnly committed to their oath of 
office, to abiding by the letter and the spirit of all officially governing covenants, 
and to steadfastly and energetically doing their job of fulfilling the people’s 
business in a timely manner. 
2.      Secondly, a fifth councilmember must be committed to the transparent 
exercise of public reason. Such a person must resist accepting obstruction as an 
end-state, and they can never throw up their hands and merely agree to disagree. 
Moreover, they must constantly continue to publicly deliberate, clearly and 
logically laying out their reasons and subjecting them to the circumspection of 
their peers. 
3.      Finally, a fifth councilmember must uphold fairness and equity as guiding 
lights. They must not espouse or ensconce double standards. The rights and 
responsibilities of all must be treated equitably. 
  
With these basic qualifications met to the fullest, we can look forward to effectively 
advancing the city’s work. In particular, the update of the city’s Housing Element in 
compliance with state law will be a testing ground for all of the aforementioned qualities 
of a fifth councilperson. 
  
In short, it is imperative that the scorched-earth power plays of one half of the council 
be held in check. Only in this way will all reasonable and responsible parties be 
permitted to carry forth the urgent work of the city unobstructed, in good faith, and in a 
way which respects the time of the hardworking people of San Mateo. 
  
I thank you for your diligent consideration of this matter.
