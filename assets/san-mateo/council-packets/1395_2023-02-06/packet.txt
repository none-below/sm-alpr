and amend the 5-year funding plan.
CEQA: This resolution is in accordance with California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) Guidelines Section
15378(b)(4), submitting an application for grant funding is not a project subject to CEQA to the extent that it is a
funding activity that does not involve commitment to a particular project that will impact the environment.
However, in accordance with Public Resources Code Section 21065, to the extent that the funds are committed to
specific projects, the City’s commitment of those funds is not a project subject to CEQA because the funds will be
used to subsidize operational expenses which will not impact the environment.

General Plan Amendment to Adopt the City of San Mateo's Housing Element for 2023-2031
Eloiza Murillo Garcia, Housing Manager, and Zach Dahl, Community Development Deputy Director, and Housing 
Consultant Diane Elrod provided a presentation on an overview of the Housing Element process and actions taken 
to address HCD comments from the first submittal that was provided. They reviewed changes to the plan and 
responses to the comments.

Council asked additional questions of staff.

modify it to provide housing. In Person (12): Ken Abreu, Sierra Club; Daniel Shefen; Edward Evans; Michael Ragan; 
Tom Lease; Karyl Eldridge; Ted McKinnon; Mitch Speigle; Jeff LaMont; Dennis Keane; Fernando Pena, SAMCAR; and

Souza; Connie W.; George; Rick Sakuda; Robin Gage; Jordan Grimes; Jacqueline; Angelina Soldatos; Laurie; Rhovy 
Lyn Antonio; Paul Beaudreau; Peter Mandle; George; and Adam Alberti.
Council asked additional questions of staff. Discussion ensued including concern with the Builder’s Remedy; 
Planning Commission actions; continue to work with HCD; share and bring HCD requested modifications to Council 
as soon as possible; concern with fines and deadlines; and the importance of supporting this draft to keep moving 
forward.  Overall consensus expressed was to approve the staff recommendation with the amendment 
Motion to Adopt a Resolution, as amended to include the supplemental sites, to approve a General Plan 
Amendment to update the Housing Element for the Sixth Housing Cycle (2023-2031) that is compliant with State 
law, provides a plan for at least 7,015 new housing units and affirmatively furthers fair housing, and determine 
that adoption of the Housing Element is exempt from review under the California Environmental Quality Act, based 
on the listed findings included in Attachment 3. Motion passed 5-0.

reconvened at 7:25 pm.

applicant agreed to that modification. Manira Sandhir, Planning Manager; Christina Horrisberger, Community 
Development Director, and Sue-Ellen Atkinson, Principal Transportation Planner, answered additional Council  
questions.

Architectural Review (SPAR) and Site Development Planning Application (SDPA) for construction of a new five-
story mixed-use building consisting of 10 below market rate (BMR) residential units, 17,658 square feet of retail

Housing Plan; and to adopt an Addendum to the 2010 General Plan Environmental Impact Report (EIR) and 2009 
Downtown Area Specific Plan Initial Study/Mitigated Negative Declaration (IS/MND) under the California 
Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), based on the Findings for Approval and as conditioned. Motion passed 4-0.

SUBJECT:
Permanent Local Housing Allocation – Grant Application
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to re-apply to the State of California for Permanent Local Housing Allocation grant program and amend 
the 5-year funding plan.
BACKGROUND:
The purpose of the Permanent Local Housing Allocation (PLHA) grant is to provide a permanent source of funding to all 
local governments in California to help cities and counties implement plans to increase housing affordability. This is a 
flexible funding source that can be used for a wide range of activities from increasing the housing supply to providing 
supportive services. The PLHA application requires the City to develop a 5-year plan to allocate spending. The City has 
received a total allocation of $873,303 in the first two years of funding, $341,894 in the first year (2019) and $531,409 in 
the second year (2020). The third-year allocation amount is $584,800 (2021).
Council directed staff to use PLHA funds for rapid rehousing during a special meeting study session on June 1, 2020. Rapid 
rehousing includes rent assistance for a period of approximately 6 to 12 months combined with supportive services to 
households who are homeless or at risk of homelessness. A rapid rehousing program was developed and the non-profit 
organization LifeMoves was selected to administer the program.
PLHA funds have also been used to address a second need. The new affordable housing development, Montara, includes 
16 units for formerly homeless households. These targeted units include supportive services from the Veterans Affairs and 
the County in addition to rental assistance as part of the financing package provided by the County of San Mateo. In 
response to concerns raised at stakeholder meetings with neighboring properties, the City has worked with the developer, 
BRIDGE Housing, to expand its residence services at the property to make more services available as well as expanding 
onsite hours for all of the tenants. To achieve the expanded services, a full-time services coordinator rather than a half 
time position was created. This cost was not anticipated when the developer initially sought its funding commitments and 
resulted in a gap in the operating budget for the first four years of the project.
For the first year of PLHA activities, the grant amount of $341,894 was used to provide $280,353 in rapid rehousing 
assistance and $61,541 in tenant services at the Montara. However, for the second year, the City of San Mateo was 
awarded $531,409 and will be awarded $584,800 in the third year through the PLHA grant. With the increased amount of 
funding, staff has identified three additional housing affordability needs that can be served.
HIP Housing was a long-time recipient of the Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) funds through the City and has 
been a City partner in both providing affordable housing units and maintaining their home-sharing program, which 
matches tenants who are unable to afford the high rents in the City with home-owners who are willing to rent out spare

rooms in their homes. The strict guidelines of the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) forced HIP 
Housing to forgo participating in the City’s Community Funding program and left a gap in funding. In order to preserve the 
147 low-income units on their affordable properties and return assistance to the home sharing program, staff 
recommended accepting the request of funding HIP Housing $105,000 through the PLHA program, which the Council 
approved on September 6, 2022.
Samaritan House operates the Safe Harbor emergency shelter, a facility which provides case management, site-based 
emergency and transitional housing support to the clients in their 105-bed facility. The City has provided consistent 
support to this program through local housing funds in the past in order to ensure they would not have to compete with 
other organizations in the limited CDBG Community Funding program. As a service which assists persons experiencing 
homelessness, funding this program in the amount of $15,000 would be compatible with the design of the PLHA program 
and would preserve flexible local housing funds that can be reserved for other long-term projects.
Similarly, LifeMoves operates a network of shelters across San Mateo County with each servicing clients of various needs. 
At the First Step for Families shelter, housing, intensive case management and supportive services are provided to 
approximately 85 City of San Mateo residents each year. Staff recommends that the remainder of the PLHA budget in Year 
2 be utilized to assist shelter operations for LifeMoves. As Year 3 is an even larger grant, there remains the possibility of 
entering into PLHA contracts with current CDBG Community Funding recipients who provide PLHA eligible services, such as 
Legal Aid of San Mateo County (eviction defense).
To utilize these funds for the aforementioned causes, staff recommends that the Council approve amending the 5-Year 
PLHA plan to allow for the new expenditures in Year 2 of the grant funding, as well as adopting a resolution to re-apply for 
PLHA funding for Year 3 with the new contracts taken into account.
The five-year plan that is submitted to the State can be amended in the future if changes are desired.  
On January 4, 2023, the California Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD) notified the City that they 
would not accept the re-application and plan amendment for the PLHA program due to the City submitting a resolution 
that did not match the State’s mandated template.  Staff will resubmit the PLHA application and plan amendment using a 
revised resolution that matches the State’s template (Attachment 1).

Staff has set up a fund (referred to as Fund 36) for this grant. PLHA is a noncompetitive grant that will be allocated to the 
City over a course of 5 years. The grant amount for the second year (2020) is $531,409, while the grant amount for the 
third year (2021) is $584,800.  These funds have not yet been received by the City, as they need to be requested through a 
release of funds after the approval of the 5-Year Plan amendment. Overall, the City can expect to receive $1,116,209 in 
new revenue in Fund 36 over the next two years. The exact dollar amount for the final two years will vary based upon the 
collection of the real estate recording fees, but the total estimated dollar amount the City will receive over 5 years is

SUBJECT:
Enterprise Resource Planning Software Licensing, Maintenance and Support Services – Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve Amendment No. 1 to the agreement with Tyler Technologies, Inc. for Eden financial, human resources, and 
payroll annual maintenance and support in the amount $112,636.15 for the period covering January 1, 2023 through 
December 31, 2023, and authorize the Information Technology Director to execute the amendment in substantially the 
form presented.
BACKGROUND:
The City annually renews maintenance and support for the Eden enterprise resource planning (ERP) software. Renewal 
ensures the City uses a fully licensed financial and human resources information system and payroll processing system, 
along with vendor-supplied technical and end-user support. These maintenance and support services are vital to the City’s 
ongoing operations.  Contract renewal requires Council attention due to annual rate increases. 
The current agreement, approved by City Council on March 21, 2022, automatically renewed on January 1, 2023, in 
accordance with the terms in the executed agreement. However, the annual cost and any cost increases of Tyler 
Technologies, Inc.’s (Tyler) support and maintenance services requires City Council approval.  For this year, the cost for 
support and maintenance increased by $3,280.69 since the original agreement’s execution, and an amendment is required 
to cover the fully renewed term and ensure maintenance of the software through December 31, 2023.  The total amount 
of the agreement for the period January 1, 2023 through December 31, 2023 is $112,636.15.  Because the City continues 
to require Tyler’s services, staff recommends approval of Amendment No. 1.
Staff is currently working on implementing Workday to replace the Eden software, and expects 2023 to be the last year of 
paying for Eden software and maintenance.
BUDGET IMPACT:
Annual maintenance and support for Eden is included in the annual operating budget for the Information Technology 
Department.

SUBJECT:
Minimum Wage Enforcement Services – Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with City of San Jose's Office of Equality Assurance for Minimum Wage Ordinance Enforcement 
services in the amount of $30,000 and authorize the City Manager to execute the agreement in substantially the form 
presented.
BACKGROUND:
In August 2016, the City Council adopted an ordinance to incrementally increase its local minimum wage from $10.00 per 
hour to $15.00 per hour by 2019. From 2019 to 2022, the local minimum wage has increased based on the regional 
Consumer Price Index (CPI). In the fall of 2022, City Council amended the minimum wage ordinance to cap the CPI 
increases to 3.5% per year, include a catch-up provision, and round the wage to the nearest nickel. As of January 1, 2023, 
the minimum wage for all employees who perform at least two hours of work per week in San Mateo is $16.75 per hour.
To administer the program, the City contracts with the City of San Jose’s Office of Equality Assurance (OEA) for all 
enforcement efforts on behalf of the City. The OEA provides this service to most cities in San Mateo and Santa Clara 
Counties who have a local minimum wage. Much of their work includes answering questions regarding the ordinance, 
conducting complaint intake, investigating employers, calculating back wages owed, calculating the new minimum wage, 
and preparing and sending written notices to violators. 
From 2017 to 2020, the City’s contract with OEA was a pay per service fee with a cap of $30,000 per year, meaning the City 
was charged based on the hours spent on enforcement each year. However, in 2020, OEA changed their fees from pay per 
service to a flat fee of $15,000 per year. The change was made to accurately compensate the volume of calls and cases 
they receive for each city with which they contract. In the past, OEA had difficulty tracking and billing for the phone 
inquiries that they took in for the various cities and therefore typically did not bill for the majority of their work and was 
therefore operating at a significant loss to the City of San Jose. The change to a flat fee covers all costs for enforcement. 
Since the adoption of the policy and contracting with OEA, OEA has conducted investigations into four complaints received 
from 2017 to 2022. Two of the cases closed because the business closed, sold, or transferred to new owners. The third 
case, which took place in 2018, resulted in the restitution of $1,485.88 in wages to three employees. The fourth case, 
which took place in 2022, resulted in the restitution of $673.44 in wages to six employees. 
The City’s current agreement with San Jose is set to end June 30, 2023, but the City has the option to extend the term to 
June 30, 2025. Staff does not have the capacity to administer and enforce the minimum wage in-house and therefore 
recommends continuing to work with the City of San Jose. Although the agreement amount threshold is within the City 
Manager’s approval authority, City Council approval is required because this is an interagency agreement.  Following

SUBJECT:
Property Tax In-Lieu of Vehicle License Fee Shortfall – Letter of Support
RECOMMENDATION:
Authorize the Mayor to sign a letter of support on behalf of the City Council to State officials requesting their assistance in 
getting the Property Tax in-lieu of Vehicle License Fee shortfall backfill included in the Governor’s May Revise budget.
BACKGROUND:
The Vehicle License Fee (VLF) "Swap" was an integral part of the 2004 budget compromise (SB 1096). Under the terms of 
that compromise, the State permanently reduced VLF revenues to cities and counties by 67.5% and also shifted $1.3 billion 
in local property taxes to pay the State's school funding obligation for two fiscal years. Through these actions, the counties 
and cities gave up significant revenues to address the State's budget deficit. In exchange, the State guaranteed the 
counties and cities an in-lieu VLF payment that is adjusted annually by the growth in property tax. The VLF Swap legislation 
identified two sources of funding to pay the State's in-lieu VLF obligation: (1) ERAF distributions to non-basic aid schools 
and (2) property tax revenues of non-basic aid schools. The State would then backfill those losses by the schools to ensure 
minimum funding obligations were met. In recent years as more school districts within the county have moved into basic 
aid status, there has not been funding available to fulfill the State's in-lieu VLF obligation, which has resulted in a shortfall. 
For 2021-22, the countywide shortfall was approximately $32 million, which resulted in a nearly $2 million shortfall for the 
City. When these shortfalls have occurred in the past, the State has made the County and its cities whole by reimbursing 
the shortfalls 14 months after the fiscal year in which the shortfall occurred through special appropriations in the State 
budget. 
The backfill for the 2021-22 VLF shortfall was not included in the initial release of the 2023-24 State budget.  As such, the 
County and its cities are initiating efforts to advocate for this funding to be included in the Governor’s May Revise budget.  
One of the initial steps in this process is to send letters to State officials requesting their assistance in getting the VLF 
shortfall backfill included in the May Revise.  The draft letter to be signed by Mayor Lee and sent on behalf of the City is 
included as Attachment 1.  Staff is also requesting authorization from Council to send any additional letters from the 
Mayor on behalf of the City to the State delegation or other stakeholders as requested by the County on this issue.
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no direct budget impact for authorizing the Mayor to sign this letter of support. However, should the State not 
include backfill funding to address the VLF shortfall, the City’s fiscal year 2023-24 budgeted revenues will be reduced by 
nearly $2 million.

RE: Budget Request for San Mateo County and its Cities for FY 21-22 VLF In-Lieu Shortfall

Dear Senator Becker, Senator Wiener, Assemblymember Berman, Assemblymember Papan, and

Assemblymember Ting:

On behalf of the City of San Mateo, we respectfully request your assistance securing $32,898,051 in in-
lieu Vehicle License Fee (VLF) shortfall funding due to the County of San Mateo and its cities for FY 21-
22 through the state budget process. Of the total, the City of San Mateo is due $1.9 million.

Historically, the State has made counties and cities whole by reimbursing in-lieu VLF shortfalls through
a special appropriation in the State budget. This appropriation ensures that all counties and cities
receive their full in-lieu VLF payment required by law. Unfortunately, an appropriation to cover San

94 of 201


--- page 95 ---
Mateo County’s shortfall for the fiscal year 2021-22 was not included in the Governor’s January Budget
this year.

As background, the State’s in-lieu VLF payment obligation arises from the VLF “Swap,” which was an
integral part of the 2004 Budget compromise. Under the terms of that compromise, the State
permanently reduced the annual VLF rate, which significantly decreased counties’ and cities’ revenues
and, for two fiscal years, shifted an additional $1.3 billion in property taxes away from counties, cities,
and special districts to pay the State’s school funding obligations and address the State’s budget deficit.
In exchange, the State guaranteed counties and cities an “in-lieu” VLF payment for the lost revenues.
This ongoing in-lieu VLF obligation is adjusted annually based on growth in the assessed value of
property within local agency boundaries (Rev. & Tax. Code § 97.70).

In San Mateo County, the direct funding sources identified in the statute are insufficient to cover the
full amount of the in-lieu VLF payment, causing a funding shortfall. The shortfall specifically arises
because there are insufficient funds from ERAF and property taxes of the relatively few non-basic aid
schools in San Mateo County to cover the State’s total payment obligation. (The State backfills the
school districts, so they do not suffer any loss from the in-lieu VLF payment.) As a result, these
shortfalls are beyond the counties’ and cities’ control and cause cash flow issues and significant fiscal
harm until reimbursed by the State.

Failure to make the full in-lieu VLF payment will cause significant financial harm to San Mateo County
and cities, which rely on these revenues to fund core services such as police, fire, libraries, parks,
streets, traffic and transportation. It would also be contrary to the 2004 Budget Compromise in which
these payments were guaranteed in exchange for substantial financial contributions by local
governments. Failure to provide the total payment would devastate local budgets and services that
depend on these funds.

For these reasons, the City of San Mateo requests your assistance in ensuring that the $32,898,051 in
FY 21-22 VLF in-lieu shortfall due to the County of San Mateo and its 20 cities is included in this year’s
budget to ensure there is no adverse fiscal impact on our local governments.

SUBJECT:
Parks and Recreation Bulk Printing and Mailing – Purchase Order Authorization
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to approve an alternative bidding process, approve a purchase order with Folger Graphics for bulk 
printing and mailing of the recreation activity guides and summer booklet in the amount of $150,000, with the 
understanding that approximately $60,000 in expenditures have already been incurred but not paid, and authorize the 
Director of Parks and Recreation to execute the purchase order in substantially the form presented.
BACKGROUND:
The Recreation Division of the Parks and Recreation Department creates, prints and mails four activity guides and one 
summer booklet per fiscal year. The department has historically worked with Folger Graphics for the printing and mailing 
of the activity guides and booklet. Past department practice has been to obtain informal bids through open market 
purchasing procedures to select a supplier. Invoices are received after the work is complete and paid for with a purchase 
order. In February 2022, staff requested quotes from four local suppliers and received a response from three.  All vendors 
notified staff that the cost of paper was increasing due to inflation and supply chain issues. At the time the quotes were 
received, the cost of mailing was not included. 
The City’s purchasing ordinance requires that purchases of $100,000 or more be competitively bid. However, at the time of 
the original quotes the cost fell below $100,000. Due to the rising cost of paper, supply chain issues and the inclusion of 
mailing services, this purchase will surpass the $100,000 limit. Municipal Code Section 3.60.050(b) provides that 
competitive bidding is not required when it would be unavailing or impracticable to obtain bids. In this instance, it would 
be unavailing and impracticable to obtain bids, because over 60% of the work has been completed. 
Municipal Code Section 3.60.070(h)(3) provides that the City Council can authorize alternative purchasing procedures. 
Staff is requesting that the City Council authorize this purchase order, absent competitive bidding, on the grounds that 
over 60% of the work has been completed and that staff is confident executing a purchase order with Folger Graphics at 
this time is the best value for the City. For next fiscal year, the department will implement the formal bidding process for 
our printing needs.
The purchase order is attached (Attachment 2) and a purchase order number will be generated once authority has been 
granted.
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact associated with this purchase order authorization as the cost of printing and mailing the activity 
guides and booklet is included in the operating budget.

RECOMMENDATION:
Deny the appeal and uphold the notice to the Property Owner requiring reimbursement to the City of San Mateo for 
permanent relocation benefits provided to the tenants residing at 1654 S. Grant St. Unit #2 due to unlawful substandard 
living conditions pursuant to San Mateo Municipal Code Chapter 7.50 “Property Owner Obligations with Respect to 
Tenants Displaced from Unsafe or Substandard Units;” and direct staff to draft a Statement of Decision reflecting the City 
Council’s determination.  
BACKGROUND:
History 
County records show that current property owner, Lydia Candila (Ms. Candila), purchased the subject four-plex apartment 
building located at 1654 S. Grant St. in April of 2004. Since that time, there have been a total of seven code enforcement 
cases, with three active cases on units #2, #4, and #5. The open case for unit #5 is due to its unpermitted status. The unit is 
unoccupied, and Ms. Candila is working with the City to legalize the unit. The open case on unit #4 was initiated for 
substandard conditions, including, mold, lack of heat, and a non-functioning toilet. The substandard conditions in unit #4 
were addressed and the City is working with Ms. Candila to address other non-substandard violations.  The open case for 
unit #2, which is the subject of this appeal, is the second code enforcement case related to this unit for substandard 
conditions. The first was in 2019 for visible mold and lack of heat and was closed without relocating the tenants.   
Case summary
On August 31, 2022, Code Enforcement received a complaint of substandard conditions in unit #2, which is a 2-bedroom 
unit with seven occupants (four adults and three children). The complaint consisted of cockroaches, rats, mold, and 
electrical issues. On the same day, Code Enforcement Officer Kristen Aiu (CEO Aiu) conducted an inspection with one of 
the tenants. During the inspection, the following substandard conditions were visually observed: 
•
Mold in one of the bedrooms and bathroom 
•
Cockroaches and evidence of rodents in the kitchen 
•
Inoperable smoke detectors 
•
Lack of hot water in the kitchen sink

•
August 31, 2022:  Warning Letter to Abate a Public Nuisance (Att. 1); compliance date of September 6, 2022
•
September 9, 2022: Final Notice to Abate (Att. 2); compliance date of September 15, 2022
•

2.

Appeal: 
The property owner has appealed payment for the cost incurred for the permanent relocation of the tenant household. 
Ms. Candila states that all items in the Notice to Vacate were addressed; however, on November 1, 2022, City staff

Analysis: 
SMMC section 7.50.030(a) defines when tenants are eligible for relocation payments.  It states, in pertinent part:
(a) A tenant household shall be eligible for relocation payments from a property owner under this chapter if the 
tenant household is displaced from its dwelling or room due to the City's code enforcement activities. For 
purposes of this chapter, a tenant household shall be deemed to be displaced from its dwelling or room due to

Penicillium ’Aspergillus types: Penicillium and Aspergillus are among the most common molds found growing both indoors and outdoors (even

SUBJECT:
2022-23 Mid-Year Budget Update and Measure CC Options
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive the 2022-23 Mid-Year Budget Update; adopt a resolution to approve supplemental budget appropriations in the 
amount of $2,488,852 of additional property tax to: 1) increase transfers out from the General Fund by $2,400,000 to the 
2% TOT Fund (Fund 56); and 2) to increase the Department of Information Technology operating budget by $88,852 for 
software subscription fees; and provide direction on Measure CC options.
BACKGROUND:
This agenda report provides a financial update on the City of San Mateo's major funds - the General Fund, the Construction 
Services Fund, and the Sewer Fund - based on revenues and expenditures through October 2022. The primary focus of the 
analysis is on the General Fund, as it is the City's primary operating fund, which accounts for nearly all tax revenues and 
funds the cost of core services. The agenda report will also provide an update on the impact of investment losses by the 
California Public Employees' Retirement System (CalPERS) and provide context for City Council to provide direction on 
Measure CC options. 
General Fund 
General Fund Revenues 
The adopted 2022-23 budget assumes $159.5 million in total revenues. Based on year-to-date results through October 
2022, total revenues are projected to be $163.1 million, which is a favorable budget variance of $3.6 million. The 
narratives below provide detailed analysis of the primary contributing factors of the overall favorable budget variance.
PROPERTY TAX - $80.3M ADOPTED | $83.4M PROJECTED 
The primary variance is largely a result of additional property tax in lieu of vehicle license fees (VLF). The adopted 2022-23 
budget assumed the ongoing and incremental shift of non-basic aid school districts, which are unable to meet their state-
determined minimum funding requirement through property tax revenues, to basic aid status. As such, the county-wide 
VLF shortfall would continue to grow. In the prior fiscal year, the VLF county-wide shortfall was $32.9 million, of which the 
City’s share was $1.9 million, or 5.9% of the total shortfall. 
Consistent with the County of San Mateo (County) and State of California’s (State) prior practice, the County filed a claim 
for the 2021-22 VLF shortfall of $32.9 million in August 2022 with the State. On January 10, 2023, the State released its 
191 of 201

2023-24 proposed budget, which projects a $22.5 billion shortfall, and appropriations to make the County whole for the 
2021-22 VLF shortfall were not included. While this has no impact on the current fiscal year, if the County’s advocacy 
efforts are not successful, it will have an impact on the City’s 2023-24 property tax revenues. The City Manager is part of 
the task force that is addressing this issue, and there is a separate item on this agenda regarding next steps. 
Despite the above potential outcome for 2023-24, the County anticipates that the VLF shortfall for the current fiscal year 
will be less than expected, due to a near-term result of school districts being in non-basic aid status, whose property tax 
revenues serve as the funding source for the VLF owed to the cities and County. Combined with the $5.7 million of 2020-
21 VLF shortfall that the State appropriated for its 2022-23 budget, VLF is projected to have a favorable variance of $3.7 
million, with a year-end estimate of $19.8 million compared to the $16.1 million in the adopted budget. 
SALES TAX - $29.4M ADOPTED | $30.3M PROJECTED 
As illustrated in the chart below, sales tax revenues declined by 20% in response to the immediate and severe financial 
impact of COVID-19 and recovered back to the 2018-19 pre-pandemic baseline by the end of 2021-22. In the near-term, 
the primary drivers for the projected favorable budget variance are the elevated cost of gasoline and sustained high 
inflation. While the near-term results are favorable, staff will continue to monitor economic indicators for signs of a 
recession, and if so, the order of magnitude for severity and duration, and update the General Fund long-term forecast 
accordingly.
TRANSIENT OCCUPANCY TAX - $3.2M ADOPTED | $5.0M PROJECTED 
Transient occupancy tax (TOT) revenues declined by nearly 90% due to the impact of COVID-19 on tourism. In particular, 
the City’s TOT base is driven by business travel, which has been relatively slow to recover, albeit faster than what was 
initially anticipated in the General Fund long-term plan. The graph below illustrates the TOT recovery trend, starting with 
the 2018-19 baseline to the year-end estimate for 2022-23. TOT is the only General Fund tax revenue that has not 
recovered back to the pre-pandemic baseline. It is uncertain when that will occur, if at all. Staff will continue to monitor 
TOT performance metrics and adjust long-term assumptions as appropriate.

As mentioned above, San Mateo voters approved Measure CC in November 2022, which increased the RPTT rate to 1.5%

One option is to wait until a material amount of Measure CC funds are received and become available to appropriate.

General Fund Expenditures 
The amended 2022-23 budget assumes $165.4 million in General Fund total expenditures. Based on October 2022 year-to-
date transactions, total expenditures are projected to be $161.5 million, a favorable budget variance of $3.9 million or 
2.3%. The majority of the favorable budget variance stems from $2.7 million in vacancy savings, which is largely reflected 
in the Library and Parks & Recreation Departments.  Library Department savings stem from open merit and per diem staff 
positions that they are targeting to fill in the next months. Parks & Recreation savings are due to ongoing merit position 
vacancies that they are hoping to fill while conducting a broader department assessment. Per diem staff savings largely 
offset vacant staff positions and fluctuate based on seasonal requirements/expectations.
The 2022-23 operating budget for the Department of Information Technology (DoIT) includes appropriations for the cost 
of the City’s new enterprise resource planning (ERP) system, Workday, which is on a subscription basis. The Workday ERP 
and Teller cashiering system are shared with the San Mateo Consolidated Fire Department (SMC Fire), as the City of San 
Mateo provides contractual Finance and Human Resources services to SMC Fire. As such, 22.99% of the cost for Workday 
and Teller are allocated to SMC Fire. However, the subscription agreements for Workday and Teller are with the City of San 
Mateo, as it was not feasible to have separate contracts. DoIT’s adopted 2022-23 operating budget only includes the City’s 
proportional share of Workday and Teller subscription costs, but it should have included the full cost, as the City is the 
contracting authority. SMC Fire reimburses the City for its proportional share of Workday and Teller through its operating 
budget. The proposed resolution rectifies this issue by appropriating $88,852 of unassigned fund balance in the General 
Fund to increase DoIT’s operating budget.
Construction Services Fund 
The amended 2022-23 budget assumes $8.8 million in total revenues, $12.3 million in total expenditures, and $4.5 million 
in transfers out. Based on year-to-date trends, total revenues are projected to be $13.8 million, total expenditures are 
projected to be $10.9 million, and transfers out are expected to be consistent with the budget. Projected revenues reflect 
an elevated demand for development-related services, with high permit volumes for single-family and commercial/multi-
family projects, and a surge in applications at the end of 2022 ahead of the tri-annual California Building Code update 
going into effect. Projected expenditures reflect a continued need for consultant support for development services, in 
particular plan checking, as well as savings from operational efficiencies and some merit positions vacancies.  If the 
projections come to fruition, the Construction Services Fund would end the fiscal year with a drawdown of $1.5 million in 
fund balance.

Sewer Fund 
The amended 2022-23 budget assumes $85.5 million in total revenues, $42.6 million in total expenses, and $41.2 million in

2% TOT Fund
The 2% TOT Fund accounts for Measure C TOT revenues and expenditures. Measure C was approved by San Mateo voters 
in 1998 to increase its TOT rate by 2% to fund needed seismic upgrades and other safety improvements to the City’s safety 
facilities. Measure C’s ongoing commitments are primarily the debt service for the lease revenue bonds that were issued in 
2007 to finance the cost of capital for the Police Department headquarters. Most recently, it funded part of the cost for 
the new Fire Station 25. However, due to the immediate and severe impact of COVID-19 on local tourism, the Fund did not 
account for sufficient TOT revenues to pay for Measure C’s full share of Fire Station 25. As such, the $2,400,000 of the 
additional property tax revenues that are projected for 2022-23 are recommended to be appropriated to increase the 
transfers out budget in the General Fund to offset negative fund balance in the 2% TOT Fund (Fund 56). 
In addition, the lease revenue bonds that that were issued in 2007 to finance the cost of the Police Department 
headquarters were variable rate bonds, which gets reset on a weekly basis. Due to the escalation of the Federal Funds 
Rate, the adopted 2022-23 budget assumed that the interest rate would escalate to 1.75%. As shown in the chart below, 
the weekly rate reset has fluctuated, but has been dropping since December 2022:

While its 21.3% return on investment (ROI) in 2020-21 reduced the City’s unfunded pension liability by over $70 million, 
the negative 7.5% ROI for 2021-22 is expected to increase the unfunded pension liability by nearly $100 million. By 2028-

million in aggregate in additional pension contributions. As of January 19, 2023, CalPERS has reported an ROI of 4.7%, still 
well below its discount rate of 6.8%.
In response to the immediate and severe financial impact of COVID-19, the City suspended its pension funding policy, 
which aimed to use half of what was received in Excess Educational Revenue Augmentation Fund (Excess ERAF) revenues 
and contributed to CalPERS as an additional discretionary payment (ADP). Contributing ADPs is effectively paying down on 
the principal of the loans (amortization bases) and resulted in interest savings. For example, if the City were to contribute 
an ADP of $5.0 million to CalPERS, the net savings in pension contributions over time would be $20.0 million. In response 
to the impact of CalPERS’ 2021-22 ROI on the City’s pension contributions, staff recommends that the City’s pension 
contribution policy be reviewed and discussed at a study session this later this year.
