BACKGROUND:
In 2004, the City Council added Chapter 2.80 to the San Mateo Municipal Code entitled “Contributions and Loans to City 
Candidate Campaigns.” This chapter (Attachment 1) established limits on the amount of the contributions that may be 
made to City Council candidate campaigns to $250 by an individual and $500 by an organization, as well as limiting the 
amount a candidate can loan to their own campaign to $15,000. In 2017, the City Council amended the limits by increasing 
the amount an individual can give to $500 and an organization to $1,000. In 2012, section 2.80.070 was amended to read: 
“Penalties. Any person who violates the provisions of this Chapter is guilty of a public offense.” 
Section 2.80.080 requires that the City Council conduct a post-election review of the requirements of this chapter no later 
than three (3) months following the certification of the results of the election for the purpose of allowing the City Council 
to consider the ongoing appropriateness of the provisions of this chapter, including, but not limited to the amount of the 
contribution loan limits established in Sections 2.80.030 and 2.80.040. The City Council certified the results of the

Campaign Dashboard
On March 1st, 2021, City Council reviewed the provisions of Municipal Code 2.80 “Contributions and Loans to City 
Candidate Campaigns”. During this study session, with the broader intention to increase public transparency into 
candidate campaign finances, direction was given to staff to develop a candidate finance dashboard. The campaign 
dashboard can be found on the City’s website at:
https://www.cityofsanmateo.org/4664/Council-Campaign-Financing-Dashboard  
The City Clerk’s Office led an interdepartmental scoping exercise and created the Campaign Finance Dashboard with a 
focus on the November 8, 2022 General Election. To date, the City’s Campaign Finance Dashboard is the only campaign 
disclosure dashboard available within the County of San Mateo.  The City’s campaign dashboard provides voters with 
interactive visualizations of campaign finance activity. 
This includes, as illustrated in the chart below, an overview of financial activity, detailing the dollar amounts being received 
and being spent by candidates. Users are provided with user-friendly breakdowns of the type of transactions, including 
contributions, loans, debts, liabilities, and expenses.

Due to time-constraints, the initial launch of the campaign dashboard contained only the data provided by candidates

inquiries from members of the public to consider adding independent expenditures within the dashboard. During its most

While prior to the release of the City’s campaign dashboard, this information was made publicly available, the value added 
from the campaign dashboard is an all-inclusive summary of campaign finance activities. This saves the user from 
individually pouring over pages of complex filings and manually comparing filings against multiple candidates. Staff’s 
objective is to automate the campaign dashboard through an Application Programming Interface (API) in partnership with 
NetFile. 
Senate Bill 1439 (SB 1439):
In September 2022, the State legislature adopted Senate Bill 1439 (SB 1439), which the Governor signed, in an effort 
strengthen regulations concerning campaign contributions. Effective January 1, 2023, SB 1439 expanded campaign 
contribution-related restrictions on local officials, particularly with respect to local elected officials such as members of city 
councils and boards of supervisors. State law now provides that campaign contributions of more than $250 would be a 
conflict of interest for local officials and would require recusal in certain situations, such as where the donor was an 
applicant for a license or permit. Furthermore, the previous three-month restriction on campaign contributions following a 
decision was extended to a twelve-month restriction, for both appointed and elected officials. Finally, if local official does 
not willfully and knowingly accept, solicit, or direct a prohibited contribution, the violation can be resolved by returning 
the contribution. 
Options for Council Consideration:
1. 
Repeal the Existing City Campaign Contributions Limits  
Assembly Bill 571, effective January 1, 2021, applied a default campaign contribution limit to city and county candidates 
when the city or county has not already enacted a contribution limit. The 2023-24 contribution limits for city and county 
candidates in cities and counties that have not enacted limits is $5,500 for individuals, business entities, and committees.
Recognizing that State law limits campaign contributions in municipal elections, the Council could choose to repeal the 
current code provisions in their entirety. One advantage of this approach is that there would be one consistent system of 
campaign finance rules applicable to State and local elections. The one set of definitions of the key terms of the law 
applicable throughout the state, which might help avoid confusion and make it easier for campaign candidates and 
treasurers to comply. The other advantage with this option is that enforcement would be performed by the FPPC freeing 
up city attorney resources.  
On the other hand, the limits are significantly higher under the State law and concerns might be raised about the 
perception that wealthier donors might garner greater influence through large donations to council candidates.
2.
Adjust the Amount of the Contribution Limits
The current code limits contributions by individuals to $500 and contributions made by organizations to $1,000. These 
limits were last adjusted in 2017.  The Council may choose to leave the limits as they are or direct that the limits be 
adjusted. The City of Berkeley’s limitation on contributions includes an adjustment on dollar amount for cost-of-living 
changes in January of every odd-numbered year (City of Berkeley Municipal Code 2.12.415).

3.
Research Alternative Campaign Finance Models Adopted by Other Agencies
Towards the goal of protecting public confidence in the political process, Council may direct staff to research recent 
initiatives taken in other jurisdictions within the area of campaign contributions and offer Council the opportunity to 
consider their application to San Mateo. Such initiatives may include, but may not be limited to, Public Financing for 
Berkeley Candidates, whereby candidates may receive a 6-to-1 match on donations to campaigns, up to $60. Voters in the 
City of Oakland similarly voted to introduce Public Financing of City and School Board Officials.

2.80.010 FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) The City Council hereby finds that preserving integrity and openness in the political process
is a matter of the highest public interest; that the people of the City of San Mateo can be better
served through a more informed electorate; that the trust of the people is essential to
representative government; and that regulation of certain campaign contributions and loans is
necessary to promote public confidence in government and to protect the integrity of the electoral
process.
(b) The City Council further finds that regulation of campaign contributions is required because
the costs of running political campaigns have reached levels that lead to a public perception that
special interests and wealthy individuals may have undue influence on or access to elected
officials.
(c) It is the policy of the City to promote and encourage broad-based citizen involvement in the
financing of election campaigns.
(d) The City Council further finds that allowing candidates to lend unlimited amounts of their
personal funds to their campaigns tends to undermine public confidence in the election process,
because the public may perceive that candidates with large outstanding loans will seek
contributors to repay their loan after the election, and may be predisposed to favor their post-
election contributors.

2.80.020 Definitions.
As used in this chapter, the words and phrases shall be defined in the same manner as they are
defined in the Political Reform Act of 1974 (including any amendments thereto) as found in
California Government Code Section 81000 et seq., unless defined otherwise in this section. For
the purposes of this chapter, the terms below are defined as follows:
(a) "Candidate" means any individual listed on the ballot for election to any City office, or who
otherwise has taken affirmative action to seek election to City office, or who receives a contribution
or makes an expenditure, or who gives his or her consent for any other person to receive a
contribution or make an expenditure with the purpose of bringing about his or her election to any
City office. "Candidate" also means an officeholder in connection with a recall election relating to
such officeholder.
(b) "Candidate committee" means a committee that is controlled directly or indirectly by a
candidate or that acts jointly with a candidate in connection with the making of expenditures.
(c) "Contribution" means a payment, a forgiveness of a loan, a payment of a loan by a third
party, or an enforceable promise to make a payment except to the extent full and adequate
consideration is received unless it is clear from the surrounding circumstances that it is not made
for political purposes. Except as provided herein, "contribution" includes (1) an expenditure made
at the behest of a candidate or candidate committee unless full and adequate consideration is
received for making the expenditure, and (2) any goods or services received by or behested by a
candidate or candidate committee at no charge or at a discount from the fair market value, unless
the discount is given in the regular course of business to members of the public.
The term "contribution" does not include a payment made by the occupant of a home or business
for costs related to any meeting or fund raising event held in the occupant's home or business; or
volunteer personal or professional services if the services are donated without any understanding
or agreement that they shall be directly or indirectly repaid to him or her.

2.80.030 LIMITATIONS ON CONTRIBUTIONS.
(a) For any particular election, including a recall election, no individual shall make, and no
candidate, candidate committee treasurer or other person acting on behalf of a candidate
committee shall solicit or accept from any individual any contribution which will cause the total
amount contributed by such individual to exceed $500.00.
(b) For any particular election, including a recall election, no organization shall make, and no
candidate, candidate committee treasurer or other person acting on behalf of a candidate
committee shall solicit or accept from any organization any contribution which will cause the total
amount contributed by such organization to exceed $1,000.00.
(c) Any contribution or portion thereof that exceeds the limits in this section shall be returned to
the contributor within 72 hours of receipt.
(d) The limitations imposed by this section shall not apply to contribution of a candidate's
personal funds to the candidate's own campaign, or to any loan which is personally guaranteed by
the candidate or is secured by property owned by the candidate.
(Ord. No. 2017-2 § 1; Ord. No. 2005-1 § 1.)

2.80.045 Aggregation of Contributions.
(a) To determine when contributions are aggregated under this chapter, "majority owned" means
ownership of more than 50 percent.
(b) If an individual directs or controls an organization's contributions, the organization's
contributions shall be aggregated with contributions made by both of the following:
(1) That individual.
(2) Any other organization whose contributions that individual directs or controls.
(c) If two or more organizations make contributions that are directed or controlled by a majority
of the same persons, the contributions of those entities shall be aggregated.
(d) Contributions made by organizations that are majority owned by a person shall be
aggregated with the contributions of the majority owner and all other organizations majority owned
by that person, unless those organizations act independently in their contribution-making
decisions.
(Ord. No. 2021-23 § 2.)

4. Recall Campaign Committees
•
Recall committees (in support or opposition) are considered 
ballot measure committees, and as such, are not subject to 
contribution limits, consistent with state law and caselaw. 
•
Campaign ordinance application of contribution limits to 
candidate committees (those running to replace an officeholder) 
could be clarified in light of Charter language prescribing an 
appointment process rather than an election.
