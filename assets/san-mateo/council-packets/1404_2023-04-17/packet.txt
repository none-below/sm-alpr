BACKGROUND:
Operating Budget 
The focus of the operating budget section of this agenda report is on the General Fund, which is the City of San Mateo's 
chief fund, as it accounts for all major tax revenues and pays for core services, including police, fire, library, parks and 
recreation, and city administration. 
2022-23 BUDGET UPDATE 
The 2022-23 mid-year budget update presented to the City Council on February 6, 2023 estimated General Fund total 
revenues of $163.8 million and total expenditures of $164.0 million. Based on February 2023 year-to-date trends, total 
revenues are expected to be $157.5 million, and total expenditures are projected to be $164.6 million. 
GENERAL FUND REVENUES
The $6.3 million reduction of total revenues compared to the previous mid-year budget estimate reflects expected 
revenue estimate changes for property tax in lieu of vehicle license fees and real property transfer tax.
Property Tax In-Lieu of Vehicle License Fees (VLF)
The previous VLF estimate of $19.8 million was greater than what was assumed in the adopted 2022-23 budget, which 
anticipated that the VLF shortfall would be greater than what was estimated at the time of the previous mid-year budget. 
However, based on a recent presentation by the San Mateo County Controller’s Office, the San Carlos School District and 
the Ravenswood School District are only $100,000 and $45,000 below the basic aid threshold, respectively. The lower VLF 
estimate of $16.4 million presumes that both school districts will shift to basic aid status, which will increase the county-
wide VLF shortfall to $70 million, of which the City’s projected share would be $4.1 million. The difference between the 
previous estimate and the revised estimate is a reduction of $3.4 million. Consistent with prior practice, the County of San 
Mateo, on behalf of itself and all the municipalities within the county, will file a claim with the State of California in August 
2023 for the $70 million VLF shortfall, which the State will consider as a part of its 2024-25 budget for appropriation. As a 
matter of reference, the State’s proposed 2023-24 budget that was released in January 2023 does not include any 
appropriations for the 2021-22 VLF county-wide shortfall of $32.9 million (of which the City’s share is $1.9 million). The 
City Council approved a letter that was signed by Mayor Lee which was addressed to state representatives to address this 
matter.

Real Property Transfer Tax
The remaining revenue reduction is directly related to the continued escalation of the federal funds rate, the direct nexus 
to the mortgage interest rate, and the resultant impact on the City's real property transfer tax (RPTT) revenues. Through 
the collection period of February 2023, the City has received $4.9 million in RPTT. During the months of November, 
December, January, and February, RPTT revenues were 43% of the average of what was received in fiscal years 2020-21 
and 2021-22 for the same months. Assuming the remainder of fiscal year (FY) 2022-23 will be consistent with this recent 
trend, the collection period of March through June will account for $1.4 million in RPTT, and result in a revised year-end 
estimate of $6.3 million, which is a reduction of $2.7 million from the previous year-end estimate of $9.0 million.
GENERAL FUND EXPENDITURES
The additional $0.6 million in expenditures reflects subsequent supplemental budget appropriations that have been 
approved by the City Council since the last mid-year budget review in February 2023, which include but are not limited to 
the following:
•
Police Department Headquarters roof design - $150,000
•
Community Fund contribution for winter storm relief - $150,000
•
Pedestrian Mall Design Project - $130,766
If the updated year-end estimates come to fruition, the General Fund will realize a net loss of $7.1 million, which is an 
unfavorable variance of $2.5 million from what was assumed in the 2022-23 adopted budget. 
2023-24 BUDGET PREVIEW 
Since 1984, the City of San Mateo's practice has been to prepare a two-year business plan. Year one of the business plan 
focuses on the operating budget, in which the cost to provide core services to the community is scrutinized to ensure that 
resources are allocated appropriately. Year two focuses on the capital improvement plan (CIP), providing more detail in 
the CIP section of the budget document than is provided in year one. The five-year CIP is updated on an annual basis, 
reviewing assumptions and availability of funding sources and the prioritization of capital needs. FY 2023-24 represents 
year two of the two-year business plan. As such, requested changes to the operating budget are relatively modest, and the 
information provided for the CIP is more robust. 
Based on preliminary operating budget assumptions, General Fund total revenues are projected to be $160.7 million and 
total expenditures are projected to be $172.2 million. If these preliminary budget assumptions come to fruition, the 
General Fund would realize a net loss of $11.5 million. The narratives below articulate the budget assumptions for major 
General Fund revenues and expenditures that contribute to the projected net loss.
GENERAL FUND REVENUES
Property tax
Property tax revenues are the City’s top revenue source, and are projected to be $79.6 million in FY 2023-24, which is 
expected to constitute half of total General Fund revenues. The FY 2023-24 estimate is a decrease of $3.8 million 
compared to the FY 2022-23 year-end estimate, as the latter includes funds from the State of California for the City’s share 
of the property tax in-lieu of vehicle license fees (VLF) shortfall from fiscal year 2020-21 of $5.7 million. Current year 
secured property taxes, which constitute 61% of total property tax revenues, is assumed to increase 4%, which reflects the 
change in assessed value (AV). The change in AV is the lesser of the change in the California Consumer Price Index (CCPI) 
which was 7.27%, or 2% in accordance with Article XIII of the California Constitution. The remaining 2% increase in AV 
reflects: 
1)
Properties that have sold, which triggers recapture of fair market value, as the AV is reassessed based on the sales 
price of the property; and
2)
New development that has been added to the City’s property tax roll or properties that have had significant 
improvements that trigger an increase in AV.

Sales Tax
Sales tax revenues are projected to continue slow and steady growth in the wake of completing the recovery from the 
pandemic-driven recession that reduced sales tax revenues by 20% compared to the 2018-19 baseline. The Local 1%, 
known as the Bradley-Burns Uniform Sales and Use Tax (Bradley-Burns), is projected to account for $23.9 million while the 
local ¼%, known as Measure S, is projected to account for $7.3 million. The slow and steady growth is anticipated to 
continue throughout the General Fund Long-Term Plan.
Real Property Transfer Tax
While the 2022-23 results are unfavorable compared to the adopted budget due to the high interest rate environment, if 
the projections from the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) for the Federal Funds Rate (FFR) come to fruition, the 
FFR will decline from the current range of 4.75% to 5% (which reflects the 0.25% increase from March 2023) to a range of 
3.0% to 3.25% by 2025. This scenario should provide a more favorable real estate market, especially for first-time 
homebuyers. Rather than try to determine the year-over-year trend, the General Fund Long-Term Plan uses an average of 
$8.0 million, including fiscal year 2023-24. Finance staff will continue to monitor market trends at the local and national 
level and adjust assumptions accordingly.
Transient Occupancy Tax
Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) revenues are the only tax revenue that has not recovered from the COVID-19 pandemic. 
Compared to the 2018-19 baseline of $7.0 million (in addition to a projected $1.0 million due to the voter-approved 2% 
increase in TOT rate in November 2020), TOT revenues are projected to be $6.0 million in 2023-24. However, based on 
projections from Tourism Economics and Visit California, business travel is projected to return to the 2018-19 baseline by 
2024-25, which is reflected in the General Fund Long-Term Plan.  
GENERAL FUND EXPENDITURES
Salaries and Benefits
Total employee compensation, including salaries, wages, overtime, healthcare, and pension, are projected to be $89.8 
million. As a full-service city, in order to provide core services, over half of total General Fund expenditures are for 
employee compensation. The projected cost reflects negotiated increases in employee compensation, increases in the cost 
of healthcare, and increases in pension contributions. The latter of which will begin increasing significantly in fiscal year 
2024-25 as a result of the California Public Employees’ Retirement System (CalPERS) investment loss of negative 7.5% in 
fiscal year 2021-22. As noted during the previous mid-year budget update, the investment loss will cost the City $177 
million in additional pension costs over the next 20 years starting in fiscal year 2024-25, and by fiscal year 2028-29, an 
additional $10 million per year, which has been folded into the General Fund Long-Term Plan.
While the focus of fiscal year 2023-24 is on the CIP, as 2023-24 represents Year 2 of the 2-year business plan, the proposed 
2023-24 operating budget includes $1.2 million in personnel additions; $0.4 million of which is attributable to the General 
Fund.

NET REDUCTIONS
Net Reductions reflect potential favorable deviations from revenue or expenditure assumptions. For example, the City’s 
long-term assumption for the increase in AV for property tax is 4%. In any given year, if the AV is greater than the long-
term assumption, the City would recognize additional revenue. Conversely, the City’s vacancy assumption for non-safety 
positions is 2%. If the actual vacancy rate is greater in a fiscal year, then the expenditures would be less than projected. 
The General Fund Long-Term Plan assumes net reductions of $3.1 million to $3.7 million each year.

Transportation Improvement Fee and The General Plan
The City’s Municipal Code Chapter 27.13 established a transportation improvement fee (TIF) for new development to help 
fund transportation improvements necessary to maintain intersection improvements that will accommodate future 
growth.  The TIF currently funds transportation improvement projects that are necessary to maintain consistency with the 
General Plan Policy (C2.1), which requires that intersections not exceed a vehicle-specific level of service or a certain 
amount of delay. Policy C2.1 is changing with the forthcoming General Plan Update. As such, the list of projects that are 
required to accommodate future growth will change in order to be consistent with the new General Plan policy. Once the 
General Plan is adopted, staff will develop a new list of traffic improvements that aligns with the updated General Plan 
policy, and then update the TIF. Future transportation improvement projects will be programmed into the Capital 
Improvement Plan once this effort is completed.

The City should rebalance the budget to align with the City’s priority for complete 
and sustainable streets.
The explanation given in the past for the high investment in repaving (50-60% 
of capital budget) is that if you repave a road while it's still good it costs a lot 
less than if you wait and then have to repave a road from poor quality up to 
good. The costs rise the longer you wait. But cost of deferred maintenance is 
just one cost to examine in a thorough cost-benefit analysis. A more 
thorough cost-benefit analysis would include the total costs of our streets, with 
a review of climate change, health, travel time, costs of not investing in safety 
or stormwater management. At this very study session, the Council will 
consider a Winter Storm Damage Relief Fund.
Permeable surfaces should be considered for bikeways, parking lanes, and 
streets with lower vehicle traffic volumes and limited heavy vehicles, which was 
recently adopted by New Orleans, another community that faces severe 
flooding exacerbated by climate change.  
Paving can be approached through the lens of safety, sustainability and financial 
resource alignment opportunities. The city has already started heading in that 
direction to find opportunities to combine paving with ped/bike and stormwater and 
that trend should continue in a bold way - deprioritize projects that only repave 
roads with no other safety or stormwater management improvements and 
prioritize paving projects that can be leveraged to also bring ped/bike safety 
and green infrastructure. Also, reducing the paving portion to fund stormwater 
and ped/bike safety outside the Paving budget bucket, like reducing paving down to 
even just 40 percent the city could fund the entire Rapid Implementation Network 
($4M) of the Bike Master Plan over 3 years.
