SUBJECT:
Solid Waste Rate Setting Introduction and Overview
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive information on the Solid Waste Program and annual rate setting process and provide general feedback.
BACKGROUND:
The City’s Solid Waste Program (Program) includes waste diversion efforts related to solid waste, recycling, and 
composting services; litter and illegal dumping abatement; and ensures compliance with all county, regional, state, and 
federal regulations. Program services are available to all residents and commercial customers in the City and support the 
City’s sustainability goals, regulatory compliance mandates, and Council goals such as litter abatement. The Program is 
funded through solid waste rates set by Council to sufficiently cover the cost of these services and other City fees such as 
the franchise, landfill closure, street sweeping, and trash capture capital improvement fees.  
 
Solid waste rates are set each year by Council and adjusted as required to generate enough revenue to cover the cost of 
the provided services and previous shortfalls. Costs include collection and disposal services provided by Recology San 
Mateo County (Recology), disposal and processing fees by South Bay Recycling, LLC, our share of costs of the South Bayside 
Waste Management Authority (SBWMA) and Rethink Waste, and the City fees mentioned above. While the courts have 
not ruled that Proposition 218 applies to solid waste collection rates from a franchise hauler, in an abundance of caution, 
the City has historically followed the “majority protest” proceedings set forth in Proposition 218.   
 In 2006, to improve its waste diversion rate mandated by AB 939, the City adopted a progressive solid waste rate 
structure to encourage recycling and, some years later, composting. This rate structure, known as “Pay as You Throw” (the 
more waste you generate, the greater the per-gallon cost) is widely used throughout the state and country and was very 
successful in improving recycling rates. State laws, AB1826 for recycling and SB1383 for organics, now mandate residential 
and commercial subscription for garbage, recycling, and organics. 
 
Eighty percent of our residents now have a 32-or 20-gallon garbage can, which reduced their monthly costs. However, the 
progressive rate structure has resulted in the single-family residential and roll-off rates not covering their cost of service, 
while commercial and multi- family rates do cover their cost of service.
 
In 2021, Council directed staff to phase in a multi-year cost-of-service implementation plan to bring all rates into alignment 
with their actual cost-of-service and fund increases necessary to pay for the costs of collection, processing, the Program, 
street sweeping, and other City fees. We have now completed two years of this multi-year plan. 
 
The City’s rate consultants, HF&H Consultants (HF&H), developed this multi-year plan as they specialize in calculating and

establishing solid waste rate structure recommendations and provide similar services to the SBWMA, other SBWMA 
member agencies, and other jurisdictions throughout California. Each year, HF&H evaluates the appropriate revenue 
requirement for each customer class (e.g., single-family residential, commercial) to determine whether the current rate 
structure aligns with the cost-of-service for each customer class and service levels (e.g., 20-gallon service, 32-gallon 
service), within each class. This evaluation has two goals: (1) ensuring the customer classes are generating enough revenue 
to meet their costs and (2) ensuring each customer within the class pays for the services they receive. 
 
The Solid Waste Reserve Fund was authorized to be used in 2020 to forego rate increases because of the pandemic. It was 
used again in 2021 and 2022 to help phase in the cost-of-service implementation plan and reduce required rate increases 
to customers. 
 
The 2023 rate plan, adopted in November 2022 and currently in effect, uses the same reserves to offset the full effect of 
the phased in implementation plan while maintaining a healthy reserve fund balance, but does draw it down substantially. 
Future rate increases will be necessary to cover the full cost of City services and increased costs from Recology, South Bay 
Recycling, and the SBWMA.
 
Later this summer, staff will present the 2024 Proposed Solid Waste Rates to City Council, which will include the 
forthcoming compensation application from Recology, the 2024 disposal expense projection from the South Bay Waste 
Management Authority, and discussion of the reserve fund in more detail. Tonight, staff are available to answer any 
questions about the Solid Waste Program, solid waste rates, the cost-of-service implementation plan, and the appropriate 
level of reserves.  
 
Staff proposes to continue evaluation and implementation of the cost-of-service model as previously directed. We request 
general feedback on our proposed direction.

SUBJECT:
US-101/Peninsula Avenue Interchange Improvements – Project Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update and provide feedback on the US-101/Peninsula Avenue Interchange project.
BACKGROUND:
The US-101/Peninsula Avenue Interchange Improvements project (Project) proposes to relocate the southbound US-101 
on- and off-ramps from E. Poplar Avenue to Peninsula Avenue, creating a full northbound and southbound access 
interchange at Peninsula Avenue and Airport Boulevard. The Project would address safety issues at intersections along E. 
Poplar Avenue in the immediate vicinity of several schools and substantially improve traffic congestion in the surrounding 
area, which indirectly helps reduce cut-through traffic through the neighborhoods. As shown in Attachment 1, two 
alternatives for the southbound on- and off-ramps at Peninsula Avenue have been evaluated: a tight diamond interchange 
and a spread diamond interchange. It is important to note that each of the alternatives requires the full or partial 
acquisition of private properties along N. Amphlett Blvd. for the right-of-way (ROW) needed to construct the interchange. 
The Project was first identified in San Mateo County Transportation Authority’s (SMCTA) Measure A Expenditure Plan in 
1988, indicating that issues from these ramps have been prevalent for several decades. Given that issues with the non-
standard ramps at E. Poplar Avenue are primarily impacting city streets rather than creating freeway-related congestion or 
safety, it has been the responsibility of the City (rather than Caltrans) to sponsor a project to address these issues. In 2012, 
Council directed staff to pursue the Project. In 2015, staff developed the Project Initiation document, which identified the 
two alternatives (i.e., tight and spread diamond options) to evaluate in the Project Approval and Environmental Document 
(PA/ED) phase. The PA/ED effort was awarded a $2.5 million grant from SMCTA and was kicked off in early 2016. As an 
interim measure, however, the Poplar Avenue Safety Improvements project – a short-term improvement effort to help 
address safety issues identified at the Poplar Ave./Amphlett Blvd. intersection - was completed in 2017. 
 
Given that Peninsula Ave. crosses jurisdictional boundaries with the City of Burlingame, cut-through traffic was raised as a 
Project concern by Burlingame residents and their City Council.  As a result, a preliminary analysis to determine the 
Project’s effect on traffic in the broader community was conducted. Findings from the analysis were presented to San 
Mateo City Council in February 2020 and indicated substantial vehicle travel time savings for motorists traveling on El 
Camino Real, Peninsula Avenue, and Poplar Avenue as a result of the Project, ranging from two minutes to 10 minutes 
under the near-term conditions, and three minutes to 33 minutes in the long term. The analysis also identified minor 
delays along the 3rd and 4th Avenue intersections at Humboldt of less than a minute during the morning and afternoon 
peak hours as a result of redirected traffic to these areas. Staff also presented preliminary cost estimates for the two 
Project alternatives. At that time, construction costs were estimated at $42 million. In addition, the cost for the utility 
relocation work and ROW acquisition for the tight diamond alternative was estimated at $57 million and $71 million for 
the spread diamond option. Council directed staff to continue to pursue the Project.

Council adopted a resolution in November 2021 for staff to pursue additional funds from SMCTA from their Measure A 
Highway Program grant. In December 2021, SMCTA awarded $1.4 million to complete PA/ED, $4.2 million to fund the full 
Project design, and $400,000 to begin the administrative portion of the ROW acquisition process. Matching funds were 
also required from the City in the amounts of $600,000 for PA/ED, $500,000 for design, and $100,000 for ROW 
Administration phases, of which the $600,000 for PA/ED has already been invoiced from the City.
The Project anticipated requiring an Environmental Impact Report (EIR)/Environmental Assessment (EA). A Notice of 
Preparation of a Draft EIR for California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) review was submitted to the State on April 7, 
2021. The initial project scoping process, which informs the community about the Project’s environmental documentation 
as well as solicits feedback about the various studies that should be included in the environmental analysis, was open for 
approximately 45 days starting on April 7, 2021, and closed on May 24, 2021. The EIR/EA determines environmental 
impacts for several key areas including transportation, community, air quality, construction, and noise impacts as the 
results of the Project’s implementation. The Project is not anticipated to result in any significant impacts to the 
environment.
In late 2022, staff received updated Project cost estimates for both alternatives, indicating a significant increase in ROW 
acquisition costs (Table 1). An uncommon element of both Project alternatives is the high ROW costs compared to the 
construction costs, with the ROW cost being 80% greater than the construction cost in the tight diamond alternative and 
170% greater than the construction costs in the spread diamond alternative. As shown in Attachment 2, the tight diamond 
and spread diamond alternatives require full or partial acquisition of 29 and 34 properties, respectively. The determination 
of full or partial acquisitions is based on how substantially existing structures will be impacted and if portions of those 
impacted structures can be rebuilt rather than fully removed. Where full building removal is necessary, a full acquisition 
was recommended, and where either no structure is impacted or where a portion of the structure can be rebuilt, a partial 
acquisition was recommended. For the tight diamond alternative, 15 partial acquisitions and 14 full acquisitions were 
identified. For the spread diamond alternative, 19 partial acquisitions and 15 full acquisitions were identified.

In the most recent traffic analysis, it was identified that the Project would provide significant travel time savings in the 
Project area if it were constructed. In the Project’s opening year of 2029, there would be a travel time savings of 
approximately 131,000 hours per year for motorists on city streets. With future growth projected in the City, these travel 
time savings increase to 1.4 million hours per year by 2048. Similar findings were identified in the preliminary traffic 
analysis discussed with Council in 2020. The maps of this congestion, shown in Attachment 3, demonstrate gridlocked 
conditions along Delaware, Humboldt, and N. Bayshore in the north-south direction, and along Poplar and Peninsula in the 
east-west direction. These images further demonstrate that the Project’s implementation will alleviate much of this 
gridlock. It is also expected that existing and future traffic in the vicinity of schools along E. Poplar Avenue will be 
redirected to Peninsula Avenue, and some to 3rd and 4th Avenues with implementation of the Project. 
While the Project would provide substantial benefits in vehicle travel time savings and safety improvements, identifying 
sources of funding to implement either alternative will be very challenging. Grant funding may be available for the 
construction costs, although not for the ROW acquisition. Revenue sources would therefore need to be identified for the 
purchase of the land needed for the interchange. As such, staff is seeking direction from Council on the potential next 
steps for the Project. These options are outlined below:

Dear City Clerk, Mayor Lee & Council Members,
I am writing this in response to tonight's meeting discussion on the Peninsula Avenue Southbound
Ramps project.
Unfortunately of all days I cannot attend this meeting but will state my comments here.  I live in close
proximity to this project and have been involved and personally following this since 2006! There has been
no updates from the city on the status of this project for almost two years until now.  Many of the residents
in this area that attended many workshops and meetings in years prior and voiced their concerns have
since moved or passed but I am still here and we have many new young families that have made their
home on our block, the 800 block of North Idaho.
Some of our concerns are whether we can really have a say in some of the issues we are concerned
about which are as follows:
1.  Circulation and cut-thru traffic - How can we prevent or ease any cut thru traffic that may occur as a
result of this project?
2.  Since Peninsula Avenue will become a busier street can we make it safer for pedestrians by making
sure a concrete walkway is installed underneath the overpass as was done with the original overpass way
back in 1946?
We requested this in 2007 when the current overpass was built but we were given a lame excuse.  If they
did it way back in 1946 why not in 2023?
Since then this area underneath the overpass has become a dumping ground for construction dirt and
what have you.
If we truly are concerned about making our streets safer for pedestrians this would greatly make a
difference in our neighborhood.
3.  Trees and landscaping - we need these included as part of the project.  Being so close to the freeway
it is well known that trees help with pollutants.
4.  Parking - We have already lost over 200 parking spaces with the bike lanes.
How many more spaces will we lose with this project?
If anyone has any additional additional questions feel free to contact me.  I would appreciate working with
our Public Works and our CIty on ways we can make this a better project with special consideration to our
neighborhood surrounding this project.
Thank you,
Joanne Bennett
