SUBJECT:
Campaign Finance – Voluntary Expenditure Limits and Public Financing Models
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide feedback and direction on research relating to voluntary expenditure limits and public financing models for 
election campaigns.
BACKGROUND:
In recent years within campaign finance reform discourse, growing concerns over the influence of money in local elections 
have led to a greater interest in both the adoption of voluntary expenditure limits and public financing models for election 
campaigns. At its Fiscal Year 2022-23 Goal Setting the City Council directed staff to study campaign finance reform policies, 
in particular relating to voluntary expenditure limits, and at its February 21, 2023 post-election study session on campaign 
contributions Council directed staff to return with research into public financing of election campaigns.

amendments?

VOLUNTARY EXPENDITURE LIMITS 
Voluntary expenditure limits in local elections, as implemented in neighboring cities such as Burlingame and Redwood City, 
are self-imposed spending boundaries that candidates choose to adhere to during the electoral process. These limits may 
serve to foster a more equitable and transparent political landscape by curbing election campaign spending and reducing 
the potential influence of monetary resources on election outcomes. Typically, candidates who opt to participate in a 
voluntary expenditure limit program commit to capping their campaign expenses, in exchange for some form of benefit. 
Note that mandatory expenditure limits are constitutionally impermissible, hence the interest from some jurisdictions in 
voluntary limits.
Following the examples established within Burlingame and Redwood City, a similar ordinance for San Mateo, as seen in 
Attachment 1, may include an optional spending threshold, where candidates would indicate their intention to adhere to 
the voluntary expenditure limit when submitting their nomination documents during the candidate filing period. 
Depending on Council direction, such an ordinance could go into effect beginning on January 1, 2024, in order to line up 
with campaign reporting periods.  It would then apply to the November 2024 general election.

The agreement to voluntarily limit campaign expenditures may include all expenditures incurred by the candidate or the 
candidate’s committee in support of their candidacy. However, the Council may determine certain expenditures to be 
exempt from this, such as filing fees, expenses required for the publication of the candidate’s statement of qualifications in 
the County’s Voter Information Pamphlet and Sample Ballot, returned contributions, non-monetary contributions, and 
legal costs incurred in connection with litigation arising out of a campaign for City office. 
A three-business day window following the conclusion of the candidate filing period would be provided for candidates to 
reconsider their choice. Those who opt to embrace the voluntary expenditure limit would receive recognition on the City's 
official website, eNews, as well as in widely circulated newspapers.  If a candidate who had agreed to abide by the 
voluntary limits surpasses those limits, the City's staff would proceed to publicly announce the violation through the same 
avenues.
Using the Redwood City and Burlingame model of capping expenditure on a per resident basis, the Council may wish to 
consider campaign expenditure ceiling limits of $1.50, $2.00, $2.50, or another amount, per resident in the district for each 
election in which the candidate is seeking elective office. Residency numbers for each district will be determined by the 
latest available Decennial Census data. The expenditure ceiling amount for each district (rounded to the nearest $100),

OTHER PROPOSED ORDINANCE AMENDMENTS
Recall Elections
Municipal Code Chapter 2.80 requires amendments to align with changes in the law governing recall elections.  Assembly 
Bill (AB) 2584 (2022) amended the process whereby a recall election occurs. Generally, the question of whether an elected 
officer shall be removed from office and their successor has historically been put to the voters in the form of one ballot 
measure. (Note that in San Mateo, the filling of a Council vacancy is left to the Council or the Mayor to decide, under the 
Charter.)  However, AB 2582 has removed question of the officer’s successor from the recall election, and instead the seat 
would remain vacant until the agency is able to fill the vacancy in accordance with its process for doing so. In addition,

recall committees are more akin to ballot measure committees than candidate committees, in that the issue submitted to

For these reasons, and based on court decisions, recall elections are not subject to candidate contribution limits. An

Aggregation of Contributions
Recent experience with implementing the City’s campaign ordinance suggests that clarification is needed when applying 
contribution limits to aggregated contributions.  Contributions by an individual are aggregated with contributions from 
organizations that the individual directs or controls.  Also, contributions by two or more organizations are aggregated if 
they are directed or controlled by a majority of the same persons. However, there are two contribution limits - $500 for 
individuals and $1000 for organizations.  Staff recommends amending Section 2.80.045 “Aggregation of Contributions” to 
clarify that aggregated contributions shall be subject to the $1,000 contribution limit.    
Next steps:
Following direction from the Council, staff would return with a draft ordinance to amend Municipal Code Section 2.80 
“Contributions and Loans to City Candidate Campaigns”. Council may provide direction on aspects of the draft ordinances 
during this meeting, such as the expenditure ceiling amount and expenses that should be exempt.  
Note that this report does not address independent expenditure committees, as U.S. Supreme Court caselaw and other 
court decisions prohibit the restriction of independent expenditures, which are not made at the behest of a candidate or 
their controlled committee.  Under state law, independent expenditures must already be reported, including late 
contributions totaling $1,000 or more made after the last campaign statement but before the election. 
At the direction of the Council, with the broad intention to increase public transparency into candidate campaign finances, 
including independent expenditures, staff developed and launched the first Campaign Finance Dashboard in the County for 
the November 2022 general election. Towards the goal of transparency in campaign finances, the Dashboard provides an 
interactive overview of financial activities (including contributions and expenditures), summaries of contributions by 
individuals, organizations, etc., summaries of contributions by contribution amount, and contributions by location. The 
campaign dashboard can be found on the City’s website at: https://www.cityofsanmateo.org/4664/Council-Campaign-
Financing-Dashboard 
PUBLIC FINANCING
The research has shown that broadly speaking, agencies that have adopted public financing of election campaigns have 
done so to reduce the reliance of candidates on private donations and to widen the interest in seeking elected office. 
These agencies include San Francisco, Oakland, Berkeley, Los Angeles, and Seattle.  Based on preliminary modeling, if 
public financing had been in place in San Mateo for the 2022 election cycle based on a 6:1 financing formula ($6 of public 
funds for each $1 of privately raised contributions), similar to San Francisco’s model, a disbursement of public funds to San 
Mateo candidates would have likely been in the amount of $127,000. Alternatively, the voucher model of public funding, 
implemented by Seattle, would likely have cost approximately $436,500 in public funds distributed to candidates.  Neither 
of these figures include administrative costs. These models and associated costs are described in more detail below.  These 
costs would have been paid for out of the General Fund.  
An overview of some of the different public financing models that have been implemented for local election campaigns is 
provided below in the Public Financing Models section.
Currently, city council candidates in San Mateo, like most cities in California, rely on private donations to finance their 
election campaigns. At its February 21, 2023 Study Session, Council expressed interest in exploring public financing of 
campaigns, in part due to concerns of the high cost of running for office making it difficult for potential candidates to 
compete in an election. Table 1 below, taken from the City’s Campaign Finance Dashboard, provides a breakdown of 
campaign costs per voter, which equates to $8.45 median average per vote received for successful candidates.

PUBLIC FINANCING MODELS
Matching Funds 
The matching funds model of public financing provides public funds to candidates who agree to limit their spending and/or 
raise a minimum amount of money from small donors. Under the matching funds model, for every dollar raised from small 
donors, the agency matches the contribution with a predetermined amount of public funds. Some of the key elements of 
matching funds models that have been adopted by agencies include:
Eligibility: candidates who agree to abide by the agency’s campaign regulations and demonstrate a minimum level 
of support from the community are eligible to participate in the program.
Matching Funds: matching funds at various rates are provided to qualified candidates, in some cases this funding 
can only be used for specific activities and expenses.
Contribution Limits: some programs place limits on contributions a candidate may receive, including the number of 
self-contributions and loans, as well as contributions from individuals residing in the candidate’s district.  (Note 
that such restrictions would be legally problematic if imposed on all candidates instead of voluntarily accepted by 
candidates participating in a public financing program.)  
Ethics and Compliance: programs may require compliance with relevant campaign finance laws and regulations, as 
well as the program's own code of ethics.
Oversight and Enforcement: in some cases, such as the City of San Francisco, the City of Los Angeles, and the City 
of Oakland, matching funds oversight, including establishing eligibility, post-election auditing and enforcement, is 
performed by a dedicated Ethics Commission. In agencies, such as the City of Long Beach and the City of Santa Fe, 
the City Clerk administers these programs with external auditing contract support.  
The City and County of San Francisco’s matching funds model, known as the ‘Public Financing Program’, was first 
established by Proposition O, a ballot measure approved by the voters in November 2000, and offers eligible candidates a 
six-to-one (6:1) match for campaign donations. This means that a candidate receives $6 in public funding for every $1 of 
private contributions raised.
During San Francisco’s 2022 election through the Public Financing Program, candidates certified as eligible to receive 
public financing could have received up to the maximum of the following amounts:

Grant Funds
A grant-based model of public financing bares many of the same characteristics of the matching funds model, and in some 
cases, such as the City of New Haven, Connecticut, public contributions are provided in a hybrid form of matching funds 
and grants. 
The City of Santa Fe, New Mexico, provides a dedicated public finance fund, which is administered by the City Clerk. Each 
fiscal year, a total of $150,000 is budgeted for this fund. Eligibility for candidates to participate in the Public Campaign 
Finance Fund, include meeting the requirements to be a certified candidate for municipal office, collection of qualifying 
contributions from separate qualified electors (600 for the office of mayor, and 150 for the office of city council), with 
some allowances for accepting seed money contributions to cover the costs incurred in obtaining qualifying contributions, 
as specified.   Within three business days of certifying as a participating candidate, the city clerk is required disburse from 
the Public Campaign Finance fund $60,000 to candidates for mayor and $15,000 to city council candidates.  
In the event of an uncontested race, only 10% of the amount that would be due to a candidate in a contest race is 
dispersed. All payments received by a participating candidate from the fund can only be used to pay expenses reasonably 
incurred during the candidate’s current campaign. During its most recent election, of the total number of candidates 
appearing on the ballot in Santa Fe, 33% of candidates participated in the public financing program. 
Reimbursement Funds
From 1999 until more recently, the City of Oakland provided public funding to candidates running for elected office 
through its “Limited Public Finance Act” (LPF). The LPF provided eligible candidates with reimbursements for qualified 
expenditures used for campaign expenses. During the November 2022 election, the total amount of LPF reimbursement 
funds came to $159,038, which amounted to 90 percent of the total funds available.

Voucher Program 
In a voucher program, eligible residents are provided with a dollar value in the form of a voucher that can be used to 
contribute to a participating candidate of their choice during an election cycle.  
In 2015, voters in Seattle approved the ‘Honest Elections Seattle’ ballot initiative, establishing the Democracy Voucher 
Program, which is administered by the Seattle Ethics and Elections Commission. Under this program, voters in Seattle 
receive four $25 vouchers to allocate to candidates running for local offices. Candidates may qualify for participation in the 
program by collecting qualifying signatures and small contributions from Seattle residents. Participating candidates may 
use Democracy Vouchers to finance certain campaign activities, which is governed by the City of Seattle’s campaign

STATE LEGISLATION DEVELOPMENTS
Currently in California, five charter cities have adopted public financing of election campaigns (Los Angeles, San Francisco, 
Berkeley, Oakland, and Long Beach). State law currently prohibits counties, special districts, and general law cities from 
pursuing public funding of elections. Two identical bills – SB 24 (Lee) and AB 270 (Umberg) would place the matter on the 
November 5, 2024, statewide election by asking voters to remove this prohibition. In 2016, the state legislature passed SB 
1107 (Allen), which would have removed this prohibition, however the courts ruled that the issue must be put to the

SB 1439
As a reminder for the City Council, Effective January 1, 2023, Senate Bill 1439 (SB 1439) strengthened conflict of interest 
regulations concerning campaign contributions.  The bill extended the reach of these rules to local elected officials, such as 
members of city councils and boards of supervisors.  Previously, these rules applied to appointed officials, such as board 
and commission members, but excluded elected officials.
State law now provides that campaign contributions of more than $250 would be a conflict of interest for these local 
officials and would require disclosure and recusal in certain situations, such as where the donor was an applicant for a 
license or permit, if the contribution was made within the 12 months before the decision.  SB 1439 also prohibits local 
officials from accepting, soliciting, or directing contributions of more than $250 from parties to a proceeding on a license, 
permit or entitlement for use (such as a land use permit) while the proceeding is pending, and for 12 months after the 
decision (which expanded the 3-month period previously in effect).  However, if local official does not willfully and 
knowingly accept, solicit, or direct a prohibited contribution, the violation can be resolved by returning the contribution, as 
specified.  Finally, the Fair Political Practices Commission has issued detailed regulations implementing SB 1439 that are 
now in effect.
CONCLUSION:
This report shows there is a wide scope of approaches that exist for agencies to provide public financing programs for 
election campaigns. Within each approach, agencies have tried a variety of matching rates and grant amounts and 
adjusted the eligibility criteria to make their programs more successful. Significant staffing resources and general fund 
amounts would be required to administer such a program. 
In discussions with staff from these agencies, a common sentiment was shared that there is not a one-size-fits-all approach 
to public funding of election campaigns. Moreover, for any agency wishing to pursue public financing, it is important to 
clearly define the criteria for success. This research has shown a variety of criteria for success provided by agencies who 
have adopted public financing, including:
•
Limiting campaign contributions as a proportion of total contributions from outside the jurisdiction;
•
Increasing the number of small contributions provided to candidates;
•
Increasing the number of first-time candidates running for office; 
•
Minimizing the number of uncontested elections and increasing number of candidates competing for vacancies; 
•
Increasing engagement and participation from historically underrepresented communities.
This research shows that public financing models for city council elections has the potential to help reduce the influence of 
money in politics and increase citizen participation in the political process. However, these public financing models can be 
costly and would require administrative support, both paid for out of the General Fund.  Each model has its own strengths 
and limitations and requires careful consideration and planning to ensure its effectiveness. Should the Council wish to 
pursue public financing of election, further direction will be required on which model should be considered for further 
analysis.

VOLUNTARY EXPENDITURE LIMIT.
(a) Each candidate for election to the City Council in November 2024, and for each City Council 
election thereafter, shall, prior to the time they file their nomination papers with the city clerk, advise 
the city clerk in writing on a form provided by the City whether or not the candidate will opt to 
voluntarily limit their campaign expenditures in accordance with the voluntary campaign expenditure 
limits set forth in this section and by resolution.  The agreement to voluntarily limit campaign 
expenditures shall pertain to all expenditures incurred by the candidate or the candidate’s committee in 
support of their candidacy and shall include all such expenditures that a candidate or candidate’s 
committee is required to report pursuant to the California Political Reform Act of 1974, as amended, 
whether those expenditures are made before or after the filing of nomination papers.
(b) Withdrawal Period: within three (3) business days after the deadline to file nomination 
papers with the city clerk, a candidate that previously accepted the voluntary campaign expenditure 
limit will have one (1) opportunity to notify the city clerk that they have decided not to accept the 
voluntary campaign expenditure limit.  The candidate shall thereafter be relieved of abiding by the 
expenditure limit.
(c) Candidates who agree to abide by the voluntary campaign expenditure limit shall receive the 
following benefits and incentives at no cost to themselves:
(1) The City’s website will clearly identify which candidates have agreed to the voluntary 
expenditure limit.
(2) The City will publish in its regular digital newsletter which candidates have agreed to 
the voluntary expenditure limit.  This will be published twice (once at the next publishing of said 
newsletter after the withdrawal period and once when the ballots are mailed to residents.)
(3) The City will publish notices in newspapers of general circulation the candidates who 
agreed to the voluntary expenditure limit.
(d) Calculation of Voluntary Campaign Expenditure Limit.
(1) A candidate for district City Councilmember who voluntarily agrees to expenditure 
ceilings shall not make qualified expenditures exceeding [$1.50, $2.00, $2.50, or other] per
