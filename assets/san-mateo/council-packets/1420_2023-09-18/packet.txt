SUBJECT:
Boards and Commissions – 2023 Demography Study
RECOMMENDATION:
Review the 2023 Commissioner Demography Study results and provide direction for ongoing surveying and outreach. 
BACKGROUND:
From July through September this year, staff conducted its second demographic survey of individuals who serve on the 
City’s appointed boards and commissions. The City has five commissions and two boards, each with five members. In total, 
34 individuals were serving on these advisory bodies at the time of this survey.  
The primary role of boards and commissions is to increase public input and participation in the determination of City 
policies and programs. Members of the boards and commissions are appointed by Council and are considered volunteers, 
as it is an unpaid position under the City Charter. 
Recruitment Efforts to Date:
The City Clerk’s office conducts annual recruitment for scheduled vacancies in the spring of each year and recruits for any 
unscheduled vacancies. Recruitment efforts traditionally include using the City’s website digital newsletters and social 
media to announce that the recruitment period is open; contacting a subscriber list of those who have expressed an 
interest in service throughout the year; posting flyers at city facilities; and contacting City Services Academy graduates and 
Leadership program graduates; as well as utilizing our sitting commissioners and council members to spread awareness. In 
2017, the City Clerk’s Office began reaching out to local places of worship, with a particular focus on under-represented 
communities, as well as churches with diverse congregations. 
Equity, Inclusion, and Diversity:
The City is integrating equity and inclusivity into all aspects of its operations. The conduct of this demography study 
reflects the City’s commitment to two of its key values, transparency, and inclusivity. During a July 17, 2023, study session 
on the qualifications and compensation for service to boards and commissions, City Council received data from the original 
2017 demography study, in addition to recommendations from the Diversity Equity and Inclusion (DEI) Task Force, and a 
survey of cities providing compensation to boards and commissions. With the goal of diversifying representation on these 
bodies, City Council provided direction to staff to move forward with consideration of a ballot measure on whether to 
amend the City Charter to provide compensation for service and to remove the elector requirement for service.

Staff plans to return to the Council with a further study session this fall to receive direction on the priorities for prospective 
charter amendments. This updated demography study is intended to provide the Council with updated information to help 
provide additional context to that decision-making. 
Design and Procedure
Staff set out with the goal to remain attentive to the potential sensitivity of demographic survey questions, with the 
objective to obtain a one hundred percent completion rate of board members and commissioners, while also building 
upon the original survey’s language and responses to facilitate inclusivity. Input on the survey was provided by members of 
the Council, as well as the DEI Task Force. A range of feedback was provided and used to help modify the language 
contained within the range of responses and led to a new question on the age of dependents living with members of 
boards and commissions. Questions were carefully vetted with staff reviewers from other departments, with consideration 
given to ensuring comparable data exists for analysis and alignment with the purpose of the study. 
The survey was sent to all current board and commission members. To provide additional data, staff also attempted to 
survey committee members, with the caveat that only one response can be provided for individuals serving on multiple 
bodies. There was a one-hundred percent response rate from the boards and commissions, one-hundred percent 
responses from Measure S Oversight Committee members, two responses from the Civic Arts Committee, and zero 
responses from the Investment Advisory Committee. 
Survey results were then compared, whenever possible, to data for the whole city population as reported on the 2021 
American Community Survey and 2020 Census results. Furthermore, the 2023 demography study was compared to the 
2017 study to help identify any trends or developments.
Results
1.
Duration of Residence in San Mateo: In 2017 the average number of years lived in San Mateo was 25, with over 
80% residing in San Mateo for over a decade. In 2023, that average dropped down to 20 years, with only 70% of 
respondents living in San Mateo for over a decade.
2.
Race: As was seen in the 2017 study, the 2023 survey revealed a smaller proportion of people of color serve on 
San Mateo’s commissions as compared to the City’s general population.
3.
Language: A smaller proportion of commissioners speak a language other than English at home as compared to 
the general population.
4.
Gender: The percentage of board members and commissioners who identify as female has increased from 54% to 
61% in the last 6 years. This is 13 percentage points higher than the general San Mateo population. 
5.
Educational Attainment: San Mateo’s Boards and Commissions members are highly educated, with over 3 in 4 
members having post-graduate degrees.
6.
Age: There continues to be general age diversity amongst Boards and Commissioners, with those who are 65 and 
older being the largest age group. There were no young adults under 25 serving on the commissions. 
7.
Housing Status: Renters remain under-represented; while 17 percent of boards and commissioners rent, nearly 
half of San Mateo residents are renters. 
8.
Income: Of those who reported their average household income, four out of five Board Members and 
Commissioners fell above the San Mateo County median household income in 2021 of $136,837.
9.
Dependents: 34 percent of Board Members and Commissioners have dependents living in their home, which is 
approximately 5 percentage points higher than the number of households in San Mateo with dependents. 
10. Districts: Almost half (47.9%) of Board Members and Commissioners reside in District 1. District 5 is the second 
highest represented district (20.8%), followed by District 3 (14.6%), District 2 (10.4%), and District 4 (6.3%).
Options to Bolster Recruitment Efforts:
The data contained within the 2023 survey provides the opportunity for staff to identify specific target areas where further 
outreach and education on the role of advisory bodies is required. Some methods of reaching under-represented 
communities may include providing bilingual materials on the role and benefits of serving on an advisory body; and 
seeking additional partnerships with community organizations, school districts, parent groups, neighborhood associations, 
businesses, nonprofit rental housing providers, and groups in underrepresented communities. The City will continue to
