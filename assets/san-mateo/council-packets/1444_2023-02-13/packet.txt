RECOMMENDATION:
Review eligible submissions and select one or more artist(s) to submit design proposals. 
BACKGROUND:
In July 2020, the City Council adopted a resolution reaffirming the City of San Mateo’s commitment to racial equality and 
equity. The Council discussed the concept of a community mural or other community art project that would reflect and 
promote racial equity and diversity. The Civic Arts Committee was directed by the Council to implement the project.
With the creation of the Pedestrian Mall on B Street, a location was identified as a unique and large space for the Racial 
Equity Art Project street murals to be painted in the center emergency vehicle lane of the Pedestrian Mall. The Civic Arts 
Committee helped develop a Request for Qualifications (RFQ) and that was sent out through many networks to find artists, 
with a particular interest for those from San Mateo and the greater SF Bay area. The methods of sharing included, but 
wasn’t limited to, through the Civic Arts Committee, the City’s social media, City’s website, the DEI Task Force members, 
individual contact with artists, downtown businesses sharing through their networks, and through the Northern California 
Public Art Administrators Network (PAAN).
A primary focus was to invite local BIPOC (Black, Indigenous, and People of Color) artists, from San Mateo and through the 
greater Bay Area. Some of the key information requested in the RFQ included their interest in the project, their inspiration 
and possible aesthetic direction or narrative of their artwork, their experience with similar art projects of scope and size, 
their approach to public art and implementation of the project, and examples of their work within the document or online.
There was a total of twelve applications, of which, five were determined ineligible due to incomplete applications or 
inappropriate art medium for this project. The seven eligible applications include three that are within San Mateo and Bay 
Area, the remaining four are from Southern California and out of state. All submittals are included in Attachment 1 - RFQ 
Submissions. The focus on Attachment 2 is the Summary of Eligible Submissions. This spreadsheet summarizes the 
responses to the questions posed, the website addresses for their portfolio and other information about their art, and 
brief notes from staff regarding the key eligibility considerations.
The Civic Arts Committee will review the qualifications and portfolios of the applicants and identify the artist(s) they would 
like to have develop a proposal for the project design as noted in the RFQ:

Valerie Margolin has a Bachelor of Fine Arts and BGD of Graphic Design from 
Rhode Island School of Design. She has been working as a creative since receiving 
her degree in 1993. Originally from Detroit, Valerie hails from an exceptionally creative and musical 
family. Concert dates and studio sessions often meant long hours on the road — providing Val with 
childhood experiences which shaped her love of travel as an adult. Valerie thrives on learning about the 
places she has visited. And she loves any opportunity to incorporate elements from her travels and 
experiences into her work. Some of her favorite mediums are: paint (acrylic, oils, watercolor), ceramics, 
linocuts, block printing, knitting and macrame. Currently, her artistic focus has been on surface design, 
illustration, and large scale murals. 
We are Design Girl Magic — a creative 3-person team, based in the San 
Francisco area, with over 20 years of collective experience creating art — in the 
corporate arena, public sector, and as freelance ﬁne artists. We are strong, 
talented, and complex women of color who live and breathe art. We collaborate 
to create. We are visionaries who work to tell stories and strive to promote 
community engagement and awareness through what we create. Our approach 
to large scale projects involves brainstorming, research, community outreach, and 
planning. We feel it is important, not only to create a beautiful aesthetic, but also to 
fully understand the project’s objective and successfully convey the intended message.
Notable Achievements
In 2021, we were invited to create a mural in Chinatown to commemorate Chinese New Year. The project 
was commissioned by the Paint the Void project and San Francisco Creative Corp. Our team fully immersed 
in the experience—drawing inspiration by visiting the space, speaking with the local community, and 
researching color palettes, imagery, and techniques historically used in Chinese art.
We ended 2021 by participating in the inaugural Let’s Glow SF event - an annual projection mapping 
project sponsored by the Downtown SF Partnership (Still images presented - “San Francisco Trees”, 
“Dandelion Forest”, “Ornament Drop”, and “Snowﬂake Garden”).
In 2022, our team was selected to paint a Tabletop Heart sculpture for ZSFGeneral Hospital “Hearts in SF” 
series, as well as a Mini Heart sculpture to commemorative ZSFGH’s 150th Anniversary.

The mural has 9 special and rare koi swimming upstream bringing with them good fortune

Dear Denise Garcia, 
My approach to public art has always been to create work that responds to the surrounding area. I do my best to avoid 
a particular style. Every project calls for a different voice; a different cadence because the surroundings are different. 
I was born in San Mateo and my family has owned a small business in the ShoreView shopping center for over 30 
years. I have watched the community change and grow standing behind the counter of our family restaurant. I have 
painted murals and signs in San Mateo and on the peninsula at large for the past 14 years. My first mural was the 
Jazz Duet in the 4th ave parking structure across from Poncho Villa. My Second was the two grenadier guards on the 
facade of the Swingin' Door Pub formally known as the Prince of Wales on 25th ave. Since then I have worked with 
various small businesses and community organizations around the Bay Area. 
I’ve always felt as though San Mateo had the potential to be a cultural hub on the peninsula. Growing up in Shoreview 
I was surrounded by people from all parts of the planet. Over the years I learned how to speak Spanish fluently by 
simply engaging with the people around me. I would like to emphasize the abundance of cultural diversity in the city. 
This cultural diversity creates an environment where no matter where you are from as long as you come wanting to be 
a part of the community you will be accepted. 
What I bring to this project is my commitment to my community. I have worked and lived in the community hand-to-
hand and face-to-face with the people that make San Mateo what it is. Over the years I have developed a keen sense 
of art direction. The work I make is highly conceptual and encompasses many mediums. All of my projects are more of 
a response to a concept than my particular style. The mural will reflect the lives and experiences of the community.  
I have worked on several large-scale projects that either utilize typography, shapes, or images. I worked on a 
large-scale billboard for Found hotels that sit 36 feet above the ground and 48 feet across. As well as a series of 
murals in their basement which encompasses multiple rooms at a total of 270 sq feet. 
I have been making site specific artwork since I was 19.  I have worked with many businesses and community 
organi-zations to create work that speaks directly to its environment. Whether it be a community mural, a typography 
treat-ment on a historic building, or a mural to bring an alleyway to life. Recently I have been making work that has 
multiple applications.
Most of my inspiration comes from my surroundings. I do a lot of street photography and reference a lot of these 
pho-tos in my projects. The people, the streets, the businesses, the street vendors, and the culture that is created 
when all of this is combined have a huge influence on my work. Documenting my surroundings is a regular practice, 
and this informs my work. 
My approach to this project will be to highlight my deep commitment and understanding of my community. This 
project is important to me because growing up in the most diverse neighborhood of San Mateo, I experienced cultures 
from Central America, The Pacific Islands, Asia, and the Middle East. As an artist, the history of my family’s migration 
to the United States and the Geopolitical issues overseas that still affect my family has a huge influence on the type 
of work I make. Conflict, colonization and migration are very much a part of family discussions and a huge part of my 
upbringing. Fortunately, San Mateo has afforded my family the opportunity for a much more prosperous and upwardly 
mobile life. My passion for the project is not only because I have a history in San Mateo, but, as a lifelong resident, 
I would like to be a part of the growth and future of San Mateo. San Mateo is rapidly changing and I would like to be 
a part of it.
Regards,

STATEMENT
As Mexican-American Queer artists living in the Bay Area, we would like to propose our names and talent for the
Racial Equity Art project in San Mateo. We -Simon Malvaez & J Manuel Carmona- strongly believe that we have
created community in every intersection of our career. Our work in public spaces always aims to say something
meaningful with the spotlight that comes with it and we are very fortunate to have experienced this throughout
several years of our careers beautifying San Francisco.
In 2020, we were asked to design a mural covering the facade of a 3-story building that houses a Latino
low-income housing project and a historic queer community bar. In collaboration with Mission Economic
Development Agency (MEDA), an organization working 40+ years to keep Latinos and working families in San
Francisco’s Mission District, and El Rio -a fabulous 40+ year old queer neighborhood bar, we embarked on our
biggest mural challenge to date. This mural depicts the many faces, traditions and ethnic backgrounds within the
Latino community in the US while it champions sexual and gender diversity.
We fulfilled a dream as a Queer artist to paint the SF LGBT Center on one of the busiest intersections of San
Francisco in 2021. With hundreds of people driving by the purple building every hour we felt the need to say
something relevant to our city, our current community, and the LGBTQ history. We started with the colors of the
Progressive Pride Flag because to us it represents the amazing diversity within the Queer community. We
wanted to showcase “heroes” from the past and present who we feel should be celebrated in the LGBTQ+
community as well as the intersection with our Latino Heritage. The mural “Queeroes” has since become a
symbol of Diversity and Pride of San Francisco and its amazing Communities.
In 2022, we partnered with three other local artists in what was to this date our biggest mural project called
“showtime”. We worked with D’arcy Drollinger, SF Arts Commission, and the Leather District of San Francisco to
design a mural that depicts a variety of tropes and symbols associated with the queer nightlife community in
which SF OASIS is the epitome of a safe space. The mural was received with an overwhelming positive response
by the community where Senator Scott Weiner and Mayor London Breed attended the unveiling ceremony and a
non profit -OASIS ARTS- was created.
As queer, latinx artists, we would like to approach this San Mateo project by talking about our rich cultural
backgrounds, the places where we grew up -experiences- and the community that influences our lives and
artistic approaches. We would like to portrait and depict people who are part of our everyday lives in the Bay
Area, celebrating our differences and our diversity through unity and inclusion: Images of our queer community,
the latino community, and the local people that live in our neghboorhoods.
In terms of aesthetic guidelines, we would like to use bold black lines defining diverse ethnic backgrounds as
part of the portraits that we will paint. Basic geometry, strong blocks of color and other graphic elements and
words that reflect our mixed culture and diversity in the Bay Area. Our intention is to communicate a feeling of
Community to every pedestrian crossing this piece of art and create an “experience”
As we continue to explore the power of public art, we hope to use our work and talent to inspire individuals with
the pride and love that we feel for our two countries of heritage, our LGBTQI community, and to bring focus to
the duality of millions of Mexican-Americans living and traveling between both sides of this border.

I have seen firsthand how public art can make a positive impact on communities and I would be honored
to be a part of that impact, especially as it relates to racial equity in San Mateo.
I approach public art projects like this as a collaborative effort. I am the physical creator, but the project
team and community are integral parts of the project. I envision creating a mural that has a vibrant color
palette and depicts figures interacting with each other. They would be all sorts of sizes and bright colors,
in a style that is fun and funky, not realistic. There would be shapes throughout that tie the design
together and also are representative of Indigenous histories, unity, and racial and social movements. I
would also like to involve local artists and those wanting to learn more about murals to help with this
project, giving it even more of a community touch.
I have worked with teams of diverse stakeholders to create site-specific murals across the country for
public art organizations, government agencies, neighborhood improvement programs, and more. I have
consistently received positive feedback regarding bringing the artistic vision to life and managing the
demands of a project. I’ve worked with a variety of exterior surfaces, including painting directly onto the
street and know the materials to create something that is durable and low maintenance.
I believe what makes public art like this successful is to involve the community in the process. I have

opportunities for communities to help with mural installations, which I’ve seen be hugely beneficial in

I am a professional artist specializing in mixed-media public art design, fabrication, 
and installation.  My interest in public art has always been to expand the 
combination of diverse techniques in hand-brushed acrylic, fired ceramic tile, 
stone, and metal in ways that complement the built environment and show the 
culture and history of the community. 
 
I approach my projects as a painter.  In my interior work, I use murals and mobiles 
hand-painted in my color and tonal gradation style. In my exterior work, I use 
ceramic tile with an airbrush-over-photosilkscreen technique.  This style is unique 
in its ability to use color and tone to control the level of abstraction of the fairly 
realistic subject matter.   But in creating the design, any subject matter can be 
used. My interest in this project is to present this style to the widest audience.  And 
as a painter, the “canvas” of the work would be framed by the design and 
architecture of the built environment.  Exterior tile work is priced at $275/square 
foot inclusive of design, fabrication, and installation. 
 
My interior murals and mobiles are painted in-studio and made of thickly applied 
hand-brushed acrylic polymer (NovaColor www.novacolorpaint.com ) on a Phifer 
SunTex 95/97 fiberglass mesh substrate  (https://www.phifer.com/product/suntex-
95).  The mobiles are made in-studio using the same SunTex fiberglass mesh 
bonded to shaped Gatorfoam (www.gatorfoam.com) or aluminum honeycomb 
(www.pacificpanels.com).  The substrate is adhered with Sherwin-Williams Roman 
555 adhesive (https://romandecoratingproducts.com/product/pro-555/).  The work 
can be made flame retardant with any of a number of coatings such as Flamort X 
or Flame Stop I-DS (www.flamestop.com). Interior murals and mobiles are priced 
at $155/square foot inclusive of design, fabrication, and installation. 
 
All work has involved the community, including the architects, engineers, planners, 
and most importantly, the construction contractors and their crews.  Many have 
been design-build.   
  
In my design process, I always take into consideration the community’s connection 
to past and present history. I visit to talk to the people, take photos, and look into 
archives.  With pandemic protocols, this can be done on-line. The culture and 
history of the area’s specific neighborhoods are always of interest. Audio-visual 
show & tell and Q & A with schools and local community centers can be arranged. 
This is not only useful as a design resource, but would serve to develop a sense of 
participation in the project, giving the youth and their families a proprietary interest 
in the artwork. Needless to say, this mitigates vandalism and graffiti.

East Public Library Campus, Fishers IN (Katelyn Coyne Coynek@hepl.lib.in.us 
317-579-0309), and murals and pavers for the Washington State University new 
Life Sciences Bldg. (Michael Sweney mike.sweney@arts.wa.gov 360-228-4080).

Public Art and Equity (sections a and b)
As a muralist, my goal is to create art that not only beautifies the community but also
brings people together. I believe that public art should be for the public, and that is why I
am committed to involving the community in every aspect of my work. From the planning

A statement of interest outlining your approach to public art 
As a 2-D public artist, my intention is to paint humanity in all forms. I’m interested in creating large-scale and site-specific 
artworks that transform specific sites while making a visual and positive impact in the community.  
With just some paint, large-scale murals have the potential to completely change the perception of empty, boring and 
forgotten places. Instead of selling a product, like all the billboards around us, murals don’t sell anything. They are artworks 
for the people, no matter their social status or who they are. As a result, these creative installations have created positive 
impact in cities, bringing tourism and media interest, and in many cases have become iconic sites and “must-see” locations.  
 
Your interest in this specific project 
As an immigrant (now resident), I had experienced my fair share of racial inequality through my life. I deal with diversity 
every day: my wife is from Mexico, I’m from Spain, and our kids were born here in the US. We constantly have to navigate 
into each other’s values and have find a common ground for the three cultures to work under one roof. I believe this personal 
experience to be the drive to my public art. I’m interested in creating work that highlights diversity and inclusion in a way that 
is powerful and positive. I truly believe it is not until we celebrate our differences that we will understand our commonalities. 
 
The possible aesthetic direction or narrative; 
Colorful, dynamic, figurative (portraits of the community), hopeful, and powerful.

Artist Statement 
 
Interlaced. Celebrating our connection and accepting different cultures in our community. 
I grew interested in this project because, as an immigrant from the Philippines, I felt welcomed 
in the land of opportunity. I have been creating art ever since I got here from Manila as a 
teenager. I wanted to share the meaning of being part of the community. We are all intertwined 
somehow even though we come from different parts of the world. I named this concept 
Interlaced as I wanted to show the connections that each unique person has that makes up our 
San Mateo community.  
The experience I have with public art are the iconic Hearts sculptures I was chosen to paint by 
the San Francisco General Hospital Foundation in the beginning of 2023. I also paint on     
large-scale canvases. My paintings are also hung in private and commercial businesses.  
My sources of inspiration for this project came from my Filipino heritage. The background I used 
on this proposal is a basket weave. It represents the interwoven unity of our Filipino traditions, 
interconnecting to us to make a whole. One of the things I love the most about the Philippines is 
our love of Christmas. Christmas is a time for bringing people together to unite in joy and 
laughter, and the Philippines has the longest Christmas celebration in the world. It starts when 
the months end with “ber”, in which we start playing Christmas songs and celebrating in 
September. I chose the Christmas lantern that we call “Parol” as it symbolizes the star that 
pointed at the manger in Bethlehem. It’s colorful, bright and twinkling. The jasmine flower, we 
call Sampaguita, is the Philippines national flower. It is associated with love and dedication 
which Filipino families strive for. To Filipinos, family mealtimes are very important to us. Rice is 
something you will always see on our dinner table and is the staple food of the Philippines.        
It symbolizes abundance and fertility and represents our cultural unity. To represent the 
Philippines as a unified nation, I also wanted to paint our flag which consists of the sun and 
stars. I created an equal symbol in the center as it refers to fairness and justice for everyone.     
I colored it purple as it’s the same color as Ube (pronounced as “ooh-beh”), which is a root crop 
that looks like a sweet potato and only grows in the Philippines. 
My approach for this project is to bring Filipino culture and voices to the Bay Area. I wanted to 
introduce our food, our traditions, and our part of the world to the people, letting them and other 
Filipinos know that we are here. I’ve been seeing more and more Filipino owned restaurants 
open in the area and I’m glad to know that we are beginning to make our mark here. Any 
representation is great to our community. Slowly but surely, I believe that Filipino culture will be 
recognized as part of the complex interwoven basket that makes up the Bay Area.

MEMORIAL
Washington, DC
Thornton Tomasetti provided structural engineering for a new 
memorial that occupies a four-acre site along the tidal basin 
on the National Mall.
A crescent-shaped plaza is surrounded by curving, stone-clad retaining walls 
carved with inscriptions. Two large stones, the “mountains of despair,” form a 
gateway flanked by waterfalls. On the plaza, the “stone of hope” incorporates a 
31-foot-tall sculpture of Reverend King. Thornton Tomasetti’s engineers worked 
closely with the geotechnical consultant to overcome extremely challenging soil
conditions. The entire memorial rests upon piles driven to bedrock.
The water features posed an additional design challenge. Plans originally called for 
a remote pump-room with underground piping running to the falls. A design 
change added below-grade pump rooms beneath each of the waterfalls, with a 
175-foot tunnel providing access to both. This required the tunnel to run directly 
below the massive “mountains of despair.” Thornton Tomasetti reanalyzed and 
reconfigured the arrangement of piles supporting the stones to accommodate the
tunnel. The design of the tunnel itself was complex, requiring precise integration 
with many different components. Highly detailed and coordinated drawings 
ensured that the design intent was clear and simplified construction.
The project includes a 3,000-square-foot structure across the street from the 
memorial. The building, which features a 12-foot roof cantilever, houses a 
bookstore and ranger station with public facilities.

Transparencies in Time: 
 
 
The present visual art exhibition/installation proposal is both an interactive and multidisciplinary.  It’s a 
multiphase comprehensive exposé on Mesoamerican lore and more specifically on a pre-Columbian 
notion of time. Here, there would be three simultaneous and concurrent installations that center on 
concrete poetry. All three will utilize plastic transparency films.  In the 1st will be as titled above.  While 
the second installation is called Cyclones in the DNA2: Double Helix/Haiku Transparencies in [Space] 
Time.  In the 3rd, gallery and/or installation that will be titled Dream Images: TransparenXcies Pyramid 
Shifts: 3D public sculpture. I am interested in this opportunity to let contemporary society at large know 
that poetry is alive, and that the oral tradition still lives, though it’s thought that this ancient culture is 
dead. In synthesis this can be both an exhibition in a gallery and/or an installation of sculptures made with 
the two primary elements; plastic transparency films and acrylic/plexiglass to form shapes, for these will 
be embedded within.  Installation #1 will be two hollow cylinders on whose inside surface will be placed 
the transparencies. In the 2nd installation I have not made models yet, but the gist is to use the same 
transparencies to make the shape of the X and Y chromosomes of the DNA double helix.  In installation 
#3 the idea would be just a plexiglass screen just as Installation #2 that is approximately 8 ft by 7 ft and in 
this case, transparencies will be placed in the shape of a pyramid.  My work is rooted in the original 
peoples and 1st nations so it’s original by default, therefore my visual art speaks for itself.

Artist Statement 
 
I am a remnant of the Nahuatlacah oral tradition a tonalpouhque mexica, a commoner 
from the lowlands (i.e. Mexico) from a time and place that no longer exists. At present my poetry 
has been reified as it has been published in the UK, US, India, Spain, Australia, Argentina, 
Germany and Venezuela. I have been exhibited in different venues with a work that’s titled: 
Transparencies in Time: Cuahpohualli embedded in ethnopoetic language poetry:. I seek to 
expand on this work into a comprehensive exhibit in a gallery in 3D (i.e. three simultaneous 
exhibitions that expand into multiple levels of perception and/or dimensions). I was born in 
Celaya, Guanajuato, Mexico, where I spent my childhood in the small neighboring rural pueblo 
of Tarimoró and wherefrom my family immigrated in 1988. My inspiration(s) include 
Netzahualcoyotl, Humberto Ak’abal, Ray A. Young Bear, and James Welch. 
 
 
 
 I have a work in progress of a short-story series were I interpolate the sunstone calendar 
(i.e. the primary source of the Cauhpohualli Computo del tiempo azteca y su correlacion actual 
Anahuacayotl de Tlaxcalancingo, Puebla); to bring to life a micro-fiction project titled San 
Miguel de Tarimoro ca. 1546. It’s a micro-fiction work of short stories that delve into the 
immaterial aspects of time. It will be digitized into video with simultaneous audio in video-book 
view narration. Namely, it’s a bridge from poetry to narrative storytelling. The work is a catalog 
of the 365 days in the Tonalpohualli or count of days in hologram. My work is rooted in 
Mesoamerican lore and a pre-Columbian notion of time that’s extant in the poetics and as such 
it’s the foundation and bedrock. While in ceremony with Chololo medicine men in the Tule 
River Reservation he dreamt this written prophecy…At, present the submissions here remitted 
are merely a glimpse of a wider panorama that would be made into a broader mosaic(s) of 
installation/sculptural art to be made for indoor or outdoor exhibition.
