RECOMMENDATION:
Accept and file the basic financial statements for the San Mateo-Foster City Public Financing Authority for the fiscal years 
ended June 30, 2022 and 2021.
BACKGROUND:
The basic financial statements for the fiscal year ended June 30, 2022 and 2021 marked the fourth fiscal year in which the 
Authority had financial activity to account for. The Authority's sole purpose is to issue debt on behalf of its member 
agencies, the City of San Mateo and the City of Foster City/Estero Municipal Improvement District. The Authority ended 
the fiscal year with $990.0 million in total assets and $1.1 billion in total liabilities, which results in a net position of 
negative $105.0 million. The changes in assets, liabilities, and net position largely reflect the financing agreements 
between the Authority and the member agencies for the 2021 Wastewater Revenue Notes (2021 Notes) and Refunding 
Bonds that were closed in July 2021. The 2021 Notes were issued to take advantage of the interest rate spread of 
approximately 50 basis points (0.50%) between the Environmental Protection Agency Water Infrastructure Finance and 
Innovation Act (WIFIA) loans and the interest rates that were available from the market at the time for short term notes. 
As the Authority makes debt service payments, it will concurrently amortize the bond premiums from the outstanding 
debt, thus its net position will gradually approach $0. The Authority's operating income of $17.4 million is the net between 
$38.4 million in loan and lease interest income from the member agencies pursuant to the financing agreements and $21.0 
million in interest expense paid for the outstanding debt.

Acceptance of the basic financial statements has no impact on the Authority's budget.

AUTHORITY PROFILE  
 
 
  
 
The San Mateo – Foster City Public Financing Authority was formed through a joint exercise of powers 
agreement between the City of San Mateo (San Mateo) and the City of Foster City/Estero Municipal 
Improvement District (Foster City) to finance the capital improvements of each member agency. Both 
member agencies are located in San Mateo County and are situated south of San Francisco and north 
of San Jose. Both have a Council-Manager form of government and are full service cities that provide 
a broad range of services, including police, street construction and maintenance, sewer, parks, 
recreation, planning, and engineering. San Mateo is a charter city, while Foster City is a general law 
city. 
 
ECONOMIC CONTEXT   
 
The information presented in the financial statements is perhaps best understood when it is considered 
from a broader perspective of the specific environment in which the Authority operates. The 
Authority’s operating revenues are remitted from its member agencies, San Mateo and Foster City, 
pursuant to the respective financing agreements that ultimately reflect each member agency’s 
respective share of debt service. In July 2021, the Authority issued $334 million in wastewater revenue 
notes and $14 million in wastewater revenue refunding bonds on behalf of the City of San Mateo (City) 
and the Estero Municipal Improvement District (EMID) to provide debt service savings to its rate 
payers. The debt service for the 2019 Bonds and 2021 Wastewater Notes (2021 Notes) are backed by the 
net revenues of each member agency’s sewer enterprise funds. As such, the economic stability of the 
member agencies’ sewer enterprise has a direct impact on the Authority’s long-term financial 
sustainability. The debt service for the 2020 Bonds, which the Authority issued in the prior fiscal year 
on behalf of the City of San Mateo to finance the cost of flood control and street reconstruction, is solely 
the responsibility of the City of San Mateo and is backed by revenues held by the Trustee in any fund 
or account established under the indenture with the City. 
 
The member agencies’ service area includes the City of San Mateo, City of Foster City, and portions of 
the Town of Hillsborough, City of Belmont, Crystal Springs County Sanitation District, and some 
unincorporated areas of San Mateo County. The service area includes over 41,000 customer accounts 
and a population of approximately 150,000. The customer base is primarily residential: 93.7% for San 
Mateo and 96.3% for Foster City. Sewer rates are below median in comparison to neighboring agencies.

MAJOR INITIATIVES 
 
 
  
 
CLEAN WATER PROGRAM 
The Clean Water Program (Program) is a comprehensive 10-year capital plan totaling approximately 
$1.0 billion to fund capital improvements to the jointly-owned Wastewater Treatment Plant and the 
San Mateo-owned wastewater collection facilities. The Program will provide the capital improvements 
necessary to comply with Cease and Desist Order No. R2-2009-0020 from the San Francisco Bay 
Regional Water Quality Control Board (RWQCB) to eliminate sanitary sewer overflows that primarily 
occur during heavy rain events.

FINANCIAL HIGHLIGHTS 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
As of June 30, 2022, the Authority had $990.0 million in total assets; an increase of $539.8 
million or 119.9%, which was primarily consisted of $315.3 million in cash and cash 
equivalents, reflecting the close of $334.3 million in 2021 Wastewater Revenue Notes (2021 
Notes) and $14.3 million in 2021 Wastewater Revenue Bonds Refunding Series A (2021 
Bonds) in July 2022. The Authority’s assets also included loans receivable of $616.4 million 
reflecting the related financing agreements with the City of San Mateo and Estero 
Municipal Improvement District related to the 2021 Notes.  
 
 
Total liabilities increased $507.7 million to $1.1 billion, which primarily reflected the close 
of the 2021 Notes, which took advantage of the interest rate spread of approximately 0.5%

Note 1 – Reporting Entity 
 
San Mateo-Foster City Public Financing Authority (the “Authority”) is a joint exercise of powers authority established 
pursuant to a Joint Exercise of Powers Agreement, dated as of February 29, 2016, between the City of San Mateo (San 
Mateo) and the City of Foster City, Estero Municipal Improvement District (Foster City) under the laws of the State 
of California. The governing board consists of two members appointed by each of the member agencies. The Authority 
was organized to provide financial assistance to San Mateo and Foster City by financing the members’ capital 
improvements. 
 
The criteria used in determining the scope of the reporting entity are based on the provisions of GASB Cod. Sec. 2100, 
“Defining the Financial Reporting Entity”. The Authority is the primary government unit. Component units are those 
entities which are financially accountable to the primary government, either because the Authority appoints a voting 
majority of the component unit's board, or because the component unit will provide a financial benefit or impose a 
financial burden on the Authority. The Authority has no component units. 
 
The accounts and records of the Authority are maintained by the City of San Mateo. 
 
 
Note 2 – Summary of Significant Accounting Policies 
 
The basic financial statements of the Authority have been prepared in conformity with accounting principles generally 
accepted in the United States of America (U.S. GAAP). The Governmental Accounting Standards Board (GASB) is 
the accepted standard setting body for governmental accounting financial reporting purposes. The more significant of 
the Authority’s accounting policies are described below: 
 
A. Financial Statements 
 
The financial statements (i.e., the statement of net position, the statement of revenues, expenses, and changes in 
net position, and statement of cash flows) report information on all of the activities of the Authority.  The statement 
of revenues, expenses, and changes in net position demonstrates the degree to which the direct expenses of a given 
function or segment are offset by program revenues. Direct expenses are those that are clearly identifiable with a 
specific function or segment. 
 
B. Measurement Focus, Basis of Accounting, and Financial Statements Presentation 
 
The financial statements are accounted for on a flow of economic resources measurement focus, using the accrual 
basis of accounting. Under this method, all assets, deferred outflows and inflows of resources, and liabilities 
associated with operations are included on the statement of net position, revenues are recorded as earned and 
expenses are recorded at the time liabilities are incurred. 
 
In accordance with GASB Statement No. 63, Financial Reporting of Deferred Outflows of Resources, Deferred 
Inflows of Resources, and Net Position, the Statement of Net Position reports separate sections for Deferred 
Outflows of Resources, and Deferred Inflows of Resources, when applicable. 
 
 
Deferred Outflows of Resources represent outflows of resources (consumption of net position) that apply to
 
future periods and that, therefore, will not be recognized as an expense until that time.

E. Disclosures Relating Custodial Credit Risk 
 
Custodial credit risk for deposits is the risk that, in the event of the failure of a depository financial institution, the 
Authority will not be able to recover its deposits or will not be able to recover collateral securities that are in the 
possession of an outside party. The custodial credit risk for investments is the risk that, in the event of the failure 
of the counter-party (e.g., broker-dealer) the Authority will not be able to recover the value of its investment or 
collateral securities that are in the possession of another party. The California Government Code and the Authority's 
investment policy do not contain legal or policy requirements that would limit the exposure to custodial credit risk 
for deposits or investments, other than the following provision for deposits. The California Government Code 
requires that a financial institution secure deposits made by state or local governmental units by pledging securities 
in an undivided collateral pool held by a depository regulatory under state law (unless so waived by the 
governmental unit). The market value of the pledged securities in the collateral pool must equal at least 110% of 
the total amount deposited by the public agencies. California law also allows financial institutions to secure 
Authority deposits by pledging first trust deed mortgage notes having a value of 150% of the secured public 
deposits. 
 
As of June 30, 2022 and 2021, none of the Authority's deposits with financial institutions were in excess of federal 
depository insurance limits. As of June 30, 2022 and 2021, no Authority investments were held by the same broker- 
dealer (counterparty) that was used by the Authority to buy the securities.
