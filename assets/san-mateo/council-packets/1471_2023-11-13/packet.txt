RESOLUTION NO. 27 (2022) 
PARKS, OPEN SPACE LANDS AND PUBLIC FACILITY NAMING POLICY
WHEREAS, the existing Policy for Naming City-Owned Park and Open Space Lands, Public Facilities and
Places dates from 1979 ; and
WHEREAS, staff determined that the policy was outdated and needed better criteria to assist staff, the
Park and Recreation Commission and the City Council when evaluating the merit of facility naming requests;           
and
WHEREAS,  the Park and Recreation Commission provided general policy direction and comments before
approving the proposed updated policy at their meeting on February 2, 2022 by a vote of 5-0 ; and
WHEREAS, the Park and Recreation Commission is recommending that the City Council approve the new
policy in order to provide better guidelines for staff, the Commission and the City Council when evaluating
facility naming requests.  
NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED THAT THE CITY COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF SAN MATEO, CALIFORNIA,  
hereby:  
1. 
Rescinds Resolution No. 49 (1979) Establishing Policy and Procedures for Naming City-Owned Park
and Open Space Lands, Public Facilities and Places; and
2. 
Approves the Parks, Open Space Lands and Public Facility Naming Policy in substantially the formed
attached to this Resolution.

CITY OF SAN MATEO
PARKS, OPEN SPACE LANDS AND PUBLIC FACILITY NAMING POLICY
Section I:  Purpose and Application
A. Purpose: To provide guidelines and procedures for the naming of City Parks, Open Space
lands and public facilities and amenities within parks.  
B. Application: This policy has been created to guide staff, the Park and Recreation
Commission and the City Council in taking action concerning requests to name City parks, 
open space lands and public facilities and amenities within parks.  This process does not
apply to the naming of streets or other non-park civic facilities.  
C. The City Council shall have the final authority to approve names of parks, open space lands
and public facilities and amenities within parks after review and recommendation by the
Park and Recreation Commission and Department of Parks and Recreation staff.   
Section II: Definitions
A. Parks: Real property owned and managed by the City used for public recreation purposes, 
whether developed or undeveloped, including lands identified as open space.  
B. Public Facilities and Amenities: City owned facilities/ amenities that are located within a park
or within part of a facility.  Examples include, but are not limited to, athletic/ ball fields, 
recreation buildings, picnic areas, trails, sports courts, playgrounds, gardens and meeting
rooms within a facility.   
Section III: Policy
A. Parks: 
1)  Each park and open space shall be named after its geographical location or by its
significant or unusual natural, cultural, geographical, historical or environmental
feature.  Names shall not be duplicated and shall be compatible with the area in
which the park and open space is located.  
2) Naming parks after individuals or organizations may be considered under special
circumstances such as a significant donation of land to the City and Department of
Parks and Recreation or in recognition of an individual who contributed
extraordinary service to the City of San Mateo.  
3) Renaming of parks where existing names have common community usage and
acceptance is strongly discouraged and will be considered only under rare
circumstances.

B. Public Facilities and Amenities:  
1) Public facilities and amenities within parks may be named after selected individuals
or organizations in their honor.  Naming is a special recognition that is considered
for persons and organizations deserving exceptional recognition for their unique, 
substantial or outstanding contribution to the Department of Parks and Recreation
or to a specific park or amenity.  Criteria that will be considered include, but are not
limited to: donation of land or significant financial gifts to improve a facility; a record
of meaningful volunteer service to the Department of Parks and Recreation or to a
facility; and substantial and noteworthy public service as a community member. 
2) Satisfying one or more of the criteria listed above does not assure approval of any
naming recommendation.  
3) The preference is for an individual to be deceased, but this is not a requirement.  
4) In the case where an existing named facility is demolished or decommissioned, the
City Council may choose to discontinue the use of the name, re-use the name for a
replacement facility or re-assign the name to another similar facility.  
Section IV: Process for Naming Parks and Public Facilities and Amenities
A.  Any member of the public may submit a written request for naming a Park or Public Facility
or Amenity to the Director of Parks and Recreation for consideration by the Park and
Recreation Commission and City Council. Nominators should provide supporting
background and justification for one or more of the criteria outlined above in Section B.1. 
The Director will conduct any research necessary to make an informed recommendation to
the Park and Recreation Commission. The Commission will review the proposed naming
recommendation, conduct a public hearing and make a recommendation to the City Council
to either support or deny the request
The City Council will review the proposed recommendation, conduct a public hearing and
vote whether to approve or deny the naming.  Nothing in the policy shall preclude either
the Park and Recreation Commission or City Council from initiating a naming request on
their own.     
B. Upon approval by the City Council, the site shall officially be designated as such on all maps
and records.  
C. A dedication plaque consistent with the existing setting and landscape shall be placed at
each named facility and where possible, include information on the contributions of the
individual.  Responsibility for the purchase and installation of any dedication plaque shall be
borne by the requesting party unless otherwise negotiated with the Department of Parks
and Recreation.  On delivery to the City, the dedication plaque shall become the property of
the City. The location, size and type of recognition objects shall be approved by the
Department of Parks and Recreation.

Section V: Naming Rights as Part of a Fundraising Campaign
A. When undertaking major park or facility renovations and/ or new construction, the
Department of Parks and Recreation may choose to engage in fundraising campaigns in
order to raise community awareness and provide financial assistance to the City.  This policy
allows for Naming Rights to fund amenities within a park, such as buildings, playgrounds, 
athletic/ ballfields, picnic areas, etc.  Naming Right opportunities and levels of sponsorship
shall be identified in advance and publicized as part of the fundraising campaign.  
Recognition should be commensurate with said donations or funding.   
An agreement between the City and major donors shall be negotiated which outlines the
terms of the donation, including the length of time the Naming Right shall be in effect.  
B. Naming Rights shall be aligned with the mission and values of the Department of Parks and
Recreation and the City of San Mateo and shall avoid products, services or affiliations that
promote political or religious organizations,  alcohol, tobacco and cannabis products, or are
otherwise considered derogatory or offensive by community standards. The City Council
shall have the right to approve or deny any proposed facility name.  
Section VI: Non-Facility Naming Opportunities
As an alternative to the facility naming opportunities described above, the City may choose
to recognize individuals, either living or deceased or organizations by the placement of
objects such as public benches, flagpoles, rocks, trees, or other features within a park or
facility.  The approval, placement and identification of these dedications will be at the
discretion of the Parks and Recreation Department staff.  A dedication plaque, appropriate
to the setting and landscape will be installed.       
Section VII: Miscellaneous
A. No park, open space or facility shall be named after a seated, elected or appointed official.  
B. This policy encourages naming of parks and facilities in a manner which reflects the City’s
ethnic and cultural diversity and elicit a positive image for all residents.

Why Age Friendly?
-
The population of the United States is rapidly aging. By 2030, 1 of every 5 people in the 
U.S. will be 65 or older. By 2034, the number of adults older than 65 will be greater than 
the number of children under 18.
-
Aging well requires a supportive environment – not just about individuals
-
Need to address barriers across all dimensions of social and physical environment 
-
Age-friendliness is about including everyone and enabling them to take part regardless of 
age

Age-Friendly Communities 
-
The AARP Network of Age-Friendly States and Communities supports the work of 
local, regional and state governments as they prepare for the nation’s changing 
demographics.
-
Established in 2012, the network includes towns, cities, counties and states that have 
made a commitment to being more livable for people of all ages, and especially older 
adults.
-
The program’s framework equips local leaders and residents with resources for 
assessing the needs of older adults related to housing and transportation options, 
access to key services, and opportunities to participate in community activities. Once 
identified, those needs can be incorporated into an action plan.
