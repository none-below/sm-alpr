BACKGROUND:
The 2020 Climate Action Plan (CAP) was adopted in April 2020 and includes strategies and measures to reduce greenhouse 
gas (GHG) emissions. The 2020 CAP contains implementation measures in the following categories: building electrification, 
renewable energy, energy efficiency, municipal energy efficiency, clean transportation fuels, sustainable transportation, 
solid waste, water and wastewater, and off-road equipment. This report provides an overview of the CAP Technical 
Update that is underway and key highlights from CAP implementation over the 2022 calendar year.
CAP Technical Update 
In addition to laying out a suite of strategies and measures to reach the City’s climate goals, the CAP was developed to 
meet a series of criteria to serve as a qualified GHG reduction strategy which allows new development projects that are 
consistent with the CAP to streamline their environmental review. Although the CAP was recently updated and adopted, 
the City’s 2040 General Plan Update and new State laws, including AB 1279, necessitate a partial and focused technical 
update to the CAP so that the CAP will maintain consistency with the 2040 General Plan when adopted and can continue 
to serve as a qualified GHG reduction strategy. 
The 2020 CAP proposes to reduce emissions based on per-capita targets in the 2017 California Air Resources Board (CARB) 
Climate Change Scoping Plan for the years 2030 and 2050. The updated 2022 CARB Scoping Plan no longer recommends 
the use of per-capita targets for local jurisdictions and requires that 2045 be used as a threshold year, as opposed to 2050. 
Because the 2040 General Plan Update and EIR will reflect the new state laws and Bay Area Air Quality Management 
District (BAAQMD) Guidance, the 2040 General Plan, once adopted, would no longer align with the CAP. It is important for 
the CAP to be consistent with the 2040 General Plan and state reduction targets because the CAP serves as a qualified GHG 
reduction strategy.
On February 21, 2023, City Council approved Agreement Amendment No. 4 to amend the scope of work with PlaceWorks 
Inc. to perform a partial and focused technical update of the CAP to ensure that it maintains consistency with the 2040 
General Plan. This six-month project is currently underway. 
The CAP technical update will focus on updating quantitative data points, including providing a 2019 GHG inventory and 
updating GHG reduction targets and forecasts, to align with state laws and regional guidance for the new 2045 horizon 
year. This scope of work will not provide a comprehensive review and update of the CAP’s GHG reduction measures. Staff

2019 GHG Inventory
As part of the CAP Technical update, PlaceWorks completed a 2019 GHG inventory. This quantitative analysis is typically 
done every five years as it is a time-intensive process to gather the data, adjust the data based on current CAP guidance 
and methodologies, and implement the appropriate data quality assurances. The City opted for a 2019 GHG inventory as 
opposed to a 2020 GHG inventory so that the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic and shelter-in-place orders would not 
skew the data. The 2019 GHG inventory shows that the City reduced emissions 22% below the 2005 baseline, 
demonstrating that the City continues to exceed the 2020 goal to reduce emissions 15% below the 2005 baseline. Table 1 
summarizes the community-wide GHG emissions by sector and highlights the change in emissions from 2005 to 2019.
There were a few adjustments made to the 2005 baseline and annual inventories to reflect updated methodologies and 
thus, this inventory data is slightly different than what was included in the 2020 CAP. The inventories now include the 
transmission and distribution losses for electricity and natural gas use in the built environment, resulting to slight changes 
to the commercial/industrial built environment and residential built environment emissions. PlaceWorks also revised the 
off-road equipment emissions using the updated EMFAC model from the CARB and updated EPA emission factors to maintain 
consistency with previous inventory methodologies. Additionally, the City of San Mateo has little or no control over the day-
to-day operations of larger industrial or institutional facilities, such as point sources. BAAQMD draft guidance on GHG 
reduction efforts recommends that the CAP should disclose these emissions, but that they can be excluded from the 
inventory for the purposes of GHG target setting and development of GHG reduction protocols. For these reasons, point 
sources are no longer included in the inventories and are instead provided as an informational item. Finally, based on new 
guidance, the PlaceWorks team is working on calculating carbon sequestration data and emissions from greenfield 
development to include in the inventories. PlaceWorks anticipates this would account for a small number of emissions and 
not dramatically impact the inventories. This data is not yet available.

GHG emissions changes from 2005 to 2019 include the following:
•
The largest reduction from 2005 to 2019 occurred in the commercial/industrial built environment sector, with 
emissions decreasing by 50%. There was also a significant reduction of 30% in emissions from the residential built 
environment sector. Emissions from the built environment have decreased since the baseline year due to a higher 
proportion of energy coming from renewable sources. Peninsula Clean Energy was introduced in 2017 and now 
provides a significant portion of San Mateo’s electricity. PG&E has also increased the portion of clean energy sources 
in its portfolio in recent years. 
•
Waste emissions have decreased year over year due to the closure of the 3rd Ave landfill in 2000. 
•
While vehicle miles traveled (VMT) has increased year over year, emissions from on-road transportation have 
decreased slightly (3%), likely due to a higher proportion of low- and zero-emission vehicles. 
•
Emissions from the water and wastewater sector decreased 34% from 2005 levels, which is likely due to efficiency 
upgrades at the San Mateo Water Quality Control Plant. 
•
The rail sector saw a slight increase, with emissions increasing by 2%, which is driven by an increase in Caltrain 
ridership. 
•
Emissions from point sources increased 145% from 2005 to 2019, potentially as a result of changes in business 
operations or how data are tracked. These are emission sources that are often outside the City’s jurisdiction. 
PlaceWorks is now working on preparing forecasts of future GHG emissions for the years 2030, 2040 and 2045 and updating 
the GHG reduction benefits from existing and planned state activities through 2045. An update of this effort will be provided 
at a future Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission meeting.

Building electrification: In November 2022, the City Council adopted reach codes requiring that the construction 
of all new buildings be all-electric, expanding the City’s previous requirement requiring all-electric construction of

new residential and office buildings. In addition, the City Council adopted reach codes impacting residential 
building upgrades to encourage electric-readiness and require electric equipment. The City of San Mateo is one of 
a handful of cities with electrification requirements for existing residential buildings. In recent news, on March 15, 
2023, the Bay Area Air Quality Management District (BAAQMD) adopted rules to phase out the sale and 
installation of gas water heaters by 2027 and gas furnaces by 2029. Electric panel upgrades are often identified as 
a barrier for replacing gas equipment with electric. The City’s reach code that requires breaker space and panel 
capacity for future electric equipment will help prepare homeowners for future electric equipment requirements.
•
Induction Cooktop Loaner Program: In June 2022, the City launched the Induction Cooktop Loaner Program that 
offers hands-on experience with a portable induction cooktop. Residents can borrow an induction cooktop and 
cookware for two weeks. 15 residents borrowed the induction cooktop kit in 2022. 
•
Traffic Signal Optimization: In 2022, the City implemented traffic signal optimizations on the Fashion Island Blvd 
corridor from Delaware Street to Norfolk Street, and on a short segment of Humboldt Street from 3rd Avenue to 
4th Avenue. Traffic signal optimizations help reduce vehicle emissions related to vehicle idling.
•
Complete Streets Plan: The City received $490,882 in grant funding for the development of a citywide Complete 
Streets Plan. The outcome will be an actionable plan with a list of prioritized multimodal infrastructure projects 
that strive to provide safe, reliable, and accessible travel options for all modes. The City procured a consultant to 
assist with developing the Plan and anticipates community engagement beginning in Spring 2023.
•
Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance: The City Council adopted the “Disposable Food Service Ware 
Ordinance” modeled after the County of San Mateo’s ordinance. The ordinance requires food facilities distribute 
disposable items that are fiber-based and compostable. The ordinance went into effect in October 2022. In the 
months leading up to October 2022, the County of San Mateo Office of Sustainability spearheaded outreach and 
engagement with San Mateo food facilities. On August 16, 2022, the County hosted a workshop for food facilities 
to showcase compliant food ware and meet food ware vendors. 332 people attended the event. The County’s 
consultant also contacted 553 businesses in the City through phone calls, emails and in-person visits and made 
direct contact with decision makers at 62% of those businesses to provide information. This included outreach and 
education in Spanish and Chinese where appropriate. 
•
OKAPI Reusables: The City of San Mateo is partnering with OKAPI Reusables, thanks to leadership and funding 
from the County of San Mateo Office of Sustainability. OKAPI is a simple borrow and return cup service featuring 
trendy insulated stainless-steel cups for hot drinks and glass tumblers for iced drinks. 3 Bees Coffee (224 E. 3rd 
Avenue), Urban Ritual (140 B Street), Meet Fresh (227 S. B Street) and Heere Tea (253 S. B Street) are the first 
cafes to join the OKAPI Reusables network. The program launched in December 2022. 
•
Cal Water’s Climate Efforts: Cal Water completed a Climate Change Risk Assessment & Adaptation Framework in 
December 2021. The report identifies and prioritizes climate-driven risks to Cal Water facilities, operations, and 
water supply portfolio. It also projects and assesses changes to water supply and demand. This will allow Cal Water 
to more effectively develop adaptation strategies that reduce the impact of climate change on Cal Water’s 
operations, prioritize infrastructure investments that increase resiliency, and monitor and address risks to 
disadvantaged and vulnerable communities. 
•
Rain barrel rebates: The City had a record number of 82 rain barrel rebates distributed in 2022.

Subject: Comments on Agenda Item 2 - Climate Action Plan Update

Dear Commissioners,

These comments are submitted on behalf of the 180+ members of the San Mateo Climate Action Team
(SMCAT) regarding Item 2 on the April 12, 2023 San Mateo Sustainability and Infrastructure meeting agenda:
“Climate Action Plan - Annual Progress Report.”

We thank the City for its past climate leadership and efforts to implement its Climate Action Plan (CAP).

As you know, the CAP was adopted in 2020, and contains a number of measures for meeting certain targets
for reducing greenhouse gas emissions (GHGs). Since the time that the current CAP was adopted, the
IPCC has made clear that the climate crisis has become dire. Indeed, we have only 7 years left to emit
GHGs at the current rate before we exceed 1.5 degrees celsius of warming, a dangerous level that begins to
breach planetary boundaries. It is thus incumbent upon every level of government, including our City, to do
everything possible to address the climate crisis by swiftly ending the use of fossil fuels.

We ask that the City of San Mateo do its part to combat the climate crisis by taking bold action to meet
its GHG emissions targets for 2030 and the new threshold year of 2045. We contend that, in order for
the CAP to be an effective tool for meeting these targets, the City must:

» Analyze actual progress towards the 2030 and 2045 GHG emissions targets;

» Analyze the impact of the new BAAQMD tules restricting the sale of: (i) NOx-emitting natural gas water
heaters beginning in 2027, (ii) NOx-emitting furnaces beginning in 2029, and (iii) large commercial
water heaters beginning in 2031;

» Analyze the impact of the City’s newly adopted Reach Codes; and

» Create a roadmap that details how the City will achieve its GHG emissions targets, which includes
specific actions and specific dates.

Since the above steps were not taken in connection with the current CAP update, we request that these
steps be completed in connection with the development of the 2040 General Plan, and in connection
with each subsequent year’s CAP update.

Finally, we note that electrification of existing buildings is one of the most impactful reduction measures set
forth in the current CAP. The City of San Mateo has begun to make progress on this measure with its passage
of a new Reach Code last December requiring certain electric retrofits of existing buildings, and engaging a

consultant to complete a decarbonization study. We applaud the City’s building decarbonization efforts, and
ask that the City continue those efforts at the pace necessary to address the climate crisis.

Thank you for your consideration.
Sincerely,

Robert Whitehair & Michelle Hudson, on behalf of
The San Mateo Climate Action Team
