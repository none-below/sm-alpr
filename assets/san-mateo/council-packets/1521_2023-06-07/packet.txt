SUBJECT:
Youth Commissioners to the Park and Recreation Commission – Ordinance Introduction
RECOMMENDATION:
Introduce an ordinance to amend San Mateo Municipal Code Chapter 2.27 “Park and Recreation Commission” to provide 
for Council appointment of two non-voting youth advisory members. 
BACKGROUND:
A City Council objective for fiscal year 2022-23 is to “research practices in other jurisdictions that have youth 
representation on Boards and Commissions; to analyze best practices; develop policy options for implementing enhanced 
youth participation in the City’s decision-making process.”
The City Charter contains several requirements for members of City boards and commissions created by ordinance or the 
Charter.  
Members are appointed by the Council and while serving must possess the same qualifications under the Charter for 
election or appointment to the Council.  Charter §6.05.  Councilmembers must be a qualified elector and resident of the 
City and must have continuously lived in the City for 30 days immediately preceding the appointment.  Charter §2.01(b).   
Qualified electors are U.S. citizens at least 18 years of age, under state law.  Elec. §321.  As such, applicants to boards and 
commissions must be at least 18 years old. 
Charter section 6.05 contains a number of additional requirements.  For example, the membership of a particular board or 
commission cannot exceed the number of Councilmembers, unless the ordinance establishing the additional number is 
approved by the voters or a unanimous vote of the Council.  Also, members of boards and commissions are appointed for 
terms of four years, except to allow for staggered terms of the initial members of a body.  Finally, board and commission 
members are limited to serving two consecutive four-year terms, except that partial terms do not count towards the term 
limit.
The Park and Recreation Commission consists of five (5) commissioners appointed by the Council. Each member of the 
Park and Recreation Commission serves a term at the pleasure of the Council of four years and until the appointment and 
qualification of a successor. 
On October 3, 2023, staff presented a practice adopted by the City of Belmont to increase youth involvement in Boards 
and Commissions and received direction from Council to follow the City of Belmont model by adding two youth non-voting 
members to the Park and Recreation Commission. 
The two non-voting youth advisory members must be at least 13 years old, and no more than 17 years old at the time of

appointment (“youth commissioners”).  They must present written documentation of parental or guardian consent to be 
considered for appointment, and if appointed would not count towards a quorum.  Like other members of the 
commission, youth advisory members would serve at the pleasure of the Council.  
Under this proposal, youth commissioners would be appointed for one year but could be reappointed for an additional 
one-year term.  The youth commissioners would not be “members” for purposes of the Charter but would instead serve as 
non-voting advisory members.  Thus, a unanimous Council vote is not required to introduce and adopt the ordinance, and 
youth commissioners could serve for less than a four-year term.  
The City currently offers opportunities for youth engagement in local government through the Youth Activities Council 
(YAC), which seeks to provide a voice for youth in the community and offers opportunities for civic engagement and youth 
advocacy. YAC members are required to be a resident of the City of San Mateo or student attending a high school in San 
Mateo; entering 9th through 12th grade by the beginning of each fall term; and willing to attend monthly meetings and 
events.

ORDINANCE NO. 2023-1
Amending San Mateo Municipal Code Chapter 2.27 to amend the organization of the Park and Recreation
Commission to include two non-voting youth advisory members ( youth commissioners). 
WHEREAS, San Mateo Municipal Code Chapter 2.27 establishes that the San Mateo Park and Recreation
Commission shall consist of five members appointed by the City Council; and
WHEREAS, the City Council Strategic Plan for fiscal year 2022-23 included exploring the expanding of
youth participation and representation on Boards and Commissions; and
WHEREAS, on October 3, 2022, the City Council provided direction during a special meeting to add non-
voting youth members to the Park and Recreation Commission.  
NOW, THEREFORE, THE COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF SAN MATEO ORDAINS AS FOLLOWS: 
Section 1. Section 2.27.010 and Section 2.27.020 of the San Mateo Municipal Code are amended as follows: 
Chapter 2.27
PARK AND RECREATION COMMISSION
2.27.010
ORGANIZATION. 
A park and recreation commission shall continue as heretofore established in and for the City, to be
known and designated as the " San Mateo park and recreation commission." It shall consist of five members
appointed by the Council, and two non-voting youth advisory members who must be at least 13 years old, and
no more than 17 years old at the time of appointment (“ Youth Commissioners”). Youth Commissioners must
present written documentation of parental or guardian consent to be considered for appointment. Youth
commissioners shall serve only in an advisory capacity and shall not count towards a quorum of the commission.   
Each member of the commission shall serve at the pleasure of the Council for a term of four years and
until the appointment and qualification of a successor.  Each Youth Commissioner shall serve at the pleasure of
the Council for a term of one year and until the appointment and qualification of a successor. Youth
Commissioners may be reappointed for one additional one-year term. 
2.27.020
MEETINGS. 
The commission shall meet monthly at a time and place to be fixed by it, shall elect a chair and vice-
chair, and may adopt rules for its proceedings. It may hold special meetings upon call of the chair or two
members upon written notice as required by the public meeting law. 
Section 2. Environmental Determination. In accordance with California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) 
Guidelines, section 15378(b)(5).), this action is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or
administrative activity that will not result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment.

SUBJECT:
Bay Meadows Park Name Change – Recommendation
RECOMMENDATION:
Recommend to the City Council to adopt a resolution changing the name of Bay Meadows Park to Stephen Clark Park.
BACKGROUND:
Reserve Officer Stephen Evans Clark served as a Volunteer Reserve Officer with the San Mateo Police Department for nine 
years. Officer Clark’s full-time employment was with the City of San Mateo as a Lead Sweeper, and he had worked for the 
City a total of fifteen years at the time of his death. Officer Clark and his wife lived in an apartment at the City’s 
corporation yard located at 1949 Pacific Boulevard. 
On August 19, 1956, Officer Clark was struck and killed by a stock car while on duty at the Bay Meadows Racetrack (now 
Bay Meadows Park), while attempting to render aid following a crash on the track. Officer Clark was preparing to leave the 
track to direct traffic at the intersection of El Camino Real and Hillsdale Boulevard when two race cars collided and crashed 
through the track fence at the south turn. A mass of spectators was attempting to reach the crash scene. Officer Clark 
called out to a fellow officer, “You take the outside fence to keep those people away from the track, and I’ll take the 
inside.” Those were Officer Clark’s last words. As he attempted to cross the track to reach the inside fence, he was struck 
by one of the stock cars. He was transported to Mills Memorial Hospital for medical treatment and was pronounced dead. 
San Mateo City Manager Arthur Sullivan described Officer Clark as an outstanding employee who had devoted much of his 
off-duty hours to service in the community. He was a member of the San Mateo City Employees Association and the 
Peninsula Chapter of the American Motorcycle Club. Officer Clark was buried with full police honors at the Alta Mesa 
Memorial Park in Palo Alto. He was survived by his wife, Lillian, and his brother. 
The One Hundred Club is dedicated to supporting the Peace Officers of San Mateo County by providing immediate 
financial assistance to the families of law enforcement officers killed in the line of duty, granting scholarships to their 
children, and funding instruction that will better prepare our officers to protect the communities they serve and protect 
themselves from harm’s way in the performance of their duties. Approximately three years ago, based on a member’s 
research, The One Hundred Club of San Mateo County notified the San Mateo Police Department of the circumstances 
surrounding Officer Clark’s death. Prior to this discovery, Officer Clark’s ultimate sacrifice in the line of duty had gone 
unrecognized. The One Hundred Club has successfully added Officer Clark’s name to the San Mateo County Peace Officers 
Memorial and the National Registry of Officers Killed in the Line of Duty. 
The One Hundred Club has submitted a request that the City consider naming the park where he died in his memory. Staff 
recommends that the Park and Recreation Commission consider this request, per the City of San Mateo Parks, Open Space

CITY OF SAN MATEO
PARKS, OPEN SPACE LANDS AND PUBLIC FACILITY NAMING POLICY
Section I:  Purpose and Application
A. Purpose: To provide guidelines and procedures for the naming of City Parks, Open Space
lands and public facilities and amenities within parks.  
B. Application: This policy has been created to guide staff, the Park and Recreation
Commission and the City Council in taking action concerning requests to name City parks, 
open space lands and public facilities and amenities within parks.  This process does not
apply to the naming of streets or other non-park civic facilities.  
C. The City Council shall have the final authority to approve names of parks, open space lands
and public facilities and amenities within parks after review and recommendation by the
Park and Recreation Commission and Department of Parks and Recreation staff.   
Section II: Definitions
A. Parks: Real property owned and managed by the City used for public recreation purposes, 
whether developed or undeveloped, including lands identified as open space.  
B. Public Facilities and Amenities: City owned facilities/ amenities that are located within a park
or within part of a facility.  Examples include, but are not limited to, athletic/ ball fields, 
recreation buildings, picnic areas, trails, sports courts, playgrounds, gardens and meeting
rooms within a facility.   
Section III: Policy
A. Parks: 
1)  Each park and open space shall be named after its geographical location or by its
significant or unusual natural, cultural, geographical, historical or environmental
feature.  Names shall not be duplicated and shall be compatible with the area in
which the park and open space is located.  
2) Naming parks after individuals or organizations may be considered under special
circumstances such as a significant donation of land to the City and Department of
Parks and Recreation or in recognition of an individual who contributed
extraordinary service to the City of San Mateo.  
3) Renaming of parks where existing names have common community usage and
acceptance is strongly discouraged and will be considered only under rare
circumstances.

B. Public Facilities and Amenities:  
1) Public facilities and amenities within parks may be named after selected individuals
or organizations in their honor.  Naming is a special recognition that is considered
for persons and organizations deserving exceptional recognition for their unique, 
substantial or outstanding contribution to the Department of Parks and Recreation
or to a specific park or amenity.  Criteria that will be considered include, but are not
limited to: donation of land or significant financial gifts to improve a facility; a record
of meaningful volunteer service to the Department of Parks and Recreation or to a
facility; and substantial and noteworthy public service as a community member. 
2) Satisfying one or more of the criteria listed above does not assure approval of any
naming recommendation.  
3) The preference is for an individual to be deceased, but this is not a requirement.  
4) In the case where an existing named facility is demolished or decommissioned, the
City Council may choose to discontinue the use of the name, re-use the name for a
replacement facility or re-assign the name to another similar facility.  
Section IV: Process for Naming Parks and Public Facilities and Amenities
A.  Any member of the public may submit a written request for naming a Park or Public Facility
or Amenity to the Director of Parks and Recreation for consideration by the Park and
Recreation Commission and City Council. Nominators should provide supporting
background and justification for one or more of the criteria outlined above in Section B.1. 
The Director will conduct any research necessary to make an informed recommendation to
the Park and Recreation Commission. The Commission will review the proposed naming
recommendation, conduct a public hearing and make a recommendation to the City Council
to either support or deny the request
The City Council will review the proposed recommendation, conduct a public hearing and
vote whether to approve or deny the naming.  Nothing in the policy shall preclude either
the Park and Recreation Commission or City Council from initiating a naming request on
their own.     
B. Upon approval by the City Council, the site shall officially be designated as such on all maps
and records.  
C. A dedication plaque consistent with the existing setting and landscape shall be placed at
each named facility and where possible, include information on the contributions of the
individual.  Responsibility for the purchase and installation of any dedication plaque shall be
borne by the requesting party unless otherwise negotiated with the Department of Parks
and Recreation.  On delivery to the City, the dedication plaque shall become the property of
the City. The location, size and type of recognition objects shall be approved by the
Department of Parks and Recreation.

shall have the right to approve or deny any proposed facility name.  
Section VI: Non-Facility Naming Opportunities
As an alternative to the facility naming opportunities described above, the City may choose
to recognize individuals, either living or deceased or organizations by the placement of
objects such as public benches, flagpoles, rocks, trees, or other features within a park or
facility.  The approval, placement and identification of these dedications will be at the
discretion of the Parks and Recreation Department staff.  A dedication plaque, appropriate
to the setting and landscape will be installed.       
Section VII: Miscellaneous
A. No park, open space or facility shall be named after a seated, elected or appointed official.  
B. This policy encourages naming of parks and facilities in a manner which reflects the City’s
ethnic and cultural diversity and elicit a positive image for all residents.
