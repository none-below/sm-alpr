California State Government Funding Cuts
The City Librarian will provide information on the impact of proposed state governments cuts in support for
valuable programs introduced at the library in recent years. Support for programs such as the California State
Parks Pass Program, which provides free passes for checkout to library patrons, is scheduled to be excluded from
the next state budget in an effort to address deficit. Financial support for public library programs represents a
tiny fraction of the state budget. City Librarian James Moore will inform the Library Board and consider potential
service impacts as well as weigh potential library and community responses to the impending cuts.

A. Request for an Official Thank You to Dedicated Library Volunteer for Completed 
Inventory of Robert Loomis Art Collection: San Mateo Public Library (SMPL) 
Librarian II Paul Vaughn provided the Board with information about the Robert 
Loomis Historical Paintings and Line Drawings of the City of San Mateo.  In 
1981, the Library Board of Trustees acquired a group of oil paintings and line 
drawings of scenes in San Mateo by local artist Robert Loomis.  The agreement 
made between the Board and the donors included that the paintings and 
drawings would remain at the Library in perpetuity for future San Mateans to 
see this historical asset, and the paintings would be on exhibit in the Library or 
City Hall so that the public may view the collection.  SMPL loaned many of the 
paintings out to other City of San Mateo departments for display.  Mr. Vaughn 
has been working on a project to get all the paintings returned to the Library.  A 
dedicated Library volunteer and retired art teacher assisted in the project by 
completing an inventory of the collection.  Bob, the volunteer, went above and 
beyond and provided a historical overview of the buildings and areas captured 
in all of the drawings including dates the buildings were constructed, 
neighborhood details, etc.  Mr. Vaughn requested that the Board write an 
official thank you letter to this dedicated volunteer and the Board agreed to do 
so.

7. REPORTS AND ANNOUNCEMENTS 
 
A.  City Librarian James Moore: 
• 
There is nothing new to report on the Marina Library foundation repair 
project. 
• 
Mr. Moore attended the The Right To Read documentary at the Fox Theater 
in Redwood City. The documentary follows a first-grade teacher in Oakland 
who was given a whole language learning reading curriculum that did not 
work.  Without the district’s approval, she incorporated a curriculum based 
on the science of reading and received promising results.  
• 
The study session for the Draft Art Exhibit Policy went well.  Mr. Moore 
thanked the Board for their questions and suggestions that helped him to 
anticipate the questions that would come from the City Council.  Next, the 
City Manager will consider the Council’s suggestions and incorporate the 
appropriate ones in the new citywide policy that will be adopted.  This policy 
will not be one that needs the Council’s approval.   
• 
For the Library’s strategic planning process, the Stanford Alumni Consulting 
Team (ACT) will not be accepting applications for consulting projects until 
June, 2024.  In the alternative, ACT does offer two-hour online “springboard 
sessions” in which Stanford Graduate School of Business alumni generate 
ideas to aid nonprofits and civic entities with a specific strategic question.  
Each virtual brainstorming session includes four to eight GSB alumni 
volunteers that will help an organization increase its impact and solve a 
focused problem.  Applications will be accepted starting December 6.  
• 
The African American Library Advisory Committee (AALAC) in partnership 
with Library staff scheduled Domingo and Friends in concert in the Oak 
Room at the Main Library on Saturday, November 18.  Domingo Balinton, 
who learned his craft during the Fillmore’s “Harlem of the West” Era, 
performed R&B, Blues and Soul during this special evening event.  The 
AALAC hopes to be able to schedule a future event with Lavar Burton.  
• 
On Saturday, December 2, there will be an event in the Oak Room with 
pianist and producer Ronny Michael Greenberg.  Piano Roulette will begin 
with a selection of piano works by emiment composers such as Liszt, 
Rachmaninoff, Debussy, and Chopin.  Then it will shift to the Wheel of 
Fortune.  Members of the audience will be chosen at random and invited to

The Project is no longer aiming to start construction in July 2024 because our structural engineer 
recommended that we complete additional testing before the design phase can be complete. 
Speciﬁcally, helical pile testing and destructive concrete testing are deemed essential to gather 
comprehensive data that will inform the foundation engineering design. These tests, anticipated to 
occur in March 2024, play a crucial role in ensuring the structural integrity and stability of the 
proposed foundation work. 
 
We have communicated our plan for additional testing to the State Library Board, who administers 
both the Targeted State Grant and the Building Forward Grant. The State Library Board has 
approved our grant extension request, so no funds are at-risk with this change. We will have more 
time to ensure the design is done right and will include library staff to give input on the ﬁnal scope.

1855 S Norfolk St., Multi-Family Development 
 
Project Description 
This project includes the proposal to redevelop the existing Fish Market Restaurant site with a 260-
unit multi-family development. The 3.46-acre site at 1855 S Norfolk St. is bound by S. Norfolk St. to 
the west, Seal Slough to the east, Susan Ct. to the north, and Fashion Island Blvd. to the south. The 
proposed development consists of: 
o 
A ﬁve-story “South” building, and a four-story “North” building 
o 
260 residential units (for-rent), of which 26 units would be affordable to the Very Low-
Income level. The unit mix would consist of studios, one-, and two-bedroom units. 
o 
1,200 square-feet of commercial uses in the North building 
o 
325 above-grade parking spaces 
o 
Public open spaces along Seal Slough 
o 
Private residential open spaces on building rooftops 
 
Project Update 
The applicant completed a study session with the Parks and Recreation Commission on 
September 19, 2023. Please see related materials below. 
This application was ﬁled on January 3, 2023. Since the Pre-Application, the applicant has reduced 
the total units from 321 units to 260 units to conform with local density limits and State Density 
Bonus law. This application is currently under review with City Departments. Technical studies 
regarding air quality, noise, and traffic are in progress. 
The applicant completed the required Pre-Application, which included a neighborhood meeting 
held on June 22, 2022 and a Planning Commission Study Session held on July 26, 2022. Please see

2024 LEGISLATIVE PRIORITIES
The California Library Association — through its members and advocates — works to make a difference
for all Californians by providing adequately funded libraries, which transform lives through their programs
and services.
At the state level, CLA actively advocates for the following:
1.
Protection of 2021-22 and 2022-23 State Budget Augmentations in Light of a
Challenging 2024-25 Budget year
During the 2021-22 and 2022-23 Budget years, the legislature and the Governor
made a series of historic funding commitments to public library programs under
the jurisdiction of the California State Library. This funding allowed public libraries
to address much needed health and safety construction-related deficiencies, fed
children at the library in need of a meal during the summer, provided books to
homebound seniors, and much more. As such, the California Library Association
respectfully requests that the legislature and Governor protect the valuable
investments made in the areas of:
* The California Library Services Act (CLSA) that fosters resource sharing among
libraries, particularly in rural areas where funding challenges persist.
a.
*Lunch at the Library, which provides 300,000+ free and nutritious lunches and
literacy-rich programs annually at hundreds of libraries across the state during the
summer months.
b.
* Zip books, a program which supports the diverse informational needs of people in rural
and underserved populations who cannot get to a physical library.
c.
* Library infrastructure grants through the Building Forward Library Improvement Grant
Program, which is providing critically important construction dollars to public libraries
throughout the state for health and safety facility upgrades, ADA compliance, essential
library building expansions and more.
2.
New and Ongoing Funding Requests ( 2024-25 State Budget)
a.
$3 million in one-time funding grants to assist public libraries in offsetting costs
associated with the implementation of SB 321-Ashby, Statutes of 2023 (library
cards for third graders).
b.
$10 million in funding to support online job training and workforce development.
c.
$5 million in ongoing funding for the training of library staff and hiring of social workers to
support the de-escalation of behavioral trends in libraries.
3.
Equitable Access to Critical Library and Information Services
a.
Broadband equity and digital literacy for unserved and underserved communities.
b.
Support the freedom to read and access to material with diverse points of view.
c.
Support libraries as a safe haven for all community members.
d.
Accessible buildings, including upgrades to critical infrastructure and support in times of
disaster, such as wildfires and extreme heat.
e.
Early literacy, school readiness, adult literacy, career development, vocational support,

Urge Governor and Legislature to Restore Funding for Popular Pass Program
 
In 2021, California State Parks received funding to create pilot park pass programs 
providing free access to state parks. Those programs included the California State Library

Parks Pass. The program provides free day-use entry to California State Parks anyone with

Last year, California State Parks Foundation released compelling survey data highlighting 
the success of the California State Library Parks Pass program. The survey yielded these 
key insights which show the impact and importance of the program:    
• 
Majority of respondents (63%) previously considered cost to be their main reason 
for not having visited state parks previously.    
• 
Thanks to the park pass program, a staggering 90% of respondents now plan to visit 
state parks over seven times a year.   
• 
Nearly 70% of the survey's respondents indicated an income level of $60,000 or 
less.   
• 
Over 63% of respondents indicated that they are Black, Indigenous, and People of 
Color (BIPOC).   
Unfortunately, the funding this popular and impactful program relies on is expiring at the 
end of this fiscal year. If new funding is not identified, libraries will not receive new passes 
for checkout after December 31.   
California State Parks Foundation estimates that $6.5 million in funding would keep the 
California State Library Parks Pass operating at its current level next year – a fraction of the 
$291 billion overall budget proposed by the Governor.  
Tell Governor Newsom and Legislative leaders to restore funding for this popular and 
effective park access program!
