provided an update on the Marina Library Improvement Project.  Negotiations with the 
developer of the Fish Market property proposing contributions toward Marina Library 
improvements remain open and fluid to the ever-evolving nature of financial and commercial

San Mateo Public Library 
 
Collection Development Policy & Procedures 
 
Introduction 
 
This policy is established to guide the library staff in the development and growth of the 
collection and to inform the public of the principles upon which the library makes 
decisions regarding the selection, maintenance and use of the collection. 
 
I.  Mission Statement 
 
The mission of the San Mateo Public Library is … 
• To provide resources that preserve the past, reflect the present and explore 
the future 
• Serve as a gateway for information through technology 
• Engage in partnerships 
• Promote literacy 
• Invest in a knowledgeable, skilled and responsive staff who offer quality 
information to a diverse community. 
 
 
In order to implement the Library's Mission, the primary goals of the Collection 
Development Policy are: 
 
• To provide reference materials and services that answer the informational 
needs of the community, or that provide direction to additional sources of 
information. 
 
• To provide high-demand, high-interest materials of a popular nature in a 
variety of formats to meet the entertainment and recreational needs of the 
community. 
 
• To provide materials and services that enable community members of all 
ages to pursue practical and/or intellectual self-directed learning independent of the 
formal education process. 
 
• To supplement and complement, without duplicating, the collections of area 
schools, colleges, universities

II. The Community 
 
Community Profile 
 San Mateo is located midway on the San Francisco Peninsula and is both a residential 
and business community in an area of 14 square miles.  San Mateo is the second most 
populous city in the County of San Mateo with a population of 92,482 (2000 US 
Census). The library’s service area additionally includes residents of the Town of 
Hillsborough, a neighboring community. The total library population served is 94,930. 
 
San Mateo is changing, as the city becomes more ethnically diverse. Results from the 
2000 US Census showed substantial changes in the city’s demographic profile. Since 
the Asian population increased 41%, the Asian communities now represent 15% of the 
City’s total population. Nearly 50% of the population of the Asian communities is 
Chinese. 20% of the City’s total population is of Hispanic Origin having increased 43% 
since the previous census. Other groups with a strong presence in the community are 
Blacks and Pacific Islanders.    
 
A Language Census conducted by the San Mateo-Foster City School District Spring 
2000 found more than 42 languages other than English were spoken in the students’ 
homes. Nearly 21% of enrolled students were classified as “English Learners”, lacking 
verbal and written English skills. An additional 23% of the students who speak English 
speak a language other than English at home. Aside from English, Spanish followed by 
Chinese dialects are the primary first languages spoken by children in the City’s public 
schools. The school district has Spanish immersion and magnet school programs to 
meet the diverse ethnic and academic needs of the children. Due to the diversity of 
languages spoken in our community the library continues to develop and expand 
bilingual and language specific collections.  
 
The age group that increased significantly in the recent census was youth 5-19 years 
old with a 37% increase from the previous census. This age group now represents 
16.2% of the city’s total population. The senior population, 65 and older, continues to 
represent 15% of the City’s total population. The median age in San Mateo is 37 years 
old. 
 
The public schools in San Mateo include 9 elementary schools, 3 middle schools, 3 high 
schools and one community college, as well as a number of privately funded schools. 
Ninety-one percent of residents over the age of 18 are high school graduates, sixty-
three percent have attended college and thirty-four percent have college diplomas. This 
high level of educational attainment ensures demand for library materials on a wide 
variety of subjects.  
 
With approximately 58,450 jobs and a mean household income of $84,500 (Sources: 
2000 US Census and ABAG's "Projections 2000"), the City of San Mateo offers an 
attractive marketplace for retail businesses and personal service providers.  San Mateo 
is the largest job center in San Mateo County and has the highest proportion of Finance, 
Insurance, and Real Estate businesses. The City has an active economic development 
program and the outlook for the addition of new businesses in San Mateo is very 
positive.

Meeting Community Needs 
 
The Library evaluates the changes in the community to develop collections that reflect 
its customer's needs and wants.  With a change in demographics, literacy focus and 
school makeup, the library is committed to offering a collection that mirrors these 
changes.   
 
With a significant increase in the ethnic makeup, the library focuses on increasing world 
language collections, especially Spanish and Chinese. As a result of diversity changes 
an increase in ESL materials and adult literacy programs and materials is also 
anticipated. The population of 5 through 19 year olds has increased to guarantee a 
further expansion of the children’s and young adult collections, including early literacy 
programs and materials. Due to continued development of small business in the 
community, the library maintains a strong, current business collection, including print 
materials, electronic resources and periodicals.  The city’s well-educated clientele 
impacts the breadth and depth of the circulating collections and the need to provide a 
wide range of reference resources. 
 
III. Selection Policy 
 
A. Philosophy of Selection  
 
Fundamental to the philosophy governing selection of materials are the principles 
expressed in the American Library Association's Library Bill of Rights  (appendix) 
adopted by the Library Board of Trustees on February 6, 1982. 
 
The library plays an essential role in the process of free communication by making 
easily accessible the whole spectrum of recorded thought. Materials available in the 
library present a diversity of viewpoints and modes of presentation, enabling citizens to 
make the informed choices necessary in a democracy. Inclusion of material 
representing a particular belief, opinion, or point of view in the library's collection does 
not constitute endorsement by the library. 
 
B. Scope of Collection 
The library meets the civic, educational, informational, cultural, and recreational needs 
of San Mateo’s residents through collections that include a wide variety of print, non-
print, and electronic resources for all literacy levels. The Library’s collections are 
supplemented by access to materials of the Peninsula Library System and other 
libraries through borrowing agreements (Interlibrary Loan). 
 
 
1. Scope of the Main Library  
The role of the Main Library is to serve as an information and resource center where 
the public has access to an in-depth collection of up-to-date and accurate 
information.  The Main Library promotes on-site, telephone and e-mail reference and 
information services to assist users in locating information on subjects ranging from

sources and special collections in a variety of formats for people of all ages. 
 
 
2. Scope of the Branch Libraries  
The Hillsdale and Marina branch libraries provide library services, programs, books 
and non-print materials to the neighborhoods adjacent to them. While each branch 
serves basic reference needs of its neighborhood with a core of reference materials, 
it does not duplicate the in-depth sources or special collections of the Main Library. 
Budget and space limit the branch collections to materials of high interest to their 
customers. 
 
C. Responsibility for Selection  
 
Ultimate responsibility for materials selection rests with the City Librarian who operates 
within the framework of policies determined by the Library Board of Trustees. Actual 
selection and management activities are shared among professional staff that 
discharges this obligation consistent with this policy and established procedures. 
 
Selectors are members of the Library’s Collection Development Team. The selectors 
are responsible for: 
• choosing appropriate materials that reflect tastes, needs, and reading abilities 
of the community 
• providing a broadly based, diverse and active collection  
• weeding collections to keep current with need and demand 
• maintaining materials in good physical condition 
• replacing materials when necessary 
• spending funds in a timely and organized manner

Since the library cannot collect everything published, criteria are established for 
subject areas that describe the scope of materials purchased.  The following scale is 
used to define the subject strengths and collection emphases: 
 
Popular Best-sellers and popular materials are acquired based on demand or 
anticipated demand. 
 
Basic 
Works that serve to introduce or define a subject are selected, along with 
current materials, and significant works/classics that provide a broad range with 
minimum depth. 
 
Study 
Broad, current, in-depth and retrospective coverage of a subject matter is 
provided, along with the “best and most important” resources in a subject area. 
 
Comprehensive All available current and retrospective works for comprehensive 
coverage of a field are acquired and retained in the collection. 
 
 
For most subject areas, materials are acquired and retained at the Popular or Basic 
levels. 
 
2. Duplication of Material  
Multiple copies of materials are purchased in response to customer demand as 
evidenced by the number of reserves placed, school assignment needs, anticipated 
popularity, and repeated request. For popular fiction and nonfiction titles the Library 
maintains a reserve ratio of one copy for every four reserves per library location.  
 
3. Suggestions for Purchase  
Suggestions for purchase from library customers and staff members are encouraged. 
The suggestions are forwarded to the selector for the specific subject area to review. If 
the material meets the library’s selection criteria and funds are available it will be 
purchased.  
 
4. Gifts  
The Library welcomes the donation of books and other materials for its collections.  
Those materials that meet the selection criteria may be added to the collection or given 
to the Friends of the Library book sale. See Section V for the guidelines for monetary 
gifts and donations.

A.  Materials for Adult Readers 
 
Fiction  
The library purchases a wide range of fiction reflecting the diverse interests of a public

such as mysteries and romances to literary classics and prize-winning works of fiction.  
Multiple copies are purchased for authors whose books are frequently requested and for

The library acquires materials that provide the community with current information on

the selection of non-fiction materials, the library does not assume responsibility for 
inaccuracies or errors in the works included in its collections. 
 
The library’s collection supplements the curriculum of local high schools and community

lower undergraduate textbooks may be purchased as necessary to provide coverage of

for adult new readers, and adapted classics.  The non-fiction materials address

V.  Funding for the Collection 
 
General Fund 
Funding for the collection is primarily provided from the City’s General Fund. Funds for 
the purchasing of materials are allocated in the library’s Annual Operating Budget. 
Funds for Books Media, and Electronic Resources are allocated within the Technical 
Services budget; funds for Periodicals and Government Documents are allocated within 
the Reference Services budget. 
 
Grants  
Grants provide another source of funding for the purchase of materials. The San Mateo 
Public Library Foundation provides grants to either enhance the existing collection or to 
add a new type of resource or service to the collection.  Other sources for grant funds 
include Federal LSTA grants administered by the California State Library, the Peninsula 
Community Foundation and other agencies and foundations. Examples of collections 
started with grant funds include the Library’s Chinese and Farsi collections. 
 
Donations 
Donations are a third funding source.  In addition to receiving unsolicited donations; the 
Library has two formal donation programs:  
Tribute Fund 
Through the Library’s “Tribute Fund” library users can donate funds to purchase 
library materials as memorial tributes or to honor an individual or organization. 
 
Bucks for Business 
All applicants acquiring a City Business License receive an appeal to contribute the 
Library. All funds received via this appeal are used for the library’s Business 
Collection. 
 
Material Budget Allocations 
At the start of each fiscal year the materials budget is reviewed and adjustments are 
made. Factors that influence adjustments include: 
 
1.  Addition of new materials by subject or format (e.g. adding Farsi books or 
DVDs) 
 
 
2. Increasing and/or decreasing demand for select subject areas 
 
 
3.  The need to update a particular subject area for currency 
 
 
 
4. The changing demographics of the community 
 
The above factors are also use for the awarding of Library Foundation grants, assigning 
of donated funds, and applying for other grant opportunities.

Guiding Principles:  Developing and maintaining a collection of library materials for the community is 
one of the core services of the San Mateo Public Library.   The San Mateo Library Board of Trustees 
endorses the Library Bill of Rights (as adopted by the American Library Association in 1939; latest 
revision in 1996 by the ALA Council) and the Right to Read Statement (adopted by the ALA Council and 
the Association of American Publishers in 1953; latest revision in 2004) as they apply to library 
collections.  These documents serve as guiding principles for the selection and disposal of library 
materials at the San Mateo Public Library. 
 
I. From the “Library Bill of Rights”: 
a. “Books and other library resources should be provided for the interest, information, and 
enlightenment of all people of the community the library serves. Materials should not be 
excluded because of the origin, background, or views of those contributing to their creation. 
b. Libraries should provide materials and information presenting all points of view on current 
and historical issues.  Materials should not be proscribed or removed because of partisan or 
doctrinal disapproval. 
II. From the “Freedom to Read Statement”: 
a. “It is in the public interest for . . . librarians to make available the widest diversity of views 
and expressions, including those that are unorthodox, unpopular, or considered dangerous by 
the majority. 
 
Selection:  The final responsibility for the selection of library materials is delegated to the City Librarian, 
who operates within the framework of the policies determined by the Board of Library Trustees and funds 
available in the Library’s budget and from gifts for collections.  The responsibility for selection of library 
materials is shared with professional librarians and other library staff who have special expertise.  The 
Library’s collections are developed for a diverse community of varying age groups and backgrounds 
reflecting a broad spectrum of interests, viewpoints, genres, subjects and in various formats. Suggestions 
from the public are welcome and titles are carefully considered. The Library exercises the final judgment 
on the suitability for addition to the Library collections. 
                                                                                                                        
Disposal:    Library materials may be withdrawn for a variety of reasons including subject matter that is 
no longer timely, accurate or relevant; the physical condition, insufficient use; or space limitations.  
Library materials which are withdrawn from the collection (as determined by the City Librarian and staff 
assigned collection development duties) are offered to the Friends of the Library who, in turn, may decide 
to add to the inventory of book sale titles or donate to nonprofit organizations.  Titles unwanted by the 
Friends of the Library are sent to the  Internet Archive, a non-profit digital library with a mission is to 
provide access to all knowledge.   
 
Gifts:  The Library welcomes the donation of library materials for its collection.  The Library reserves the 
right to determine whether or not donated titles will be added to the collection.  Materials that meet the 
Library’s selection criteria may be added to the collection.  Donated titles that are not added are routed to 
the Friends of the Library who raise funds through their book sales.  The revenue from the book sales is 
administered by the Library Foundation which awards grants to the Library for new materials.  The 
Library Board also accepts monetary donations for the Library’s collection and to the Memorial Fund for 
the purchase of library materials in memory of or in honor of designated persons.

Purpose of the Collection Development Policy 
The collection development policy is intended to provide the community and library staff 
documentation which informs and supports the development of the library collection. This 
document will provide guidance to San Mateo Public Library staff and inform the public of the 
principles upon which the library makes decisions regarding the development and maintenance of 
the collection. While this policy will not replace the judgment of librarians, it serves to assist them in 
making decisions when choosing from available materials. 
 
Library Mission Statement  
San Mateo Public Library builds community by inspiring imagination, discovery and reading. San 
Mateo is a resilient, innovative, and inclusive community that is knowledgeable and empowered, 
embracing the future while honoring its history. 
Scope of the Main Library 
The role of the main library is to serve as an information and resource center where the public has 
access to an in-depth collection of up-to-date and accurate information.  The Library promotes on-
site and remote reference, and information services to assist users in locating information on 
subjects ranging from practical questions to in-depth research.  The Library’s popular materials 
collection features current, high-interest materials, both fiction and non-fiction, in a variety of 
formats for all ages. 
 
Scope of the Branches 
The Branch Libraries provide high quality, personalized library services, programs, and books and 
materials to the neighborhoods adjacent to the Hillsdale and Marina Libraries. The interests and 
needs of the actual and potential users of the branch are continually evaluated so that each library 
has a collection reflecting the community that it serves. While each branch serves the basic 
information needs of its neighborhood, it does not duplicate the in-depth sources or special 
collections of the Main Library. Budget and space limit the branch collection to materials of high 
interest to their communities. 
Materials Selection 
Library materials are chosen to serve the information, education, cultural, and recreational needs of 
the community. The Library selects materials on a large variety of subjects and in different formats, 
responding to the wide range of ages, ethnicities, languages, educational backgrounds, interests, 
and reading skills of library patrons. Materials are available in a variety of formats, quantities, and 
titles.

Collection development decisions are based on staff judgement and expertise, knowledge of what is 
already in the collection, and reviews in professional journals. Selection criteria includes but is not 
limited to past and anticipated public demand; contemporary significance and timeliness; literary or 
artistic merit; the need for and availability of information or materials in the subject area; 
authoritativeness of the author or publisher; favorable reviews in standard selection sources; cost; 
and shelving space available in the library. Selections are made to build and maintain a balanced 
collection and to include a variety of viewpoints and opinions to meet community needs.  
 
Responsibility for Selection 
Ultimate responsibility for materials selection rests with the City Librarian who operates within the 
framework of policies determined by the Library Board of Trustees. Actual selection and collection 
management maintenance activities are shared among professional staff that perform this 
obligation consistent with this policy and established procedures. 
Suggestions for Purchase 
Library patrons are encouraged to suggest materials for the library to purchase. To do so, patrons 
should complete a ‘Suggest a Purchase’ form via the library website. Suggestions from the public will 
be evaluated by selectors based on the Library’s criteria for selection. All suggestions will be 
considered although not all suggestions may be added to the collection. This includes self-published 
works by local authors or otherwise. 
Donations & Gifts 
The Library welcomes the donation of library materials for its collection. The Library reserves the 
right to determine whether or not donated titles will be added to the collection. Materials that meet 
the Library’s selection criteria may be added to the collection. Donated titles that are not added are 
routed to the Friends of the Library who raise funds through their book sales. The revenue from the 
book sales is administered by the Library Foundation which awards grants to the Library for new 
materials. The Library Board also accepts monetary donations for the Library’s collection and to the 
Memorial Fund for the purchase of library materials in memory of or in honor of designated 
persons.  
The Collections

print books, reference books, and periodicals; non-print collections composed of CDs, DVDs and 
audiobooks; and an electronic-resource collection in the form of databases, e-books, streaming 
music, video and audiobooks, and various other services made available online.  
Materials in the collection are supplemented through access to the materials of the Peninsula 
Library System’s member libraries and by obtaining materials via interlibrary loan. Collections are 
developed in three major areas - for children, teens, and adults. 
Children’s Collections: A robust collection of print and media materials for children, items are 
provided in multiple formats for a wide range of interests, ages, and reading levels. Although the 
library strives to provide basic curriculum support, we do not attempt to purchase in sufficient 
quantity for required reading or other district-wide projects. Special collections include:

Collection Management 
Collection maintenance and evaluation is important to ensure the materials, both print and digital, 
in the Library are balanced and responsive to community needs. The Library strives to maintain an 
up-to-date, useful collection. Statistical tools such as circulation reports help librarians to determine 
how the collection is being used, and community demographic information will be used to help keep 
abreast of changing community needs.  During the process of evaluation, the librarian will have the 
opportunity to identify subjects where materials are needed, missing titles that should be replaced, 
older editions of titles which should be updated, and subjects, authors, and titles that are no longer 
in demand in the community. Titles and items are withdrawn from the collection through periodic 
and systematic review by librarians. Materials may be withdrawn because they are worn, obsolete,

INTELLECTUAL FREEDOM & ACCESS TO LIBRARY MATERIALS  
San Mateo Public Library fully endorses the principles documented in the Library Bill of Rights and 
the Freedom to Read Statement of the American Library Association. San Mateo Public Library does 
not censor library materials of any nature for any member of the community. Library staff and 
patrons may not impose their views, values, and biases upon one another.   
The Library's collection is the reflection of its diverse community, and inclusion in the library’s 
collection does not reflect endorsement of any specific contents or belief. Library materials are not 
identified or marked to show approval or disapproval based on its content. The library upholds the 
right of the individual to secure information, even though the content may be controversial, 
unorthodox, or unacceptable to others. Parents and guardians have the right and responsibility to 
make decisions about the materials suitable for their own children. The Library does not regulate 
what a child reads, views, or checks out. 
Request for Reconsideration 
 
Members of the San Mateo community wishing to recommend the removal of a particular item in 
the library collection may submit a Request for Reconsideration of Library Materials form, which will 
be reviewed by the Library Director and the staff in relation to the library's mission statement and 
the selection criteria of this collection development policy. After evaluating journal reviews and 
other materials submitted by the patron and the staff, a response will be made by the City Librarian 
within 30 days of receiving the formal objection.
