SUBJECT:
Commercial Cannabis Ordinance and Permit Options – Discussion 
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive information on various commercial cannabis permits and policies to consider and provide direction on which 
cannabis-related priorities, policies, and permits, if any, are desired in the City. 
BACKGROUND:
In 2016, California voters passed Proposition 64 legalizing cannabis recreational use for adults 21 and over. In San Mateo 
County, 63% of voters approved the measure. Proposition 64 also allows local jurisdictions to regulate or prohibit 
commercial cannabis activities within their borders. 
Proposition 64 and later clarifying regulations legalized personal cultivation of up to six plants and delivery of cannabis to 
adults 21 years or older throughout the State. Therefore, even if a local jurisdiction prohibits all types of cannabis 
businesses, local jurisdictions cannot prohibit cannabis deliveries into their city borders from an outside delivery service. 
In 2017, the City Council passed Ordinance No. 2017-4, which added Chapter 7.45 to the San Mateo Municipal Code to 
prohibit the commercial cultivation, processing, or sale of marijuana, which will be referred to as cannabis throughout this 
report.  Current San Mateo law prohibits all recreational commercial cannabis operations.  Per State law and City 
Ordinance No. 2017-4, residents are allowed to have up to six cannabis plants indoors at their private residences. 
During the 2022-2023 City Council Goal Setting Workshop on January 29, 2022, the Council approved the following 
priority: Adopt a Retail Cannabis Ordinance with restrictions around schools and significant public benefits including license 
fees and safety standards. While discussing the goal, councilmembers mentioned a need for an equity approach, the 
possibility of revenue, and neighboring cities newly allowing retail cannabis. During this fiscal year’s Goal Setting Workshop 
on February 11, 2023, the City Council voted to keep the retail cannabis priority on the Council’s Strategic Plan but did not 
engage in further discussion on the matter. 
Permitting commercial cannabis operations does provide an opportunity for new tax revenue. However, revenue is 
dependent on how many businesses the city allows to operate within its jurisdiction.  A revenue measure would need to 
be placed on an upcoming general election ballot and would need to be approved by San Mateo voters, in order for the 
City to collect a local tax on commercial cannabis activities.
Establishing and implementing a thoughtful and successful commercial cannabis business program requires significant staff 
time from many City departments including: the City Manager’s Office, City Attorney’s Office, City Clerk’s Office, 
Community Development Department, Police Department, Finance Department, Public Works-transportation, and the San 
Mateo Consolidated Fire Department. The steps needed to establish a program include: placing a cannabis revenue

Status of the Cannabis Industry
Staff reached out to a local cannabis industry expert with the Silicon Valley Cannabis Alliance who provided staff with a 
current industry snapshot (state of the market). Currently, the cannabis industry is experiencing a recession/correction 
from the industry's peak during the height of the COVID-19 Pandemic. The industry is down approximately 25-35% in sales 
from its peak a few years ago.  This is due to a number of factors including the reduction in overall cannabis consumption, 
tax laws, a growing competition from the black market and unlicensed, unregulated businesses, and a weakened debt 
market which limits lines of credit and access to capital for operators in order to continue their business expansion efforts. 
These factors have the commercial cannabis industry hunkering down at this time and there is a reduced demand from 
operators to establish new retail cannabis businesses. However, according to the industry expert, a retail storefront 
businesses' location is a determining factor whether a business will be successful during these times. 
This report summarizes research on the type of permits approved by the State and the evaluation criteria for determining 
which, if any, types of permits the City Council could explore allowing. This report includes other non-retail commercial 
cannabis permits for background information and consideration. 
Other Cities and Types of Permits 
Many cities in San Mateo County and the Bay Area have successfully implemented commercial cannabis programs, with 
twelve jurisdictions in San Mateo County permitting at least one type of commercial cannabis activity.  In total there are 
six primary types of commercial cannabis licenses, and all require approval from the local jurisdiction as well as a state 
license. There are currently 35 active state licenses across San Mateo County. A full list of where commercial cannabis 
activities are permitted in the Bay Area is provided in Attachment 1, while the six types of permits are summarized below, 
along with data on where they are issued in San Mateo County: 
1.
Retail (storefront): A storefront retailer has a physical location where cannabis goods are sold directly to 
consumers. They are also sometimes referred to as “dispensaries.” Cities can allow a subset of these permits, 
known as “consumption cafes,” where cannabis products can be consumed onsite. Four cities in San Mateo County 
allow for storefront retail, however, no cities in San Mateo County permit onsite consumption. 
2.
Retail (non-storefront): A non-storefront retailer sells cannabis goods to customers only through delivery but has a 
physical premise where its business activities take place. Six cities in San Mateo County allow for non-storefront 
(delivery) retail and there are nine active licenses across the County. Recently, there has been less interest from 
businesses in this permit type. For example, while Burlingame and Mountain View both allow delivery-only retail, 
neither has any businesses in operation.
3.
Distribution: Procurement, sale, and transport of cannabis and cannabis products between other licensed 
businesses. This is distinct from delivery or non-storefront retail as they have no direct interaction with customers. 
For example, they transport cannabis from growers to manufactures, or from manufacturers to retailers. Four 
cities in San Mateo County allow for cannabis distribution and there are four active permits across the County. 
4.
Testing: A laboratory or facility that tests cannabis or cannabis products. While allowed in six jurisdictions in the 
County, none are currently operating. 
5.
Manufacturing: Production, preparation, propagation, or compounding of cannabis or cannabis products. While 
allowed in four jurisdictions in the County, none are currently operating. 
6.
Cultivation: Small, medium, and large-scale indoor, outdoor, and combined indoor/outdoor commercial

on the scale and type of light. There are 11 active cultivation licenses but primarily in the agricultural area of

If the Council is interested in pursuing a cannabis program in San Mateo, it could consider an ordinance that allows all six 
types of permits or a subset of these permit types.
Given the lack of interest from businesses to pursue testing or manufacturing operations elsewhere in San Mateo County, 
San Mateo is unlikely to attract these businesses either. Additionally, San Mateo does not have the appropriate 
infrastructure to be appealing in terms of cultivation (lack of agricultural zoned land) nor distribution (limited industrial 
space away from residential communities). Should the Council be interested in allowing commercial cannabis operations, 
the main areas of business interest locally according to the industry expert is in the retail brick and mortar cannabis 
storefronts or a combination of retail storefronts and delivery services within the same footprint.  Therefore, the rest of 
this report will primarily focus on retail and delivery cannabis permits.
Attachment 2 details how the permits are deployed in San Mateo County cities. 
Evaluation Criteria
When considering how and which, if any, permits to implement, staff or Council may utilize evaluation criteria such as 
revenue, zoning, public safety, and staff time. For example, while delivery-only permits require less staff time and fewer 
zoning considerations, there is little business interest and, therefore, little to no revenue. Storefront retail requires 
substantially more staff time to regulate, but has the opportunity for increased revenue. 
Revenue: 
The state imposes a 15% excise tax on retail sales of cannabis or cannabis products, now collected by the retailers. 
Most cities that allow commercial cannabis have a voter approved revenue measure, especially cities that allow storefront 
retail or “dispensaries.” The tax rates vary significantly across cities in San Mateo County from 0.6% to 6%. The revenue 
measures adopted by cities typically have built in flexibility with sliding scales between 0% to 10% which the City Council 
can determine or change the tax rate as they see fit. Cities often have different tax rates for different commercial cannabis 
activities. See Attachment 2 for the current tax rates of San Mateo County jurisdictions by type of permit. 
If the Council is interested in a specific tax on cannabis businesses, a revenue measure would have to go to the voters for 
approval at an upcoming general election, possibly by a simple majority, depending on the nature of the measure. The

Permitting commercial cannabis operations does provide an opportunity for revenue. However, thoughtful 
implementation can require significant staff time and revenue is dependent on how many businesses choose to operate 
within the jurisdiction. Tax laws and ongoing competition from the black market have hindered financial success of the 
legal cannabis industry in California in recent years. 
Some city cannabis programs require cannabis retailers to provide community benefits to local community-based 
organizations and nonprofits. For example, in Redwood City, retail cannabis providers will provide annual community 
benefit contributions to a range of activities such as educational, and children and youth serving programs. Redwood City 
also requires retailers to provide a $10,000 annual contribution for youth cannabis prevention education programs.
Cannabis advocates are pushing for lower tax rates at the state and local level to give smaller businesses more ability to 
sustain themselves. 
Zoning: 
Like with most business types, cities can determine in which zoning designations cannabis businesses can operate. Given 
their different needs, the zoning designations vary by type of permit or license. For example, storefront retailers are often 
in retail areas, while delivery-only retailer need large warehouse spaces located in light industrial zones. Some cities such 
as Daly City and Pacifica developed specific “Cannabis Eligibility Zones” or “Cannabis Operation Overlay Districts” to specify 
where retail operations are allowed. 
Additionally, many cities, such as Redwood City, San Bruno, or Berkeley, cap the number of retail operators, both 
storefront and delivery, in their city. For example, Redwood City allows six storefront retail permits in all areas that allow 
retail in their zoning code while San Bruno allows just one. 
Under state law, cannabis businesses, regardless of use type, must be at least 600 feet from sensitive receptors such as all 
K-12 schools, day care centers and youth centers.  Jurisdictions can also add additional sensitive uses to the separation 
requirements such as libraries, parks, or residential areas. Many jurisdictions also require buffers between retail cannabis 
locations to avoid concentration. 
Public Safety: 
Under the current ordinance, San Mateo already does face public safety concerns from commercial cannabis activities 
originating in other jurisdictions. In the last year, San Mateo has experienced significant crimes of violence associated with 
cannabis businesses, the majority of which involved cannabis delivery. In those cases, the victims, employees of the 
cannabis delivery companies, were followed into San Mateo before being accosted and robbed. In one such instance, the 
suspects shot several times at the victim as the victim drove away to flee (fortunately the victim and no other community 
members were hit). 
In another of these cases, the suspects forced the victim from their car at gunpoint before fleeing in the victim’s vehicle 
with cannabis goods. In each of the cannabis delivery robberies, the crimes were clearly premeditated by criminal groups 
that were armed, ready to engage in violence, and specifically targeted the cannabis deliveries. 
One of the other crimes involving cannabis business involved the home invasion of a subject that had traveled from 
Southern California to San Mateo to sell cannabis. The suspects in that case laid in wait for the victims, were able to force 
entry into the home, and held the victims at gunpoint while taking their large amount of cannabis. All four of these crimes 
had in common suspects that were criminally sophisticated, armed, violent, and motivated by the value that cannabis 
offers.
Storefront cannabis businesses have specific public safety concerns, especially as they historically often rely more heavily 
on cash. There are also concerns of minors under 21 years old accessing cannabis. However, with well-designed ordinances 
with specific safety and security guidance, the risks can be mitigated, and many cities reported relatively few issues. 
Therefore, the state has specific security requirements for licensees including, but not limited to:

Cannabis Regulation Implementation: 
All the criteria above greatly impact the amount of staff time required to research and implement a commercial cannabis 
ordinance. Between research, application review, selection, and appeals, a commercial cannabis ordinance can require 
significant staff time. For example, Redwood City’s process took four phases over six years, with upwards of 90 hours per 
permit application. Redwood City’s fee study found the cost of just reviewing one application to be $22,604, excluding 
background checks. This does not include the opportunity cost of staff working on other priority projects for the city. 
Additionally, there are future costs related to enforcement of the various provisions laid out above.
Any commercial cannabis program would require substantial staff time from the Police Department, San Mateo 
Consolidated Fire, the Community Development Department, Finance Department, Public Works Department, the City 
Attorney’s Office, the City Manager’s Office and various other departments depending on the specific ordinance. 
Staff time is particularly dependent on the approval process determined by the City Council. For cities that restrict the 
number of permits, the jurisdiction must establish a permit issuance criteria and application process. Cities can do a lottery 
open to all applications that passed an initial review process or offer permits on a first-come, first-serve basis. In order to 
have more control and input over the applications selected, other cities do a more time-consuming comprehensive multi-
round selection process based on pre-determined criteria. Criteria can include Business Plan, Community Benefit Plan, 
Location, Security Plan, Safety Plan, Owners Qualifications, and Diversity, Equity and Inclusion Plan. 
Next Steps 
Considering the information provided and the substantial upfront cost, staff requests the City Council clarify whether it 
would like to move forward with having staff continue its exploration of establishing a retail cannabis program at this time. 
As part of the critical path to develop a local program, staff would need to begin reviewing and developing a commercial 
cannabis tax revenue measure for potential placement on the November 2024 ballot. Alternatively, staff can move 
forward with a wait and see approach (come back in 12 to 24 months) and wait for the data to come in from our local 
neighbors currently embarking on their retail cannabis storefront program (Redwood City) and see the amount of tax 
revenue generated and community benefits distributed to local groups once their program is fully up and running in the 
next year. This also allows additional time to observe how the cannabis industry responds to the current recession it is in.
