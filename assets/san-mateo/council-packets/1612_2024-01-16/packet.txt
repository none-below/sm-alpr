SUBJECT:
Sustainable Buildings Strategy – Project Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on the development of "Electrify San Mateo - Building for the Future: City of San Mateo Sustainable 
Buildings Strategy" and provide direction on the policy options to include in continued project outreach.
BACKGROUND:
Building energy use represents 38% of communitywide greenhouse gas emissions in San Mateo. In January 2022, the City 
Council prioritized the decarbonization of existing buildings to help meet the City’s climate goals and reaffirmed this 
priority in 2023. The decarbonization, or electrification, of buildings refers to the transition from gas equipment to electric 
equipment for space and water heating, clothes drying, cooking and more. In San Mateo, Peninsula Clean Energy (PCE) 
provides electricity Citywide that is 50% renewable and virtually 100% carbon free, and PCE is committed to delivering 
100% renewable and carbon free electricity by 2025. Electrifying existing buildings is critical to further reducing 
communitywide greenhouse gas emissions and is a key strategy in the City’s Climate Action Plan. Technical analysis and 
community engagement are needed to develop a roadmap to clean energy buildings in San Mateo.
In late 2022, staff released a Request for Proposals (RFP) seeking an experienced consultant team knowledgeable in 
decarbonization policy planning, analysis, and implementation, to help San Mateo develop a decarbonization strategy that 
centers on equity and promotes a collaborative pathway to electrification. After reviewing the proposals and interviewing 
the consultants, staff selected Rincon Consultants, Inc. because of their compelling approach to analysis and outreach and 
their experience with developing similar strategies for other cities. The City Council approved an agreement with Rincon 
for this project at the March 20, 2023 City Council meeting. This project, which is now titled, “Building for the Future: City 
of San Mateo Sustainable Buildings Strategy,” launched in May 2023. On September 10, 2023, the project team presented 
to the Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission on the Sustainable Buildings Strategy. The item received a high volume 
of public comments including 150 written and verbal comments as discussed later in this report.
This report provides an overview of the project and summarizes the local building electrification policy context, upfront 
electric equipment costs, community feedback staff has received so far, and policy options for San Mateo to consider. Staff 
is seeking Council feedback on the policy options to include in continued project outreach.
Project Overview
The goal of this project is to develop a long-term strategy document to electrify San Mateo’s existing building stock, 
including policies and actions for both residential and nonresidential buildings. Electrifying existing buildings is expected to 
produce many positive outcomes in San Mateo beyond reducing greenhouse gas emissions and helping meet climate 
goals. Electrification benefits include the added health benefit of reducing gas use in the home. Peer reviewed studies 
have documented that residences with gas stoves have 50 to 400% higher average nitrogen dioxide concentrations than

homes with electric stoves.1  Living in a home with a gas stove may increase children’s risk of asthma by 42%.2 Additionally, 
gas infrastructure carries an inherent safety risk due to the volatile nature of gas, including risk of methane leakage, 
damage during earthquakes, accidental explosions, or fires. Nationwide, gas ignites over 4,000 home structure fires 
annually, causing on average 40 deaths and $54 million in property damage each year.3 When coupled with solar and 
battery energy storage, all-electric buildings can mitigate impacts of power outages and increase community resilience. 
There is a common misconception that having gas appliances provides a redundant system during power outages; 
however, gas appliances like water heaters, stoves, and space heaters typically cannot function without electricity to 
power the fans and controls needed for operation.
The concept of energy equity recognizes that disadvantaged communities have been historically marginalized and 
overburdened by pollution, underinvestment in clean energy infrastructure, and lack of access to energy efficient housing 
and transportation (Department of Energy, Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy)4. Equity will be centered in 
the development of the Sustainable Building Strategy and the equity criteria drafted by the project team builds upon the 
work done by the City of San Mateo’s Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Committee, and draws inspiration from equity work 
conducted in conjunction with its housing element from 2022, the DEI Strategic Framework Report, and City Council 
resolution work. As part of future policy exploration and analysis, staff will address how vulnerable communities will be 
supported in each electrification policy implementation.
Local Policy Context
As part of the FY 2022-2023 goal setting session, the City Council directed staff to present to them policy options to 
decarbonize and electrify existing buildings and eliminate natural gas use by 2030. The City’s Climate Action Plan also 
identifies electrifying existing buildings as a key strategy to reduce communitywide greenhouse gas emissions. The City 
promotes electrification through marketing electric equipment rebates and incentives and hosting workshops. 
Additionally, the City adopted reach codes to encourage and require building electrification. In 2020, the City Council 
adopted an ordinance requiring all new residential and office buildings be all-electric starting on January 1, 2021. In 2022, 
the City Council approved an ordinance that expanded the all-electric requirement to all new buildings and requires 
electric-readiness and electric appliances for specific home remodels as of January 1, 2023. City staff are monitoring 
federal litigation involving the City of Berkeley’s ban on gas infrastructure and are reviewing recent Ninth Circuit caselaw 
on this issue.
In addition, the City is working on electrifying appliances at City facilities. The Senior Center kitchen replaced a gas stove 
with an electric cooktop. Staff are evaluating gas water heater replacement options with heat pump water heaters at 
multiple City facilities. Staff worked with PG&E’s program implementer, Willdan, and completed site assessments at 17 
City facilities. Willdan staff identified seven sites that show the most potential for replacement through the turnkey 
program that would leverage PG&E and PCE rebates, resulting in new electric equipment replacement at no cost to the 
City. Staff are also planning to apply for PCE’s Member Agency Energy Grants. There is $11.5 million available, and monies 
will be disbursed based on customer count in the jurisdiction. The funding must be used for purposes consistent with PCE’s 
mission, such as efforts to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and support building decarbonization. Staff looks forward to 
PCE’s release of the grant funding and will allocate funding thoughtfully and strategically to advance the City’s climate and 
decarbonization goals in alignment with PCE’s grant guidelines.

On-bill costs are another important piece of the overall economics of electrification. On-bill costs describe how much the

from gas to standard-efficiency electric appliances, and even more if high-efficiency systems are installed. More details are 
provided in Attachment 1 and the PCE and SVCE Study.
Community Feedback 
Outreach for this effort is ongoing and will continue throughout the project. To date, outreach has focused on educating 
the community about the costs and benefits of building electrification and about the project’s general goals. Outreach also 
focused on gathering feedback on the equity and effectiveness criteria the City will use to evaluate policy options.
At the September 13, 2023 Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission meeting, staff and the consultant team presented 
the building inventory and market segmentation analysis as well as building electrification cost analysis. Over 120 written 
public comments were submitted for the item with the majority in support of decarbonization. 24 verbal comments were 
also provided at the meeting. Proponents of the City’s decarbonization support electrification policy efforts to reduce gas 
use in the community to combat climate change and to encourage the leveraging of incentives and rebates for 
electrification. For those opposed, the number one concern about electrification is the costs to the property owner. Other 
concerns include power outages, growth of the grid to accommodate future electric buildings, and passed on costs to 
renters. Commissioners' top concern was a robust engagement and outreach approach to make sure all voices of the 
community were heard.
In Fall of 2023, five General Plan Town Hall meetings were held to solicit feedback on the City’s General Plan Update. Staff 
had a poster board summarizing the Sustainable Buildings Strategy project efforts and received a mix of both supportive 
feedback for working on electrification requirements as well as concerns about costs related to electrification. On 
September 22, 2023, staff presented to the City’s Diversity Equity and Inclusion Task Force. Feedback from the Task Force 
included: the need to include education, data, and storytelling in outreach. The Task Force’s biggest concerns regarding 
electrification were related to the electric power outages and costs to homeowners. The Task Force also helped identify 
follow up opportunities for partnerships and project outreach. 
The project team also launched an online survey to assess community priorities for building electrification that was open 
December 2023 through January 10, 2024. Results of the online survey will be shared orally during the January 16, 2024 
City Council Study Session. Early survey results show that the major concerns and values related to building 
decarbonization are consistent with the feedback received and during fall outreach events as summarized above.
Policy Options
At this point in the project, the project team has focused on education and gathering feedback to prioritize the 
community’s goals for the Sustainable Building Strategy. The goal of this report is to outline potential policy options to 
include in continued project outreach, research and analysis, including legal review. 
These policies are meant to be considered as a menu of options to achieve the City’s climate goals, while considering 
implementation and cost challenges for property owners across the City. Most local governments considering a building 
decarbonization strategy combine multiple strategies together to produce the most comprehensive, and equitable policy

Adoption of an “End of Flow Ordinance” was considered but not provided in the ultimate policy options. An “End of Flow 
Ordinance” establishes a long-term carbon-neutrality aligned target to end natural gas use in a jurisdiction. The adoption 
of such an ordinance sends a signal to regional planners and utilities that the jurisdiction has taken long-term action to end 
the flow of natural gas in the community. The City of Half Moon Bay is the only jurisdiction that has adopted such an 
ordinance; their ordinance requires fuel gas lines to be capped or decommissioned in existing buildings by 2045. This type 
of approach should not be adopted as a standalone policy because it requires additional short-term actions to electrify 
existing buildings to meet such a goal. 
This list of potential policies includes a range of options to address different building types and equipment. Each policy 
option requires legal review, further research, outreach and engagement with the community, and cost resource 
allocation for implementation. In future policy analysis, staff will identify how vulnerable community members may 
receive the technical or financial support needed to comply with a potential policy.
 Staff is seeking Council direction on which of the five policy options to include in its ongoing outreach efforts and future 
analysis. Building electrification is a complex topic and by providing example policy options for community feedback, the 
project team hopes to receive deeper engagement and provide a clearer understanding of potential building 
decarbonization strategy options to the community and for inclusion in the final Sustainable Building Strategy.
Project Timeline and Next Steps
The project team adjusted the original timeline to allow for deeper community engagement. Council feedback will inform 
the next phase of outreach, which will begin in July 2024. The final Sustainable Building Strategy would return to City 
Council for adoption in 2025.

Upfront Electrification Costs 
Electrification upfront costs include all costs to purchase an appliance, install and permit it, and make 
it operational. This includes wiring, labor, and contractor mark ups. Upfront costs were analyzed using 
the most recent and location specific data available. See Appendix A for data sources.  
For most upfront costs, median install values were selected to inform the cost analysis. Using median 
costs helped remove outlier quotes from contractors, which would have influenced the average value. 
Quotes for some equipment, such as furnaces, could vary between contractors by as much as $15,000 
for the same project. Where median costs were not available, averages were used. 
In general, the costs for single-family homes and the costs per unit for low-rise multifamily buildings (3 
stories and under) will be similar. Low-rise multifamily buildings tend to use similar equipment to 
single-family homes and one piece of equipment may even serve multiple units in some buildings. For 
example, an 80-gallon hot water heater being used by both units in a duplex. Therefore, the per unit 
costs for electrification in Table 2 are assumed to be conservative for multifamily buildings which use 
residential (as opposed to commercial) sized equipment. High-rise multifamily homes (over 3 stories) 
look more like commercial buildings with a wide variety of equipment and the potential for larger 
centralized systems.

Whole-Home Electrification Incremental Costs  
Electrification in existing buildings will likely happen on an individual appliance level instead of at the 
whole-home level, as building owners will likely only upgrade appliances as they fail. However, looking 
at the incremental cost to electrify on the whole-home level is useful for understanding the overall 
upfront cost impact of building electrification.  
Over the course of 10-20 years a building is expected to replace each of its major gas consuming 
appliances including the water heater, furnace, air conditioner (if applicable), dryer, and stove which 
all have effective lifespans of less than 20 years. During this time a homeowner would spend between 
$11,000 and $18,500 dollars to replace gas equipment with new gas equipment. During this same 
time period upgrading to electric appliances would cost between $24,300 and $32,000 before any 
incentives and depending on varied HVAC costs and need for a panel upgrade. This means the 
incremental or increased costs associated with whole-home electrification could be between $5,800 
and $21,000.

Federal Electrification Incentives under the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) & the High 
Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Act9 
The IRA was signed into law in August 2022, ushering in significant federal policy support and funding 
for building decarbonization. The High Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Act (HEEHRA) is the official 
name of the IRA’s rebate program for electric home technology including heat pumps, electrical wiring, 
and induction stoves, which will be available starting in 2024. Low-income households up to 80% of 
Area Median Income (AMI) can access 100% of upfront discount incentives, while moderate-income 
households from 80-150% AMI can access up to 50% of the upfront discount, in addition to tax credits 
that vary based on household income. Total HEEHRA discounts are capped at $14,000 across all 
electrification projects.  
Cost reductions under HEEHRA are not included in this analysis as details are still developing and the 
incentives and most City of San Mateo building owners are generally classified as high income and 
would be ineligible. However, for low and moderate-income households up to 150% of AMI in San 
Mateo, IRA incentives may further increase the effectiveness of local electrification incentives 
available via PCE and BayREN, and help to close the gap for appliances where subsidies don’t always 
zero-out incremental costs, like HVAC systems, dryers, and induction ranges.  
Table 5 details the rebates available through HEEHRA. These rebates are also extended to multifamily 
buildings where fifty percent of the residents are LMI.10  With all HEEHRA rebates applied, LMI 
households in the City could fully close the incremental cost gap to electrify, and potentially entire 
project costs up to $14,000.11

Once incentives are applied, heat pump hot water heaters and heat pump HVAC systems which replace

for home electrification could continue to move the needle towards upfront cost-effectiveness for

upfront cost of these appliances is expected to decline over time as these electric technologies

BAAQMD Zero NOx Covered Appliances: Water Heaters and Furnaces 
Upcoming BAAQMD Zero NOx rules require water heater equipment replacements to be zero NOx in 
2027 and space heating equipment replacements to be zero NOx in 2029. Based on the current 
incentives and installation costs, installing a heat pump water heater was found to be no-cost, resulting 
in savings of $2,800 dollars without a panel upgrade or a savings of $700 when paired with a panel 
upgrade. This represents a major opportunity area for building electrification in San Mateo. By 
electrifying water heaters now and installing a panel upgrade where needed, residents can save money 
compared to installing a new gas water heater while preparing for future electrification.  
The electrification of furnaces with the addition of air conditioning also offers immediate cost savings 
of between $1,622 to $5,567 when compared to replacing both a furnace and AC. This means that 
electrification of water heaters as well as furnaces (when also replacing an AC offers immediate cost 
savings over a ‘like for like’ replacement scenario. Working to facilitate these replacements would both 
reduce GHG emissions and save residents money upfront in San Mateo while also preparing for these 
upcoming regulations.

To better understand costs and other constraints related to multifamily and nonresidential building 
electrification, Rincon reviewed best-available existing resources on this topic, namely, the PCE 2035 
Decarbonization Plan (2022), and The Rocky Mountain Institute The Economics of Electrifying 
Buildings: Medium Size Commercial Retrofits (2022)1. This document review is also supplemented by 
original contractor interviews conducted by Rincon for the upcoming Sacramento Electric Buildings 
Strategy (2022). Though not strictly City of San Mateo specific, the technical considerations of 
challenges to electrify oversized buildings are consistent between Sacramento and San Mateo, so are 
referenced here as recent region-specific resources for commercial electrification in Northern 
California. 
Electrification Up-Front Costs and Other Considerations 
While single family and low rise multifamily residential buildings tend to use similar equipment and 
operate in similar ways, commercial buildings and high-rise multifamily buildings tend to be highly 
variable. In original research conducted by Rincon for the City of Sacramento in the summer of 2022, 
all interviewees2 communicated that larger multifamily and nonresidential buildings are more 
complicated to electrify than single-family homes and estimating costs is typically only possible on a 
project-by-project basis. This is because for most larger buildings, replacing a gas system with a 
similarly sized electric system is cost-prohibitive and therefore, often requires building re-engineering 
to determine appliance replacement and sizing options. For this reason, a detailed cost analysis for 
larger multifamily and commercial buildings was not developed and included here. Smaller buildings 
with domestic-sized space and water heating end uses, such as small to medium-sized retail or service 
buildings, have similar electrification costs as seen for single-family homes.  
However, some equipment types are similar across building types and the cost to electrify can be more 
readily analyzed at a high level. For example, the PCE analysis notes that packaged commercial HVAC 
equipment, namely, packaged Single Zone (SZ) Gas Furnace with Cooling, are easy to electrify, as 
existing building 2-way A/C ordinances can cover most units without the requirement for incentives.  
The Rocky Mountain Institute (RMI) ‘The Economics of Electrifying Buildings: Medium-Size 
Commercial Retrofits’ Report notes that local policy, or expectations of new local building 
electrification policies, will be a key driver in advancing commercial energy electrification, as portfolio 
owners will want to avoid stranded assets unless they undertake electrification. The RMI report 
focused on the electrification of HVAC systems from rooftop units (RTUS) with Heat Pump Rooftop 
Package Units (RTUs), on a prototypical 50,000 sq ft office building.  
The RMI report modeled 5 cases to assess the economics of RTU retrofits: Case 1: No electrification, 
Case 2: Partial electrification, Case 3: Full electrification, Case 4: Efficient electrification, Case 5: 
Efficient electrification + demand management, Case 6: Efficient electrification + demand

Option 3: Electric equipment replacement requirements for space and water heating

potentially continue to operate past the City’s target for carbon neutrality.

Option 4: Building Performance Standards (BPS) for Commercial Buildings 
Option 4 introduces new policy standards for commercial buildings (Building Performance Standards) 
aimed at increasing energy efficiency, and eventually, electrifying commercial buildings as a phased 
efficiency-focused approach.  
Commercial buildings feature frequently oversized systems for heating and cooling. Process loads also 
vary significantly across commercial building types. For these reasons, electrifying commercial 
buildings necessitates a flexible approach where building managers can first make strides towards 
energy efficiency before electrifying to avoid potentially increased energy bills post installation of an 
all-electric system. 
Typically, a BPS Program is implemented in phases where buildings first report energy use for several 
years, then perform a retro-commissioning process to identify opportunities before finally reducing 
emissions below an established threshold. Energy benchmarking must be completed prior to setting 
BPS thresholds. Best practices for implementing a BPS threshold that would likely be a feature of the 
City’s BPS policy include:  
• 
Standards and methods for energy benchmarking: Covered Buildings (buildings and 
properties with buildings totaling the specified size threshold or larger) report their annual 
energy usage in Energy Star Portfolio Manager. This process is already mandated by AB 
802, which requires energy usage reporting to the CEC for buildings over 50,000 square 
feet. The data collected in the benchmarking phase improves building manager and City 
understanding of building energy usage and will inform GHG-emissions performance 
standards. 
• 
Retro-commissioning: Retro-commissioning is a process through which a building’s energy 
consuming systems are analyzed for efficiency and proper operation. The retro-
commissioning process serves two primary purposes. The first is to uncover inefficient 
operations, increase energy efficiency, and save building owners money. The second is to 
identify cost-effective opportunities to reduce building GHG emissions through 
electrification and energy efficiency. Building owners of covered buildings may complete 
retro-commissioning as a means of improving building performance and prioritizing 
retrofits prior to BPS implementation. Retro-commissioning would be a voluntary approach 
that building managers could pursue to identify the most cost-effective and efficient 
investments available for their building. 
Other Cities:    
Building performance standards are a well-established policy mechanism that has been adopted by 
many local governments across the country including the City of Reno, City of Chula Vista, City of 
Seattle, City of St. Louis, City of Boston, and City of New York.7
