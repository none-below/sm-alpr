SUBJECT:
City Council Guidelines - Updates
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and provide direction on proposed changes for consideration to the City Council's Guidelines and Code of Conduct
BACKGROUND:
The City Council adopted its “Guidelines” to provide rules on how it conducts its business in August 2015. These were 
compiled from several stand-alone resolutions that the Council had historically relied on. The Guidelines have been 
amended seven times since its original adoption. This is due to several factors including law changes, process changes, 
experience with the rules and the realization of unintended consequences, trends in local governance practices, and in 
response to public concern raised on local issues. At the May 1, 2023 City Council meeting, a majority of Council supported 
bringing forward for consideration a more robust code of conduct in the Guidelines.
Staff is seeking direction from the Council on proposed language changes in the Guidelines in response to new or changing 
processes; to codify practices that have evolved since the last update in October 2022; and on the overhaul and update of 
a few major sections of the Guidelines having to do with meeting item hearing order, liaison assignments, and the Board 
and Commission appointment process. In addition, separate guidance is sought on replacing the current Council Member 
Conduct section with a more robust Code of Conduct that could entail a longer discussion and additional research.
The City Council has also adopted “Rules and Procedures” for its Boards and Commissions in a document aligned with the 
Council Guidelines (where applicable). At this study session, staff is looking for direction on the City Council Guidelines and 
then, as is our standard practice, will return for either another study session or for action on both the City Council’s rules 
and with an updated Board and Commissions rules that mirrors changes directed in the Guidelines. If Council directs a 
Code of Conduct, one will be proposed and adapted specific to the Boards and Commissions also.
Name Change
Staff is recommending changing the name from “City Council Guidelines” to “City Council Rules and Procedures” to more 
closely align with the intent; which was to establish rules for conducting business. Calling them Guidelines diminishes the 
importance of this document and causes confusion. Keeping it a simple title and being more specific to the intent would be 
beneficial to everyone’s understanding.

Council Liaison Roles – Chapter 12
Staff is seeking Council direction specifically on whether the designee of “continuity” continues to make sense for these 
assignments. There was very little movement on this year’s assignment list from last year’s assignments. The Rules actually 
state (Chapter 12, Section 5) that continuity is intended for external agencies liaison roles; but the Council had designated 
Planning Commission and the Sustainability & Infrastructure Commission as continuity assignments. Council elected this 
year to change Planning Commission from Continuity to Mayor and Deputy Mayor as the standard. An unintended 
consequence of these Continuity designations is that a single council member could be on a high-profile assignment for 
years and that may not be fair for other council members seeking a high-profile assignment. A discussion is desired on 
whether to continue the practice of Continuity designation or to limit or cap it in some way.
Board and Commission Appointment Process – Chapter 13
Several years ago, the City established an appointment sub-committee comprised of the Mayor and the Council liaison to 
the commission to be the interviewing body and to make the recommendation to the Council for any openings on the 
City’s Boards and Commissions. Any Council Member could join any appointment sub-committee by requesting to do so. 
All appointment sub-committee meetings are designated as Brown Act bodies and are open and public. In addition, they 
established the practice of creating a ranked eligibility list from those interviewed. The intent was to address long term 
vacancies that were occurring on commissions and be more efficient and timely in filling those vacancies. The staff liaison

Council Member Conduct – Chapter 6
Staff would like to receive direction on strengthening this section to provide more clarity on expectations, clearly laid out 
rules, relevant definitions to increase a common understanding, a process for public concern and complaints, and a 
discussion around repercussions for not following an adopted code of conduct. 
First, many cities, towns, counties and states are actively looking at establishing or strengthening a Code of Conduct in 
response to the national trend highlighting a loss of civility and decorum in the political sphere and in public processes. In 
California, there is a significant increase in our professional ListServ inquiries to share codes of conduct as many 
organizations struggle with addressing this need. At the City Clerk’s New Law conference this past December, “When 
Decorum and Civility Do Not Work, Then What?” which has not previously been an educational offering at past 
conferences. From the national to the local level there is a need to fearlessly address this. In the City of San Mateo, we 
have personally experienced issues on and off the dais and this has led to a loss of public trust. There is a great deal of 
public interest and comment in defining a robust Code of Conduct which includes a procedure to hold public officials 
accountable and includes an avenue and process to empower action when an inappropriate action occurs that will meet 
the needs of the City of San Mateo community.
It is incumbent upon the City Council to take the lead on establishing a robust Code of Conduct as the governing body sets 
the example for its appointed bodies, employees, and residents to follow. By modeling adherence to rules of civility and 
decorum, accountability, and ethics the City Council sets the tone for public discourse and action.

Purpose and Overview  
This handbook provides guidelines describes the way for the San Mateo City Council that 
describe the way the Councilto conduct does its business and the expectations for members, 
staff and the public to operate and engage with each other and the community as a whole . It is 
a compilation of all Council‐related rules and procedures that have been formally adopted by 
Council.  
The purpose of these Rules and Procedures (Rules) guidelines is to set a standard of 
professionalism, civility and decorum for the conduct of the City Council’s business. San Mateo 
has a long and proud tradition of open government and civil, intelligent public discourse. These 
guidelines are intended to enhance public participation and Council debate so that the best 
possible decisions can be made for San Mateo.

Chapter 3. 
Key Governance Principles 
In 2022, the City will conducted its first by‐district election for City Council members, with 
districts 1, 3, and 5 holding district elections in November 2022 and districts 2 and 4 to holding 
its first district elections in November 2024. This new election process changes how council 
members are elected, but not how they govern. It is the intent of the City Council to continue 
the practice of governing with a citywide perspective. The City Council acts as a body, not as 
individuals. An overriding principle is that all members of the City Council continue to serve all 
of the people of San Mateo. 
Key Governance Principles 
1. 
Serving the Entire Community 
A. 
The City Council wishes to remain united in its service to the community and will 
govern by considering the needs and interests of the community as a whole. 
B. 
Members of the City Council will continue to serve all residents, regardless of 
whether they live within their district. Any member of the City Council may be 
responsive to requests from anyone in the community. 
2. 
City Management and Governance 
Professional management in a Council‐Manager form of government will continue, with 
all of the best management practices that the City Council currently receives.  
3. 
Use of City Resources 
A. 
The City Council will strive to use taxpayer resources efficiently and effectively, 
while providing consistent services for all residents. Resources will be allocated 
based on the City’s long‐term strategic plan and citywide considerations of service 
levels and financial capacity.  
B. 
Budget allocations and financial considerations will not be made based on districts. 
4. 
Distribution of Council Duties and Events 
Fair distribution of duties and assignments is a priority. Long‐standing practices regarding 
invitations to members of the City Council to events and activities, appointments to 
committees and various bodies, and the process for board and commission appointments 
will remain in effect.

Chapter 5. 
Selection of Mayor and Deputy Mayor 
1. 
City Council reorganization, including rotation of the office of Mayor and Deputy Mayor 
occurs annually at the regular meeting in December.  
2. 
The City Council believes that experience as a Council Member will assist those who are 
selected to serve as Mayor or Deputy Mayor, and that it is in the City’s best interest that 
these selections be made in a manner that permits the Mayor and Deputy Mayor to gain 
that experience in cycles of governing and to acclimate themselves to the jobs, tasks, and 
roles prior to assuming their respective offices.  
3. 
It is appropriate to base such succession on rotation, and seniority and election results 
according to the following: 
A. 
Each Council Member has a specified position in the rotation sequence. In any 
year, the first person in the sequence is typically selected as Mayor, and the 
second in the sequence as the Deputy Mayor.  
B. 
After serving as Mayor, that Council Member moves to the end of the rotation 
sequence, and the other four Council Members move forward. 
C. 
When a Council Member is newly elected or appointed (as the result of a cancelled 
election), that new Council Member goes into the fourth position in the rotation, 
after all incumbents, but before the current outgoing Mayor.  
D. 
If two or more Council Members are newly elected or appointed at the same time, 
the newly elected or appointed Council Members shall draw lots to determine 
their position relative to one another in the rotational sequence.  
E. 
If a Council Member is appointed to fill a mid‐term vacancy, that Council Member 
will go to the bottom end of the rotational list. 
F. 
Incumbent Council Members, when re‐elected, do not begin anew at the bottom 
end of the list, but rather retain their placement in the rotation. 
G. 
If for any reason (death, resignation, recall, not seeking re‐election, etc.) a Council 
Member is removed from the rotation, the remaining Council Members move 
forward in the sequence.  
H. 
A Council Member may decline to serve as Mayor or Deputy Mayor. In this case, 
the office would pass to the next Council Member on the list. The Council Member 
who declined may will drop back one position in the rotation.

desired, that item may be removed by a Council Member and considered 
separately. Members of the Public may speak to any Consent Calendar item 
without the item being pulled.

Members of the public wishing to comment on any item not appearing on the 
agenda may address the City Council at this time. State law prevents Council from 
taking action on any matter not on the agenda. Comments may be referred to staff 
for follow up. Public comment is limited to a total of 15 minutes unless Council by 
majority extends the total time; however, an opportunity for additional public 
comment maywill be provided later in the agenda, if needed. 
E. 
Public Hearing 
Duly noticed hearings as mandated by local, state, or federal law, providing an 
opportunity for public review and comment of a proposed action by the Council.

PROPOSED RULES 
 
1. Social Media 
City Council member use of social media, personal webpages and messaging boards are a 
First Amendment right; however, the use of these media could result in violations of these 
Rules, and potentially of the Brown Act. As has been decided by the U.S. Supreme Court 
in numerous cases, Freedom of Speech is not unlimited. 
 
a. City Council members are prohibited from discussing or publishing any information 
regarding any item that is, or will be, before City Council, Commission or Board on 
any social media platform, message board, or personal webpage, except their official 
City of San Mateo webpage. 
 
b. No City Council member shall serve or operate as a moderator, approver, lead, or any 
similar position that includes functions of censorship, suspension or removal of 
posting permissions, or removal of accounts on any social media platform. 
 
2. Electronic Communications During Meetings 
 
a. City Council members are prohibited from possessing or using any electronic 
communication device during City Council meetings unless they are city-owned and 
monitored as part of the city’s IT network. 
 
b. Make public any communications received by a Council Member on an allowed 
device during a City Council meeting that is not automatically being displayed and 
memorialized for public record.

3. Conflicts of Interest, Financial
The California Fair Political Practices Act clearly defines financial Conflicts of Interest.
The city currently monitors the submission of Statements of Financial Interest; however,
the current Guidelines state, “With full consideration of the City Attorney’s advice, it is
up to each individual Council Member to decide for him/herself whether there is a
conflict of interest”. The City Attorney should determine if there is a financial Conflict of
Interest.
a.
In addition to the requirement to file truthful reports of Economic Interests, City
Council members are to follow the California Fair Political Practices Act and follow
the City Attorney’s determination as to financial conflicts of interest.
b. Any City Council member with a Conflict of Interest shall follow the recusal process.
4. Conflicts of Interest, General
Ethics San Mateo believes that more general conflicts of interest create bias. Bias, even
if subconscious, can violate the requirement to be fair and impartial. Membership in
general advocacy or support of any organization would not necessarily be evidence of
bias, unless there is sufficient evidence that the council member could be biased due to
their membership or support. It also would not include activity with government
organizations.
a.
To avoid bias in decision-making, any Council member determined to have a conflict
of interest due to active membership in, alignment with, advocacy for, or is an activist
with any advocacy, activist or special interest groups shall recuse themselves from
discussions about and voting on any item which falls within the group’s
concentration.
b. The City Attorney would review and make a recommendation to the City Council
regarding a potential general conflict of interest. The City Council would decide
whether the Council/Commission member has a specific conflict of interest. If the
decision differs from the recommendation of the City Attorney, mediation or
arbitration shall be employed to make the determination.
c.
Any City Council member determined to have a conflict of interest on a specific issue
shall follow the recusal process.

Statement of Position

Conflicts of Interest, Activism, and Standards for Ethical Conduct

INTRODUCTION

Ethics San Mateo is issuing this Statement of Position to outline our concerns about the effect of
conflicts of interest and activism on decisions made by City of San Mateo government officials.

The mission of Ethics San Mateo is to lead and support efforts by the residents of San Mateo to
ensure our city is served according to the highest level of ethical standards and behavior. We
advocate for the establishment of a comprehensive Code of Conduct for all city officials,
including council members commissioners and staff, which sets standards for ethical behavior
that are enforceable.

Accountability on the part of our city’s leaders is key to good governance. We believe the
common core element to much of the turmoil observed by the residents of the city is that the
current rules are insufficient. Several Statements of Position have previously been issued
regarding the lack of ethical standards and behavior. Please refer to our website,
www.EthicsSanMateo.com, for these publications.

SUMMARY

The City of San Mateo does not have a comprehensive set of rules and regulations that set
standards regarding conflicts of interest or ethical behavior which are reasonably enforceable.
There is no codified method available for city government to address breaches of ethical
standards unless they are considered crimes. A commissioner cannot be removed without a super
majority vote (majority plus one) of council members, except in the case of a commission of a
crime of moral turpitude, missing meetings without permission, or losing qualifications.’ There
is no censure process for city council to employ when a commissioner or council member
commits a clear breach of ethical norms.

GENERAL OBSERVATIONS

One of the reasons Ethics San Mateo was founded was because, far too often, San Mateo
residents were expressing their views but were not being really listened to, or given any weight,
and were even subjected to personal insults by city officials. ESM believes that this is the
result of some of our city leaders aligning themselves with powerful organizations and
individuals who seek favorable treatment.

Of significant interest is the Boards and Commissions Rules and Procedures section which deals
with Conflicts of Interest for Planning Commissioners. “With full consideration of the City
Attorney’s advice, it is up to each individual Commissioner to decide for themselves whether
there is a conflict of interest.”? ESM concludes that this clearly indicates that the fox is truly
watching the henhouse. It is mind-boggling that a commissioner can decide on their own
whether they have a conflict of interest. There seems to be no oversight or recourse.

The primary role of the Planning Commission is “to increase public input and participation in the
determination of City policies and some programs” “Boards and Commissions are appointed by
the Council to seek and hear input from San Mateo's residents and businesses. Members make
recommendations to the Council based upon public input.” *

Ethics San Mateo has, on several occasions, publicly expressed concerns about the Planning
Commission’s ability to be fair and unbiased. ESM believes the observed events and published
items are examples of what show bias caused by conflicts of interest, the embrace of activist
agendas, or other breaches of ethical norms.

These are complex issues. The research into factual and verifiable information has also been
complex. ESM does not, and will not, state as facts something that is unsubstantiated and
unverifiable. If we make an error in fact, we will publish a correction. Our opinions in this
Statement of Position, based on the stated facts and reviewed and approved by our entire Board
of Directors, are clearly stated as such.

Social media is also of concern. Because of a “reviewers” ability to make comments and posts

technologically enabled, have taken advantage of their ability to limit distribution, hide or erase
postings, messaging, emails, etc. Some of the information ESM has received qualifies as the
latter. However, we simply will not state as factual any information that is not verified.

appointed council members and commissioners from participating as leads or reviewers on
social media sites such as Nextdoor.

CONCLUSIONS

Ethics San Mateo has concluded that the city does not have a comprehensive set of enforceable
rules and regulations that set standards regarding conflicts of interest or ethical behavior for non-
criminal acts. There is no codified method available for city government to address breaches of
ethical standards which are not crimes. A commissioner cannot be removed without a super
majority vote (majority plus one) of council members, except in the case of a commission of a
crime of moral turpitude, missing meetings without permission, or losing qualifications.*” There
is no censure process for city council to employ when a commissioner or council member
commits a clear breach of ethical norms.

Staff conducted a site-by-site review of all potential development sites, citywide. As will be demonstrated
below, staff currently believes that the RHNA, plus a reasonable buffer, can be accommodated within the

Development Potential Ranking. Each site — or potential aggregation of sites — was analyzed to discern
the likelihood and feasibility of development during the period 2023-2031. Factors such as
underperforming or vacant uses, owner or developer interest, age and size of current improvements, site

size, and site constraints were reviewed. Depending on these considerations, sites were ranked from 1 to
