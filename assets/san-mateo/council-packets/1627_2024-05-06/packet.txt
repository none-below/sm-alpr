SUBJECT:
Private Development and Right-of-Way Support Services – Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve Amendment No. 1 to the agreement with Anchor Engineering for private development and right-of-way support 
services to increase the agreement by $180,000 for a total not-to-exceed amount of $680,000; extend the term of the 
agreement through June 30, 2026; and authorize the Public Works Director to execute Amendment No. 1 in substantially 
the form presented.
BACKGROUND:
On July 18, 2022, City Council approved an agreement with Anchor Engineering for private development and right-of-way 
support services for $500,000 to address an anticipated increase of Wireless Communications Facilities permit (Wireless 
Permits) applications requiring Public Works staff review.
Wireless Permits:
Wireless Permits are a type of Public Works permit that require significant staff resources to review when compared to 
other types of Public Works permits. The City currently has four (4) master license agreements with telecommunications 
companies. There is currently one company that is actively submitting Wireless Permit applications for approximately 200 
planned sites.
As required by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), City staff must review Wireless Permit applications within a 
specified time ranging from 60 – 90 days (depending on the type of permit), this specified time is known as the “shot 
clock.” The shot clock starts when the applicant submits the application and stops when the permit is approved. If staff 
cannot complete the review within the shot clock, the application is automatically deemed approved by existing federal 
law. To pay for all costs associated with City staff time, consultant review, and processing of Wireless Permit applications, 
the Fiscal Year 2023-24 fee schedule charges a minimum deposit of $4,000 or $2,500, depending on the whether the 
application is for a standard Wireless Permit or Section 6409 Permit.
Support Services for Wireless Permits Review and Processing:
With the existing staff and Public Works review workload, Public Works does not have sufficient resources to review and 
process all the anticipated Wireless Permit applications within the timeframe allowed by the FCC shot clock. Since Anchor 
Engineering has come on board in 2022, they have provided satisfactory turnaround times to meet growing demand for 
Wireless Permit review and processing. The Achor Engineering expense rate was $311,986 in one calendar year. At this 
time, there is $273,429 remaining of the original $500,000 contract amount.
To meet the short deadlines, Public Works recommends extending the existing agreement with Anchor Engineering for an

SUBJECT:
Permanent Local Housing Allocation – Grant Application
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to reapply to the State of California for Permanent Local Housing Allocation grant program fund and 
approve an amendment to the five-year funding plan.
BACKGROUND:
The purpose of the Permanent Local Housing Allocation (PLHA) grant is to provide a permanent source of funding to all 
local governments in California to help cities and counties implement plans to increase housing affordability. This is a 
flexible funding source that can be used for a wide range of activities from increasing the housing supply to providing 
supportive services. The PLHA application requires the City to develop a Five-Year Plan to allocate spending. The City has 
received allocations of $341,894 in the first year (2019); $531,409 in the second year (2020); and $584,800 in the third 
year (2021). To receive the fourth year allocation (2022), the City needs to adopt a resolution (Attachment 1) to reapply for 
the PLHA fund, approve an amendment to the Five-Year Plan (Attachment 2) based on the new allocation, and submit an 
application to the State Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD) to receive the fourth year grant 
allocation of $293,103 (Attachment 3). The fourth year allocation is substantially lower than the third year, which drives 
the need for a plan amendment and funding adjustment. The PLHA entitlement allocation is based upon the previous 
year’s collection of title recording fees, which can vary from year to year, which resulted in the large discrepancy.
For the fourth year PLHA grant funding allocation, four subrecipient organizations have been identified: Bridge Housing, 
LifeMoves, HIP Housing and Samaritan House. 
LifeMoves – Council directed staff to use PLHA funds for rapid rehousing during a special meeting study session on June 1, 
2020. Rapid rehousing includes rent assistance for a period of approximately 6 to 12 months combined with supportive 
services to households who are homeless or at risk of homelessness. A rapid rehousing program was developed and the 
non-profit organization, LifeMoves, was selected to administer the program. The program provides clients who are at risk 
of homelessness and below 30% Area Median Income (AMI) with 10 months of rental stabilization and case management 
for a wide range of services including employment, education, mental health, and more. The program aims to serve 5 
individuals and 5 families annually. The rapid rehousing program has been a PLHA grant fund recipient for the past three 
years.
BRIDGE Housing - The affordable housing development Montara, which was completed in 2020 and is located at 2775 S. 
Delaware Street, includes 16 units for formerly homeless households. These targeted units include supportive services 
from Veterans Affairs and San Mateo County, in addition to rental assistance as part of the financing package provided by 
the County. In response to concerns raised at stakeholder meetings with neighboring properties, the City has worked with 
the developer, BRIDGE Housing, to expand its residence services at the property to make more services available as well as

expanding onsite hours for all of the tenants. To achieve the expanded services, a full-time services coordinator, in place of 
a half-time position, was created. This cost was not anticipated when the developer initially sought its funding

HIP Housing - HIP Housing was a long-time recipient of the Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) funds through 
the City and has been a City partner in both providing affordable housing units and maintaining their home-sharing 
program, which matches tenants who are unable to afford the high rents in the City with home-owners who are willing to 
rent out spare rooms in their homes. The strict guidelines of the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) 
forced HIP Housing to forgo participating in the CDBG Community Funding program and left a gap in funding. Staff 
recommends funding HIP Housing with a PLHA grant in order to return assistance to the home sharing program.
Samaritan House - Samaritan House operates the Safe Harbor emergency shelter, a facility that provides case 
management, site-based emergency and transitional housing support to the clients in their 105-bed facility. The City has 
provided consistent support to this program through local housing funds in the past in order to ensure they would not 
have to compete with other organizations in the limited CDBG Community Funding program. As a service that assists 
persons experiencing homelessness, funding this program would be compatible with the design of the PLHA program and 
would preserve flexible local housing funds that can be reserved for other long-term housing related projects.
To utilize these funds to support the aforementioned organizations, staff recommends that the Council approve an 
amended Five -Year PLHA Plan, as well as adopting a resolution to re-apply for PLHA funding for Year 4. The Five-Year Plan 
that is submitted to the State can be amended in the future if changes are desired.

received by the City, as they need to be requested through a release of funds after the approval of the Five-Year Plan 
amendment. The exact dollar amount for the final year will vary based upon the collection of the real estate recording 
fees, which is the revenue source for the PLHA fund. The total estimated dollar amount that the City will receive over five 
years is $2,051,364. Based on the funding available, the proposed budget for the fourth year of the PLHA grant is as

SUBJECT:
Downtown Parking Garage Safety Project – Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with Golden Bay Construction, Inc. for the Downtown Parking Garage Safety Project in the amount 
of $380,439; establish a contingency reserve in the amount of $76,000; and authorize the Public Works Director to execute 
the agreement in substantially the form presented and issue change orders as allowed by the contract documents and 
within the contingency amount.
BACKGROUND:
The Downtown Parking Garage Safety Project (Project) includes the design of pedestrian safety improvements at the 
entrances and exits of the Central Parking Garage and Main Street Parking Garage. Pedestrian-friendly improvements 
include audible and visual pedestrian and vehicle warning devices, flexible delineators to provide physical separation 
between the drive aisle and walking paths, speed bumps, and updated pavement markings and traffic signs to address site-
specific safety needs at each facility. The engineer’s estimate is $400,000.
 
The Project was advertised on March 5, 2024, and a total of three (3) bids were received and opened on April 2, 2024. The 
bids received are listed below in order of increasing bid amount. 
 
Contractor
Grand Total Bid Amount
BuildCorp, Inc.
$326,700.00
Golden Bay Construction, Inc.
$380,439.00
Tennyson Electric LLC.
$589,125.00
 
BuildCorp, Inc., which submitted the lowest bid, was found nonresponsive because Bid Item No. 1 – Mobilization was listed 
at 12.2% of the total contract value, which is above the maximum 10% allowable set forth in the contract book. Therefore, 
BuildCorp, Inc. did not submit a bid that conformed to the contract book requirements and found nonresponsive. Staff 
then reviewed the second lowest bid, submitted by Golden Bay Construction, Inc. and determined that Golden Bay 
Construction, Inc. is the lowest responsible, responsive bidder, and recommends it be awarded the Agreement. 
Given the limited information available on the existing electrical system in the two garages, there is a high likelihood that 
unforeseen issues will arise during construction. These unanticipated costs will need to be covered by the contingency 
reserve. Accordingly, staff recommends a 20% contingency to be established given the small size of the construction 
agreement and the possibility of significant unforeseen issues arising.

SUBJECT:
Underground Flow Equalization System – Contingency Amendment 
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an increase to the contingency reserve associated with the Anderson Pacific Engineering Construction, Inc. 
construction contract for the Underground Flow Equalization System (UFES) project in the amount of $771,000, for a 
revised total contingency reserve of $5,671,000; and authorize the Public Works Director to issue change orders within the 
contingency amount.
BACKGROUND:
The Clean Water Program includes various projects to improve the City of San Mateo’s sanitary sewer system to improve 
flow management and increase capacity to prevent sanitary sewer overflows. Included in the Basins 2 and 3 project is the 
Underground Flow Equalization (UFES) project (Project), which consists of construction of a 5.3-million-gallon underground 
concrete wastewater holding structure beneath the San Mateo County Event Center’s parking lot and new sewers along 
Delaware Street and Saratoga Drive. Wet weather flows are designed to be conveyed toward UFES for temporary storage 
until the storm event is over and thus help prevent sanitary system overflows. 
On May 4, 2020, Council awarded the UFES construction contract to Anderson Pacific Engineering Construction, Inc. (APEC) 
in the amount of $55,277,330; established a contingency reserve of $4,500,000; and authorized the Public Works Director 
to execute the agreement and issue change orders within the contingency amount. The contingency reserve was 
established to address and mitigate potential unknowns that may arise during construction and was approximately 8.14% 
of the agreement value. 
On August 16, 2021, City Council approved Contract Change Order No. 4 to the UFES construction contract in the amount 
of $3,877,875 adding construction of approximately 745 linear feet of new 30-inch sewer on Delaware Street between 
25th and 28th Avenues to connect the southern end of the original UFES construction contract scope to the northern end 
of the Delaware Relief Sewer construction package. Council also approved an increase to the contingency reserve in the 
amount of $400,000, to bring the total contingency reserve to $4,900,000. To date, approximately $4.68 million of the 
approved contingency has been utilized to issue necessary negotiated change orders for the Project. A summary of the 
executed contract change orders is included in Attachment 1. 
The Project is nearing substantial completion and full beneficial use and operation by the City. However, the Project has 
encountered challenges during construction that resulted in additional work, including modifications to mechanical, 
electrical, and civil related construction activities that are required to achieve final completion of the Project. These 
additions will require contract change orders that will exceed the remaining contingency balance of $223,414. Staff is 
currently evaluating and negotiating the change order proposals submitted by APEC to complete the additional scope.

SUBJECT:
Citywide Small Trash Capture Project – Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with United Storm Water, Inc. for the Citywide Small Trash Capture Project in the amount of 
$697,277; establish a contingency reserve in the amount of $105,000; and authorize the Public Works Director to execute 
the agreement in substantially the form presented and issue change orders as allowed by the contract documents and 
within the contingency amount.
BACKGROUND:
The San Francisco Bay Regional Water Quality Control Board (Regional Board) has mandated the City of San Mateo (City) to 
reduce trash discharged to the San Francisco Bay through the City’s storm drain system based on a 2009 baseline trash 
generation rate. The permit requires a 100% reduction rate, relative to the 2009 baseline values, by June 30, 2025. 
To date, the City has achieved approximately 87% reduction through the following methods: existing trash capture 
devices, source control measures, creek and shoreline cleanups, street sweeping, and other control mechanisms. The 
credits for jurisdiction-wide source control efforts and creek and shoreline cleanups will no longer count toward 
compliance after June 30, 2025. In order to ensure the City achieves the 100% trash reduction mandate by June 30, 2025, 
the City must install additional trash capture devices in strategic trash generation areas throughout the City. Non-
compliance with trash reduction targets can result in enforcement actions or fines from the Regional Board, or other 
exposures.
The Citywide Small Trash Capture Project (Project) is designed to satisfy Regional Board requirements, bringing the City 
closer to the required 100% reduction by installing approximately 450 small trash capture inlet devices at strategic trash 
generation areas throughout San Mateo.

BACKGROUND:
The Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) Program provides flexible funding for community development projects 
and programs to assist individuals and families from extremely low- to moderate-income households as defined by the 
Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). The City receives an entitlement grant based on a national 
formula and has participated in this program since 1977.
The City also received an entitlement amount of HOME Investment Partnerships Program (HOME) funds from 1994 to 
2015 that was used to assist housing programs and projects for very low-income households. In 2016, the City joined a 
consortium with the County of San Mateo and the City of South San Francisco to pool HOME funds to provide more 
efficiency and timeliness in expending those funds. The City continues to collect loan payments from loans made prior to 
2015 and must re- program those funds into HOME-eligible projects.
2024-25 Annual Action Plan
The Annual Action Plan, which is included as Attachment 1, outlines the City’s budget for the coming program year with 
details of the programs and projects that will be undertaken to address the needs identified in the Consolidated Plan. The 
Consolidated Plan is the Five-Year Plan which outline’s The City’s long-term needs low-income households face and the 
goals which they aim to address with HUD funding. The City has not yet received an award letter from HUD detailing the 
exact amount of funding it will receive for the coming year. However, the CDBG grant is estimated to be $740,000 for the 
2024-25 program year using past year averages. The following budget was drawn based upon this estimate and will be 
adjusted slightly. The remaining funds for the budget will come from program income ($8,000) and unspent funds that 
have carried over from the prior year ($142,000). The program income is generated by repayment on housing 
rehabilitation loans and there was a significant carryover from the prior year because of funds which have remain unspent 
in various contracts over the years which will now be repurposed into a street lighting project.  The annual plan also 
includes programs to address affordable housing and other community needs. The major CDBG activities for the upcoming 
year are:
•
Administration – A maximum of 20% of the CDBG grant is eligible for administrative costs ($105,000). Funds will 
mainly be used to cover City staff salaries who administer the grant, plus material and service costs.  Also included 
in this category is one grant to Project Sentinel for the provision of fair housing services in the amount of $25,000.

Community Funding – The cap for public services is 15% of the grant and prior year program income ($111,000). At

square footage in excess of 5,000 square feet pay a fee to mitigate the impact of new jobs on local housing supply.

programs that City will watch and apply for as they become available including Local Early Action Planning grants to support planning efforts to 
increase the supply of housing, as well as the Local Housing Trust Fund Program.  The City also tracks and supports legislation that promotes and

Introduction
The City is committed to using available tools to increase the availability of affordable housing. Current programs include an inclusionary zoning

Actions it planned to remove or ameliorate the negative effects of public policies that serve as barriers to affordable housing such 
as land use controls, tax policies affecting land, zoning ordinances, building codes, fees and charges, growth limitations, and 
policies affecting the return on residential investment
Streamlining the approval process for residential developments is a high priority for the City of San Mateo.  A number of efforts are planned this 
program year.
•
Through 21 Elements, the City has joined with a County-wide effort to establish an ADU center to support additional ADU construction 
and monitor affordability. 
•
In 2023, the City initiated research with consultants to analyze economic feasibility to incentivize affordable housing production in large 
projects, to evaluate minimum residential density requirements for large commercial mixed-use projects, and promote more 
inclusionary family housing (two- and three-bedroom affordable housing units. The City adopted code amendments to allow Zoning 
Administrator approval for housing projects up to 25 units, subject to Objective Design Standards.
•
In 2024, the City will streamline the development review process by removing the pre-application and third-party design review 
requirements through an updated resolution with City Council. The City plans to amend the Zoning Code to reduce off-street parking 
requirements for residential uses and will initiate comprehensive code amendments to permit residential uses for all Commercial and

Council for feedback and direction and will amend the code to be consistent of AB 2162 to allow by-right 100% affordable housing that 
has 25% or 12 units of permanent supportive housing where multi-family or mixed-use housing is permitted.
•
The City continues to study and evaluate all development fees to measure the impacts on residential construction. As of FY2024, the 
study continues to be underway as planners try to find ways to lower construction fees to make them comparable to our neighboring 
cities.

Housing Preferences and Distribution 
In light of the huge response, usually resulting in hundreds of applications for affordable housing 
projects, San Mateo adopted its Marketing Policies for Affordable Housing to establish a fair and 
effective process to lease rental units, and sell ownership units to new eligible buyers. The policy 
establishes household size priorities for bedroom sizes and a local preference for people who either live 
or work in San Mateo.  The locality preference is a result of the City’s desire to prioritize local funds for 
local residents and to reduce commutes and resulting traffic congestion for those who work in the City.  
The City has analyzed the racial/ethnic make-up of San Mateo using census data and compared it to the 
local region, defined as the County of San Mateo, and found no material differences in racial/ethnic 
percentages, therefore determined that this policy does not have a disparate impact on any minority 
group. This preference does not apply if a particular funding source prohibits a local preference.
The City has provided subsidies for a variety of housing types including rental, ownership, transitional 
and supportive housing units in a variety of locations and for a range of income levels, in an effort to 
serve as many households as possible. This is extremely challenging in a city of only nine square miles in 
size with one of the highest land and housing costs in the nation.  Despite these challenges, only 26% of 
the subsidized housing in San Mateo is located in the CDBG Priority Neighborhood, which consists of the 
lowest income households and highest percentage of minorities.  This planning plus the local preference 
provides residents opportunities to live in a variety of neighborhoods citywide.  This is further assisted 
by the fact that all private developments, regardless of location, provide inclusionary units which results 
in advancing a variety of housing choices for community members.

By replacing the phrase, “the mayor” with text calling for a special election, the draft wording would remove the authority 
of the Mayor to fill vacancies and instead send the decision to the voters, if the Council is unable to decide within the 
allotted time. Notably, in 1983 Measure C was submitted to the voters that would have accomplished the same purpose 
with somewhat different wording, but that measure failed by a 46.4% to 53.6% vote.  
 
The time for the Council to decide on filling a vacancy would also be increased from 30 to 60 days. This is important 
because if a vacancy occurs due to a Councilmember being elected to another office, that will likely occur in November. 
The existing 30-day period would then expire in December during the holiday season. Extending the period to 60 days 
would allow for increased outreach to the community and a more deliberate application, interview, and selection process. 
The Council would also have more time to weigh the benefits and drawbacks (such as expense) of a special election, versus 
deciding to fill the vacancy after extensive community input. 
 
Finally, the draft language eliminates text allowing candidates receiving the most votes to serve the longer of unexpired 
terms (and addressing tie votes) where a majority of the Council seats become vacant.  Following the shift to district

CHARTER AMENDMENT PROCESS
Under state law, the City Council may propose a charter amendment and submit it to the voters at the next statewide 
general election, at least 88 days before the election. Govt. §34458(a). The charter amendment will not be adopted until
it is approved by a majority vote.  Before approving the submittal of a charter amendment to the voters, the City Council 
must hold at least two public hearings, with published and posted notice, at least 21 calendar days prior to the date of 
each public hearing. Govt. §34458(b). April 1 was the first public hearing.  
Tonight is the second public hearing, which is being held at least 30 days after the first public hearing, as required.  At least 
one of the two hearings must be held outside of normal business hours to facilitate public participation.  Govt. §34458(b). 
In this case, both public hearings met that requirement. The Council cannot vote on whether to approve submitting the 
proposal to the voters until 21 days after the second public hearing. Govt. §34458(b). The Charter amendment must also

2.13
Quorum.
A majority of the council shall constitute a quorum for the transaction of any business, but a less

2.14
Voting.
The affirmative vote of at least a majority of the council shall be necessary to adopt any resolutions, and 
the affirmative vote of a majority of the council shall be sufficient for adoption of ordinances except 
where a larger vote shall be required by law or other provisions of this Charter. Such votes shall be taken 
by "ayes" and "noes" and entered upon the record.
2.15
Ordinances, Generally.
(a) All proposed ordinances introduced in the council shall be in printed or typewritten form. 
The enacting clause of all ordinances passed by the council shall read as follows: "The Council of 
the City of San Mateo ordains as follows:". The enacting clause of all ordinances initiated by the 
people shall read as follows: "The People of the City of San Mateo ordain as follows:".
(b) No ordinance other than an emergency ordinance shall be passed by the council on the day 
of its introduction, nor within five days thereafter, nor at any time other than a regular or 
adjourned regular meeting.
(c) A notice of the general nature of the proposed ordinance or a copy thereof, and of the time 
and place for its consideration by the council shall be given to those news media to whom notice 
of special meetings of the council would be required under the general law of the state. A 
reasonable number of copies of the proposed ordinance shall be made available for public 
inspection at reasonable times in the clerk's office and at each of the city libraries, and such 
notice shall be given by the city clerk as soon as practicable following introduction of the 
ordinance. Failure to comply with this subsection shall not invalidate the ordinance, but a willful 
violation shall constitute a misdemeanor and malfeasance in office.
(d) A proposed ordinance may be amended or modified between the time of its introduction 
and the time of its final passage, provided its general scope and original purpose are retained. 
All ordinances shall be signed by the mayor and attested by the city clerk. Ordinances shall be 
published at least once in the official city newspaper before becoming effective; in lieu of 
publishing the full text of an ordinance, the city clerk may publish a summary of the scope and 
general purpose of the ordinance in the official city newspaper and post the full text of the 
ordinance in the office of the city clerk as well as publish the full text of an ordinance on the 
official city website.
2.16
Emergency Ordinances.
Any ordinance declared by the council to be necessary as an emergency measure for preserving the 
public peace, health, safety, or welfare and containing the reasons for its urgency, may be introduced 
and passed at one and the same meeting and, if passed by a majority of the council plus one, shall 
become effective immediately. As soon as practicable after its passage an emergency ordinance shall be 
published in the official city newspaper, but the validity of the ordinance shall not depend upon such 
publication. In lieu of publishing the full text of an emergency ordinance, a summary thereof may be 
published in the same manner as other ordinances. Every such ordinance, except one for emergency 
appropriations or calling an election, shall automatically stand repealed as of the ninety-first day 
following the date on which it was adopted, but this shall not prevent reenactment of the ordinance if 
the emergency still exists.

In addition to members of the city council, the mayor, city manager, and city attorney, the officers and

incompatible, the city council may combine in one person the powers and duties of two or more 
officers. There shall be a separate Police Department and Free Public Library, each of which shall remain 
as a separate department with its own department head, provided, however, that the city council may 
enter into agreements with other agencies for the joint provision of police or library services.
The city council may assign additional functions or duties to offices, departments or agencies. No office 
provided by this Charter to be filled by appointment by the city manager may be consolidated with an 
office to be filled by appointment by the city council.

New Department.
Each department created by the city council shall be headed by an officer as department head who shall 
be appointed by and serve at the pleasure of the city manager.

5.01
Fiscal Year.
Unless changed by ordinance adopted by the affirmative vote of a majority of the council plus one, the 
fiscal year shall begin on the first day of July and shall end on the last day of June of the following year.
5.02

information required by the city manager to develop a budget, including capital accounts, conforming to

The council shall establish reserves which in its discretion are proper.

of the city clerk at least 10 days prior to said hearing, and copies of the proposed budget shall be 
furnished at a reasonable charge to persons requesting same upon payment therefor.

at the same time budget recommendations for the next succeeding fiscal year are submitted to 
the council by the city manager.
(b) The capital program shall include:
(1) A clear, general summary of its contents.
(2) A list of all capital improvements which are proposed to be undertaken during the 
five fiscal years next ensuing, with appropriate supporting information as to the 
necessity for such improvements.
(3) Cost estimates, method of financing, and time schedules for each such improvement.
(4) The estimated annual cost of operating and maintaining facilities to be constructed 
or acquired.

To the extent that the Constitution does not limit the setting of the ad valorem property tax rate, the

SUBJECT:
  Council Member Compensation – Ordinance Introduction
RECOMMENDATION:
Introduce an ordinance increasing compensation for City Council members from $600 per month to $1000 per month, 
effective January 2025.
BACKGROUND:
On January 2, 2024, staff presented information to the City Council regarding a potential increase to Council member 
compensation.  As stated in that staff report, the City Charter states that, “The mayor and other members of the city 
council shall each receive $600.00 per month compensation for their services as council. Compensation may be adjusted in 
the manner authorized by the general laws of the State for general law cities of the same population as San Mateo.” Chart. 
§2.03. The Charter therefore builds in flexibility to adjust Council compensation consistent with general law cities without 
a Charter amendment.  
Under state law SB 329 (2023) effective on January 1, 2024, compensation for council members in general law cities may 
be increased, and for cities between 75,000 and 150,000, the maximum compensation will be $1,900 per month, although 
inflationary or other limited increases are permitted as specified. Automatic future increases are prohibited.  Govt. 
§36516(a)(5).  Any adjustment to Council compensation must be accomplished by ordinance.  At the January 2 study 
session, a majority of the City Council directed staff to proceed with increasing monthly compensation to $1000.
The current Charter language and compensation amount was set by Measure F, approved by the voters on November 5, 
2002.  Thus, more than twenty-one years have passed since the last increase in Council compensation.  Applying a salary 
increase of 5% per year since the last adjustment in 2002 would have resulted in a current monthly salary of one thousand 
two hundred and thirty dollars ($1,230).  According to a California Attorney General opinion, the 5 percent increase is not 
compounded, meaning that the 5 percent salary increase authorized for city council members “is to be applied to the 
currently received salary amount for the total number of years since the last salary adjustment.”  89 Ops Cal Atty Gen 159, 
161-62 (2006).  This information is also included in the draft ordinance as proposed findings, as required by state law.
Also, a salary increase for the Council may only take effect once a council member commences a new term. Specifically, “a 
change in compensation does not apply to a council member during the council member's term of office. This prohibition 
shall not prevent the adjustment of the compensation of all members of a council serving staggered terms whenever one 
or more members of the city council becomes eligible for a salary increase by virtue of the council member beginning a 
new term of office.” Govt. §36516.5. What this means is that if the Council chooses to approve a salary increase, it will not 
apply until after the next municipal election.  To that end, the proposed ordinance is drafted to provide for increased 
compensation as of January 6, 2025, which is the beginning of the first full pay period in 2025, after the November 5, 2024 
consolidated election.  Certification of the election is anticipated at the December 9, 2024 regular Council meeting.  
Procedurally, the Council must consider the proposed ordinance during at least two regular meetings at least seven days

DRAFT ORDINANCE
Adding Section 2.03.020 “City Council Compensation” to the San Mateo Municipal Code 
WHEREAS, on November 5, 2002, the voters approved Measure F, which amended San Mateo City 
Charter section 2.03 by increasing the monthly compensation for members of the City Council from $300 per 
month to $600 per month and by further providing that compensation may be adjusted in the manner 
authorized by state law for general law cities with the same population as San Mateo; and
WHEREAS, Government Code section 36516, as amended by Senate Bill 329 (2023) effective January 1, 
2024, specifies the maximum amount of salary that may be approved by an ordinance passed by the City 
Council, based upon the population of the City; and
WHEREAS, the same state law authorizes salaries up to and including one thousand nine hundred dollars 
($1,900) per month in cities with a population of over 75,000 up to and including 150,000; and
WHEREAS, the City of San Mateo has a population of approximately 105,000; and
WHEREAS, Government Code section 36516 also authorizes salary increases beyond the amounts 
therein specified, in an amount equal to five percent (5%) for each calendar year from the operative date of the 
last salary adjustment; and
WHEREAS, more than twenty-one years have passed since the last compensation increase.  Applying a 
salary increase of 5% per year since the last adjustment in 2002 would have resulted in a current monthly salary 
of one thousand two hundred and thirty dollars ($1,230); and 
WHEREAS, the City Council finds that the City Council salary has not kept pace with inflation and 
adjusting its salary to that provided by state law may help the city council to become more diverse because 
increased compensation can help individuals from across different income levels receive sufficient income from 
their service to help ensure that they can continue to serve the public and support their families; and
WHEREAS, the City Council further finds that the time spent by councilmembers has increased, and 
those who are employed potentially have their earnings adversely affected; and
WHEREAS, the City would benefit from a diverse candidate pool in future elections from differing 
demographic backgrounds and geographic areas of the City.
