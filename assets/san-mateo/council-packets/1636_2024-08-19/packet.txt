SUBJECT:
Housing Element Programs for Minimum Residential Densities in Mixed-Use Development and Family Housing 
Requirements
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive a presentation and provide direction on potential approaches to establishing minimum residential densities in new 
mixed-use developments and setting minimum requirements for family-sized units in new housing development.
BACKGROUND:
On May 20, 2024, the City Council adopted the City’s 2023-2031 Housing Element, which provides a plan for at least 
10,286 new housing units and affirmatively furthers fair housing. The Housing Element establishes goals, policies, and 
programs to meet existing and projected housing needs for all segments of the community, including various household 
types, special needs populations, and affordable housing at all income levels. The adopted Housing Element was 
subsequently found in compliance with State Law by the Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD) on 
July 23, 2024.
Two policies in the Housing Element have priority status based on their timeline for implementation (2024/2025) and 
specific Council interest in these topics. These policies (H 1.5 and H 1.9) require further study and analysis of increasing 
production of family housing and setting minimum densities in mixed-use projects, as further described in their associated 
implementation actions:
Policy H 1.5 – Encourage Family Housing. Increase the number of new construction housing units that 
can accommodate families (larger units) when possible and require subsidized projects to include 
family-sized units. 
Implementation Actions:
a) Present options to City Council for requiring a minimum percentage of three-bedroom units in City 
subsidized projects.
b) Present options to City Council for requiring a minimum percentage of family-sized units in private 
development projects as part of the Inclusionary Policy update.
c) Should such policy recommendations not materialize into code amendments, explore alternative 
methods to promote family sized units through the community benefits program.
Policy H 1.9 – Establish Minimum Densities for Mixed-Use Projects. Create minimum residential density 
requirements to ensure mixed-use development will contain an appropriate amount of housing in 
commercial/office developments. 
Implementation Actions:

In addition to the Housing Element, the Land Use Element in the City’s General Plan 2040 (General Plan) includes polices 
that address this topic. The Land Use Element and Housing Element both encourage the development of a range of 
housing densities with a broad variety of housing types and sizes, located near Caltrain and El Camino Real to help reduce 
vehicle miles traveled (VMT) and greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The following General Plan Land Use policies address 
mixed-use development and minimum densities for new development in proximity to transit: 
Policy LU 1.3 – Optimize Development Opportunities. Encourage new development in major 
commercial and transit-oriented development areas, including the Downtown, Caltrain station areas 
and the El Camino Real corridor, to maximize the density and intensity specified in the Land Use Plan 
and to efficiently use land and infrastructure resources.
Policy LU 1.4 – Mixed-Use. Encourage mixed-use developments to include increased residential 
components to provide greater proximity between jobs and housing, promote pedestrian activity and 
reduce traffic congestion and vehicle miles traveled (VMT).
Policy LU 2.2 – Caltrain Stations and El Camino Real Minimum Densities. Require new residential 
development within a half mile of a Caltrain station or within one block of the El Camino Real corridor to 
meet the minimum density established by the applied land use designation and encourage new 
development to achieve maximum density.
DISCUSSION:
The goal of this meeting is to seek Council direction on setting minimum residential densities in mixed-use projects, setting 
minimum residential densities for mixed-use projects near transit, and creating requirements to increase the production of 
family housing. Establishing such standards would further the goals, policies, and programs of the Housing Element and 
the General Plan to produce a variety of housing types within the City and optimize opportunities to increase the use of 
multi-modal transportation.   
To support implementation of the policies noted above, the City retained Economic and Planning Systems, Inc. (EPS) to 
conduct a financial feasibility analysis for requiring minimum densities for mixed-use development with a residential 
component (Attachment 1); and requiring family-sized units in new development (Attachment 2). The key findings of the 
EPS analysis are included below for each policy.
Establishing Minimum Densities in Mixed-Use Development
The General Plan introduces a new land use designation, Mixed-Use, which allows for a range of commercial, retail, office 
and/or residential uses within the same site or building at variable intensities that would encourage people to live near 
where they work, play, or shop. Areas designated as Mixed-Use provide the greatest flexibility for the type of projects that 
may be developed. Mixed-use zones currently allow for housing, commercial and/or office, or a combination of these uses. 
Not all projects in mixed-use zones are required to include residential uses, and projects are allowed to be developed as 
non-residential projects, depending on property owner/developer interest, market conditions, and other factors.
Over the last several years, the City has seen a growing trend of mixed-use development projects with a residential 
component (See Table 2 in Attachment 1). Most of these projects have included significant residential components that 
meet or exceed the zoned base density. However, in a small number of cases, projects have provided  small residential 
components in order to qualify for concessions and waivers under the State Density Bonus Law (“SDBL”) (Govt. 65915 et

The SDBL provides general guidance that mixed-use developers, in partnership with an affordable housing developer, may 
be granted a density bonus for the commercial component of their project if they meet certain affordability thresholds on 
the residential component. Specifically, Government Code Section 65915.7 authorizes the City to grant a developer up to a 
20 percent density bonus (or other incentives) on the commercial portion of a project when at least 15 percent affordable 
units are provided in the residential portion. The law does not indicate a minimum standard for the residential component 
as a proportion of the overall project. This statute applies to projects that include a partnership between a commercial 
developer and an affordable housing developer. However, mixed-use development projects within the City are typically 
developed by a single developer that is responsible for both the commercial and residential components.  
The SDBL defines a “housing development” as “a development project for five or more residential units, including mixed-
use developments.”(Govt. 65915(i)). Most recent mixed-use development proposals in the City have included significant 
residential components that have met or exceeded the zoning district’s base density. However, for one approved mixed-
use project, the residential portion represented only 16 percent of the project’s total square footage. The developer 
requested a concession pursuant to SDBL to increase the commercial portion’s floor area ratio (FAR) while only meeting 
the minimum requirement for a housing development project under state density bonus law (five units with one 
affordable unit). The City had to grant the concession for increased FAR, and the City had no legal mechanism to require 
that a greater percentage of the project be for residential uses.
If there is City Council interest, the City has the option to establish specific regulations, such as a minimum residential 
density or minimum residential square footage requirements for mixed-use projects with a residential component. The 
City Council could also consider setting higher minimum residential densities for new development within a particular 
geographic boundary, such as within a radius of a Caltrain Station or along the El Camino Real corridor (See Map of Transit 
Stations and El Camino Real in Attachment 3). 
The EPS analysis (Attachment 1) shows that the City could also explore establishing a minimum residential density for 
mixed-use projects with a residential component in targeted mixed-use zones (such as sites close to transit). The 
prevalence of mixed-use projects in the development pipeline indicates market interest in including residential 
components in mixed-use projects and suggests that requiring a minimum amount of residential could be a viable policy 
option. Currently, there are eleven mixed-use projects in the San Mateo pipeline. For these projects, the residential 
components range from 9 to 75 units per acre, with many taking advantage of density bonus law, with the exception of the 
Draeger’s and 435 E 3rd Ave projects (see Table 1 below). The Table below summarizes the residential components within 
each mixed-use project, their residential proportion to the total square feet, and their relative proximity to transit.

Staff evaluated whether other cities have implemented minimum requirements for residential in mixed-use projects and

 Based on the information above and in the EPS report, staff seeks City Council direction on the following questions:
1.
Should the City establish minimum residential requirements for mixed-use development with a residential 
component?
2.
Should the City establish specific minimum residential densities within a certain radius of Caltrain stations or along 
the El Camino Real corridor? 
3.
Should commercial incentives be tiered, depending on the proportion of residential units provided?
Family Housing 
While the affordable housing need in San Mateo is greatest for one-bedroom units (Figure 2 in Attachment 2), 
overcrowding also remains an issue. The Housing Element identifies the need for larger units, including to better serve the 
needs of single female-headed households that are considered a special needs group with a severe housing shortage. 
Specifically, Housing Element Policy H 1.5 – Encourage Family Housing seeks to increase the number of new housing units 
that can accommodate families (larger units with three or more bedrooms) when possible.
Recognizing this need and high demand, the City has worked with its affordable housing partners to ensure that projects 
on City land, such as the Kiku Crossing, include a minimum number of three-bedroom units. However, private developers 
of market-rate housing or mixed-use developments are overwhelmingly producing studio, one-, and two-bedroom units. 
The City does not currently have any requirements for larger family-sized units within these projects and cannot mandate 
them without an adopted policy or ordinance.
The need for family-sized housing units is supported by data and additional analysis completed by EPS in the attached 
memo (Attachment 2). The memo suggests setting a requirement for at least one out of every five (20%) affordable units 
to be a minimum of three-bedrooms in size is a viable option that would result in roughly double the rate of three-
bedroom units produced and would not have a substantial negative impact on project feasibility.  
Based on the adopted Housing Element policy and EPS analysis, staff requests direction from Council on the following 
questions:
1.
Should the City require all developments with affordable units to provide a minimum of 20% of these units as 
three-bedroom units? 
2.
Should the City evaluate the feasibility of requiring a percentage of all units in a residential development (not just 
affordable units) to be family-sized?
Community Benefits
The Land Use Element and Housing Element also include policies related to updating the City’s Community Benefits 
program and better aligning it with State Density Bonus law. However, staff recommends not proceeding with this work 
until after the ballot measure to amend the Measure Y height and density limits has been decided by the voters in 
November of 2024. The outcome of the ballot measure will significantly influence a community benefits program 
framework, so any further work or analysis related to updating the City’s Community Benefits Program is recommended to 
wait until after the November election.

MEMORANDUM

To: Manira Sandhir, City of San Mateo
From: Darin Smith and Andrew Williams

Subject: San Mateo [nclusionary Housing Ordinance Amendment to
Promote Family Units; EPS #231040

Date: April 11, 2024

Background

The City of San Mateo (“City”) is considering updating its
Inclusionary Housing Policy (Inclusionary Policy) to meet demand
for family housing through the increased production of three-
bedroom units. The City’s 2023-2031 Housing Element contains
policies and programs to address the need to increase housing
units that can accommodate families and reviewing the City’s
Inclusionary Policy is part of this process.

The City engaged Economic & Planning Systems, Inc. (EPS) to
evaluate policy options that will target the construction of larger
new housing units to meet family demand through the City’s
Inclusionary Policy, To accomplish this task, EPS developed a
menu of inclusionary policy options with guidance from City staff
that evaluated the feasibility and economic impacts of adapting
inclusionary requirements. As it stands, the current City
Inclusionary Policy requires 15 percent of rental units in a new
development to be reserved as Low- or Very Low-income units,
with the expectation that these Below Market Rate (BMR) units
will have sizes and bedroom counts comparable to the mix of
market-rate units in the project. As an alternative compliance
measure within the City’s Inclusionary Policy, developers can
reserve as few as 10 percent of rental units as low-income if the
total number of BMR bedrooms is at least 15 percent of all
bedrooms in the project.

This memorandum details the larger context of the City’s
Inclusionary Policy and housing demand, EPS’s methodology and
development assumptions, and the multiple project and policy
scenarios analyzed. EPS anticipates that this memorandum will
be used by City staff and elected officials to guide decision-
making for updating the City’s Inclusionary Policy to address the
corresponding policies and programs in the Housing Element.

Analytical Approach Methodology

The assessment of financial feasibility for real estate development projects is based on
estimating development costs and potential revenue from rents or sale prices and
calculating associated financial] return metrics. These return metrics reflect the value of
investing in a development project and are a critical element in informing a developer’s
“go/no-go” decision. If a project is not expected to meet these targets, it is unlikely that
the project will be able to attract investors and unlikely that it will be built.

In this analysis of income-producing rental development projects, the feasibility threshold
is based on the return metric of “yield-on-cost,” calculated by dividing the annual net
operating income (NOI) once the project is complete and occupied by the total costs of
development. Yield-on-cost estimates are used widely in the real estate industry as one
of several key metrics to assess the performance and profitability of a development
project. Based on current market data, EPS believes that developers in San Mateo will
require a yield-on-cost of at least 5.5 percent. A yield-on-cost percentage lower than 5.5
percent indicates developers would face a more challenging environment where
developers could see lower profitability and other financial obstacles.

The model for this analysis was developed to compare the financial return of different
projects using multiple densities under a selection of policy scenarios. Through this
modeling, EPS engaged in an array of sensitivity testing to explore the impacts of
stipulating the creation of larger bedroom units.

based on simplified, generic prototypes meant to represent a typical or average
