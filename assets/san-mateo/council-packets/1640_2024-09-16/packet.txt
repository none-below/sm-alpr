SUBJECT:
4th and Railroad Parking Lot – Bespoke Development Term Sheet Options
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and provide direction on the proposed Term Sheet options and possible scenarios for the Bespoke public-private 
partnership development project, which includes the City-owned parking lot at 4th and Railroad Avenue.
 
BACKGROUND:
The City is currently a party to an Exclusive Negotiating Agreement (ENA) for the redevelopment of the City-owned surface 
parking lot located at 4th and Railroad Avenue adjacent to the parcel where the former Talbot’s building is located. The 
ENA is with a three-party development team comprised of Prometheus, Harvest Properties, and Alta Housing (a non-profit 
affordable housing developer) and is intended to be in place throughout the entitlement phase of the project. The City 
entered into the ENA in October 2021 (Attachment 1) and has since been working with the development team to refine 
the project, now referred to as Bespoke, through the planning entitlement process. The ENA is set to expire on October 14, 
2024 but may be extended for six months by mutual agreement between the Developer and the City Manager, who was 
delegated this authority by the City Council (ENA Section 1.3(b)(1)). 
The Bespoke project completed the pre-application phase of its planning review and submitted their formal planning 
application in February 2023. The project was deemed complete on July 17, 2024 and the environmental analysis, in the 
form of an Initial Study/Mitigated Negative Declaration, is nearly ready for public review and comment. 
The Project includes a parcel owned by the City, which means the City is not merely acting in a regulatory capacity but is 
also acting in a proprietary capacity as a partner. As the project has evolved, so have the proposed deal terms related to 
the City’s provision of land and funding support for the affordable housing component of the project. The last deal terms 
considered by Council at a study session on May 15, 2023 are included for reference (Attachment 2). 
This report is intended to provide the City Council with an update on the project status and to get Council direction on 
possible options relating to the currently proposed deal terms and the ENA and whether to extend it by six months.  
Project History
The City Council adopted a resolution declaring the surface parking lot at East 4th Avenue and South Railroad Avenue, 
known as the Talbot’s parking lot, as “Surplus Land” at the January 4, 2021, City Council meeting. Following this 
declaration, staff released a Notice of Surplus Lands Availability to all housing developers that have registered with the 
State Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD). In response to this notice, the City received four letters 
of interest for the development of affordable housing on the site.

The City requested that the interested parties provide proposals addressing the following criteria:
1.
Develop a 100% affordable housing project featuring a range of unit sizes from studios up to 3-bedrooms. 
Project should accommodate a mix of incomes and age ranges.
2.
Maximize the number of units that can be accommodated on the site by building a 7-story project.
3.
Provide a ground floor space that can be used by the non-profit Self-Help for the Elderly. It can be assumed 
that they can contribute some amount to rent, but not likely a market rent, such as would be charged to a 
retailer. They will also provide some funding for their own tenant improvements.
4.
The City will contribute the land for the project in the form of a long-term ground lease.
5.
Specify the expected ask of financial assistance for the affordable housing from the City. It is expected that the 
development team will minimize the City’s financial assistance to the best possible extent by applying for 
multiple outside sources of financing.
6.
Indicate if the development team would consider trying to acquire the parcels immediately fronting the 
Talbot’s parking lot along B Street for a larger development site.
The City received three responsive proposals based on these criteria. After going through an evaluation process, on July 
19, 2021, the City Council directed staff to negotiate an Exclusive Negotiating Agreement (ENA) with the 
Prometheus/Harvest Properties/Alta Housing team to explore the redevelopment of the City’s parking lot site. The City 
Council subsequently approved the ENA at their October 4, 2021 meeting. 
The development team was selected based on their proposal to develop the full block, removing awkward transition and 
existing easement issues; proposed mix of unit sizes that make the project family friendly; offering the highest ratio of on-
site parking for the housing; and serving income levels up to 70% of Area Median Income (AMI) which addresses the 
Council’s interest in serving both low- and middle-income households. A mixed-use project on this block would help re-
energize this part of B Street, would have synergy with the adjacent Draeger’s Market project, and would contribute to the 
economic vitality of the city and downtown.
The proposed Project includes the following components:  
•
A seven-story 71-unit 100% affordable housing building on the City’s lot with community space for Self-Help 
for the Elderly on the first floor,   
•
A six -story 156,000 sq-ft retail and office building,   
•
128 parking spaces on-site in an underground parking garage (of which 37 would be for residents' use and 25 
would be shared between commercial and residential uses), access would be on City lot; the remaining 
commercial garage would be accessible to the public on evenings and weekends,
•
A combination of at grade open space and roof top common areas, and   
•
Improvements in the public right-of-way area along all four street frontages.   
Key Deal Terms
In May 2023, additional draft deal terms were presented to Council (Attachment 2). The draft deal terms form the basis of 
the Development Agreement (DA)/Disposition and Development Loan Agreement (DDLA) that the City and developer 
would enter into at the time that the project receives its entitlements. These have since been refined further to reflect 
additional negotiations and details that are being included in the DA/DDLA. A summary of the key project terms are as 
follows:
•
The development team will enter into a 99-year ground lease and a reciprocal easement agreement for the 
City’s parcel at the rate of $1 per year. 
•
The City will contribute $3 million (m) to support the construction of the affordable housing from the City’s 
dedicated affordable housing funds. The specific source of funds from within the City’s housing funds is still to 
be determined. Alta has requested the partial use of these funds for predevelopment costs, should there be 
impact fees due at building permit issuance.

•
The development team will contribute $2.9m to support the pre-development costs for the affordable housing 
building.
•
The development team will finance and construct the shared underground parking garage across the entire 
site and create separate pads for the development of the affordable housing building and the commercial 
building. The commercial developer will cover the full cost of the construction of the parking garage, including 
the mechanical lift spaces dedicated to the affordable housing.
•
The development team will provide ground floor space in the affordable housing building at a substantially 
subsidized rent (the proposal from Alta is $1 per year) to Self-Help for the Elderly to allow for their relocation 
from the Central Park Recreation Center, which will be necessitated by the implementation of the Central Park 
Master Plan.
•
In order to allow for the construction of the underground parking garage across parcel lines, the development 
team will use a lot tie agreement.
•
Due to the construction readiness requirements of affordable housing financing, the underground parking 
garage must commence construction prior to the affordable housing project applying for funding. As a result, 
the commercial component is likely to be completed prior to the housing. The length of this differential 
between the completion of the commercial and the affordable housing components of the project will depend 
on whether the affordable housing has to go through multiple cycles of funding requests in order to obtain 
construction funding from the typical affordable housing financing sources, including the federal tax credits. 
This deal term is explained further below under Assurances to See Units Built as it has spurred additional 
conversation about how to safeguard the City’s interest in the project, which is to ensure the housing units are 
built, preferably within the current Housing Element Cycle.
•
The project will be subject to prevailing wage and Department of Industrial Relations (DIR) registry 
requirements.
•
The shared parking will include a minimum of 37 reserved residential spaces, 25 shared residential and 
commercial spaces; and the commercial garage will be publicly accessible on evenings and weekends. The 
publicly accessible part is a recent add to the project based on other Downtown projects.
•
A 10-year DA/DDLA term (additional details provided below under Project Schedule).
The majority of these draft deal terms have remained constant. However, there has been progress and refinements 
associated with publicly accessible parking, the lease amounts for Self Help for the Elderly, and movement on assurances 
to getting the units built. The site plan and architectural design also went through several iterations to address building 
design, public realm improvements, trash pickup, utilities etc., which is typical of any major development project proposal. 
The environmental review is nearly complete and poised for release to enable public comment and input. 
Since May 2023, the negotiations have been challenging on two primary fronts. First, the developers had not agreed to 
ensure that the affordable housing units will actually be constructed if Alta fails to procure funding or encounters some 
other hindrance toward Project completion. Second, the development team had originally declined to fully commit to 
delivering the housing units within staff’s proposed specific and reasonable timeframe (i.e., this Housing Element Cycle). 
  
Assurances to See Units Built
Broadly, the October 2021 and May 2023 terms involved (1) City contribution of land for the parking structure and 
residential units and additional funding for the residential development; (2) commercial developer construction of the 
parking garage (roughly valued at $5-7m for the residential parking) and a financial contribution of $2.9m, and (3) 
affordable housing developer construction of the development with 71 affordable housing units and space for Self Help for 
the Elderly. The mixed-use nature of the project allows the commercial developer to obtain concessions and waivers under

CITY OF SAN MATEO
state housing density bonus law for greater floor area and height. 
Typically, partnership projects of this nature require the residential components to be built first, to ensure the commercial 
developer cannot walk away from their commitments on providing housing units. However, given the unique shared 
nature of the parking garage and the project construction phasing, the residential development can only seek final federal 
tax credit financing after parking podium construction, which would create a lag of 1-2 years between the start of the 
commercial superstructure, which benefits from density bonus hinging upon the residential and parking access on City 
land, and the residential superstructure construction.   
  
City staff was concerned that in a worst-case scenario, the commercial project could potentially start operating and 
profiting from the benefits provided from driveway access and shared parking garage on City land (rough collective value 
at $13.5-15m), without meeting its commitment to deliver a housing project. Staff believes the housing component is the 
project lynchpin and without assurances that it would be built to the satisfaction of the City Council, the project cannot 
proceed further.   
To allow for the commercial project to move forward and protect the City’s land interests and contributions, staff 
proposed the following plan to the development team with four stages:  
1.
Certificate of Occupancy (C of O) issuance for the commercial project tied to building permit issuance for 
residential development OR a significant financial contribution (such as $10m) at C of O of the commercial 
project that would enable construction of the affordable housing. If the residential receives tax credit financing 
or is issued building permits prior to C of O, these monies would no longer be required; and  
2.
A secondary deposit of $3m dollars at building permit issuance for the garage, to be used if the residential 
project requires major changes to obtain financing (and to be returned if project meets its schedule of 
performance); and  
3.
Should Alta be unable to proceed, the development team would find an alternate affordable housing partner, 
with City’s approval, who can revise and deliver the affordable housing component within a prescribed 
timeframe; and  
4.
All plans and entitlements to revert to City to find an affordable developer who can deliver the residential 
units or explore alternatives such as a market-rate project.   
The development team has agreed to these terms, with the exception of tying the C of O of the commercial to the 
residential project or holding building permit issuance of commercial building with the residential building, citing financial 
considerations. They are also not willing to provide a $10m dollar true-up at C of O of the commercial building if the 
residential project fails to move forward to account for the contribution of City land towards their project. The 
development team‘s position is that they will have already made a significant monetary investment ($5-$7m) to build the 
parking garage and foundation for the housing structure. Alternatively, they are amenable to a staff proposed minimum 
contribution of $3.5m towards the City’s affordable housing fund at C of O. 
Should the residential project fail despite the above safeguards, the City can utilize the $3m secondary deposit and the 
$3.5-10m true-up contributions at C of O towards other affordable housing projects and could potentially flip the entitled 
property for a market-rate housing development (which would include a subset of inclusionary below market rate 
affordable housing units). Given the long timelines for this project, staff would include adjustments to these assurances to 
account for annual Consumer Price Index increases. 
Should the project has to flip to a market rate project, City staff would likely need to conduct an RFP process to identify a 
market rate developer to build the housing units (vertical portion) of the project. Any potential proceeds received from a 
market rate developer for the long-term lease of City land that includes physical improvements of a parking garage, 
building foundation and project plans could likely be utilized for activities such as affordable housing and City

Based on the above background and history, staff is seeking direction from City Council on the following scenarios and 
which substantive option to pursue:
       Substantive Options:

Option 1: Extend the ENA to allow staff and development team to continue to negotiate the assurances and 
require greater contributions from the commercial team in the case of failure of a residential project to move


Option 2: Develop the DA/DDLA based on the terms described herein with the $3.5m contribution from the 
development team as the final assurance at C of O. 

Option 3: Allow the ENA to expire and explore other potential stand-alone affordable housing projects at this site.  
Project Schedule
Another challenge in the project negotiations has been the Project construction schedule. While the ENA outlined a 10-
year overall timeline, the development team originally proposed 14 years for completion, with a four-year timeline just to 
submit building permit applications. This proposed schedule was not considered a good-faith effort to meet the City’s 
goals of producing housing at this site within the current Housing Element Cycle, which concludes in 2031. In comparison, 
the Kiku Crossing 100% affordable housing project with 225 units and a parking garage with over 525 spaces, was entitled 
and built within four years. 
Staff provided an alternative schedule (Attachment 3) that proposed for construction to be completed by 2031, with some 
buffers built-in to 2035 that account for unforeseen issues, keeping in mind the 10-year timeline in the ENA. This schedule 
was based on a review of other large building permit turnaround times, discussions with the Building Official and Permit 
Center Supervisor and with a written commitment to turn around City reviews expeditiously, with regular check-in 
meetings to resolve issues and ensure progress. Since these discussions, the development team has proposed an alternate 
schedule that would achieve completion of the entire project by December 2032, but with extensions or delays, could be 
delayed as much as four years (end of 2036).  
The biggest difference lies in the applicant submitting the first permit application two years after entitlement and waiting 
two years for permit issuance. This would effectively double the time to see the project break ground for the garage, 
which would significantly delay the affordable housing timeline on seeking tax credit financing (which may take multiple 
rounds, with only one application round per year), as outlined in the table below. The estimated dates below were 
contingent upon approval by October of 2024. Any extensions on the ENA would further delay the completion of the 
project.

The residential units would count towards the current Housing Element cycle if building permits are issued before the end

Given these concerns, staff is seeking direction from City Council on which scheduling option should be included in the 
DA/DDLA:
        Scheduling Options:
•
Option 1: The affordable units should be permitted and constructed in the optimal scenario within this 
Housing Cycle (by 2031). 
•
Option 2: The affordable units should receive construction permits within this Housing Cycle (by 2031).
It should be noted that either of these options will allow the City to get credit for these affordable units during this 
Housing Cycle. However, any delays or extensions in the preceding milestones could affect this timeline.

Outside Sources of Funding

Our development team understands the City’s proposed structure, which consists of the City’s

In response to this City priority, we have explored, researched and analyzed the outside sources as

our size and scope would qualify for and be able to obtain these outside funding sources.

Project Labor Agreement

the project team has reached out to the SMC Building Trades Council to initiate that discussion and we
look forward to continuing the dialogue on this topic.

BACKGROUND:

The City is currently party to an Exclusive Negotiating Agreement (ENA) for the redevelopment of the City-owned
surface parking lot located at 4th and Railroad Avenue, directly across the railroad tracks from the Kiku Crossing
development. The ENA is with a three-party development team comprised of Prometheus, Harvest Properties, and
Alta Housing (a non-profit affordable housing developer) and is intended to be in place throughout the entitlement
phase of the project. The City entered into the ENA in October 2021 and has since been working with the
development team to refine the project, now referred to as Bespoke, through the planning entitlement process. The
Bespoke project completed the pre-application phase of its planning review and submitted their formal planning
application in February 2023.

The Bespoke project will redevelop the entire block located between B Street and Railroad Avenue between 4th and
5th Avenues. The project includes 60-units of affordable housing, 150,000 square feet of office space, 12,000 square
feet of retail space, and approximately 4,500 square feet of community space intended for Self-Help for the Elderly. A
shared underground parking garage will be built across the site which will include 38 residential parking spaces and 80
commercial spaces.

As the project has evolved, so have the deal terms related to the City’s provision of land and funding support for the
affordable housing component of the project. This report is intended to provide the City Council with an update on
the project status and to get Council feedback on the proposed deal terms as outlined in the Term Sheet, included as
Attachment 1. In addition to the Term Sheet, the development team has provided a letter outlining their commitment
to the project, included as Attachment 2.

declaration, staff released a Notice of Surplus Lands Availability to all housing developers that have registered with the
State Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD). In response to this notice, the City received four
letters of interest for the development of affordable housing on the site.

Bespoke Project Summary

The Bespoke project includes 60 residential units, an increase of eight units from the Request for Proposal
response, that will be designated as family-friendly units, meaning that over 50% of the units are two- and
three- bedroom — housing up to 260 individuals. All of the units will be income restricted between the
30% to 80% area medium income threshold. The housing project is supported by 38 dedicated parking
spaces in addition to spaces shared with the commercial office after typical working hours. The ground
floor of the housing project includes a dedicated space for Self Help for the Elderly, a local non-profit
stalwart of San Mateo, which provides services to over 40,000 seniors in the San Mateo community and
the Bay Area.

The curated development locates approximately 12,000 square feet of retail storefront space along B
Street, wrapping around the corner onto 4* and 5‘ Avenues, allowing for the extension of San Mateo’s
already lively downtown core. Control over the entire block allows for the project to put forth a continuous
design aesthetic that improves the pedestrian experience across the entire block and reflects the City’s
stated goals regarding pedestrians in the downtown.

The commercial portion of the project includes a total of ~150,000 square feet of office space. This office

project reflects the City’s stated desire of developing new commercial office in smart locations; i.e. close
