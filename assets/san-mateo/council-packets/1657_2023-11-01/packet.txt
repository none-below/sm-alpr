SUBJECT:
Heritage Tree Removal Permit Appeal Hearing located at the Woodlake Condominiums, which has a main site address of 
900 Peninsula Avenue
RECOMMENDATION:
Hear an appeal of the Director's approval of a permit to remove two heritage privet trees at the Woodlake Condominiums 
and make a determination to uphold or overturn approval of the permit.
BACKGROUND:
On May 8, 2023, the Parks Department received a tree removal permit application (HT-2023-001604) (Attachment 1) to 
remove a grove of 20 privet trees at the Woodlake Condominiums. After a site visit, Managing Arborist Matthew Fried 
issued his decision and denied the removal request (Attachment 2) for all 20 trees. His decision was based on his opinion 
that the trees were neither ‘high risk’ nor ‘unsightly’, as described in the May 8th report from D.R. Tree Consulting 
(Attachment 3).
Subsequent to his denial of the tree removal application, the Managing Arborist received a second, more comprehensive 
report from D.R. Tree Consulting (Attachment 4). This report, dated June 26, 2023, more closely examined the condition of 
each of the 20 trees and assigned a risk rating to each tree. Four of the 20 trees (#25, 27, 40, and 41) were assigned a 
rating of “high” risk. The remaining trees were classified as “low” or “moderate” risk. 
On June 30, 2023, the Woodlake Association appealed the Managing Arborist’s decision to the Director of Parks and 
Recreation (Attachment 5). Director Magrini reviewed both reports from D.R. Tree Consulting, all the application and 
appeal materials and visited the grove of trees. During a site visit, she observed the very poor condition of two of the ‘high 
risk’ trees (#40 and 41) but could not identify the defects that led to the ‘high risk’ ratings of Trees #25 and 27. To better 
understand why Trees #25 and 27 were assigned ‘high risk’ ratings, she asked the Managing Arborist to collaborate with 
Dustie Rushing from D.R. Tree Consulting. During their joint assessment, both parties agreed that the principal defect in 
each tree (#25 and 27) could be addressed through either full or partial removal of the offending stem, as both trees have 
multiple stems. 
On August 14, 2023, the Director issued her determination on the appeal filed by Woodlake Association (Attachment 6), as 
follows: 
1. Uphold the Managing Arborist’s decision to deny the removal of 18 out of the 20 privet trees (#22-39) because these 
trees do not present a "high" risk after implementing mitigation measures (partial or complete removal of one offending 
stem from each tree).

2. Overturn the Managing Arborist’s decision and approve the removal of 2 trees (#40 and 41) due to their very poor 
condition and low viability, even after potential mitigation. 
On August 21, 2023, a letter from the City was mailed to 1,482 residents and property owners, notifying them of the 
Director’s decision to approve a permit for the removal of Trees #40 and 41. 
On August 23, 2023, David Rubinson, on behalf of the Woodlake Neighbors Group, filed an appeal of the Director’s 
decision (Attachment 7). The appeal included two arborist reports prepared by Michael Young of Urban Tree Management 
(UTM). The first UTM report (July 27, 2023) (Attachment 8) did not address any individual trees but concluded that all 20 
trees were “…generally in Fair Health and Fair Structure” and stated that, “These Privets, with proper care, should be able 
to endure for another 10-20 years minimum.” The UTM report did not include any discussion of tree risk. 
The second UTM report (August 23, 2023) (Attachment 9) proposed recommendations for mitigating the defects of Trees 
#40 and 41 that “make them somewhat hazardous in their current condition.” Specifically, UTM recommended: 
1. Pruning to shorten the length of decayed stems. 
2. Installation of a cable support system in at least one of the trees. 
3. Pruning every other year to maintain sprout/limb stability. 
In accordance with SMMC 13.40.130, the Park and Recreation Commission shall hear appeals of decisions made by the 
Director and make a determination to uphold or overturn approval of the permit.

Protected Tree FAQ’s 
What is the definition of a Protected Tree in San Mateo? 
1. Heritage Trees 
a. Any Oak having a trunk diameter of 10 inches (circumference of 31.4 inches) or more measured at 4.5 
feet (54 inches) above ground level. 
 
b. Any tree of any species with a trunk diameter of 15 inches (circumference of 47.1 inches) or more, 
measured at 4.5 feet (54 inches) above ground level. 
 
2. Street Trees. Any tree of any size growing along or within the public right of way. 
*Measuring the diameter of your tree is straightforward, requiring just a few simple steps with the help of common 
household items. You can find easy measurement instructions on our website at http://www.cityofsanmateo.org/trees. 
Are permits required to remove or prune Protected Trees? 
You must obtain a permit from the City’s Managing Arborist if you wish to perform any of the following work: 
o Removal of any Protected Tree (Heritage Tree or Street Tree). 
o Pruning more than 25% of the total foliage of a Heritage Tree. 
o Removal of any roots of a Heritage Tree that are within a distance equal to six times the trunk diameter of 
the tree. 
o Any pruning of the foliage or roots of a Street Tree. 
Exceptions are extended only to the Police, Fire Departments and PG&E should they declare a tree a hazard to life or 
property. 
Unless you are certain about the amount of foliage or roots you plan to remove from a tree, it is recommended that you 
consult the City or an ISA Certified Arborist before performing any work. You do not want to find yourself paying a 
penalty or harming your tree due to misguided maintenance. 
Where can I apply for a permit? 
Tree Work Applications for permits can be obtained at the Parks Office located at 2001 Pacific Blvd., San Mateo; or 
online at http://www.cityofsanmateo.org/trees. 
Is there a fee for the permit? 
A permit fee of $100.00 is due at the time of permit issuance for work on any Heritage Tree. Additionally, a tree 
replacement deposit ranging from $483 to $740 (based on size of tree removed) is due at the time of permit issuance for 
removals. The permit fee partially covers the cost of processing the permit, including an inspection of the tree by the 
Managing Arborist. The replacement deposit is returned once a new tree has been planted in accordance with the 
conditions of the permit (valid for up to 12 months from the date of permit issuance). There is no fee for applications 
unless they are approved. Fees are required with all tree work except for Street Tree pruning. 
What happens after I submit my Tree Work Application? 
Upon receiving the application, the City’s Managing Arborist will inspect the tree and evaluate your reasons for removal 
against the criteria listed in the Protected Trees Ordinance Section 13.40.100. If the Managing Arborist determines that 
your tree meets the findings required for removal, all abutting property owners are notified in writing of the decision 
and have 10 days to appeal said decision. If no appeals are filed within 10 days, the property owner is informed that the 
permit can be paid for and picked up at the Park’s Division office located at 2001 Pacific Blvd., San Mateo. For 
convenience, the office will also take payment over the phone at (650) 522-7420. The permit will then be emailed to 
you, along with a receipt.

How long does it take to get a permit approved? 
In most cases it takes approximately four weeks. It may take longer if the application is related to a construction 
development project, or if there is insufficient information provided and more is needed. 
What can I do to expedite the process of my permit? 
The more supplementary information provided along with the application, the easier and quicker it will be to assess the 
situation and reach a determination. Clear photos of the tree and any related damage or undesirable conditions are 
the easiest and most effective way to expedite application review. A detailed report from an ISA Certified Arborist is 
not necessary, but may also be helpful. 
Can I appeal the decision? 
Any person may appeal the decision of the Managing Arborist. Requests shall be in writing and mailed or emailed to the 
Director of Parks & Recreation no later than 5:00 p.m. on the tenth calendar day after the decision. 
Are tree protection measures required when building near a Protected Tree? 
Depending on the scope and location of the project, the San Mateo Planning or Building Department may require that 
you submit a Tree Protection Plan prepared by an ISA Certified Arborist for any Protected Tree that may be impacted by 
construction activities. If the plan is sufficient to prevent harm to protected trees from reasonably foreseeable 
construction activities (as outlined in S.M.M.C. Section 13.40.070) it shall be approved and monitored through 
completion of the project. 
Can the Managing Arborist come out and offer guidance about my tree before I apply for a permit? 
No. The Managing Arborist can only inspect your tree after an application has been submitted. 
Do I need a permit to remove a dead Protected Tree? 
Yes. It is important that the Managing Arborist confirms the condition of your tree and issues a permit prior to removal. 
Please keep in mind that any helpful supplementary information (clear photos, reports, etc.) will expedite the processing 
of your application. 
Will I be fined if a tree is removed without a permit? 
Yes. It is unlawful to remove or cause damage to a Heritage Tree without a permit. Penalties can be assessed to all 
parties involved in an unauthorized removal or Major Pruning, including the homeowner and the contractor. For details 
regarding fines and penalties, please refer to section 13.40.140 of the San Mateo Municipal Code. 
What happens once a removal permit has been issued? 
A Tree Removal Permit is valid for up to 12 months from date of issue. Within this 12-month period, the owner may 
remove the tree(s) and plant a new replacement trees in accordance with the conditions of the permit. A “replacement 
deposit” between $425 and $800 is collected at the time of permit issuance. The amount of the deposit is determined 
based on the size and number of trees required to be replanted. Once the replacement tree(s) has been planted, please 
contact the Parks Department to request a replant inspection. After an inspection by City staff verifies the replant, your 
deposit will be refunded by mail. If the replacement tree(s) is not planted within 12 months of the permit date, the 
deposit is forfeited and used to plant new trees in city parks, medians, and public areas. If your permit required the 
replanting of multiple trees, the Department will bill you the in-lieu amounts less your deposit for any trees not planted.

Are there any specifications regarding the replacement tree? 
Yes. Heritage Trees removed with a valid permit shall be replaced with a tree species from the Official Replant List 
according to the schedule below.  
Heritage Tree Replacement Requirements 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
15-gallon and 24-inch box trees are standard sizes available through tree growers. The replacement tree may be planted 
anywhere on the property, except in the public right of way. In some cases, the Managing Arborist will provide direction 
on the location of the replant as outlined in S.M.M.C. 13.40.110. 
Street trees shall be replaced with one 24” box tree of a species assigned by the Managing Arborist in accordance with 
the Street Tree Master Plan. 
My tree has become dangerous during a storm. Do I still need a permit for an emergency removal? 
Generally speaking, exceptions to permits are extended only to the Police Department, Fire Departments and PG&E, 
should they declare a tree a hazard to life or property. 
1. If your tree has caused a dangerous or life-threatening emergency: Call 911. 
2. If your tree has become dangerous from a storm but is not causing an immediate threat: Call the Parks 
Department at (650) 522-7420. You will be issued an “emergency permit”, at which point the Managing Arborist 
will visit your property and issue the permit on site. 
My neighbor has a tree that is causing damage to my property. Can the City do anything about it? 
The City cannot force the owner of the tree to remove or prune it. It is recommended that you work with your neighbor 
to arrive at a solution. If you are unable to arrive at a mutually agreeable solution, consider contacting the Peninsula 
Conflict Resolution Center for assistance at (650) 513-0330. 
Why does the City have a Protected Trees Ordinance? 
Protected Trees are living landmarks and an important component of the quality of life in San Mateo. It is our intent to 
explore methods of solving tree-related problems, without resorting to tree removal if possible. The ordinance also 
ensures that trees will be preserved during construction. 
Trees provide many benefits to our community including: 
• 
Raising property values 
• 
Providing wind and sun protection, thereby lowering heating and cooling bills 
• 
Providing privacy as well as natural habits for song birds 
• 
Reducing stress: in fact having trees close by has been proven to aid in recovery after surgery 
• 
Providing over $1.4 Million in net benefits to our City (City of San Mateo Urban Forest Resource Analysis, 2011)

Tree Pruning Specification Guidelines 
For any tree in the City of San Mateo requiring a pruning permit, the City requires that the 
applicant submit a written set of pruning specifications. 
The ISA Certified Arborist® who will oversee the work must prepare the pruning 
specifications. 
Pruning specifications should include the following elements, in as much detail as possible: 
1) Species and location of the tree(s) to be prune 
2) Objective 
3) Pruning System (what the tree will look like after pruning) 
4) Types of branches to be removed (i.e. living, dead, mistletoe, diseased, long or leveraged) 
5) Pruning amount – percentage or number of branches to be removed 
6) Location in the crown where the branches will be removed 
7) Pruning cut types to be used – branch removal, reduction, heading 
8) Size range of parts to be removed 
Some contractors will summarily and incorrectly state on a proposal that they will “prune to ANSI A300 
standards”.  Please note that ANSI A300 Standards are not specifications. The ANSI Standards provide 
the framework for the drafting of useful, measurable specifications. 
For the best care of your tree, and to ensure a quick permit approval process, retain a contractor who: 
- 
Is an ISA Certified Arborist® (www.treesaregood.org/findanarborist) 
- 
Understands and adheres to the ANSI A300 (Part 1) Standards revised in 2017 
- 
Understands and adheres to ISA Best Management Practices

Species Profile:  Ligustrum lucidum; Glossy Privet 
Glossy Privet trees are widely planted as fast-growing, small trees that can become very large if not 
kept under control and are also known to be invasive.  According to the North Carolina Extension 
Gardener web site: Ligustrum lucidum, “This plant is problematic and alternatives should be 
considered… 
…It can be weedy in disturbed areas has escaped and naturalized in moist areas and it is considered 
invasive in California and Florida.” 
Often planted for their tolerance to poor conditions, because they are evergreen and fast-growing 
they were widely used in developments, like Woodlake Association, for example.  Now knowing the 
invasiveness, they are not typically planted by landscape professionals. 
Cal Poly Extension Notes:

feet

Leaf Miner  
 
The subject trees have been analyzed as a group for obvious reasons.  All have required 
pruning several times a year to remove dead branches for safety purposes.  Several main 
stems have required removal for this reason, which has resulted in decay that is now 
extending into the root zones of several of the trees. 
When considering how to approach this removal, options were considered to only remove 
those in the worst condition to begin succession planting.  However, given the overall poor

Project Summary Detail 
This Level 2 Tree Risk Assessment was prepared at the request of Woodlake Association, located at 
900 Peninsula Avenue, San Mateo, CA 94403.  D.R. Tree Consulting LLC has been contracted by 
Woodlake Association to provide this tree risk assessment and report for twenty (20) subject Glossy 
Privet or Ligustrum lucidum trees located near the lagoon, which are themselves a main feature within 
the landscape. 
Request for removal was submitted to the City of San Mateo in May of this year, which was denied 
due to opposition from the community.  The application was submitted with an arborist letter, that I 
prepared on May 8, 2023 to describe several conditions of concern with regards to tree health and 
safety for the community. 
Woodlake Association has chosen to appeal the permit denial and has requested this Level 2 Tree 
Risk Assessment to inspect and evaluate twenty (20) Glossy Privet trees, referenced herein as the 
‘subject trees.’  The main purpose of this inspection and evaluation is to ensure safety for surrounding 
people and property, as the subject trees are in a high-target area, with frequent pedestrian traffic 
throughout the day.  As part of this assessment, options for mitigation are included to minimize future 
residual risk with regards to the subject trees.

There are no limiting conditions to note at this time.  Trees have been monitored for several years and

Risk Rating and Matrix Description 
Tree risk assessments are completed not only through evaluation of trees and tree health, but also by assessing 
potential targets, “Likelihood of Impact,” and “Consequences of Failure,” through a Matrix system. All of these 
factors are then evaluated to determine the overall risk rating, from Extreme to Low. 
**Definitions per the ISA Tree Risk Assessment Manual, Second Edition: 
Likelihood of Failure 
Imminent failure has started or is most likely to occur in the near future, even if there is no significant 
wind or increased load.  The imminent category overrides the stated time frame. 
Probable:  failures may be expected under normal weather conditions within the specified time frame.  
Possible:  failure may be expected during extreme weather conditions, but it is unlikely during normal 
weather conditions within the specified time frame. 
Improbable:  the tree or tree part is not likely to fail during normal weather conditions and may not fail 
in extreme weather conditions within the specified time frame. 
Consequence of Failure/Impact 
Severe:  serious personal injury or death, high-value property damage, or major disruption of important 
activities 
Significant:  substantial personal injury, moderate to high-value property damage, or considerable 
disruption of activities. 
Minor:  minor personal injury, low to moderate value property damage, or small disruption of activities. 
Negligible:  no personal injury, low-value property damage or disruptions that can be replaced or 
repaired. 
 
Risk Rating 
Extreme:  failure is imminent with a high likelihood of impacting the target, and the consequences of the 
failure are severe. 
High:  consequences are significant and likelihood is very likely or likely, or consequences are severe 
and likelihood is likely. 
Moderate:  consequences are minor and likelihood is very likely or likely or likelihood is somewhat likely 
and consequences are significant or severe. 
Low:  consequences are negligible and likelihood is unlikely, or consequences are minor and likelihood 
is somewhat likely.

Site Factors 
While I personally have been familiar with the Woodlake Association trees for many years, the initial 
site visit and tree risk assessment evaluation for the purposes of this report was conducted on 
Thursday, April 27, 2023 at approximately 10:00 am.  With the denial of the permit a second site visit 
for further inspection and analysis was conducted on Monday, June 26, 2023 at approximately 8:00 
am.  Two (2) of the subject trees were coned and caution-taped off during this visit, as they have large 
visible cracks on main stems and were deemed a potential hazard. 
The subject Privet trees are a main feature of this landscape, situated adjacent to the man-made 
lagoon with benches below and surrounded by walking pathways.  According to The Cultural 
Landscape Foundation (TCLF) web page, Woodlake Condominiums is a, “…30-acre, nearly 1000-unit 
planned community completed in 1965. The design was a result of a collaboration between developer 
Gerson Baker, architecture firm Wurster, Bernardi & Emmons, and landscape architecture firm 
Lawrence Halprin & Associates.”  On this web page, there are several beautiful photographs showing 
a lush, green landscape, which are dated 2016. 
For the past several decades, California has experienced long periods of drought conditions which 
were only recently lifted this past rain season (2022/2023).  As a result, trees throughout the area 
have continued to decline and many have required removal for safety reasons.  Landscapes, like 
Woodlake Association have been well-maintained through these periods of drought with supplemental 
watering through irrigation and tree management plans to ensure health and safety is maintained.  
Woodlake is now reaching the point where the development and originally installed landscape is 58 
years old.  While many of the trees can live well past this age, like Oak trees and Redwoods; many 
other trees do not have this type of longevity, like the subject Privet trees, for example.  Sources vary 
on their stated life span, however it is listed to be between 30 to 50 years. 
As a very well-manicured and maintained landscape, the age of the original landscape which includes 
the twenty (20) subject Privet trees is a site factor and condition of concern that must be considered 
in this evaluation.

Subject Tree Health: 
As mentioned above, because all twenty (20) of the subject trees are located in such close proximity 
to one another, are the same age (assuming they were all planted at the same time) and have grown 
in very similar conditions, the health and conditions of concern are all very similar across the board. 
All twenty (20) subject trees are in various stages of decline with several pockets of decay and cavities 
at the base.  Most of the subject trees also have pockets of decay in one or more main stems, in 
addition to dead branches throughout the upper canopy. 
 
Species Profile:  
Ligustrum lucidum; Glossy Privet 
Native to the China, Korea and Japan, Glossy Privet trees, are tolerant of very poor growing 
conditions and as a result of their prolific reseeding, they are classified as invasive by the  
Cal-IPC (California Invasive Plant Council), “Under moist conditions, a large tree may produce 
3 million or more seeds. Privet species are able to germinate and survive in shaded areas. 
Saplings grow rapidly and are able to germinate under allelopathic trees. Privet also has a 
high initial seed viability. Most propagules are thought to survive for less than 12 months, 
although one study found a single propagule after 2.5 years. Birds can disperse seeds into a 
variety of vegetation types, and seedlings have a high tolerance of shade, soil types, 
temperatures and moisture levels which may mean privet will reproduce successfully in a 
variety of habitat types.” 
Invasive plant species become problematic when they compete with native species in natural 
habitats and riparian areas, which unfortunately is happening in California with the Glossy 
Privet species, “Describe rate of spread:  Glossy Privet is documented as occurring in 15 
counties in California and is probably more widespread (Calflora and CCH). The first collection 
in CCH is from 1978, and most of the documented locations are adjacent to urban areas,” 
According to the Cal-IPC (California Invasive Plant Council).   
Showy white flowers are followed by dark-colored berries that can become very messy in the 
landscape.  Because these are evergreen, tolerant of poor, moist to dry soil and full sun to 
part shade, they are very common in the urban landscape and can reach up to (50) fifty feet

Risk Categorization 
According to the ISA Tree Risk Assessment Manual, Second Edition, Risk Categorization is defined as, 
“The process of assigning risk and risk factors to categories based on severity or hierarchy.”  In short, 
targets and tree defects are identified and categorized into a risk matrix to determine the risk rating. 
See attached Exhibit 2. Woodlake Association - Tree Risk Categorization Table, for further detailed 
information. 
Of the (20) subject trees, (4) are currently found to be a High Risk, (5) are Moderate Risk and the 
remaining (11) are Low Risk at this time.  The primary reason that most of the trees are considered 
low risk, is because their proximity to the walking path is further away, reducing the pedestrian 
occupancy, which means there is a lower likelihood of a tree failure impacting a target.  The trees are 
all in poor to critical health condition. 
Mitigation Options and Residual Risk Ratings 
In my professional opinion, due to the close proximity of all subject trees to one another in the circle, 
it is safe to assume that the tree roots are intertwined, similar to the way the upper canopies are.  
With this in mind, what happens to one tree will have a significant impact on the trees surrounding it.  
Removal of any of the subject trees will require grinding of the stumps to avoid a trip hazard and 
prevent regrowth.  The grinding of stumps will damage the roots of any remaining trees, creating a 
potentially hazardous situation as tree failure can happen with little or no warning with significant root 
damage. 
 
Removal is STRONGLY RECOMMENDED 
 
 
Residual Risk (Following Mitigation):  NONE 
Removal of Subject Trees is strongly recommended for all twenty (20) subject trees, due to poor 
health/extensive decay and potential hazard.  This is the best long-term and safest solution in my 
professional opinion. 
1. Tree Removal, cleanup and disposal of all tree debris 
2. Stump Grinding to a depth of 18” is recommended to prevent the trees from growing back; 
however may not be possible due to location in sloped/creek area.   
3. *USA (Underground Service Alert) to be completed, prior to performing work below soil 
surface  https://usanorth811.org/

Conclusion: Follow-up Inspection Intervals 
All twenty (20) Subject Trees are in poor health with significant decay in the root zones which cannot 
be cut out.  Four (4) of the trees are considered a HIGH risk at this time, with two (2) having to be 
coned and caution-taped off following the site inspection on 6/26/23 as they have significant visible 
cracks in more than one main stem.  Five (5) of the trees are considered to be a MODERATE risk at 
this time.  The remaining eleven (11) eleven trees are currently LOW risk, however removal is 
recommended due to decay in root zones and potential damage from close proximity to trees 
recommended for removal. 
After careful consideration, I am not recommending pruning as an option for mitigation as it will not 
correct the main defects, which are the decay pockets extending into the root zones.  Any pruning 
with the goal of making the trees safe would involve removal of any and all decayed/decaying plant 
parts, which would leave very little plant material in place.  Removal of dead branches will not mitigate 
the areas of decay in the lower portion of the trees.  These trees will continue to decline, and become 
more and more hazardous as the existing decay progresses.

several benches located directly beneath the trees’ canopies that families with young

pruning of the trees’ dead branches had led to decay that extended into several of the trees’ 
root zones, and rated all of the trees as being in “poor” or “critical” condition.

On May 8, 2023, you filed a tree removal permit (HT-2023-001604) (Exhibit A) to remove 20 privet trees at the 
Woodlake Condominiums. Thereafter Managing Arborist Matthew Fried issued his decision and denied the 
removal request (Exhibit B) for all 20 trees. His decision was based on his opinion that the trees were neither 
‘high risk’ nor ‘unsightly’, as described in the May 8th report from D.R. Tree Consulting (Exhibit C). 
 
Subsequent to this denial, you submitted a second, more comprehensive report from D.R. Tree Consulting 
(Exhibit D). This report more closely examined the condition of each of the 20 trees and assigned a risk rating to 
each tree. Four of the 20 trees were assigned a rating of “high” risk.1 The remaining trees were classified as 
“low” or “moderate” risk. 
 
I conducted a de novo review of the case after you filed an appeal of the denial.  In doing so, I reviewed both 
arborist reports prepared by your consultant, Dustie Rushing, and personally visited the grove of trees with Mr. 
Fried to assess the trees' condition and their significance to the landscape. After careful consideration, and in 
accordance with SMMC 13.40.130(a), I make the following determinations: 
 
1. I uphold Mr. Fried's decision to deny the removal of 18 out of the 20 privet trees (#22-39) because these 
trees do not present a "high" risk after implementing mitigation measures (partial or complete removal 
of one offending stem from each tree). 
2. I overturn Mr. Fried's decision and approve the removal of 2 trees (#40 and 41) due to their very poor 
condition and low viability, even after potential mitigation. 
Ms. Rushing's second report from June 23, 2023, identified four trees (#25, 27, 40, and 41) as having "high" risk. 
During my site visit, I personally observed the poor condition of Trees #40 and 41, with large deadwood and 
multiple stems showing signs of decay. The remaining portions of these trees would also have low vitality. 
Hence, I concur with the decision to remove Trees #40 and 41.

Species Profile:  Ligustrum lucidum; Glossy Privet 
Glossy Privet trees are widely planted as fast-growing, small trees that can become very large if not 
kept under control and are also known to be invasive.  According to the North Carolina Extension 
Gardener web site: Ligustrum lucidum, “This plant is problematic and alternatives should be 
considered… 
…It can be weedy in disturbed areas has escaped and naturalized in moist areas and it is considered 
invasive in California and Florida.” 
Often planted for their tolerance to poor conditions, because they are evergreen and fast-growing 
they were widely used in developments, like Woodlake Association, for example.  Now knowing the 
invasiveness, they are not typically planted by landscape professionals. 
Cal Poly Extension Notes:  
Foliage Type:  Evergreen 
Maximum Tree Height:   50 
feet 
 
Water Use: Medium; 
Exposure: Partial Shade to 
Full Sun 
Branch Strength: Medium; 
Root Damage Potential: 
Moderate 
Disease and pest 
susceptibility: Armillaria, 
Phytophthora, Root Rot, 
Sooty Mold and Aphids, 
Leaf Miner  
 
The subject trees have been analyzed as a group for obvious reasons.  All have required 
pruning several times a year to remove dead branches for safety purposes.  Several main 
stems have required removal for this reason, which has resulted in decay that is now 
extending into the root zones of several of the trees. 
When considering how to approach this removal, options were considered to only remove 
those in the worst condition to begin succession planting.  However, given the overall poor

Risk Rating and Matrix Description 
Tree risk assessments are completed not only through evaluation of trees and tree health, but also by assessing 
potential targets, “Likelihood of Impact,” and “Consequences of Failure,” through a Matrix system. All of these 
factors are then evaluated to determine the overall risk rating, from Extreme to Low. 
**Definitions per the ISA Tree Risk Assessment Manual, Second Edition: 
Likelihood of Failure 
Imminent failure has started or is most likely to occur in the near future, even if there is no significant 
wind or increased load.  The imminent category overrides the stated time frame. 
Probable:  failures may be expected under normal weather conditions within the specified time frame.  
Possible:  failure may be expected during extreme weather conditions, but it is unlikely during normal 
weather conditions within the specified time frame. 
Improbable:  the tree or tree part is not likely to fail during normal weather conditions and may not fail 
in extreme weather conditions within the specified time frame. 
Consequence of Failure/Impact 
Severe:  serious personal injury or death, high-value property damage, or major disruption of important 
activities 
Significant:  substantial personal injury, moderate to high-value property damage, or considerable 
disruption of activities. 
Minor:  minor personal injury, low to moderate value property damage, or small disruption of activities. 
Negligible:  no personal injury, low-value property damage or disruptions that can be replaced or 
repaired. 
 
Risk Rating 
Extreme:  failure is imminent with a high likelihood of impacting the target, and the consequences of the 
failure are severe. 
High:  consequences are significant and likelihood is very likely or likely, or consequences are severe 
and likelihood is likely. 
Moderate:  consequences are minor and likelihood is very likely or likely or likelihood is somewhat likely 
and consequences are significant or severe. 
Low:  consequences are negligible and likelihood is unlikely, or consequences are minor and likelihood 
is somewhat likely.

Species Profile:  
Ligustrum lucidum; Glossy Privet 
Native to the China, Korea and Japan, Glossy Privet trees, are tolerant of very poor growing 
conditions and as a result of their prolific reseeding, they are classified as invasive by the  
Cal-IPC (California Invasive Plant Council), “Under moist conditions, a large tree may produce 
3 million or more seeds. Privet species are able to germinate and survive in shaded areas. 
Saplings grow rapidly and are able to germinate under allelopathic trees. Privet also has a 
high initial seed viability. Most propagules are thought to survive for less than 12 months, 
although one study found a single propagule after 2.5 years. Birds can disperse seeds into a 
variety of vegetation types, and seedlings have a high tolerance of shade, soil types, 
temperatures and moisture levels which may mean privet will reproduce successfully in a 
variety of habitat types.” 
Invasive plant species become problematic when they compete with native species in natural 
habitats and riparian areas, which unfortunately is happening in California with the Glossy 
Privet species, “Describe rate of spread:  Glossy Privet is documented as occurring in 15 
counties in California and is probably more widespread (Calflora and CCH). The first collection 
in CCH is from 1978, and most of the documented locations are adjacent to urban areas,” 
According to the Cal-IPC (California Invasive Plant Council).   
Showy white flowers are followed by dark-colored berries that can become very messy in the 
landscape.  Because these are evergreen, tolerant of poor, moist to dry soil and full sun to 
part shade, they are very common in the urban landscape and can reach up to (50) fifty feet

occupancy, which means there is a lower likelihood of a tree failure impacting a target.  The trees are

Mitigation Options and Residual Risk Ratings 
In my professional opinion, due to the close proximity of all subject trees to one another in the circle, 
it is safe to assume that the tree roots are intertwined, similar to the way the upper canopies are.  
With this in mind, what happens to one tree will have a significant impact on the trees surrounding it.  
Removal of any of the subject trees will require grinding of the stumps to avoid a trip hazard and 
prevent regrowth.  The grinding of stumps will damage the roots of any remaining trees, creating a 
potentially hazardous situation as tree failure can happen with little or no warning with significant root 
damage. 
 
Removal is STRONGLY RECOMMENDED

health/extensive decay and potential hazard.  This is the best long-term and safest solution in my 
professional opinion.

Conclusion: Follow-up Inspection Intervals 
All twenty (20) Subject Trees are in poor health with significant decay in the root zones which cannot 
be cut out.  Four (4) of the trees are considered a HIGH risk at this time, with two (2) having to be 
coned and caution-taped off following the site inspection on 6/26/23 as they have significant visible 
cracks in more than one main stem.  Five (5) of the trees are considered to be a MODERATE risk at 
this time.  The remaining eleven (11) eleven trees are currently LOW risk, however removal is 
recommended due to decay in root zones and potential damage from close proximity to trees 
recommended for removal. 
After careful consideration, I am not recommending pruning as an option for mitigation as it will not 
correct the main defects, which are the decay pockets extending into the root zones.  Any pruning 
with the goal of making the trees safe would involve removal of any and all decayed/decaying plant 
parts, which would leave very little plant material in place.  Removal of dead branches will not mitigate 
the areas of decay in the lower portion of the trees.  These trees will continue to decline, and become 
more and more hazardous as the existing decay progresses.
