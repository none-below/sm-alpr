Dear Members of the City of San Mateo Parks and Recreation Commission, 
I am writing to you as a concerned resident and avid supporter of our community’s well-being, particularly in 
regards to the upcoming Central Park Master Plan. I believe that the inclusion of a dedicated dog park within the 
master plan is essential for the overall enhancement of our city’s recreational offerings and the promotion of a more 
vibrant and inclusive community. 
As a dog owner myself, I understand the importance of having safe and designated spaces where our furry 
companions can exercise, socialize, and enjoy off-leash activities. A dedicated dog park not only provides a valuable 
recreational outlet for dog owners but also fosters a sense of community among residents who share a common love 
for their pets. 
Furthermore, a well-designed dog park can contribute to the overall attractiveness and livability of our city, 
attracting new residents and visitors alike. It serves as a gathering place where neighbors can meet, connect, and 
build relationships, thereby strengthening the fabric of our community.’
Finally, as a downtown resident myself, I am aware of the planned future development of additional housing in and 
around the downtown core over the coming years. It is vital that with that additional housing development that we 
prioritize the creation of appropriate amenities to service them downtown community, including the development 
of  a dedicated off leash dog park in the Central Park Master Plan. This is especially important to our downtown 
residents, who are encouraged to live “car free” lifestyles given the vibrancy of our downtown core and the nearby 
availability of mass transit, and the lack of any nearby walkable alternatives.
I urge the Parks and Recreation Commission and the City Council to prioritize the inclusion of a dedicated dog park 
in the Central Park Master Plan. By doing so, we will not only cater to the needs of current residents but also 
demonstrate our commitment to creating a more inclusive and enjoyable environment for all members of our 
community, both two-legged and four-legged.

Permanent Dog Park request for Central Park
As a dedicated dog owner and resident of this wonderful city, I am writing to let you know that I am very 
much in support of establishing a permanent dog park within Central Park. Such a facility would not only 
benefit our furry companions but also enhance the overall community experience. Here are a few 
reasons why I believe a permanent dog park at Central Park is essential:  
Community Bonding: Dog parks allow pet owners to come together, socialize, and build lasting 
connections. These interactions contribute to a sense of community and promote positive relationships 
among residents. 
Health and Exercise: Dogs require regular exercise to maintain their physical and mental well-being. A 
dedicated dog park with ample space to run and play would ensure our pets stay healthy and active.  
Safety: A designated area for dogs within Central Park would improve safety for pets and park visitors. It 
would reduce the risk of off-leash dogs in non-designated areas and mitigate potential conflicts.  
Tourism and Recreation:  A permanent dog park would cater to locals and enhance its appeal to 
other city residents looking to enjoy outdoor activities with their pets. 
Please also consider that Central Park is surrounded by dog-friendly apartments and condos (with no 
substantial backyards) and the apartment buildings in downtown San Mateo and the surrounding 
neighborhoods. Currently, there is no permanent dog park at Central Park. Thirty-three thousand five 
hundred seventy-two people live in the surrounding area of Central Park, but there is no dog park within 
walking distance.  
I understand that implementing a permanent dog park requires thoughtful planning and consideration of 
various factors, including space allocation, maintenance, and community input. However, the long-term 
benefits far outweigh the initial challenges. I am willing to collaborate with city officials, community 
organizations, and fellow dog owners to support the establishment of a permanent dog park at Central 
Park. Together, we can create a vibrant and inclusive space that enhances the quality of life for humans 
and animals. 
I'm looking forward to hearing your thoughts on this matter and discussing potential next steps.

SUBJECT:
Proposed Five-Year (2024-2029) Parks and Recreation Capital Improvement Plan
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and comment on Parks and Recreation’s Proposed Five-Year Capital Improvement Plan for FY 2024-2029.
BACKGROUND:
Five-Year Plan. The Parks and Recreation Department develops an updated five-year Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) for 
the Parks and Recreation-related projects every Fiscal Year (FY). The development process includes staff review of 
necessary park and recreation improvements and anticipated revenues for each year of the plan. Staff then establishes 
recommendations for consideration. The Proposed FY 2024-2029 CIP is included as Attachment 1 to this report. 
This CIP provides cost estimates for new and existing projects for each of the next five years. Staff developed the plan in 
recognition of the significant revenue challenges the City faces for years 3-5. Only years 1 and 2 have enough revenue data 
to allow staff to make solid recommendations for project spending. 
Revenue. There are several sources of revenue to fund park and recreation capital improvements: the City’s General Fund 
(GF); Measure S (MS); Grants (GR); Miscellaneous Fund (MISC); Tree Planting Fund (Tree); and park in-lieu fees and park 
impact fees (ILF). The ILF provide most of the funding and are derived from residential development activity. Therefore, 
economic and real estate market conditions have a substantial impact on revenue received for parks and recreation 
projects and income fluctuates year to year. In a volatile marketplace, estimates can be very challenging, particularly in out 
years. 
Staff has investigated these conditions and has developed revenue estimates based on the information gathered. 
Currently, the Department has approximately $16.3 million in reserves. The five-year forecast, including approximately 
$3.3 million additional expected this financial year, totals $33 million. 
Prioritizing Projects. Staff uses a variety of prioritization methods when recommending projects to Council for funding. 
Included are the following methods:
▪
Available funding and funding restrictions. For example, park-in-lieu fees can only fund park projects.
▪
Previously identified Council and community priorities.
▪
The amount of investment already made into a project. 
▪
The availability and time limitations on grants or other matching funds.
▪
The location of the project – ensures that all areas of the city receive investment in nearby infrastructure.
▪
Maintenance deficiencies. These projects are classified in six categories: Priority 1 is currently critical; Priority 2 is 
potentially critical; Priority 3 is necessary but not yet critical; Priority 4 is recommended; Priority 5 is appearance;
