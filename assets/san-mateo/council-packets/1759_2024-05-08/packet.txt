BACKGROUND:
History 
During 2005, the City adopted the San Mateo Rail Corridor Transit Oriented Development Plan (Corridor Plan) with the 
goal to “to allow, encourage, and provide guidance for the creation of world class transit-oriented development (TOD) 
within a half-mile radius of the Hillsdale and Hayward Park Caltrain station areas, while maintaining and improving the 
quality of life for those who already live and work in the area.” The Corridor Plan includes a framework for (1) creation of a 
TOD, (2) implementation of a Transportation Demand Management (TDM) program with a goal of achieving an overall 
reduction in new vehicle trips by at least 25 percent corridor-wide, (3) establishment of trip generation thresholds, (4) 
establishment of parking standards, and (5) monitoring of trip generation. The Corridor Plan established a new TOD zone, 
which generally encompasses key parcels located within a half-mile of the Hillsdale and Hayward Park Caltrain stations.
This new land use includes transit-supportive development densities, building heights and design guidelines. The broader 
Corridor Plan area extends beyond the TOD zone and includes other areas in close proximity to these stations. The 
Corridor Plan also called for the creation of a San Mateo Rail Corridor Transportation Management Association (TMA). 
Participation in the TMA is (1) required for all new development within the TOD zone, (2) strongly encouraged for all new 
development within the broader Corridor Plan area, and (3) available to any existing uses outside of the Corridor Plan area 
(Policy 7.18). 
The TMA comprises developers and developer representatives that have, or are planning, significant development within 
the Corridor area. The City participates as a non-voting Board member and does not pay dues to the TMA. The City Council 
has nominated the Director of Public Works as the City representative to the TMA Board of Directors. The City’s role in the 
TMA is to provide oversight of each project’s mandated participation as required in the project’s Conditions of Approval 
(COA). 
Annual Report Process
The TMA facilitates data collection regarding the number of vehicle trips generated by each development per the 
individual project’s approved TDM plan. Trip generation is determined by collecting driveway counts at all occupied 
developments. These data are compared to the allowable trip generation threshold identified in each development’s COA. 
A development is deemed non-compliant if the observed number of vehicle trips exceeds its trip generation threshold. The

2023 Annual Report Summary
A total of 25 individual projects have been completed as of the completion of the 2023 Annual Report. Per the prepared 
report, most projects were below their short-term trip thresholds based on their individual project Conditions of Approval. 
Three (3) projects stood out as notable in the 2023 Annual TMA Report.
•
Azara Residential
For the second year in a row, Azara residential project was at their trip threshold. The project may want to 
consider reduction options so as not to exceed their threshold in the future.  
•
Delaware Pacific and 2000 Delaware (Shared Driveway)
Delaware Pacific and 2000 Delaware share a common driveway and thus the trip generation is calculated together; 
the counted trip generation during the PM peak period was five (5) trips above the short-term collective goal for 
the projects. This is a decrease from the 2022 trip counts, when the project was 15 trips above the trip threshold. 
Per the Corridor Plan, “Projects that exceed their trip generation threshold shall be required to modify their trip 
reduction and parking management plan and incorporate TDM measures that are expected to increase trip 
reduction.” (Policy 7.24). Delaware Pacific (MidPen Housing) has been participating in a Metropolitan 
Transportation Commission (MTC) regional fare pilot pass program since 2023 that offers residents free multi-
agency transit passes via the Clipper Card to encourage transit use. 2000 Delaware is not eligible for this program. 
Both projects have implemented their required TDM programs and measures to date, and City staff has been 
working with the projects to implement additional education and outreach about their existing measures. 
•
Bay Meadows
While the Bay Meadows project exceeded their trip threshold by 3%, or 77 trips, during the 2022 TMA annual 
reporting process, new tenants in the project have implemented additional TDM measures such as hybrid work 
schedules and transit subsidies. Further, based on feedback from projects the trip generation collection 
methodology was modified this year to account for through trips and construction impacts. In the 2023 trip count 
process, the Bay Meadows trip generation was estimated at 76% of the maximum trip threshold, well within their 
allowable trips.   
Staff has reviewed the data analysis and report prepared for the TMA Board of Directors, and recommends the 
Commission recommend acceptance of the report to the City Council.

Executive Summary 
 
This report details the results of vehicle trip monitoring conducted under the San Mateo Rail Corridor 
Transit-Oriented Development Plan (Corridor Plan). It also presents a qualitative overview of Transportation 
Demand Management (TDM) plans for completed projects in the Rail Corridor area and a summary of the 
results of a survey of San Mateo Rail Corridor Transportation Management Association (TMA) members. 
Data collection for the 2023 annual monitoring of the TMA was completed on October 17, 2023. The trip 
generation counts indicated that all projects except one were at or below their short-term thresholds. One 
project, Delaware Pacific and 2000 Delaware, generated 57 PM peak-hour vehicle trips, which exceeded 
the short-term threshold of 52 peak-hour vehicle trips by five trips. The count for Azara, 25 PM peak-hour 
trips, was equal to its short-term threshold. 
Compared to the 2022 counts, the 2023 trip counts indicated higher trip generation at the commercial 
office projects, with values above those reported in 2019, before the COVID-19 Pandemic, and slight 
decreases at all residential projects except Azara. 
Bay Meadows Phase II trip generation was estimated according to the full monitoring requirement using 
cordon counts and license plate studies like that employed for the 2022 Annual Report. The resulting trip 
generation was 2,178 PM peak hour vehicle trips, approximately 76% of the maximum trip threshold (2,878 
PM peak hour vehicle trips) allowed under the Conditions of Approval (COA) for the mid-term stage. This 
value is slightly higher than the estimate for 2019 (pre-pandemic) (2,113 PM peak-hour vehicle trips). 
During entitlement, all TMA member projects were required to develop TDM or trip reduction plans that 
reflected the City's guiding documents and practices. Relevant documents were reviewed to document trip 
reduction thresholds and TDM or trip reduction strategies. Bicycle parking, transit provisions, and commute 
coordination were cited most often in these documents. 
Similar to past years, TMA members completed a survey developed to solicit feedback about current 
practices. Responses were received from member representatives of all 12 completed projects, and 
essential facts about TDM and trip reduction practices, including management, specific programs offered, 
distribution of TDM information, and surveying travel behavior, were included in the responses.

Background 
In 2005, the City of San Mateo (City) adopted the Corridor Plan. The plan's stated goal is to allow, 
encourage, and provide guidance for the creation of world-class transit-oriented development (TOD) within 
a half-mile radius of the Hillsdale and Hayward Park Caltrain station areas while maintaining and improving 
the quality of life for those who already live and work in the area.  
The Corridor Plan included a framework for the creation of TOD, implementation of a TDM program that 
generates an overall reduction in new vehicle trips of at least 25% corridor-wide, the establishment of trip 
generation thresholds, establishment of parking standards, and monitoring of trip generation. The Corridor 
Plan also called for the creation of the TMA. It stipulated that participation in the TMA was required for all 
new development within the TOD zone, strongly encouraged for all new development within the broader 
Corridor Plan area, and available to any existing uses outside the Corridor Plan area (Policy 7.18).  
The City adopted the Hillsdale Station Area Plan (Station Plan) in 2011. The area addressed in the Station 
Plan was based on the site within walking distance of the now-relocated Hillsdale Caltrain Station. The 
Station Plan expanded the TMA formed under the Corridor Plan to include all properties within the Station 
Plan area and required all new developments to join the TMA. It stated that Hillsdale Shopping Center’s 
participation in the TMA will consist of optional measures and will not be subject to the TMA’s trip reduction 
goals (Policy TRA-4.2). 
Figure 1 illustrates the Corridor Plan and Station Plan areas. 
Purpose of the TMA 
As defined in its bylaws, the TMA aims to implement the Objectives of the Corridor Plan. Within the Corridor 
Plan area, the TMA provides the following functions. 
• 
Oversee TDM program implementation; 
• 
Arrange shared parking, as appropriate; 
• 
Market TDM services and programs; 
• 
Coordinate TDM services and programs; 
• 
Coordinate with the City on annual trip generation monitoring for completed projects; 
• 
Participate in annual reporting to the San Mateo City Council about development trip generation 
information; 
• 
Consult with non-compliant members about changes to their TDM programs. 
Outside the corridor area, the TMA may coordinate with other agencies. 
No TDM services or programs are currently funded by the TMA. Member projects with active TDM 
programs do so at their own expense, independently or in partnership with other members or 
Commute.org.

Trip Generation Thresholds and TDM Measures 
During each development project’s entitlement phase, vehicle trip generation is calculated using trip 
generation rates published in the Institute of Transportation Engineers (ITE) Trip Generation Manual. Trip 
reductions are calculated based on a development’s density, location, proximity to transit, the mix of land 
uses, and TDM programs. The Conditions of Approval (COA) establish short-term and long-term trip 
generation thresholds that need to be met for the life of a project. Short-term goals are based on the 
varying status of completed projects within the corridor area. Long-term trip reduction goals are based on 
the full build-out of the space into the transit-oriented neighborhood envisioned in the Corridor Plan. Many 
long-term goals are also based on the 25th Avenue Grade Separation Project, which created new roadway 
connections on 28th and 31st Avenues between El Camino Real and Delaware Street. 
TMA projects must submit individual TDM plans with a list of trip-reduction strategies to be implemented 
once the project is occupied. TDM plans are tailored to reflect the project's location, proximity and access 
to transit, walkability, proposed land uses, and other relevant factors. 
Trip Generation Monitoring 
Scope of Study 
The TMA is required to collect data annually regarding the number of vehicle trips generated by each 
development. Trip generation is determined by collecting driveway counts at all occupied projects. These 
data are compared to the allowable trip generation threshold identified in each development’s COA. All 
projects are currently required to meet their short-term trip reduction goals. 
TMA Trip Generation 
Driveway count data were collected during the PM peak period (4:00 PM to 6:00 PM) on Tuesday, October 
17, 2023, to determine PM peak hour trips generated by each project. For reference, driveway counts were 
conducted for all projects outside Bay Meadows Phase II because a different monitoring procedure was 
used for this area. Therefore, driveway counts were not performed for Montara and Nueva School within 
Bay Meadows Phase II.

Bay Meadows Phase II Trip Generation 
Trip generation for Bay Meadows Phase II was evaluated according to the full monitoring requirement using cordon 
counts and license plate studies like that employed for the 2022 Annual Report. The counts and surveys were done 
on Tuesday, October 17, 2023. The procedures and results of this monitoring effort are presented below. 
Previous Driveway Counts and Maximum Trip Threshold 
Before 2019, the City conducted annual driveway counts at each block within Bay Meadows Phase II as an interim 
method to gain insight into the neighborhood's trip generation. The driveway count program has proven to be a 
cost-effective way to capture most of the trips generated by Bay Meadows Phase II. It was noted in the 2018 Annual 
Report that Bay Meadows Phase II trip generation would likely increase when the full monitoring program is 
implemented and all trips, including vehicles that park on the street, are counted.  
Bay Meadows Phase II project development is divided into four stages: one stage reflecting pre-grade separation 
conditions and three stages of post-grade separation reflecting short-term (Phase I), mid-term (Phase II), and long-
term (Phase III) conditions. For the post-grade separation stages, trip generation goals were set as follows: 10% 
short-term, 16% mid-term, and 25% long-term reduction. The trip reduction goal was determined based on the 
grade separation project and the overall development completed.  
Bay Meadows Phase II is currently in the mid-term (Phase II) post-grade separation stage of development after the 
completion of the 25th Avenue Grade Separation Project, based on the COA. Also, per the COA, the mid-term trip 
reduction goal for this stage is 16% of the total PM peak hour ITE trip generation for completed projects. The 
maximum trip threshold for the mid-term stage is 2,878 PM peak-hour vehicle trips.

SUBJECT:
Citywide Complete Streets Plan – Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on the San Mateo Citywide Complete Streets Plan and provide feedback.
BACKGROUND:
The City was awarded approximately $490,000 from the Caltrans Sustainable Communities grant program to fund the San 
Mateo Complete Streets Plan (Plan). The Plan will complement the City’s General Plan Update by creating an actionable 
plan rooted in safety for all modes and will result in policies, goals, and prioritized projects that are focused on improving 
mobility, equity, connectivity, and sustainability to build a better circulation network for the City’s future. The Plan will 
provide the basis for projects that are planned, designed, constructed, and maintained for all roadway users, allowing the 
City to develop a safe mobility network for many years to come. The final outcome of this effort will be an adopted 
Citywide Complete Streets Plan and a Design Standard Manual. The Citywide Complete Streets Plan effort began in 
November 2022. Staff and the consultant team will present an update on the project for the Commission's review and 
feedback. 
Community Engagement 
Given the nature of the project, community engagement has been a key component to provide information and direction 
for this project. Staff and the consultant team have completed the following engagement activities to date for the 
Complete Streets Plan. Staff recently completed Phase 3 of community engagement, which is the final phase for the 
project. This phase of engagement included the following and is summarized in greater detail in Attachment 1. 
●
Community Engagement: City staff and the consultant team hosted three (3) in-person community workshops 
throughout the City’s five council districts. Following the successful implementation of district-focused workshops 
for the General Plan 2040, hosting the workshops in this manner provided engagement opportunities nearer to 
residents homes, and ability for Councilmembers to more deeply engage with the project. The workshops were 
advertised to attendees via the City’s e-newsletter, the City’s social media channels, emails to neighborhood 
associations and school stakeholders, and information on the City’s website.
Each workshop consisted of an open-house format that was modeled on prior successful engagement showcasing 
maps of potential improvements along the Plan’s ten (10) identified priority corridors. A presentation was offered 
midway through the open house to more fully explain the project, with opportunity for Q&A.
Understanding feedback that residents and other stakeholders appreciate an opportunity to participate virtually if 
not able to make an in-person meeting, staff provided an online survey modeled on the open house to provide a 
similar ability to provide in-depth comments on each priority corridor.
The workshops offered simultaneous Spanish translation, and the survey was available in Spanish as well.

Feedback: Both the workshops and online survey offered residents opportunity to provide feedback on the priority

Policies and Actions 
Previously in the project effort, staff and the consultant team developed four key policies guiding the creation of the 
Complete Streets Plan; these policies were confirmed by Commission and City Council. Staff and the consultant team 
developed actions for each policy, summarized briefly below.
•
Prioritize Safety for All Modes: This policy focuses on prioritizing user safety when designing projects and 
roadways. The actions identified for this policy include an affirmation of commitment to Vision Zero, 
implementation of safety countermeasures for safe roadways, updating local speed limits, and systematically 
updating and enhancing crosswalks.
•
Rebalance Priorities: When conflicts arise between different modal needs, this priority focuses on developing 
context-sensitive solutions to prioritize the use of the right of way. Actions identified for this policy include 
prioritizing safety for all users first, using street typologies and modal priorities described later in this report to 
balance and make prioritization decisions, implement the toolbox of strategies in the Design Standards Manual to 
enhance street safety, and create one-page project fact sheets for each project identifying performance measures 
and modal priorities.
•
Fund and Implement Projects: This policy provides direction for establishing funding mechanisms to implement 
the priority projects in the Complete Streets Plan, and to coordinate construction of complete streets in other City 
projects. Actions related to this goal include aligning roadway project funding with Complete Streets approaches, 
collaborating interdepartmentally to ensure funding and project development are consistent with Complete 
Streets, establishing guidance for appropriate times to seek Council and Commission feedback on projects, and 
requiring private developers to include Complete Streets infrastructure improvements along project frontages to 
jointly reach City multimodal goals.
•
Engage our Communities: The final policy focuses on adopting inclusive and consistent outreach and engagement 
standards promoting transparency and accountability for transportation projects. Actions identified for this policy 
include designing accessible engagement, integrating project-related webpages into a cohesive one-stop shop, and 
implementing project-level engagement strategies based on type of feedback sought and how it will be used. 
Staff and the consultant team will provide additional detail on the proposed Plan policies and actions and request 
Commission feedback.
Street Typologies and Modal Priorities
As noted above, City staff and the consultant team developed a methodology to utilize street characteristics and adjacent 
land uses to identify modal priorities as part of the policy to rebalance priorities. Determining modal prioritization based 
on the width of the roadway and the land uses that are primarily present enables staff and others to more consistently 
allocate available right-of-way as well as make operational decisions based on the modal priorities. In having this 
information available when designing future projects, staff can identify the intended modal priority for a given roadway 
and design improvements based on those priorities rather than identifying priorities on a project to project basis. This 
piecemeal approach is how the roadway design process is currently completed, and results in inconsistencies in active 
transportation implementation. 
First, land use types have been identified as neighborhood, mixed use, and commercial based on the land uses designated 
in the recently adopted General Plan 2040. 
•
Neighborhood: Primarily residential, with schools, parks, and some small businesses. Multimodal safety and urban 
greening are prioritized with a balanced approach to residential parking management.
•
Mixed-Use: Characterizes newly developing neighborhoods and transit-oriented communities. These areas are

highly walkable with multimodal access to higher density homes and businesses.
•
Commercial: Larger businesses less oriented to the street, including malls and office parks. Circulation is focused 
on enhancing safety and multimodal access. Also includes service areas with light industrial uses.
Generally, streets have been identified as compact, intermediate, or broad based on existing street widths; these 
designations in combination with adjacent land uses will be used to identify specific types of implementations in the 
Complete Streets Plan. While it is anticipated that these designations may lend to modifications in Caltrans-specified 
roadway classifications, they are not intended as a one-for-one replacement. 
•
Compact: Very narrow roadways, providing local access to homes and businesses.
•
Intermediate: Provide circulation across land use contexts, with multi-modal needs strategically prioritized based 
on modal networks.
•
Broad: Very wide roadways that carry more multimodal circulation and have high need for safety management.
The combination of land use type and roadway width provide the street typology, which aligns with design specifications 
in the Design Standards Manual. For example, a roadway classified as mixed-use intermediate would align with certain 
lane widths, bike facility types and widths, and sidewalk requirements that are all outlined in the Manual. 
Additionally, the street typologies were utilized to develop modal priorities, or transportation modes that are prioritized 
for that type of roadway and are crucial in determining the types of tools that may be used on each corridor. A modal 
priority may be pedestrian, transit, bike, or auto; not having a certain modal priority for a roadway does not mean the 
mode will not exist on that roadway. Rather, it means that people utilizing the priority mode need additional support for 
safe mobility and access. 
•
Neighborhood Streets: Safety is prioritized for all users on these streets, including those who live, walk, bike, drive, 
take transit, and play there. Design on these roadways will focus on safety for all modes, and speed management.
•
Mixed-use intermediate: These roadways, such as Alameda de las Pulgas, will prioritize bike/pedestrian and 
transit, indicating these modes need additional support for safety on these types of corridors. This does not 
preclude or remove autos from the corridor. 
•
East-West Connectors: Roadways with specific characteristics, like those that cross US 101, have modal priorities 
for pedestrians and autos to facilitate and enhance safe connectivity for these modes. 
Implementation and Funding
Implementation of San Mateo’s Complete Streets Plan will require a multi-pronged approach that not only advances the 
priority corridor and area projects but also integrates complete streets into all policies, practices, programs, and projects 
that the City advances.  Staff and the consultant team have developed the following that will be part of the Plan’s 
Implementation and Funding chapter.
•
Implementation Action Plan: The City of San Mateo Public Works Department will need to partner closely with 
other City departments and related agencies to ensure that complete streets best practices are fully integrated into 
the planning, design, operations, and maintenance of San Mateo’s streets. Staff and the project team have 
developed a matrix identifying lead department(s), timeline, and key steps for each action identified in the Plan.
•
Implementation Cost Estimates: Staff and the consultant team are developing planning level cost estimates for 
the various design elements recommended in the Plan. Each element will be estimated as a unit cost, allowing for 
total estimated project costs to be developed based on elements integrated in a design.
•
Funding Strategy: Transforming San Mateo’s streets will require substantial funding and leveraging existing 
funding resources to secure county, regional, state, and/or federal funding. The Plan identifies four components 
to securing funding for Complete Streets implementation:
o
Integrate improvements into routine maintenance, e.g. resurfacing or restriping projects
o
Leverage local resources to secure county, regional, state, and/or federal funding
o
Apply for grant funding
o
Use development funding and impact fees where feasible

Design Standards Manual
The Design Standards Manual conveys the City’s standards for all projects planned, designed, constructed, operated, and 
maintained in the public right-of-way in San Mateo. The Manual spells out the City’s basic expectations in providing 
transportation infrastructure citywide. As a result, the Design Standards Manual is expected to be a well-used reference 
for Public Works and Planning staff as well as private developers, architects, engineers, and planners working in San 
Mateo. It is also an important resource for members of the public to explain San Mateo’s design expectations clearly and 
transparently.  
The Design Standards Manual provides references to other City standards, polices, and guidance documents that are 
related to the planning, design, construction, operation, and maintenance of the public right-of-way. Following adoption of 
the Complete Streets Plan, when a member of the public, a private firm, or a public agency designs, plans, operates, 
and/or maintains transportation infrastructure in San Mateo, the Design Standards Manual must be referenced.  
The Design Standards Manual content generally includes the following, which will be described at a high level during staff’s 
presentation.
•
Design Process, Street Typologies, and Citywide Design Assumptions
