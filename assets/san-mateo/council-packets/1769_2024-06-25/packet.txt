BACKGROUND:
The applicant submitted a Pre-Application (PA-2022-071) in August 2022, and a formal Planning Application (PA-2023-003) 
in February 2023 to demolish all existing structures on the block bounded by E. 4th Avenue, S. Delaware Street, E. 5th 
Avenue, and S. Claremont Street to construct a proposed six-story mixed-use building with office and residential uses 
called Block 20. On December 13, 2022, the Planning Commission held a Study Session to provide preliminary feedback 
focused on proposed uses and design and massing of the building. On October 5, 2023, the Planning Commission held an 
Environmental Impact Report (EIR) scoping meeting for the project as the original project proposed demolition of the two 
existing historic buildings on site, necessitating an EIR due to the potential significant impact on historic resources. During 
the scoping meeting, participants and the Planning Commission expressed concern about the overall massing and the 
demolition of historic resources. 
On March 22, 2024, the applicant submitted a revised project for a portion of Block 20, with the parcels located at 415 S. 
Claremont Street (Japanese American Community Center) and 521 E. 5th Avenue (Safari Kids) removed from the project. 
By shrinking the project footprint, the proposal would avoid the existing historic resources and childcare uses within that 
block. The revised project consists of a seven-story mixed-use building instead of the previous six stories, with four levels 
of office uses, 72 residential units, and a two-level below-grade parking garage. The total floor area is reduced from 
216,000 to 170,163 square feet, which includes reductions in housing units, office floor area, open space, and parking 
stalls. The applicant proposes to utilize Assembly Bill (AB) 1287 to obtain a 100% density bonus, which would support the 
further increase in the height and floor area ratio (FAR) for the revised project. As the residential uses occupies less than 
two-thirds (2/3) of the total floor area, the project is not a housing development project under Government Code Section 
65589.5(h)(2) (Housing Accountability Act). Due to the significant changes in the proposed project, another Planning 
Commission Study Session is required as part of the application resubmittal. For this Study Session, the Planning 
Commission should focus its review on the project’s building design and other aspects necessary for the Commission to 
make positive findings.

APPLICABLE CODE AND POLICY REVIEW:
General Plan
The revised project was resubmitted on March 22, 2024, prior to General Plan 2040’s effective date on April 17, 2024. 
Therefore, the project is subject to the goals, policies, and actions within General Plan 2030 per the adopted City Council 
Resolution. The General Plan 2030 Land Use Map designates the project site as Downtown Retail Core Support, which is 
meant to provide uses at intensities that complement both the downtown core and the adjacent residential 
neighborhoods. As a mixed-use building, the revised project would conform to Land Use Element policy LU 1.8 Mixed Use 
Commercial-Residential, which promotes housing production, and Land Use Element policy LU 3.1 Downtown Plan in that 
the project would result in mixed uses at high intensities that complement the downtown. A preliminary list of General 
Plan 2030 policies applicable to the proposed project is included in Attachment 3.

The project site is zoned CBD/S (Central Business District Support), subject to development standards in Chapter 27.39 of

State Density Bonus Law and Local Inclusionary Requirements
As mentioned in the background section, the revised project is not a housing development project as defined in Housing 
Accountability Act (HAA). California Government Code Section 65915 outlines density bonus standards for residential and 
mixed-use projects that designate a portion of residential units as affordable, or below market rate (BMR). The City also

has an adopted BMR Inclusionary Program that requires residential developments to designate at least 15 percent of their 
base density units as affordable at the low-income or very low-income level for rental projects. Assembly Bill (AB) 1287, 
effective on January 1, 2024, creates a new stackable density bonus, allowing any project that is eligible for 50% base 
density bonus to receive an additional 20 to 50% density bonus if the project includes more affordable housing at very low-

The project’s zoning designation of CBD/S permits a maximum base density of 50 units per acre, which yields this site a 
base density of 39 units. The applicant proposes to devote 15 percent of the base 39 units at the very low-income level (six 
units) and additional 15 percent at the moderate-income level (six units). A combined maximum density of 78 units may be 
permitted under state density bonus provisions.

ITEMS FOR PLANNING COMMISSION DISCUSSION:
Proposed Uses
At the previous Study Session, the community voiced their concern that the subject project, combined with other nearby 
mixed-use projects, could lead to the displacement of local small businesses and a reduction in neighborhood diversity. 
The Planning Commission recommended incorporating additional neighborhood commercial uses including small-scale

--- page 12 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
retail and local restaurants at the ground level. It’s worth noting that the original submittal included a restaurant/cafe at 
the corner of S. Delaware Street and 5th Avenue, which was eliminated in this revision. At the second neighborhood 
meeting to introduce the revised project, on May 29, 2024, members of the public expressed their concerns about the loss 
of local and small businesses in and around downtown and the lack of affordable retail spaces at the ground level in new 
development. 
At a policy level, the Downtown Area Plan Central Claremont Sub-area Policy (a) “encourages, but does not require, ground 
floor retail uses on E. 4th Avenue to link this area to the downtown core. Uses that promote pedestrian activity are also 
encouraged.” While office and residential uses are allowed in this area and the City is limited in its ability to require a 
different mix of uses, there is still an opportunity to share input to the applicant about whether additional neighborhood 
commercial uses should be considered as part of the project. If the Commission has any input on the proposed uses, 
existing City adopted plans and policies should be used to support the recommendations.
Building Design
The revised project retains the Mediterranean architectural style of the original submittal with modifications based on 
input from the Planning Commission and design review consultant, such as changing the exterior color to ivory from the 
4th to 7th floors. The overall massing is similar to the original submittal and other recently constructed or approved 
projects in the area, characterized by a stacked building appearance with tiered upper floors on a larger multi-level base. 
However, the revised project features fewer and less prominent architectural articulations, shallower stepbacks at the 
upper levels, longer continuous horizontal walls, and less variation in the roof planes. Arches, a key feature of 
Mediterranean architecture are also not included. 
While the project employs a similar approach to building massing to surrounding mixed-use projects, the project site is 
adjacent to a lower density neighborhood immediately south and east of the site that are lower in scale and simpler in 
form. In particular, the proposed seven-story building is adjacent to three single-story buildings on the same block, two of 
which are historic resources. The Urban Design Element of the General Plan recommends that new multi-family 
developments respect the existing scale of surrounding uses by stepping down the building height to more closely match 
the height of surrounding uses (Urban Design Policies UD 2.2 and UD 2.7). 
The revised building, given its proximity to the historic resource, would be reviewed under the Secretary of the Interior’s 
Standards during the environmental review process. According to the City’s 1989 Historic Survey and a 2023 Historical 
Resources Evaluation (HRE) report, the historic Japanese American Community Center adjacent to the site is Gothic Revival 
in style. The 2023 HRE focused site-specific historic resource evaluation without conducting a comprehensive historic or 
cultural district evaluation related to the historic context of Japanese Americans in San Mateo. Staff will work with the 
project’s environmental review consultant to include an assessment of potential impact on the historic resources. 
To guide revisions of the project’s design, staff is seeking the Planning Commission’s input on the overall style, massing, 
and transition to adjacent buildings, as well as identification of other design revisions that could improve the overall 
project. When evaluating the proposed building design, staff recommends the Planning Commission consider the following 
questions:
•
Should the applicant consider removing or adding any design treatments to improve the Mediterranean 
architectural style and consistency of design across all floors?
•
What modifications to overall massing or landscaping should be made to improve the project’s transition to the 
adjacent one to two-story buildings? 
•
Are there other design changes or revisions that should be considered to improve the project’s architectural design, 
its overall form and function, and how it fits into and relates to the larger downtown context?

Land Use Element 
 
LU 1.5:  
Building Height. Maintain maximum building height limits contained in Appendix 
C, and as specified in Policy LU 6A.2, closely matched with the Land Use

LU 1.8:  
Mixed Use Commercial-Residential. Facilitate housing production by allowing 
commercial mixed-use development which includes multi-family dwellings in all 
non-residential land use categories except service commercial, 
manufacturing/industrial, and parks/open space. 
 
LU 1.10: 
Commercial Development. Encourage industrial, service, retail, and office 
development which is compatible with the desired character of the area and with 
adjacent residential areas in terms of intensity of use, height, bulk and design as 
delineated on the Land Use Plan, Building Height Plan and Building Intensity Plan. 
Commercial development adjacent to residential areas shall address concerns 
pertaining to traffic, truck loading, trash/recycling activities, noise, visual impacts, 
and public safety including hazardous material storage, fire safety, air pollutant 
emissions and odors. 
 
LU 2.3:  
Local Employment. Encourage uses, which provide opportunities for employment 
of all the City's residents, with emphasis placed on major employers that provide 
high value-added jobs. 
 
LU 2.4           Downtown Plan. Establish downtown San Mateo as the social, cultural, and 
economic center of the City with a wide range of office, medical, residential, 
entertainment, and retail uses at high intensities and densities while encourage 
pedestrian activity and bicycle connectivity to adjacent neighborhoods. 
 
LU 2.8:  
Convenience Retail. Encourage and preserve convenience retail uses located 
adjacent to residential neighborhoods. 
 
LU 3.1: 
Downtown Plan. As the social, cultural and economic center of the City, the 
downtown shall maintain a wide range of office, medical, residential, 
entertainment, and retail uses at high intensities and densities. 
 
LU 4.2:  
Developer's Contribution Policy. Require new development to pay on an 
equitable basis for new or expanded public improvements needed to support the 
new or changed land use or development. 
 
LU 4.32: 
Recycling.  Support programs to recycle solid waste in compliance with State 
requirements.  Designate an area on a site plan for new multi-family 
developments exclusively for recycling.

C 2.4: 
Transportation Fee Ordinance. Require new developments to pay their 
proportionate share of the costs for planned on and off site roadway 
improvements. Utilize a Transportation Fee Ordinance to finance necessary 
improvement equitably.  
 
C 2.5: 
Traffic Studies.  Require site-specific traffic studies for development projects 
where there may be a substantial impact on the local street system.  Traffic 
impacts caused by a development project are considered to be unacceptable and 
warrant mitigation if the addition of project traffic results in a cumulative 
intersection level of service exceeding the acceptable level established in Policy C-
2.1; where there may be safety hazards created; or where there may be other 
substantial impacts on the circulation system.  
 
C 2.10:  
Transportation Demand Management (TDM).  Participate in the TDM Program as 
outlined by the San Mateo City/County Association of Governments (C/CAG).  
Encourage TDM measures as a condition of approval for development projects, 
which are anticipated to cause substantial traffic impacts. C/CAG requires the 
preparation of a TDM program for all new development that would add 100 peak 
hour trips or more to the regional road network. 
 
C 2.12:  
Transportation Demand Management (TDM) in Downtown. Establish and 
implement a TDM program, a Transportation Management Association (TMA), 
and other measures to reduce vehicle trips and encourage transit use and 
promote bicycle and pedestrian accessibility for development within one-half mile 
of the Downtown transit center. 
 
C 4.1: 
Bicycle Master Plan.  Implement the Bicycle Master Plan’s recommended 
programs and projects to create and maintain a fully-connected safe and logical 
bikeways system; support the City's Sustainable Transportation Actions; and 
coordinate with the countywide system.  
 
C.4.4: 
Pedestrian Master Plan.  Implement the Pedestrian Master Plan’s recommended 
programs and projects to create and maintain a walkable environment in San 
Mateo and support the City’s Sustainable Transportation Actions. 
 
C 4.5:  
Pedestrian Enhancements with New Development. Continue to require as a 
condition of development project approval the provision of sidewalks and 
wheelchair ramps where lacking and the repair or replacement of damaged 
sidewalks. Require that utility poles, signs, street lights, and street landscaping on 
sidewalks be placed and maintained to permit wheelchair access and pedestrian

Dear Mr. Bustos: 
Please consider this letter as part of the City of San Mateo formal application package for this 
project. The Block 20 project includes redevelopment of the northern portion of the block bounded 
by East 4th Avenue, South Delaware Street, East 5th Avenue and South Claremont St in San Mateo to 
build a mixed-use office and residential project. 
The project site is approximately .77 acres and is located in downtown San Mateo within a 1/2 mile 
of the Downtown Caltrain Station. The project site is zoned CBD Support District—Central Business 
District Support and is within the City’s Central Parking Improvement District CPID). The zoning 
allows for office, commercial and residential uses (at a maximum density of 50 DU/AC) to be 
developed. The allowed Floor Area Ratio (FAR) for this zone allows development at a 3.0 FAR. 
Stacked Density Bonus 
The project proposes office space and 72 residential units. The project includes 6 very low-income 
units and 6 moderate-income units in accordance with (AB 1287) which modified Govt. Code § 
65915(v) in October 2023. As the project proposed to reserve 15% of the base units for very low-
income households, plus 15% for moderate-income households, the project is entitled to a 100% 
density bonus (50% from the very low-income units and 50% from the moderate-income units). 
State Density Bonus-Concessions/Waivers 
In addition, Government Code Section 65915 provides that a City shall grant a density bonus, waivers 
and incentives/concessions when an applicant for a housing development seeks and agrees to 
construct a housing development that includes at least 5 percent affordable units. Specifically, 
state law provides for a density bonus of 100% and three (3) incentives or concessions to be granted 
for projects that include at least 15 percent of the units for very low-income households and 15 for 
moderate-income households, as listed above. The base density for the project site is 50 DU/AC, 
allowing for 39 units.  With the allowed stacked density bonus, the project allows for 78 residential 
units in total.

Parking

Concessions/Incentives

Waivers 
Government Code 65915(e)(1) provides in no case may a city apply any development standard that 
will have the effect of physically precluding the construction of a development at the densities or 
with the incentives permitted. An applicant may submit to the city a proposal for the waiver or 
reduction of development standards that will have the effect of physically precluding the 
construction of a development at the densities or with the incentives permitted. The proposal for a 
development standard waiver shall not reduce the number of incentives to which an applicant is 
entitled. 
In order to provide the proposed BMR units, the project is requesting the following waivers: 
Waiver 1: Increase in building height. 
MECAH Ventures is requesting a concession to increase the building height to 85 feet, which is 30 
feet above the 55-foot maximum identified in Municipal Code Chapter 27.40 and the City’s Building 
Height Plan. An increase in height allows for an additional 48 units (24 units on the 5th floor and 24 
units on the 6th floor) to be included in the Project, which equates to an additional 46,206 square 
feet. Without this increase in height, the Project would be precluded the development of the 
Project at the density and with the concessions requested. 
Waiver 2: Increase in maximum floor area ratio. 
The Project is requesting a concession to increase in the maximum floor area ratio (“FAR”) from 3.0 
as identified in Municipal Code Section 27.38.060 by 2.03 FAR, which equates to an additional 
68,793 square feet. As with the height, modification of the FAR is a concession by definition. Also 
like the increased height, an increase in FAR allows for an additional 48 units (24 units on the 5th 
floor and 24 units on the 6th floor) to be included in the Project. Without this increase in FAR, the 
Project would be precluded the development of the Project at the density and with the concessions 
requested.

Waiver 3: Increase in the maximum parking ramp slope. 
MECAH Ventures is requesting a waiver of Municipal Code Section 23.40.050 which provides a 
maximum driveway grade of 18 percent for private roads, driveways and ramps that have no 
pedestrian access. To make construction of the Project physically possible, MECAH Ventures is 
requesting an increase in the maximum slope on a ramp profile of 19 percent between the upper

MECAH Ventures is requesting a waiver of Municipal Code Section 27.38.130 regarding the required

a combination of both. Private usable open space used to fulfill this requirement must have a 
usable area of not less than 75 square feet and shall not be less than six feet in any dimension. 
MECAH Ventures is requesting a waiver to allow private usable open space equal to at least 54

residential units since there is limited space allocation as part of the building. Without this

precluding the development of the Project at the density and with concessions requested.

including the affordable units which would defeat the law’s goal of increasing affordable housing.

This proposed development is subject to San Mateo’s General Plan 2030, since it 
was submitted prior to the adoption of the new General Plan.  Therefore the 2030 
Land Use Map designates this site as Mixed Use Commercial-Residential, which “is 
meant to provide uses at intensities that complement both the downtown core and 
the adjacent residential neighborhoods.”   
 
The immediate adjacent neighbors are three one-story buildings - two historic 
structures and one preschool with an outdoor play yard - located on the same city 
block as the proposed seven story structure.  The existing residential neighborhood 
is composed predominantly of single-family homes. 
 
A seven-story building is too tall and too massive and hence not a good fit for this 
location, especially given the parcel is only 29,000 square feet in size. 
 
2. Other project and design aspects that should be considered. 
 
Per State Density Bonus Law, this proposed project is eligible for three concessions 
and additional waivers.  A total of eight waivers have been requested; four of 
which include: 
a. A 55% increase in the maximum height (from 55 feet to 85 feet) 
b. A 68% increase in the maximum FAR (from 3.0 to 5.03) 
c. A 33% increase in the maximum bulk length (from 150 feet to 200 feet) 
d. A 47% increase in the maximum diagonal length (from 170 feet to 250 feet)

--- page 134 ---
The result of this high number of waiver and concession requests results in a 
proposed project that overwhelms the adjacent predominantly residential 
neighborhood and the immediately adjacent historic structures and the preschool. 
 
As your meeting tonight is a Study Session, please request that the applicant go 
back to the drawing board and make substantive changes to their proposed project 
so that it is a better fit for the adjacent neighbors, the neighborhood and the City 
of San Mateo.
