SUBJECT:
San Mateo County Transportation Authority Project Updates
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on the San Mateo County Transportation Authority US 101 San Mateo County Crossings Improvement 
Implementation Plan (Active 101) and Mid-County Multimodal Strategy
BACKGROUND:
The San Mateo County Transportation Authority (TA) has developed the 101 Corridor Connect program, an overarching 
collaborative program to identify and prioritize congestion management projects along US 101. The program, in 
partnership with the City/County Association of Governments of San Mateo County (C/CAG), Caltrans, transit agencies, 
and local cities, intends to look beyond highway infrastructure to consider the mobility needs of the corridor as a whole. As 
part of the 101 Corridor Connect program, the TA is developing two initiatives: the Mid-County Multimodal Strategy and 
the US 101 San Mateo County Crossing Improvement Plan (Active 101). These plans support the 101 Corridor Connect 
program goals of safety, connectivity, sustainability, and inclusivity.  TA staff will provide the Commission with an overview 
of both of these projects, further described below.
Mid-County Multimodal Strategy 
The TA’s vision for US 101 is to be an interconnected corridor which serves the needs of all travelers in San Mateo County, 
regardless of how they choose to travel. To meet this goal, the TA created the 101 Corridor Connect Multimodal Strategy, 
focusing on a one-mile area on either side of the highway to find and prioritize necessary, but currently unfunded, 
transportation projects. These projects will be considered for inclusion in the strategy to prioritize future funding efforts. 
The first Multimodal Strategy focused on North County and is currently in the process of being finalized. The project team 
is now beginning the Mid-County area, which includes San Mateo. 
The TA project team has identified projects from existing approved transportation plans within this one-mile area of 101, 
including projects on the freeway and on local streets, transit projects, and other types of improvements. These projects 
will be assessed based on their ability to contribute to congestion reduction on 101 and other evaluation criteria including 
grant program guidelines and community feedback. These projects will then be prioritized based on the results of the 
evaluation process and implementation strategies will be developed to ensure a coordinated approach to delivering the 
projects. 
In early October, the team began engaging the Mid-County communities to understand current travel patterns and needs 
through an online survey, presentations, pop-up events, and short videos. This feedback will help TA staff understand the 
community’s priorities for transportation along the corridor, which will then be used to develop the draft list of 
recommended projects to include in the Mid-County strategy.

Active 101 
This project will identify and prioritize US 101 crossing and corridor improvement projects for future funding. These 
projects aim to enhance access and safety for people walking, biking, and using transit across US 101 where it may be 
currently lacking. 
The TA project team has reviewed approved transportation plans such as local active transportation plans, C/CAG‘s 
Comprehensive Bicycle & Pedestrian Plan, the Metropolitan Transportation Commission’s Regional Active Transportation 
Plans, and Caltrans District 4 Bicycle & Pedestrian Plans to identify all crossings and improvement project needs within a 
quarter mile of US 101. In total, 148 project needs were identified from the existing documents and ultimately, the top 25 
projects in each of the North-, Mid-, and South-county segments (75 total) along US 101 will be prioritized based on the 
101 Corridor Connect goals and external grant funding weightings. 
In October 2024, the TA team began the first phase of community and stakeholder engagement through an online 
mapping tool, pop-up events, and a recorded multilingual informational video to raise awareness and boost participation. 
This outreach will gather community feedback to refine the prioritized project list, leading to up to 10 projects advancing 
to conceptual design for future funding. 
For additional information and to access the voting tool, please visit the TA Active 101 webpage at: 
www.smcta.com/SMCactive101.

Subject: Questions for SMCTA

Dear SIC Members,

Thank you for improving sustainability and infrastructure in the City of SM. | am glad to see that you will
be meeting with SMCTA at your upcoming meeting. As you know, SMCTA is the primary funding source

for major transportation projects in the County and thus determines the transportation system that we

want for our future.

Here are some questions to consider asking SMCTA on the behalf of SM taxpayers:

- Hillsdale Bike/Ped Bridge Project - The City of San Mateo has been trying for 25+ years to make it safe
for residents to walk or bike across 101 at Hillsdale. A driver killed a 68-year-old resident on bike

here. Despite being on the books for almost twenty years, this projects seems to be stalled. What is the
current status of this project? Why is this project stalled? Was this project killed by the recently
completed widening of Highway 101 in San Mateo, a $600M project that will increase cars onthe road
and make local streets more dangerous for people on bikes and foot? If so, what steps is SMCTA taking
to prioritize the Hillsdale project to make this up to SM residents?

- 101/92 Direct Connect Project - SMCTA is considering widening Highway 92 in San Mateo and Foster
City at a cost of >$200 million. Will SMCTA be presenting this project to City of SM officials and staff,
especially given the potential for the use of eminent domain? Will the benefit of this project mainly be for
wealthier East Bay residents who can afford the Express Lanes? (There is currently no public
transportation across the San Mateo bridge.).

- Recent 101 widening in San Mateo - SMCTA recently widened Highway 101 in San Mateo, inviting
more people to drive on and to the highway instead of taking public transit. What has been the impact on
vehicle miles traveled? What has been the impact on air pollution in lower income, diverse
neighborhoods like North Central and Shoreview. Has there been an increase in traffic violence and
congestion on neighborhood streets adjacent to highway onramps, given the increase in highway traffic
(e.g., on Poplar in front of San Mateo High and College Park Elementary schools)? Has the project
improved congestion for those who can't afford the Express Lanes (SF Chronicle, SMDJ)? Has the
widening of 101 contributed to the poor Caltrain and BART ridership (relative to driving) and revenue
shortfall? How will the increase in local traffic impact road maintenance costs on City streets and will
this be the City's responsibility to fund?

- BikePed programs mentioned in the staff report - These projects seem focused on identifying high
priority bike/ped projects. Do these programs actually increase the amount of funding available for
bike/ped projects? Why does SMCTA go through these extensive community outreach efforts for small
bike/ped projects but not for massive highway expansion projects?
