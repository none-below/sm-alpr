SUBJECT:
US 101 / SR 92 Direct Connector — Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive a status update and informational presentation about the US-101/SR-92 Direct Connector project. 
BACKGROUND:
City Council will receive an update from San Mateo County Transportation Authority (SMCTA) staff on the progress of the 
environmental phase for the US 101/SR 92 Direct Connector Project (Project) and consider the proposed public 
participation approach. The project is co-sponsored by the SMCTA and City/County Association of Governments (C/CAG). If 
approved and funded following completion of the environmental phase, the Project would add a new connection between 
the US 101 Express Lanes and State Route 92 (“Direct Connector”) to reduce congestion and to relieve cut-through traffic 
on local streets in the cities of San Mateo and Foster City. Drivers using the Direct Connector would be able to remain in 
the center lanes on US 101 and use a new elevated median ramp to get to SR 92 without having to merge and weave 
across multiple freeway lanes or use local streets to make the connection from one freeway to the other. Doing so is also 
anticipated to reduce congestion in the vicinity of the interchange, further supporting the decrease in cut-through traffic. 
The new connector would be operated as a “managed lane” to encourage more carpooling and to support future transit 
service. 
History & Planning
Improvements to the US 101/SR 92 interchange were prioritized by San Mateo County voters as part of the 1988 Measure 
A Transportation Expenditure Plan (TEP) and as a “Key Congested Area” in the 2004 Measure A TEP. In 2018, voters 
throughout the Bay Area continued to prioritize interchange improvements with the passage of Regional Measure 3 (RM3) 
that dedicates funding from increased toll bridge revenue to improve areas such as the US 101/SR 92 interchange.
In 2016, the San Mateo County Transportation Authority (SMCTA) conducted a Preliminary Planning Study (PPS) with 
cooperation from C/CAG and the cities of San Mateo and Foster City to evaluate alternatives to address the congestion 
and safety concerns for the interchange. The PPS recommendations included both short-term and long-term alternatives. 
The short-term “Area Improvements” focus on modifications with relatively low implementation costs. This alternative 
advanced as the Area Improvements Project in partnership with the cities and received Caltrans approval in 2021. A public 
meeting was held during the development of these modifications and received over 40 questions/comments. Construction 
of the Area Improvements Project is scheduled to take place from May 2025 through May 2027.
The long-term improvements advanced separately as the Direct Connector Project that is currently in the environmental 
review phase. Advancing to final design and construction is subject to input from the cities, support from the SMCTA and

C/CAG boards, Caltrans approval, and funding availability. To fully fund the approximately $200 million in estimated 
construction costs, additional federal and state funding would likely be needed to leverage the regional and county

Environmental Analysis
Caltrans, as the owner and operator of the interchange, is the lead agency under the California Environmental Quality Act 
(CEQA) and the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA). The SMCTA is serving as the implementing agency and has 
procured a consultant team to perform the preliminary engineering and environmental technical studies.
Multiple alternatives are being studied, including:
•
No-Build Alternative: Status quo, no Direct Connector project constructed
•
Build Alternative 1: Direct Connector from Westbound SR 92 to the US 101 Express Lanes
•
Build Alternative 2: Reversible Direct Connector Between the US 101 Express Lanes and SR 92
•
Build Alternative 3: Direct Connector from the US 101 Express Lanes to Eastbound SR 92
The Direct Connector Project is anticipated to require an Environmental Impact Report (EIR) under CEQA and an 
Environmental Assessment (EA) under NEPA based on the complexity of alternatives and the size of the Project footprint. 
Preliminary engineering for the geometric layouts, structures, and drainage have been initiated. Technical studies for air 
quality, biological resources, noise, sea level rise, and water quality are also underway. 
Once the studies are complete, the findings and any potential impacts of each alternative will be evaluated as part of the 
Draft EIR (DEIR) that will be circulated for public review and comment anticipated by Fall 2026. The selection of a preferred 
alternative will be informed by a variety of factors including community feedback, city input, technical feasibility, and 
project benefits. A staff recommendation will be considered by the Board of Directors for the SMCTA and C/CAG (Boards). 
Then, the Project Development Team, comprised of Caltrans and project sponsor staff, will make a recommendation to 
Caltrans for ultimate approval. The selection of the preferred alternative and completion of the environmental phase is 
anticipated by Summer 2027. Funding to advance the preferred alternative to the design and construction phase would be 
subject to approval from the SMCTA and C/CAG Boards.
Public Participation Approach
Community feedback and city input are key factors in selecting the preferred alternative and will play an important role in 
the process. This process should be guided by a comprehensive public participation approach. The proposed approach is 
structured into three phases, each focused on key engagement topics that align with progress in design and environmental 
studies.
The initial public scoping period was completed in May 2024 as the first step in the environmental process. Over 300 
comments were received from the public and agencies regarding the Project scope, proposed alternatives, potential 
environmental impacts and what the draft environmental document should study. San Mateo community members have 
also raised concerns about potential property impacts that were preliminarily identified as part of prior phase planning 
work. The scoping and public comments received to date are being considered in the preparation of the public 
participation approach detailed below. 
Phase 1: Project/Process Understanding & Early Input
The first phase is focused on establishing a general understanding of the Project and process; as well as seeking early input 
to inform the Project design. Since the environmental studies and engineering are still in progress during this period, there 
is limited information available on the Project’s performance. Therefore, outreach topics will focus on educational 
messaging on the Project development and right-of-way processes, as well as the Project’s Purpose & Need. There will also 
be early opportunities for the public to inform Project elements as the preliminary engineering and environmental studies

are being developed. These focused topics may include potential future transit service, potential ramp intersection 
modifications, and potential design options. In addition to the public scoping meeting, Phase 1 activities will include “office 
hours” with community members neighboring the Project area and focused topic discussions. 
Phase 2: Project Benefits & Potential Impacts
Phase 2 will coincide with the completion of the technical analysis and the release of the DEIR. Outreach topics will focus 
on informing the public of potential Project benefits and impacts and promoting the public circulation of the DEIR. Status 
updates and a preview of the DEIR will be presented to city staff and the City Council in Fall 2026 in preparation for the 
public circulation period for the DEIR in late 2026. During the circulation period, outreach activities will include holding a 
public hearing and attending and hosting community meetings to facilitate public review and comment on the DEIR.
Phase 3: Preferred Alternative & Next Steps
In Phase 3, the preferred alternative will be selected, and the final EIR will be published. Project reference materials will be 
updated to document the final decision. Information will also be provided regarding anticipated milestones for the next 
phase of the Project and future engagement opportunities.
The Project is currently in Phase 1, and SMCTA staff are seeking feedback to inform ongoing public participation efforts 
from the SMCTA Board of Directors during their meeting on April 3, 2025, and the San Mateo City Council during their 
meeting on April 7, 2025.

It’s encouraging that federal guidelines increasingly are
prioritizing existing highway maintenance and repairs over new
construction (as they should be!). But states and municipalities
need to focus more on the concept of reduced demand. Also known
as disappearing traffic or traffic suppression, reduced demand
refers to a phenomenon where decreasing road capacity leads to a
decrease in traffic volume. As with induced demand, reduced
demand necessitates a change in behavior — people adjust by
skipping some trips, condensing multiple trips into a single one,
traveling at different times of the day or opting for alternative
options such as walking, biking or public transport.
