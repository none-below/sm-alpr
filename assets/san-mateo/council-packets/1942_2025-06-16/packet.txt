SUBJECT:
Prohibiting Lodging in Vehicles – Ordinance Enforcement
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction on enforcement of Section 10.04.140 of the Municipal Code, entitled “Prohibiting Vehicle Use for 
Habitation and Sleep”.
BACKGROUND:
Section 10.04.140 of the Municipal Code, entitled “Prohibiting Vehicle Use for Habitation and Sleep” was adopted in 1995, 
and updated in 2016 to provide additional clarity to avoid potential legal challenges. As written, Section 10.04.140 states: 
It is unlawful: 
(a) For any person to use a vehicle as defined in the California Vehicle Code, for lodging in the City of San Mateo. 
For the purpose of this section, "lodging" means the combination of one or more activities in subdivision (1) with 
one or more activities in subdivision (2) where it reasonably appears, in light of all the circumstances, that a person 
is using a vehicle for habitation. 
(1) Storing of contents inside a vehicle that are not associated with ordinary vehicle use, such as a sleeping 
bag, bedroll, blanket, sheet, pillow, kitchen utensils, cookware, cooking equipment, bodily fluids, or the 
storing of personal possessions or belongings that obscure some or all of the vehicle's windows; and 
(2) Engaging or engaged in one or more of the following inside a vehicle: preparing or cooking meals or 
sleeping. 
(b) For any person to connect any camp car, motor home, travel trailer or any other vehicle which is equipped for 
sleeping or cooking or both, to any public utility, such as water, electricity, gas or sewer services, except as may be 
necessary for the maintenance and preparation of the vehicle. The duration of any such maintenance or 
preparation connection shall not exceed eight hours in any seven consecutive days. 
Over the years City Council direction about how diligently to enforce section 10.04.140 of the Municipal Code Citywide has 
varied. Currently, the Police Department enforces 10.04.140 by providing resource options to those that use their vehicle 
for habitation and providing information to facilitate compliance with the Code. This approach sometimes results in 
unhoused individuals taking advantage of available resources and support systems, and other times results in those 
residing in their vehicles moving from place to place in avoidance of enforcement and/or limiting the items within their 
vehicles to the bare minimum needed for habitation.

Also, in 2024 the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in City of Grants Pass v. Johnson that the enforcement of generally applicable 
laws regulating camping on public property does not constitute cruel and unusual punishment prohibited by the Eighth 
Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. This ruling effectively overturned the 2018 decision in Martin v. City of Boise issued 
by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, which had held that the Eighth Amendment barred the enforcement of 
public camping Ordinances against unhoused individuals where the number of such persons exceeded the number of 
practically available shelter beds. With the Grants Pass decision, cities have had more flexibility to enforce local restrictions 
on public camping.
 
Additionally, on July 25, 2024, the State of California’s Governor issued an executive order to State agencies regarding 
encampment removal that, while not directive or binding to local agencies, provides further guidance and models for 
assessing where and when encampment intervention should be prioritized and implemented. The executive order’s 
guidance is extremely high level but generally encourages a high level of intervention across the State of California in high 
priority areas.
As large cities such as San Francisco and San Jose begin stronger enforcement of their prohibition of vehicles used for 
habitation, and oversized vehicle policies, it is expected that vehicles from cities that stringently enforce vehicles used for 
habitation and oversized vehicles may come to San Mateo. 
Over the past several months the City has received many reports about people sleeping in vehicles in residential 
neighborhoods. This situation creates potential public health, safety, and convenience considerations, including 
obstructing access to the right of way, reducing visibility while driving, illegal dumping, causing wear on roadways, tapping 
into electricity that is not theirs, creating environmental hazards by discharging waste when parked for extended periods, 
and creating nighttime sound in violation of the City’s noise Ordinance. During the week of May 12, forty-seven different 
vehicles were reported/identified parking on City streets and potentially in use for human habitation.  
One of the City Council’s Fiscal Year 2025/26 Blue Sky priorities is to explore limiting overnight recreation vehicle (RV) 
parking in commercial corridors, and during the May 5, 2025, City Council meeting, members of the San Mateo City Council 
generally expressed a desire to explore increased enforcement and regulation of this condition and to prioritize having a 
study session on this topic. 
Outreach 
The Police Department has established a Homeless Outreach Team (HOT) - a multi-jurisdictional team comprised of law 
enforcement, mental health experts, housing facilitators, and substance abuse counselors, whose primary focus is to 
intervene and either prevent at risk situations or improve the circumstances of people who find themselves homeless in 
our City.
In July and August of 2024, PD’s HOT hosted a well-attended outreach meeting with the Recreation Vehicle (RV) 
community, which tends to be the primary focus of reports received by the City. Since then, PD has not experienced as 
many problems. There are still RVs that regularly park on Garvey Way and are occupied, but the residents appear to be 
complying with all legal requirements apart from section 10.04.140, where they move for street sweeping, and have not 
tapped into the street poles for illegal electricity. We have also not received any recent reports about wastewater 
dumping. 
HOT continues to conduct outreach and provide support to the RV community located on Garvey Way and Delaware 
Street. The individuals residing in this area generally comply with Police Department and Public Works requests, 
particularly when maintenance is scheduled. In addition to the outreach meeting, flyers were distributed recently to 
inform the RV residents of the upcoming maintenance work, and there have been no reported issues. HOT maintains 
regular contact with the RV community, offering supportive services, shelter options, and housing resources. The most 
recent count during the week of May 26 identified 14 RVs in the Garvey area, with the majority of them having been 
stationed there for an extended period. 
Earlier this month (June 2025), the HOT passed out flyers in English and Spanish to the RV community, notifying them of

Potential Enforcement Approaches 
If the Council wishes to resume enforcement of section 10.04.140 while maintaining a compassionate approach, one 
option would be to direct the Police Department to attempt contact twice prior to the issuance of a 24-hour warning, after 
which enforcement in the form of a citation or removal of the vehicle (or other appropriate enforcement action) could 
occur. Ideally, removal of a vehicle would not be necessary, and would be implemented only as a last resort, since their 
removal could leave individuals, and possibly families, unhoused. In this scenario, the City’s priority would be to provide a 
compassionate, service-oriented approach that emphasizes helping individuals receive services with a goal of permanent 
housing. In this implementation approach, a vehicle owner would be notified of needing to have their vehicle moved at 
three separate touch points (2 contact attempts, followed by a 24-hour removal warning) over a span of approximately 
seven days to move their vehicle before enforcement action is taken. First and foremost, the Police Department would 
ensure that officers are effective and compassionate, with the goal of directing individuals to needed housing and social 
service resources. 
The City Attorney’s Office could also prosecute violations as either an infraction or misdemeanor, each of which includes a 
corresponding fine. For infractions, fines would not exceed $100 for a first conviction, $200 for a second conviction, and 
$500 for subsequent convictions. Penalties for misdemeanors include a fine that cannot exceed $1,000. Again however, 
the intent is to take a compassionate approach and direct individuals to appropriate resources. If the Council chooses to 
take this approach, the HOT will serve as a critical implementation group. 
A second option would be to strictly enforce section 10.04.140 without outreach or engagement, which could simply result 
in more unhoused individuals and families in need of resources/support throughout the City. This sort of condition is 
known to make it more difficult for such individuals/families obtain services, permanent housing, and transition into a 
more stable living situation.    
The third option would be to maintain status quo, by providing resource options to those that use their vehicle for 
habitation and providing information to facilitate compliance with the Code.
Staff recommends focusing on enforcement of Municipal Code section 10.04.140 because it allows the Police Department 
to prohibit vehicle use for habitation or sleep Citywide. Staff does not recommend updating the oversized vehicle section 
of the Municipal Code (11.32.105) at this time because it only applies to residential zones. 
Options for City Council:
1.
Resume enforcement of Municipal Code Section 10.04.140 but direct the Police Department to conduct outreach at 
least twice prior to the issuance of a 24-hour warning, after which issuing citations, removal of vehicles, or other 
appropriate enforcement action could occur. 
2.
Resume enforcement of Municipal Code section 10.04.140 without directing the Police Department to conduct 
outreach as described in Option 1.  
3.
Maintain the status quo regarding enforcement of Municipal Code Section 10.04.140.
Next Steps: 
Should Council wish to direct staff to move forward with implementation of either Options 1 or 2, the Police Department 
would implement a two-week grace period prior to implementing enforcement to allow for public notification and 
information of the City’s planned enforcement approach. This would include canvassing the city and providing 
informational flyers to people who are currently dwelling in their vehicles. Informational materials will be provided in 
multiple languages.
BUDGET IMPACT:
Enforcement of this section of the Municipal Code is anticipated to be absorbed within existing resources.

The vehicle is an improved alternative to sleeping in a tent when camping (which is not

The lack of utilities may be compared to living in a dry cabin such as in Alaska (no
plumbing, heating or electrical).
The vehicle is safer than a tent and is also out of the elements.
If the purpose of this meeting is only about the prohibition of sleeping in a vehicle, then the

Questions or other considerations:
Where else may someone live, if this code is enforced? The housing problem has not
been adequately addressed in our community. The proverbial “poking someone with a
stick, to move them along”, is not a solution.
Can a pay-per-use lot be created as a designated area with a trash service? This could be
like an RV park with designated spots. Also, members of this community can provide
the labor to manage it as part of their contribution to reside there.
Some cities have provided access to showers in mobile setups for the homeless, which
helps with those types of needs, including public bathrooms.
Are there other options that can be uncovered for those people who are falling short of a
full habitation situation, who must otherwise reside in their vehicles?
How is the enforcement for this code going to differentiate between someone
SLEEPING in their vehicle from an EMPTY AND UNINHABITED, (yet legally

it is vacant as the windows do not allow visual access?
How is this enforcement going to eliminate crimes, such as burglary, noise, and
littering?  It is the latter that should have the priority of being addressed, not the former.
From the perspective of those in favor of this endorsement, is it meant to remove this
blight, which is a reminder that not everyone has the access to the same benefits in our
society?
The enforcement of this Municipal Code is missing the spirit of the purpose of its intended
benefit.
