SUBJECT:
Revenue Enhancement Options — Informational
RECOMMENDATION:
Consider General Fund revenue enhancement options, many of which will require a ballot measure, and provide staff 
with direction on which option(s) to move forward for additional review.
BACKGROUND:
At the June 16 meeting to adopt the FY 25-26 Citywide budget, the City Council adopted a General Fund budget that 
anticipated a budget deficit of $11.9 million. This was a decrease from the originally proposed budget deficit of $15.2 
million, and less than the currently estimated deficit of $13.9 million. City staff has revised its estimated budget 
deficit since the adoption of the budget because the State’s adopted budget only included about 70 percent of the 
total Vehicle License Fee (VLF) backfill owed to the City in FY 25-26. Due to this estimated budget deficit and the 
anticipated budget deficits projected over the next 10 years as part of the General Fund 10-year forecast, the City has 
implemented a Fiscal Sustainability Plan. As part of this plan, staff continues to review opportunities to reduce 
general fund expenditures, improve cost recovery and cost allocation, pursue grant opportunities, and strategize on 
Citywide economic development. Another key component of this plan is to review revenue enhancement options to 
both bring in new funding to offset the expected structural operating deficit of $5 -$7 million per year and find 
funding for significant unfunded capital improvement projects.
Transactions and Use Tax
The City can add a transactions and use tax, also known as a district or local add-on sales tax, on top of the regular sales 
tax. A transactions and use tax applies to merchandise that is delivered within a jurisdiction and does not apply to 
grocery items or prescriptions medicines. San Mateo voters approved Measure S, a transactions and use tax, in 
November 2015, which extended Measure L, a 1/4 cent transactions and use tax. Since the passage of Measure RR in 
November 2020 to support Caltrain operations, the sales tax rate in the City of San Mateo has been 9.625 percent. The 
State of California imposes a limit of two (2) percent above the statewide base sales tax rate, thus the City has 1/4 cent 
of capacity left before reaching the limit. If another entity places a sales tax measure on the ballot before the City uses 
its cap, the City may no longer have the option to add a 1/4 cent sales tax for local funding. While it may be possible for 
a local 1/4 cent sales tax to proceed along with the regional transportation tax measure under the current wording of 
proposed Senate Bill 63, that will depend on the final bill language if and when it passes the legislature and is signed 
into law.  In any event, City staff will continue to review the legal ramifications of the City proposing a sales tax measure 
at the same time as another entity in the County.
Historically speaking, sales tax measures have a high likelihood of passing. Of the 512 transactions and use tax ballot tax 
measures from California cities and counties between 2002-2024, that were structured as general taxes and were new 
or increased the sales tax rate, 402 passed and 110 failed, a pass rate of over 79 percent. If San Mateo voters were to

Parcel Tax
Pursuant to Article XI §5 of the California Constitution, a special non-ad valorem (non-value based) tax can be levied on 
parcels of property generally based on either a flat-rate per parcel or a variable rate depending on the size, use and/or 
number of units on a parcel. A parcel tax cannot be based on the value of real property. Parcel taxes are typically used 
for special purposes or new facilities and can be levied on all those who receive that service or have access to that 
facility. Parcel taxes are considered special taxes and require a 2/3 voter approval. Given the 2/3 voter threshold, 
parcel taxes are not as common as other locally imposed taxes. 
 
Since 2008, 20 non-school parcel taxes appeared on the ballot in Santa Clara and San Mateo counties. 80 percent of 
the measures passed, 16 out of 20, but this does not necessarily indicate success for San Mateo based on the small 
data set. In San Mateo County, most of the City’s that passed a parcel tax were smaller cities with a very specific 
purpose such as libraries or Police and Fire. Given the need to fund ongoing operating revenues and unfunded capital 
needs in San Mateo, City staff asked Urban Futures Inc. (UFI), our Municipal Advisor, to evaluate raising either $7 or 
$14 million in parcel taxes. They developed three different types of parcel taxes to reach each funding target and 
determined that a single-family residence would pay between $159 and $484 per parcel based on the scenario. The 
analysis for these scenarios is summarized in Attachment A. In general, this level of parcel tax is more than other local 
jurisdictions the size of San Mateo that have passed.
 
Utility Users Tax
Also pursuant to Article XI §5 of the California Constitution, a Utility Users Tax (UUT) may be imposed by a city on the 
consumption of utility services, including (but not limited to) electricity, gas, water, sewer, telephone (including cell 
phone and long distance), sanitation, and cable television. The tax would be levied by the City, collected by each utility 
as a part of its regular billing processes, and remitted to the City. As an approximation of the potential revenue impact 
of a UUT, Finance Department staff analyzed utility consumption metrics from the utility providers and applied a UUT 
rate of 5 percent for natural gas and electricity, and 4 percent for telecommunication and cable television services, 
which is the rate in place in the city of Redwood City. Based on this previous analysis on utility consumption by the San 
Mateo community between April 2020 through March 2021, passage of a UUT ballot measure would generate 
approximately $5.9 million in UUT revenues per year. If the City exempted public agencies and PG&E customers 
enrolled in the California Alternate Rates for Energy (CARE), the additional UUT would be approximately $5.4 million.
 
UUT is often disfavored because it burdens essential services needed by every household. In addition, UUT does not fare 
well at the ballot box in California. Of the 87 UUT ballot measures that were structured as a general tax for a new UUT 
from 2002 – 2024, 28 passed, and 59 failed, a 32 percent pass rate.
 
Benefit Assessment
A benefit assessment is another revenue generating option that levies a special assessment or charge on real property to 
fund either a specific public improvement or an expanded/new public service benefiting those properties assessed. This 
type of revenue enhancement is not designed to fund existing operating costs. Benefit assessments are often associated 
with an assessment district in which a fixed area receives the benefit of the assessment such as a lighting or landscaping 
assessment district or a property and business improvement district. A special assessment is approved through the 
Proposition 218 process and is subject to a ballot vote of those impacted by the assessment. An Engineer’s Report is 
typically needed to determine the amount of the assessment and the subject properties.
Since a special assessment is typically for new or expanded services or a new public improvement, it may not be the most 
suitable tool for addressing the City’s current operating deficit. At this time, staff has not developed a recommended type 
of assessment or estimated value to be raised through the assessment. However, it remains a viable revenue 
enhancement option to keep in mind within the City’s broader list of strategies and could be appropriate for later 
financing needs, especially since the Proposition 218 process provides more flexibility and does not need to be approved

General Obligation Bond
Another specific type of revenue enhancement that the City could pursue is a General Obligation (GO) bond. A GO bond 
requires 2/3 voter approval and once approved authorizes the City to sell bonds based on a levy or tax on real and 
personal property within the City limits. A GO bond is backed by the City’s General Fund and the credit of the City. For a 
GO bond, the voters approve a maximum bond issuance amount and the use of the bond proceeds, but not a specific tax 
rate or annual payment. Revenues from a GO bond can only be used for approved infrastructure improvements and not 
for operating expenses. Thus, this revenue source is appropriate for addressing the City’s unfunded capital improvement 
needs.
 
The City’s municipal advisor, UFI, analyzed three scenarios for potential GO bond issuances. In each scenario they 
developed what the tax rate per $100,000 of assessed value would be for a parcel, based on an estimated total amount of 
bond proceeds being approved. Table 1 outlines the estimated tax rates and bond proceeds.

As shown, property owners would pay approximately $30 per $100,000 of assessed valuation on their property per year if

Revenue Measure Ad Hoc Committee
City staff reviewed these options with the Revenue Measure Ad Hoc Committee at a meeting on August 6. The committee 
posed questions on some of the revenue enhancement options and ultimately agreed with staff’s recommendation. First, 
to study taking a 1/4 cent transactions and use tax to San Mateo voters in 2026 and second to study interest within the 
community for a General Obligation Bond.
 
Next Steps
Based on City Council feedback on the revenue enhancement options, City staff will work with a polling consultant to study 
interest in the proposed revenue measures. Once the polling is completed, City staff will return to the City Council with a 
summary of the results, most likely in November.
BUDGET IMPACT:
To best address the anticipated ongoing deficit of $5 - $7 million, to maintain essential services and to provide for 
capital improvement funding above the current $25 million set aside in the Capital Investment Reserve, this revenue 
enhancement study session considered the following tax revenues, and the potential additional revenue that could be 
generated:
Transactions and Use Tax: $6.8 million (operating)
Parcel Tax: $7-14 million (operating and capital)
Utility Users Tax: $5.4 million (operating)
General Obligation Bond: $162 - $325 million in bond proceeds (capital)

Utility Users Tax (UUT)
• Definition: A percentage tax on the consumption of utility services such 
as electricity, gas, water, sewer, refuse, telecommunications, and cable 
television.  
• Methodology: Cities can establish tax rates for residential and 
commercial users. Tax is collected by utility and transmitted to city. 
• Voter Approval: Requires majority of voters. Most of the cities with UUTs 
adopted the taxes prior to 1986 by vote of the city council.

General Obligation (GO) Bonds
•
Definition: Municipal bonds backed by property tax revenue levied until 
the bonds mature. 
•
Methodology: The maximum bond issuance amount and use of proceeds 
are approved by voters, not a tax rate or annual payment. 
•
Voter Approval: State law requires 2/3 approval by electorate for non-
school GO Bonds 
•
Restrictions: Revenues cannot be used for operating expenses; only for 
approved infrastructure projects.

Key Steps in Citizen Initiative Process (see Elections & Government Code):
Initiative Proposed by Citizen
• The initiative is proposed by citizen, not by the City.
Notice & Publication
• Ballot title and summary are requested from and prepared by the City. Proponents 
must publish in local newspaper.
Petition Signatures
• Proponents have 180 days to obtain the signatures of 10% of all registered city 
voters. For City of San Mateo, approximately 6,000 signatures are required.
City Clerk Review and Council Action
• If the City Clerk certifies the sufficiency and timeliness of the signatures, City 
Council is required to submit the measure to the voters and call for the election.
