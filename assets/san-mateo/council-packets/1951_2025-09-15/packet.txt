City of San Mateo Speak Up Report 
Reporting Period: June 2nd, 2025-August 6th, 2025 
Community Overcoming Relationship Abuse (CORA) is grateful to the City of San Mateo for its 
generous partnership in launching and piloting CORA’s Speak Up Program. This report covers a 
retrospective of what work was accomplished during the pilot period. 
Program Goals 
Reeling from the murder of a San Carlos mother in broad daylight during September 2022, the 
City of San Carlos teamed up with CORA to create the Speak Up program. CORA developed and 
piloted a tailored program for those who live, work and operate businesses in San Carlos 
through the Speak Up Program. This program was designed to provide local businesses with 
education to be effective and timely in helping their employees and customers who are 
impacted by domestic violence by using a three-module training designed to:  
(1) help them understand domestic violence;
(2) teach them how they can recognize when customers and employees are experiencing
domestic violence (the red flags); and
(3) provide useful tips on what to say (or not say) so they can Speak Up when they see
someone is in need due to domestic violence.
After trainings, the team conducted a one question survey to gauge the extent to which the 
presentation was helpful: after the trainings, 92% said they now know how to recognize and 
respond to domestic violence. 
In addition to presentations, the program staff handed out Speak Up bags that included 
bathroom stickers and business cards with CORA QR codes and “Teach Sheets” that provide an 
ongoing resource for businesses to refer to. The Teach Sheets feature information shared in the 
presentations, including the useful things to say/not say and CORA’s contact information so 
businesses know to refer people in need to CORA.

The Business Sectors pie chart highlights the distribution of organizations from the 2024 Speak

A Warm Reception 
“The event that occurred September 2022 was horrendous. It was an event so horrific you 
would have expected the community would distrust strangers and be resistant to the topic of 
domestic violence and intimate partner abuse” -- not so in San Mateo. The community was very 
welcoming. The hospitality extended to CORA 
-- including being brought into businesses’ 
back rooms, staff rooms and sharing cups of 
coffee, bobas or smoothies as we reviewed 
the Speak Up Program together. It was 
heartwarming and inspiring. San Mateo 
businesses and organizations care very much 
about their city and how to keep their 
community safe.  
 
Community Support 
Employees at several San Mateo businesses 
had not heard of CORA previously, and were unsure about the services we offered or how to 
speak about the topic of domestic violence. The businesses were happy the City partnered with 
CORA to help prevent future domestic violence in the community and keep their neighbors and 
community safe.  
 
Timely Help 
On more than one occasion, local business owners immediately responded that the training 
was needed at their company. Comments we have heard again and again sound like “Oh! We 
need this information!” and “We have heard from a customer recently about this!” and “Can 
you train us now?” 
 
Business owners were grateful to receive the training, and were even more excited to receive 
the CORA resources to post in their place of business to raise awareness. 
Future 
The Speak Up Program, developed by the City of San Carlos in partnership with CORA, has 
catalyzed a county-wide movement to address and prevent domestic violence. An additional 
ten cities – including San Mateo-- adopted the Speak Up campaign for 
2024, thereby amplifying the reach and influence of what we learned 
during the San Carlos Pilot program. This collaborative effort indicates a 
growing acknowledgment of how important it is to have proactive 
education to cultivate “community immunity” to domestic violence. 
 
 
And there’s more. As Speak Up comes to an end and the 10 
participating cities finish up, many cities are interested in 
renewing their contracts and bringing Speak Up annually to their 
cities to reach new businesses. We look forward to working with 
new and returning cities to increase awareness of CORA services 
and resources in the new year.

SUBJECT:
Participation in National Opioid Settlement Agreements – Approval
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve the City’s participation in no-cost opioid settlement agreements with Purdue Pharma, Alvogen, Amneal, Apotex, 
Hikma, Indivior, Sun and Zydus and authorize the City Attorney to execute documents confirming the City’s participation 
and electing to have the settlement funds be provided directly to the City of San Mateo.
BACKGROUND:
Over the last several years, a number of nationwide litigation matters have been filed against manufacturers, distributors, 
and retailers of opioids. The allegations in these lawsuits have generally been that the defendants were complicit in the 
increasing problem of opioid addiction in the United States, and that they knew or should have known that the products 
they made or sold were being misused. 
 
On December 6, 2021, the City Council first authorized the City Attorney to take all necessary steps and execute 
appropriate documents to participate in opioid settlement agreements with three opioid distributors – Cardinal Health, 
McKesson, and AmerisourceBergen (hereinafter, the “Distributors”) and the opioid manufacturer Janssen Pharmaceuticals, 
Inc., which is owned by Johnson and Johnson (collectively, “J&J”), and agree to implementing agreements. The City later 
joined settlement agreements involving Teva, Allergan, Walmart, CVS, Walgreens, and Kroger. 
 
Earlier this year, Attorney General Bonta announced a proposed settlement with Purdue Pharma and its owners, the 
Sackler family, for up to $7.4 billion. California stands to receive up to $440 million in abatement funding from this 
settlement. The State of California has also come to a global proposed settlement amount of $720 million with the 
following opioid manufacturers, distributors and retailers: Alvogen, Amneal, Apotex, Hikma, Indivior, Sun and Zydus. Like 
the other opioid settlements, local government support and sign-on are required to maximize the amount coming to our 
communities and eligible cities may elect to receive direct payment or have their allocated share go to the county in which 
they are located. 
 
The amount the City will receive from these settlements is multi-factored and dependent on how many local agencies

SUBJECT:
Decommissioned Plant Processes Cleaning Project — Bid Rejection
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to reject the sole bid received for the Decommissioned Plant Processes Cleaning Project and authorize 
the Public Works Director to negotiate a contract directly with Ancon Marine (dba Ancon Services).
BACKGROUND:
The Decommissioned Plant Processes Cleaning Project (Project) is part of the Water Quality Control Plant’s (WQCP) plan to 
safely decommission obsolete tanks and equipment following completion of the Wastewater Treatment Plant Upgrade 
and Expansion Project. The upgrade and expansion project consists of new liquids treatment process facilities and other 
plant upgrades to replace aging infrastructure and produce high-quality treated water that will protect human health and 
the environment; however, this project did not include decommissioning of the processes and equipment to be replaced. 
In accordance with Municipal Code Section 3.60.030, a competitive public process was used to obtain bids for the Project. 
A Notice Inviting Sealed Bids was issued to advertise the Project on July 3, 2025. On the July 17, 2025, bid opening date, 
one (1) bid was received from Ancon Services with a bid amount of $477,188, which exceeded the WQCP’s estimate by 
$227,188. Pursuant to San Mateo Municipal Code Section 3.60.040(a)(4), the City Council, at its discretion, may reject all 
bids received and authorize the direct negotiation of a contract for the Project if the Project is not within budget, do the 
work through City employees, or terminate the Project or purchase. Given that bids are greater than the established 
budget for the Project, staff recommends for City Council reject all bids and authorize the Director of Public Works to 
directly negotiate a potential contract with Ancon Services under which cost reductions may be achieved. If these direct 
negotiations are successful, staff will return to Council for approval of a contract at a future date. If negotiations are not 
successful, staff will either rebid the Project at a later date or modify the scope.
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for the rejection of bids. Funding for this work is budgeted for in the Water Quality Control 
Plant’s operating budget. The lowest bid of $477,188 exceeds the Plant Manager’s estimate by $327,188, which would 
exceed the project’s available budget. By rejecting all bids, if authorized to do so, staff can potentially negotiate a more 
favorable contract with the lowest bidder.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This bid rejection is categorically exempt from CEQA as an “existing facility,” because it consists of the operation, repair, 
maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, 
mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. (CEQA 
Guidelines Section 15301.)

Executive Summary
San Mateo’s first citywide Strategic Plan is a tool to aid in the implementation and prioritization of the City’s ten major Strategic 
Initiatives, which represent the City’s Values and overall Mission. This Strategic Plan incorporates extensive community feedback 
collected since 2018, and will serve as a foundational document to help achieve a shared vision of the City’s future. 
The Strategic Plan will: 
•
Offer a comprehensive framework for City Council to use when making decisions for both immediate and long-term priorities.
•
Allow the City to move in an intentional, strategic direction over a longer timeframe. 
•
Act as a tool to implement planning documents already adopted by Council in a systematic way. 
•
Integrate with the City’s budget and Capital Improvement Plan processes.
•
Help the City use its resources with the utmost efficiency and impact with budget constraints in mind.
•
Be updated as needed to remain relevant to the community. 
The following inclusive engagement of diverse perspectives helped shaped and inform the Strategic Plan:
Community Opinion Survey: In February 2025, the City of San Mateo conducted a statistically significant Community Opinion Survey to 
understand its residents’ satisfaction, priorities, opinions, and concerns as they relate to city services, facilities, and policies. The results 
provided Council and staff with an analysis of San Mateo’s strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats. 
Community Conversations: The City held a series of meetings with diverse groups to gather community input that helped inform 
priorities and challenges for the City of San Mateo’s underserved populations. Based on Census 2022 data, the Latino, Black, and Asian 
American and Pacific Islander communities represent 55% of the population and have historical underrepresentation and low 
participation rates in local government initiatives. These Community Conversations enabled the City to hear from these 
underrepresented groups and their input was incorporated into the Strategic Plan. 
General Plan 2040: From 2018 to 2024, the City developed its General Plan 2040 document. It lays out the community’s vision for how 
San Mateo will look, feel, and change over the next 15 years. Throughout the development of this document, the City conducted 
extensive community engagement to understand the community’s thoughts on their values and vision for the future throughout the City 
and on a variety of topics, which were then critically analyzed and presented to a special General Plan Subcommittee, the Planning 
Commission, and the City Council for adoption.

SUBJECT:
19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal Improvement Project — Informational Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an informational update on the initial design plans for the 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal 
Improvement Project and provide feedback about the proposed design and outreach efforts. 
BACKGROUND:
The 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal Improvement Project (Project), is a comprehensive effort to 
enhance safety, accessibility, and operational efficiency along this key east/west corridor for all roadway users, including 
bicyclists, pedestrians, and motorists. The project extends approximately 1.3 miles, from Pacific Boulevard to Mariners 
Island Boulevard.
This Project is the result of combining two previously separate initiatives: the Fashion Island Class IV Bikeway Project and 
the Norfolk Street and Fashion Island Boulevard Intersection Reconstruction Project. The bikeway component was 
originally identified as a priority improvement through the 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Smart Corridor and 
Mobility Hub Study, which recommended enhanced bicycle infrastructure to improve multimodal connectivity. Separately, 
the intersection reconstruction project was funded with the goal of addressing vehicular congestion and improving traffic 
flow at a key junction.
To maximize efficiency and deliver a more integrated solution, the two efforts have been consolidated into a single, 
corridor-wide project. The updated Project scope and name reflect this unified approach, which seeks to fulfill the original 
multimodal, safety, and congestion relief objectives in a cohesive and coordinated manner.
This multi-agency initiative, sponsored by the City with the San Mateo County Transportation Authority (TA) as the lead 
implementation agency, has an estimated cost of $27 million, funded through various local and federal sources.
The Project is in the initial design stage and includes the following key improvements:
•
Vehicle Congestion Relief: Significant congestion has been identified in the eastbound direction along the 19th 
Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard corridor, particularly during the afternoon peak period. In response, the City 
conducted a comprehensive corridor analysis in 2021 to evaluate potential improvements aimed at relieving 
congestion and improving traffic operations.
As part of this analysis, the Norfolk Street intersection was identified as a primary bottleneck in the eastbound 
direction. The study concluded that in order to address this constraint, the intersection would need to be 
reconfigured to provide an additional eastbound through lane to accommodate the observed vehicle demand.
54 of 189

--- page 55 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
 
While this can be partially addressed by modifying the existing median just west of the intersection to create space 
for another lane, the analysis also determined that a bottleneck would remain at the Seal Slough bridge, which 
currently provides only one eastbound lane and two westbound lanes.
To support this finding, a recently completed traffic count confirmed that eastbound traffic volumes during the 
afternoon peak are approximately 50 percent higher than westbound volumes in the morning, despite the 
eastbound direction having only a single travel lane. The 2021 corridor study evaluated and recommended a lane 
reconfiguration over the Seal Slough bridge, from two westbound and one eastbound lane to one westbound and 
two eastbound lanes, to better align with actual traffic patterns.
Initial traffic analysis for the Project shows a substantial reduction in eastbound queue lengths, primarily due to 
the removal of much of the median just west of the Norfolk Street intersection. This benefit is further supported 
by the proposed reconfiguration of lanes over the Seal Slough bridge to two eastbound and one westbound lane. 
These congestion improvements will come with some tradeoffs for other directions of traffic at the 
Norfolk Street / Fashion Island Blvd intersection. These findings will be discussed further during the meeting. 
As part of the Project, the City would implement intersection modifications, including the lane configuration 
changes as well as modified signal timing at Norfolk Street, and the bridge lane reconfiguration to two (2) 
eastbound lanes and one (1) westbound lane. These improvements are intended to reduce corridor congestion, 
address long- standing traffic concerns, and improve traffic flow during peak periods. 
•
Protected Bikeway Facility: The Project includes a Class IV protected bike lane along the entire length of the 
corridor. Class IV facilities are physically separated from vehicle traffic using concrete medians, curbs, posts, or 
other barriers. These facilities improve safety and comfort by minimizing conflicts between people biking and 
motor vehicles. The corridor is identified as a medium-high priority project in the City’s Bicycle Master Plan.
A two-way protected bikeway is proposed on the south side of 19th Avenue from Pacific Boulevard to the YMCA 
parking lot, helping riders avoid SR-92 off- and on-ramp traffic on the west end of the corridor. From the YMCA to 
Mariners Island Boulevard, the design shifts to one-way protected bike lanes on each side of the street, which are 
more familiar to most road users and provide better connections to surrounding bike networks.
•
Pedestrian Enhancements: The Project includes ADA-compliant curb ramps, high-visibility and raised crosswalks, 
pedestrian-scale lighting, and protected intersections designed to improve safety and visibility while reducing 
conflicts between people walking, biking, and driving. 
Also proposed with the Project is a new pedestrian walkway on the south side of the Seal Slough bridge, which will 
close a critical gap in the pedestrian network along the corridor. Currently, sidewalk exists only on the north side 
of the bridge, requiring pedestrians to cross the street multiple times to reach destinations on the south side. 
Completing the south side walkway will improve pedestrian safety by providing a continuous and direct path, 
reducing the number of street crossings required in areas with complex intersection geometry and freeway ramp 
connections.
•
Signal Modifications: Five intersections along the corridor will be upgraded with modern signal hardware and new 
timing plans to support all modes. Several of these signals fall under Caltrans jurisdiction, including the 19th 
Avenue / Delaware Street / SR-92 Eastbound On- and Off-ramps and Fashion Island Blvd / US-101 Southbound On- 
and Off- Ramps. Signal upgrades will improve operations and safety while accommodating new bike and 
pedestrian infrastructure.
•
Parking Constraints and Corridor Widening: Between Pacific Boulevard and Grant Street, the existing roadway 
width cannot accommodate all proposed uses, including protected bikeways, travel lanes, and on-street parking. 
To address this, the project proposes widening the roadway on the north side using previously unused right-of-

Currently, there are approximately 64 on-street parking spaces in this segment. The widening will allow 
preservation of about 43 spaces. As the design process continues, the project team will be reviewing the potential

On the east side of the Seal Slough Bridge, currently underutilized parking will be reduced from 25 to 11 spaces to

FUNDING AND SCHEDULE
The total estimated cost of the 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal Improvement Project, including 
planning, design, environmental clearance, and construction, is around $27 million. Of this amount, the City of San Mateo 
has budgeted approximately $500,000 to date, with the remaining funding anticipated to come from a combination of 
local and federal sources.
The construction phase of the project is estimated to cost around $25 million, of which approximately $6.5 million in 
construction funding has been secured to date. This includes $3.85 million in federal funds obtained on the Project’s behalf 
by the TA. These federal funds are being used to satisfy the local match requirement typically met through City 
contributions, significantly reducing the City's financial obligation for the Project. To help close the remaining funding gap 
for construction, the City is actively working with the TA to secure additional funding through the TA’s Highway Program, 
which may provide up to 75 percent of the total project cost in grant funding. Even with this maximum contribution, the 
Project may still face a funding shortfall of approximately $1 million.
City staff continue to pursue all available funding sources to ensure the Project remains fully funded. At the same time, it is 
critical that the City meets all deadlines and requirements associated with the federal funds already secured for the 
project. Failure to meet these deadlines could result in the loss of this funding, which would be extremely difficult to 
replace given the scale and regional significance of the Project. Without these federal funds, the Project would face a 
significant risk of delay or cancellation due to a substantial funding shortfall.
Given the accelerated schedule required to keep the Project on track for continued funding eligibility, the Project team is 
actively developing a delivery strategy that prioritizes timing and funding requirements. While the current schedule is 
achievable, it will require a highly streamlined approach. As such, the team may need to forego certain typical project 
milestones or administrative steps, such as extended design reviews or phased bidding, in order to maintain the aggressive 
timeline. Staff are coordinating closely with funding and regulatory partners to ensure that the necessary approvals and 
documentation are completed without delay.
COMMUNITY OUTREACH & STAKEHOLDER COORDINATION
As part of the formal design phase for the 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal Improvement Project, the 
City has planned two community meetings to gather public input and provide Project updates, the first of which was held 
in mid-July. In addition to the first community meeting, staff presented the Project design to the Sustainability and 
Infrastructure Commission on August 13th to obtain their early feedback on the Project. Feedback from these engagement 
efforts are described further within this section and will be included in staff’s presentation. The next community meeting is 
currently being planned for early November, and staff are reviewing the Project schedule to determine if one additional 
community meeting can be included before completing the Project design.

Community Meeting
The first community meeting was held on July 15, 2025, following completion of the 35 percent design milestone. This 
milestone represents an appropriate time to seek community feedback, as many Project components have been refined 
since the planning phase, but detailed design work has not yet commenced, allowing for adjustments based on input 
received. This meeting marked the first formal opportunity for public engagement during the design phase.
To increase accessibility and participation, an online survey was launched concurrently with the community meeting and 
remained open from June 25 through July 23. Outreach to promote the meeting and survey included the distribution of 
approximately 10,000 mailers to residents and businesses within the project area. Additionally, nearly 50 A-frame posters 
were installed throughout the corridor and in surrounding neighborhoods to raise awareness at the pedestrian and 
bicyclist level. Social media messaging was also utilized to further expand outreach and encourage community 
participation.
The online survey received over 150 responses, a strong level of engagement given the survey contained 17 questions and 
required approximately 10 minutes to complete. The in-person meeting was attended by more than 50 community 
members. The feedback gathered will inform further design refinements ahead of the second community meeting.
At the July 15th community workshop, a live poll showed attendees were split between prioritizing bicycle and pedestrian 
improvements and addressing congestion relief. While many arrived with concerns about congestion, discussions around 
the proposed solutions helped address those concerns. However, there was general interest in reviewing the traffic
analysis before forming a final opinion. Many attendees expressed strong support for the bike and pedestrian 
improvements, though some questioned the proposed transition between one-way and two-way facilities at the YMCA 
parking lot intersection, preferring a location near Grant Street/Ginnever Street for better connectivity to existing bike 
routes.
In the online survey, respondents slightly favored bike and pedestrian safety improvements over congestion relief. There 
was broad support for key elements of the Project, including the lane reconfiguration over the Seal Slough Bridge. The 
proposed bikeway transition location between one-way and two-way facilities at the YMCA parking lot intersection also 
received support, though responses were more mixed compared to other elements in the survey, indicating some 
community hesitation.
Meetings with Residents
Following the initial community meeting, several residents representing communities largely from the east side of the US-
101 requested targeted meetings with staff requesting additional detail and clarity about the proposed 19th Avenue / 
Fashion Island Boulevard corridor changes. Staff met with residents on August 25th and again on September 8th. The 
residents wanted to ensure that congestion improvements, specifically for eastbound traffic congestion in the afternoon, 
were being incorporated into the project. Staff provided preliminary traffic analysis findings for this group and identified 
the benefits the project will provide to traffic congestion, which will be further elaborated during the Council presentation. 
Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission Feedback
Public feedback was provided during the Commission meeting, with many speakers expressing strong support for 
enhanced multimodal facilities along the corridor. Several commenters emphasized safety concerns near the ARCO gas 
station and highlighted the importance of improving conditions for middle school students who use the corridor to cross 
US-101 to reach Bayside Academy. Others stressed the importance of including traffic congestion relief as a key 
component of the Project and noted that bike lane implementation should not compromise needed vehicle capacity 
improvements. The need for pedestrian-scale lighting, particularly across the Seal Slough bridge, was also raised.
Commission comments and questions reflected a range of priorities and considerations. Some Commissioners expressed 
interest in completing the sidewalk network on the north side of 19th Avenue between Grant Street and the YMCA parking 
lot. Others sought clarification on the traffic analysis, including whether the proposed improvements in the eastbound 
direction might introduce additional delays for westbound traffic. Questions were also raised about ensuring emergency 
vehicle access, the importance of clearly communicating with all relevant stakeholders, and whether preserving on-street

Stakeholder Engagement
In addition to community engagement, staff have coordinated with other key stakeholders to gather input on the project.

BACKGROUND:
Each year, the City is required to submit to the United States Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) a 
Consolidated Annual Performance Evaluation Report (CAPER). The primary focus of the CAPER is on the use of Community 
Development Block Grant (CDBG) funds, which the City receives on an annual basis from HUD. It also serves to document 
progress made towards the City’s housing and community development goals as identified in associated planning 
documents. These include the five-year Consolidated Plan and Annual Action Plan, which outline where the annual CDBG 
funding will be spent and what goals the City would like to achieve with those activities. This year’s CAPER documents 
progress made on the 2024-25 Annual Acton Plan and the 2023-27 Consolidated Plan. 
The City uses CDBG funds to assist the public in many categories, as defined by HUD. These include public services, 
economic development, community development, fair housing administration, and minor home rehabilitation. The CAPER 
responds to questions asked in a template provided by HUD and consists of narrative on the progress of San Mateo’s 
community development programs and accomplishments made possible by use of CDBG funds. 
The CAPER in Attachment 1 is the narrative portion of the report, describing program activities and achievements. Much of 
this information is generated from the HUD computer system, Integrated Disbursement and Information System (IDIS), 
and from internal reporting systems.
Several areas are highlighted in this year’s CAPER, including:
1.
Community Funding
The City funded several activities through its Community Funding program such as public services, minor home 
rehabilitation, and fair housing. A total of twelve programs were funded, all administered by local organizations who 
serve low- and moderate- income residents. In Program Year 2024-25, the City funded $310,700 in total to the 
following areas:
•
Seven public service grants totaling $98,700, which assisted 17,572 people. Each grantee received $14,100 
each.
o
CALL Primrose, is a drop-in food pantry for low-income residents and served 1,463 people. 
o
CORA, provided victims and survivors of domestic and their children with a safety net of legal services, 
and served 157 people.

2.
Public Facilities
Public facilities are another category of CBDG funding. The City uses these funds to support City street and sidewalk 
improvements and local non-profits that primarily serve low- and moderate-income households. The North Central 
and North Shoreview neighborhood areas are identified as San Mateo’s CDBG priority neighborhoods. In Program Year 
2024-25, Public Works used CDBG funds for the street and sidewalk improvements in Third Avenue and Norfolk Street. 
The Third Avenue/Norfolk Traffic Corridor Project was awarded on January 13, 2025. The first phase (civil 
improvements) and second phase (median wall modification, paving and striping) of construction have been complete. 
The last phase of construction is scheduled for the end of 2025 and will include installation of new poles and signal 
heads, new signal cabinet, and new signal operations. 
3.
Other Accomplishments
In 2024-25, the City accomplished additional efforts that address affordable housing, homelessness, and poverty 
reduction. These accomplishments help support the goals listed in planning documents, including the 2023-27 
Consolidated Plan, 2024-25 Annual Action Plan, and the 2023-2031 Housing Element:
•
Kiku Crossing, a 225-unit affordable housing development by MidPen Housing on City-owned land, completed 
construction in spring of 2024, received their Certificate of Occupancy in summer 2024, and was fully occupied 
by fall 2024.
•
Through the state-grant, Permanent Local Housing Allocation (PLHA) fund, the City funded a homeless shelter, 
rapid rehousing, and home-sharing, and supportive housing programs. A total of 212 San Mateo residents 
were supported through these programs. 
•
An Emergency Rental Assistance Program was recently adopted in July 2025 to prevent homelessness and 
displacement among low-income San Mateo residents. Local funds will support the operations of the program, 
and 37 households are projected to be served in the Program Year 2025-26.

Housing challenges in the City and throughout the County continue to increase. While the

provides funding for homeless prevention and assistance programs through CDBG and

implementing outreach, needs assessments, and services that are accessible throughout

Actions taken to remove or ameliorate the negative effects of public policies that

While many state and federal public policies are intended to protect workers and the 
environment and maintain community standards, they can also have the unintended effect of 
increasing construction costs. This in return makes it even more challenging to produce 
affordable housing. The City has no direct authority over these policies but continues to 
explore local measures that can offset their impacts. 
 
On May 20, 2024, the City adopted its 2023-2031 Housing Element; and on July 23, 2024, it 
was certified for substantial compliance with state law by the California Department of 
Housing and Community Development (HCD). The City’s Housing Element includes goals and 
policies that focus on updating policies and codes that created barriers to the development of 
affordable housing. To support this objective, the Housing Element included revisions to 
existing housing programs as well as several new programs. This includes an analysis of the 
Housing Opportunity Sites Inventory for compliance with Affirmatively Further Fair Housing 
(AFFH) requirements and a comprehensive review of all development-related fees to ensure 
they do not create an unreasonable barrier to housing production. This fee study will include a 
focus on assessing potential fee adjustments and reductions for affordable housing. 
 
On March 13, 2024, the City adopted Strive San Mateo General Plan 2040, a comprehensive 
update to the City’s General Plan. To implement General Plan 2040, a Comprehensive Zoning 
Code Update is underway. The Zoning Code Update effort will include a focus on streamlining 
the development review process and reducing barriers to housing production. Important 
strategies include the rezoning of select sites to allow for housing as a permitted use and/or 
increased residential density. Implementation of General Plan 2040 included bringing a ballot 
measure (Measure T) for voter approval in order to allow for the increased height and 
densities in General Plan 2040’s Land Use Element. On November 5, 2024, San Mateo voters 
approved Measure T and the General Plan’s Land Use Element became fully effective on 
December 19, 2024. Since going into effect, the City has seen a significant increase in new 
housing development proposals, with over 5,000 new housing units in the pipeline, which 
includes over 700 affordable units.
