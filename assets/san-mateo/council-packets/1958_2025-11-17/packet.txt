SUBJECT:
Community Workforce Agreement for City Capital Projects — Direction
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction on a Community Workforce Agreement for San Mateo and guiding principles for potential negotiations. 
BACKGROUND:
The San Mateo County Building and Construction Trades Council (SMCBCTC) requested during public comment at the 
August 18 City Council meeting that the City enter into a Community Workforce Agreement with them. During Reports and 
Announcements, the Council then directed staff to research CWAs for the earliest possible future Study Session. This 
report presents an overview of CWAs from Bay Area public agencies, discussion of the potential provisions in a negotiated 
CWA, a recommended process for considering adoption of a CWA, and recommended guiding principles for CWA 
negotiations. 
Overview of Community Workforce Agreements 
CWAs and Project Labor Agreements (PLAs) are agreements between one or more labor organizations and a public agency 
that establish the terms and conditions of work for the construction of one or more capital projects. CWAs differ from PLAs 
in that CWAs generally apply to all agency projects that meet certain criteria, whereas PLAs apply to a single project. 
CWAs/PLAs are negotiated agreements that are unique to each agency or project. CWAs are typically crafted to achieve 
some form of social policy goals through criteria for local hiring and/or access to apprenticeships. Most CWAs/PLAs include 
the following standard elements: 
•
Standards for wages and benefits; 
•
Provisions that prohibit workers from engaging in strikes, work stoppages, slowdowns, and other dispute 
mechanisms; 
•
Requirements that contractors hire workers who are referred from union hiring halls except for minor exceptions 
for “core workers”; 
•
Requirements that non-union workers pay dues or fees to the applicable union while employed on the project; 
and, 
•
Dispute and grievance resolution procedures. 
CWAs/PLAs differ from traditional collective bargaining agreements in that collective bargaining agreements are between 
the individual craft unions and contractors/contractor organizations, while a CWA/PLA is between a consortium of 
different craft unions and the public agency awarding a construction contract. CWAs are included in the bidding 
documents for construction projects with a requirement that all contractors and subcontractors who are awarded 
construction contracts must comply with the provisions of the CWA. CWAs supplement or supersede existing collective

Initially, PLAs primarily covered extensive construction projects, such as San Francisco International Airport (SFO, 1995)

began applying CWAs/PLAs to projects of significantly less value. Also, recent PLAs typically cover multiple projects in a 
single PLA, either by listing covered projects or by setting a minimum dollar threshold for covered projects (as small as 
$500,000 - $1 million).

Project thresholds and exemptions can be important for the following reasons:
 
•
Ensuring smaller contractors/subcontractors have opportunities to bid on City projects: Based on other agencies’ 
experiences, smaller contractors and subcontractors are hesitant to bid on projects that are subject to a CWA/PLA. 
Smaller contractors are more likely to be local nonunion firms, and many have little or no experience complying 
with the provisions of such agreements. Many smaller contractors have a standing workforce, and PLAs generally 
make it difficult or impossible to use non-union contractors’ existing workforce.  This is a particularly significant 
issue for specialized contractors, and contractors where English is a second language.  Smaller, non-union 
contractors also view the obligation to pay into union trust funds for pension and health as a disadvantage 
because (1) it is on top of benefits they already pay, and (2) it effectively reduced the cash paid to employees 
under prevailing wage laws.  In addition, the administrative burden of documenting compliance with the 
agreements can be significant, and small firms may be less likely to have the staff to support the effort needed. 
Lack of interest is often particularly acute with subcontractors, which are often small, specialized and/or 
disadvantaged firms. 
•
Ensuring the City’s projects, especially pavement, utility, or other time-sensitive projects, are not delayed or 
become too expensive due to a lack of bids from responsive responsible contractors: CWAs bind both 
contractors and subcontractors awarded City construction contracts. Nearly all the City’s construction contracts 
include subcontractors; therefore, even if the prime contractor is willing to comply with the CWA, they can only 
submit a bid if they can find the subcontractors (including small and/or specialized subcontractors) they need for 
their team.

Bidding Responsiveness 
To address the potential outcome of no or single bids on a project, some CWAs include provision(s) allowing the agency to 
rebid the project without the requirements of a CWA. Such a provision might also be triggered if poor bid results due to 
the CWA lead to bids significantly exceeding the Engineer’s Estimate. 
Double Benefits 
A typical component of CWAs/PLAs is the requirement that contractors pay various benefit contributions required under 
the unions’ collectively bargained agreements, whether the contractor employees are union or nonunion. A nonunion 
contractor will be required to pay the benefits portion of its nonunion workers’ wages to the union benefits plan even if 
they already have their own benefits program in place and even if the nonunion contractor’s workers may never be 
entitled to the benefits. This is referred to as the payment of “double benefits,” and it places nonunion contractors at a 
financial disadvantage when it comes to submitting bids as those costs become incorporated into increased project costs.
Wages are a less significant issue because contractors performing the City’s public works projects are required to pay 
prevailing wages. However, PLAs have the effect of reducing the portion of prevailing wage paid to employees because a 
larger portion of the total prevailing wage is devoted to trust fund payments.  Prevailing wages are determined by the 
California Department of Industrial Relations (DIR) and are usually based on rates specified in collective bargaining 
agreements. All projects are required to register with the DIR to prove they are paying prevailing wages, except for new 
construction, alteration, installation, demolition or repair projects under $25,000 and maintenance projects under 
$15,000. 
Use of Core Workers 
CWAs/PLAs traditionally establish the requirement to use union job referral systems for much of the labor required on the 
project rather than a standing workforce employed by the contractor. The various trade union signatories operate “hiring 
halls,” and the hiring halls refer workers to the employers/contractors. One element that is typically negotiated in a CWA is 
authorization for a nonunion contractor or subcontractor to use a limited number (generally five at most) of members of 
its existing workforce (“core workers”) on a project in addition to workers from the hiring hall. Most agreements that allow 
the use of core workers include a requirement that one worker from the hiring hall be employed for each core worker 
used. 
The difficulty utilizing standing workforce employees, the requirement to use employees from the hiring hall with whom 
the contractors are unfamiliar, and the limitations on the use of core employees can be a disincentive to bidding for some 
contractors, including small and minority-owned contractors, that maintain a standing workforce or have provided 
specialized training to employees. 
Sole Operator/Small Operator Carveout 
Another provision sometimes included in CWAs/PLAs are exemptions (“carve-outs”) for sole proprietors, small contractors, 
or other targeted firms that the agency does not want to exclude from bidding. Such exemptions are often capped at a 
maximum percentage of the contract amount. 
Duration of Agreement 
Of the regional peers’ CWAs reviewed by staff, agreement durations are between three and five years. This allows 
sufficient time to review the effectiveness of the CWA/PLA and evaluate outcomes, while also providing an opportunity to 
negotiate a new CWA that addresses any lessons learned in terms of achieving the City’s policy objectives and resolving

Local Hire/Apprenticeships 
In exchange for establishing CWAs, agencies have requested that the CWA give preference to local residents (“local hire”) 
when referring workers to the contractor/employer and/or create new apprenticeship positions for residents or focus on 
“Targeted workers” who are disadvantaged.  Agreements with targeted hiring provisions require contractors to target 
hiring percentages for certain categories of individuals for projects. Examples include residents of the agency or graduates 
from local schools, veterans, public assistance recipients, at-risk youth, survivors of human trafficking, or individuals 
emancipated from foster care, formerly incarcerated, homeless, or who are unemployed for a year and/or low income. A 
review of the local hire and targeted hiring performance in peer agencies’ CWAs indicates that local hire goals can be 
challenging to meet, especially if stacked. 
Apprenticeship and pre-apprenticeship targets appear to be more achievable. Apprenticeships typically focus on 
percentage goals but could be negotiated on a per-individual basis for a defined incremental dollar value of the project. In 
the latter situation, CWA peer agreements could require that contractors hire one new apprentice for the first $1 million, 
as determined by the Engineer’s Estimate. Above an additional dollar threshold, contractors would be required to hire an 
additional apprentice. In addition, agreements usually establish a minimum percentage of apprentice hours worked on a 
project. 
Examples 
The County of San Mateo and the City of Mountain View were selected as examples due to their recent experience 
implementing CWAs/PLAs in a post-COVID bidding environment. Both the County of San Mateo and the City of Mountain 
View have comparable capital budget capacities and deliver projects of similar type and scale. San Mateo County also 
draws from a similar pool of local and regional contractors, making their project examples particularly relevant should the 
City of San Mateo proceed with its own CWA implementation efforts. Additionally, as seen in Table 2 above, there are 
generally two types of CWAs negotiated, one focuses on a dollar threshold for projects, and the other being project 
specific that focuses on a set number of projects and a learning period. The County of San Mateo and the City of Mountain 
View provide examples of each CWA form.
Also provided below are some lessons learned from the City’s Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) PLA and the City of 
Watsonville’s PLA, which is currently under renegotiation. Though Watsonville is a smaller city with a different set of 
geographic and demographic characteristics, there may be some lessons to be learned from this example.

Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) PLA – Lessons Learned 
The only real project experience that the City of San Mateo has with PLAs is on the WWTP Upgrade and Expansion Project, 
which is still in progress today. There were several lessons learned on this project that could be considered as part of 
future negotiations if staff is directed to proceed. 
•
The WWTP PLA did not detail the reimbursement for eligibility for craft parking fees or bridge tolls, so it defaults to 
the union agreements to determine what needs to be covered by the prime contractor. If this is not detailed more 
clearly, it could add cost to future projects if there is insufficient nearby parking, because additional fees or busing 
may be needed to accommodate the subcontractors. The time to walk from the available parking is also included 
in the hourly wage, which can cut down on production time for the workday. 
•
The references to other union agreements need to be clear that the CWA is the controlling document so that it 
supersedes the union agreements. 
•
There were challenges in finding local union workers for certain trade work that had the necessary certifications or 
experience. It would be helpful to allow more flexibility for the prime contractor to look at additional union halls in 
the region to get the workers needed. 
•
The challenges in finding qualified union workers also highlight the lack of data available from the unions that 
identifies the trade workers‘ availability, certifications, safety training, etc. Additional reporting requirements 
would help the City evaluate the benefits of the CWA and provide metrics to consider in future negotiations. 
•
There is certain work related to the overall project that could be considered for exclusion from the union labor 
requirements. If the prime contractor is not a union member, allowing them to do certain activities related to 
construction maintenance and site cleaning would be beneficial. Some examples would be stormwater controls, 
solid waste handling, temporary bathroom maintenance, maintenance of the on-site equipment and laydown 
areas, etc. A similar consideration would be to allow a percentage of the work to not be union labor, such as an 
80/20 split. 
Watsonville PLA 
The Watsonville PLA provides useful information because on November 5, 2025, a PLA that had been in place since 2014 
was repealed by the City Council. The PLA applied to all capital projects that had a dollar value of $600,000 or more; 
however, staff were finding that it had become difficult to get sufficient bids on projects, and they were significantly more 
expensive than the design estimates. Staff had proposed raising the threshold for projects to $1,500,000 and limiting the 
type of projects the PLA covered. It appeared that one of important considerations in negotiations is the type of project 
that the PLA applies to. In this case, the City Council directed staff to diligently work with the Trade Council to get another 
PLA in place as soon as possible. 
 
Staff’s main takeaway from this example is that it could benefit San Mateo to get additional data through a learning period 
before setting a dollar threshold or identifying the applicable projects. That way the data could more easily support 
negotiating the terms of a PLA with the Trade Council and ensure the City can analyze the benefits to San Mateo residents.
Dollar Threshold Approach Compared to the Project Specific Approach 
Staff’s research indicates that the administration of a CWA is very time-intensive, with some agencies employing dedicated 
staff to administer their programs. In addition to staff required to document contractors’ compliance with agreement 
provisions, CWAs typically require the creation of a Joint Administration Committee (JAC) partially comprised of City staff 
that meet regularly to monitor projects under construction. Administration of a CWA would likely require the addition of 
at least one new staff position with subject matter expertise and a third-party administrator (TPA). The TPA would manage 
the on-boarding and support of contractors as well as the tracking and monitoring of community benefit goals like local 
hire and apprenticeship.

The advantage of starting with the project specific approach with a set number of designated projects is that it will allow 
staff to focus on a smaller number of projects of higher value and see how it affects costs especially due to sub-coverage 
issues, and whether it bends the curve on creating new local apprenticeships. Additionally, it will give staff an opportunity 
to assess the workload impacts prior to entering into future discussions to include more projects. It will also allow staff to 
complete thorough data collection and analysis to better understand the benefits and challenges a CWA presents. These 
subject matter experts can provide data-driven reports to show lessons learned and how best a CWA can benefit the San 
Mateo community and inform future agreement iterations. 
Staff’s Recommended Guiding Principles for CWA Negotiations 
If the Council directs staff to enter into negotiations for a CWA, staff has a recommended list of guiding principles for 
negotiations geared toward ensuring the CWA benefits the City of San Mateo, and its businesses and residents to the 
maximum extent possible. 
Recommended Guiding Principles 
•
Whether the City settles upon a dollar threshold or a learning period, require union reporting to allow for a data-
driven approach so the City can analyze the benefits to the San Mateo community and act responsively. 
•
Provide flexibility to look beyond the San Mateo labor pool when there is a clear evidence-based need to do so. 
•
Include provisions that prioritize the efficient use of staff resources, including those related to CWA administration 
and project delivery (e.g., pre-job meetings). 
•
Ensure the City’s capital projects will continue to be delivered in a timely and cost-effective manner by allowing 
alterations to CWA requirements when there are too few bids or bids are significantly more expensive than 
estimated. 
•
Ensure that competition and disputes between labor and building trades organizations (e.g., carpenters unions and 
labor unions) are handled by these parties directly rather than facilitated by the City. 
•
The CWA should not impact the City’s ability to use outside sources (e.g., grants) to fund projects. 
•
Include a termination clause to allow flexibility should there be significant job or market shifts affecting City 
projects, or other reasons to terminate the CWA. 
•
 Provide staff the authority to negotiate with the SMCBCTC/unions on a CWA that ensures all the guiding principles 
are represented.

Re: Inclusive Workforce Policy & Carpenters Union Participation in CWA Negotiations
Dear Mayor and Councilmembers,

On behalf of Carpenters Local Union 217 and the Nor Cal Carpenters Union (NCCU), representing
over 35,000 members in Northern California, thank you for your leadership in strengthening
labor standards on public projects. We share your commitment to ensuring that City
investments create good local jobs, strong apprenticeship pathways, and safe, high-quality
construction.

The NCCU should be included as a standalone partner in CWA negotiations

As the Council considers its approach towards negotiating a potential Community Workforce
Agreement (CWA), we respectfully make clear the need for the inclusion of the NCCU in any
discussions or negotiations. The eleven crafts represented by the NCCU make up the majority of
the workforce on public projects. Including all affected crafts from the outset ensures a policy
that is workable, equitable, and meaningfully representative of the construction industry.

To be clear, the NCCU is a standalone negotiating partner in this process. Neither the NCCU nor
any of its affiliated bodies are members of the Building Trades Council (BTC). The BTC is not
authorized to negotiate on our behalf, and we will not be signing any agreement negotiated
without our involvement.

Blanket labor standards on public works are an alternative to the CWA

We also encourage the Council to consider blanket labor standards as an alternative or
complimentary approach to the inherent limitations of CWA-style agreements. Jurisdictions
across California are increasingly using broad, enforceable standards to improve workforce
outcomes without limiting bidder participation. These standards—such as prevailing wage,
apprenticeship utilization, local hire expectations, healthcare requirements, and strong
compliance monitoring—lift conditions for all workers, regardless of affiliation, while keeping
projects competitive.

This approach is already proving effective. For example, California’s AB 2011 requires union-
level wages, health benefits, and apprenticeship access on certain housing projects without
mandating a project labor agreement. The result of this blanket labor standard-setting is
thousands of workers gaining better pay, training, and protections through universal standards.

Conclusion

We are ready to collaborate with the City—whether refining a CWA or designing an inclusive set
of universal labor standards for public works that ensures every City of San Mateo project
supports good careers and community benefits.

We look forward to participating as a stakeholder in this important process.

Thank you for your time and leadership. We look forward to partnering with you.

What is a CWA/PLA?
• Project Labor Agreements (PLAs) and CWAs are similar, but CWAs are 
broader in scope and apply to more projects meeting certain criteria
• Agreements between labor organizations and a public agency
• Establishes terms for construction projects meeting certain criteria
• Typically crafted to achieve some form of social policy goals through 
criteria for local hiring and/or access to apprenticeships

--- page 22 ---
Typical CWA Elements
• Standards for wages and benefits 
• Provisions that prohibit workers from engaging in strikes, work 
stoppages, slowdowns, and other dispute mechanisms
• Requirements that contractors hire workers who are referred from 
union hiring halls except for minor exceptions for “core workers”
• Requirements that nonunion workers pay dues or fees to the 
applicable union while employed on the project
• Dispute and grievance resolution procedures.

Differences from Collective Bargaining
• Collective bargaining: between individual unions and contractors
• CWA/PLA: between public agency and a consortium of different craft 
unions 
• Included in the bid documents to apply to all bidders and 
subcontractors on covered projects
• CWA/PLAs supplement or supersede existing collective bargaining 
agreements, but require compliance with the master labor 
agreements of all craft unions

• Initially for mega-projects (e.g., San Francisco International Airport -
 1995, Port of Oakland - 1999) that exceeded $1 billion
• Now often applied to projects as small as $500K–$1M
• Increasingly used to achieve local hiring and social equity goals

Specified Projects vs. $ Threshold Approach
