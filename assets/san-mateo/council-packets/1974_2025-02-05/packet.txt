SUBJECT:
Shoreview Skate Plaza Update Project (610038) – Project Update and Improvement Prioritization Approval
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on the Shoreview Skate Plaza Update Project (610038) and approve the recommended prioritization of 
preferred improvement options.
BACKGROUND:
Shoreview Park has served the Shoreview Neighborhood for over 70 years and features a community center, a baseball 
field, a basketball court, a tennis court and a skatepark. As part of the Capital Improvement Program, the City of San 
Mateo (City) has undertaken a process to assess the current design of the skatepark, explore potential improvements, and 
determine associated costs.
February 2023 - The City hired Wormhoudt, Inc. (Consultant) to provide professional consultant design services. The 
Consultant was uniquely qualified to assist on this project because of their experience working with the local skating 
community, local municipalities and designing skatepark facilities in the Bay Area. 
September 2023 - The Consultant conducted an online survey to gather input from the community regarding potential 
improvements to the Shoreview Skatepark. A total of 66 participants provided feedback in response to the questions, 
“What types of improvements would you like to see made to the existing skatepark?” and “Are there any new features you 
would like to see added to the existing skatepark?” The three most commonly suggested improvements were:
1.
Replacement of flatwork/repaving (approximately 20 responses)
2.
Expansion of the skatepark (approximately 19 responses) 
3.
Installation of lighting (approximately 8 responses)
October 2023 - A community meeting was held to offer additional opportunities for public input. Feedback from the 11 
attendees aligned with the survey responses. Attendees expressed general satisfaction with the existing layout and unique 
"street-style" elements of the skatepark, and echoed input received from the survey expressing a desire for 
resurfacing/repaving, expansion, and the addition of lighting.
The Consultant has compiled these findings into the Shoreview Skatepark Renovation and Improvement Report 
(Attachment 1), which summarizes the survey results and community meeting feedback. The report includes rough, 
conceptual cost estimates for the three most popular improvement options. These estimates were updated in January 
2025 to account for inflation, changes in construction material and labor costs, and other market factors. Additionally, the 
City obtained supplemental cost estimates from reputable contractors to verify the Consultant's figures.

Financial Sustainability Strategy Policy 
RECOMMENDATION:
Recommend to the City Council adoption of the proposed Financial Sustainability Policy and inclusion in the City’s Fee 
Schedule starting in Fiscal Year 2025-2026.
BACKGROUND:
The current policy, adopted in 2004, identifies two thresholds of cost recovery for each activity, a minimum and a target 
threshold. For example, Activity A may, at a minimum, recover all its direct programmatic costs while the target threshold 
may recover all direct overhead costs. Activity B may have two entirely different thresholds. These thresholds are 
established based on several factors including services, values, market conditions, costs, demand, and the general fiscal 
condition of the City. In addition, it is recommended that additional fee considerations be identified where appropriate. 
For example, a specific activity may be in a specific market and therefore fees may be benchmarked to specific 
competitors. Although this system of cost recovery worked for the department for many years in the past, it is very 
complex and requires heavy administrative management. It does not meet current goals for community access to City 
recreation programming. 
In April 2022, staff engaged 110%, Inc. to analyze the department’s financial sustainability position. After months of 
collaboration, aggregation of data and analysis of revenue and expenses, an update was presented to the Parks and 
Recreation Commission on November 2, 2022. The Commission participated in an activity to prioritize eleven (11) Service 
Categories and design the Beneficiary of Service Model which serves as the backbone of the new Financial Sustainability 
Policy. On September 30, 2024, at a joint meeting of the Parks and Recreation and Senior Commission, the department 
and consultant from 110%, Inc. lead a study session to explain the history of this project and outline the goal for a new 
policy that will help determine pricing for recreation offerings. 
This policy allows the department to balance the City’s need to reduce the proportion of subsidy provided to recreation 
programming and maintaining an emphasis on providing access to programs and services to the widest number of 
community members. In addition, this updated methodology of calculating subsidy/cost recovery is less time intensive 
than the previous methodology and empowers staff to optimize pricing while maintaining core public service offering. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
The Parks and Recreation Department budget is dependent on generating a significant amount of revenue to offset 
expenditures from the City’s operating budget. Adopting this policy will create more transparency to the subsidy/cost 
recovery process and will provide more clarity in reporting both internally and to Commission’s and City Council. We 
predict a minimal increase in revenue for the fiscal year 2025-2026. The policy will not take effect until the Fall session of

Financial Sustainability Policy

The following shall serve as the policy guidance for establishing a Recreation Activities Cost Recovery Plan,
developing annual activity budgets, and establishing fees to be charged for recreation activities.

Policy Statement

The City of San Mateo Parks and Recreation Department Financial Sustainability Strategy intends to create
organizational resilience by way of logical, intentional, and thoughtful guidelines for investment and spending
decisions. The strategy encourages revenue generation strategies and practices that are fair, equitable, and
responsible.

In alignment with the City of San Mateo’s Strive San Mateo General Plan 2040 Parks and Recreation Department
goal COS-9 - Provide stable and adequate operational and capital funding for the parks and recreation system, the
Department’s financial sustainability plan seeks to balance community investment with financial discipline to
insure long-term sustainability of the Department. This policy guides the allocation of City resources to allow for
the most equitable benefit to the community.

Subsidy/Cost Recovery

Cost recovery refers to offsetting the costs (expenses) of delivering services by way of revenues generated from
fees and charges, sponsorships, donations, grants, and other alternative revenue streams. Alternatively, subsidy
represent a tax source into the City. It is granted by a governmental entity; typically to remove some type of
burden and often considered to be in the overall interest of the public. It is also given to promote a social good or
an economic policy.

For example, a cost recovery level of 75% simply means that for each dollar spent on a service, 75-cents are
generated from a revenue source (i.e., fees) with the remaining 25-cents covered by subsidies (i.e., taxes).

Service Categories

The development of categories which include /ike services are important when it comes to justifiable and equitable
allocation of subsidy, cost recovery levels, and assignment of budget and general ledger lines to account for a
category’s fiscal performance.

The benefits of this type of approach are two-fold. First, itis inefficient for the Department to determine cost
recovery expectations by each individual service including facility, activity, or event. Secondly, categorizing by
“type of service” or “likeness of service” discourages attempts to determine fees and charges (and therefore cost
recovery decisions) based upon special interests, age-based services, or individual values.

Examples: City of San Mateo Parks such as Casanova, Beresford Park, and Trinta.(95-100%)

2. Human Services - Services designed to support independence and community connection by providing or
linking resources for those in need. Examples: Senior Transportation Services, Senior Lunch Services,
Community Lectures. (90-100%)

3. City-Wide Events - Large-scale events appealing to a broad community segment regardless of age, ability, or
family composition. These intensive events typically occur annually. Examples: Summer Concert Series, Fourth
of July, Movies in the Park, Winter Wonderland. (75-100%)

4. Child Care - In-house childcare facilitated by City staff. Examples: Summer Camps, Afterschool Care, Care on
No-School Days. (40-70%)

5. Beginner Activities - Classes, clinics, workshops, and other led/instructed activities aimed at acquiring or
learning a new skill. Examples: Beginning Dance, Fitness, Swim Lessons. (25-50%)

6. Drop-In Activities - Self-directed activities that do not require registration or instruction but include
supervision or oversight by staff and/or volunteers. Examples: Rec Lap Swim, Open Gym, Games, Crafts, Senior
Movies.(25-50%)

7. Camps Contracted - Camps operated by third parties during school breaks, providing specialized enrichment
for children. Examples: Science/Tech Camps, Musical Theater Camps, Dance Camps, Athletic Camps, Art Camps.
(0-20%)

8. Competitive Activities - Organized competitive leagues without guided instruction. Examples: Adult Softball,
Adult Volleyball. (0-20%)

9. Advanced Activities - Classes, clinics, workshops, and other led/instructed activities designed to advance or
master a skill. Examples: Advanced Athletic Classes, Advanced Martial Art, Advanced Art. (0-20%)

10. Rentals - Reservable outdoor/indoor spaces and facilities available for exclusive use by individuals or groups.
Examples: Recreation/Community Center Rooms, Sports Fields, Picnic Areas. (O-20%)

11. Private/Semi-Private Activities - Individualized classes, clinics, workshops, and other led/instructed activities
offered in a private or semi-private setting to meet specific needs, interests or skill sets. Examples: Private
Music Lessons, Tech Tutoring, Private Swim Lessons. (0-20%)

Note: Service Categories listed above are in order from those perceived to be Common Good Services (#1) to those

seen as providing a more Exclusive Benefit Services (#11) as ranked by Department staff as well as San Mateo Parks
and Recreation Commission representatives.

RECOMMENDATION:
Recommend to the City Council adoption of the proposed Fiscal Year 2025-26 fee schedule updates for Parks and 
Recreation facilities and miscellaneous fees as provided in Attachment 1 of the Administrative Report. 
BACKGROUND:
Each year the City reviews the fee schedule and makes adjustments where deemed appropriate. Generally, fees are 
adjusted based on the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for the previous year. The Finance Department identified the official CPI 
for this fiscal year to be 2.4%. Fees have all been rounded to the nearest whole dollar after calculating the CPI increase. 
The Recreation Cost Recovery and Pricing Policy (Financial Sustainability Policy) is undergoing significant changes and will 
be addressed in separate report. The recommended fee changes for the Parks and Recreation Department are detailed in 
Attachment 1. 
Below is a summary of the significant recommended fee changes and language updates by category: 
Picnic, Indoor, Athletics, Fields and Gymnasium Reservations (Att 1, Page 5-7): No changes exceeding Finance’s 
recommended CPI increase for these locations. Indoor facility fees moved up to align with outdoor facility fees and 
improve readability of document. 
Park In Lieu Fees / Impact Fees (Att 1, Page 8): Park In Lieu Fees/ Impact Fees for residential construction have increased 
in the city due to a ten (10) percent increase in median sales price of homes in the City of San Mateo. 
Golf (Att 1, Page 8): There were four (4) fees that were increased in excess of CPI for the Golf Division; weekend and 
weekend twilight for both residents and general admission were all increased one (1) dollar more than the CPI increase for 
a total of a two (2) dollar increase for each of these rates. The following rates were not increased this year; weekday super 
twilight, senior weekday and senior ten play card. 
Arbor, Parks, and Visitor Services: No changes exceeding Finance recommended CPI increase for these divisions. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Most recommended changes will not have a significant impact on the department’s budgeted revenue outside of the CPI. 
The fee updates based on CPI should allow department revenues to stay in line with current inflation rates. The increase in 
Park In Lieu and Impact Fees on residential construction should help to offset rising cost of construction and renovations of 
City Parks and Recreation assets.
