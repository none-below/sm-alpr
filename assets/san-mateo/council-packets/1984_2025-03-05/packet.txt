SUBJECT:
Proposed Five-Year (Fiscal Years 2026-2030) Parks and Recreation Capital Improvement Plan
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and comment on Parks and Recreation’s Proposed Five-Year Capital Improvement Plan for FY 2026-2030.
BACKGROUND:
Annually the City evaluates its five-year plan for capital improvements, as part of this, the Parks and Recreation 
Department develops updated project budgets for the City’s Capital Improvement Plan (CIP), considering staff capacity to 
execute projects, city-wide usage, urgency of need and budget constraints. For the next fiscal year, the department has 
prioritized additional funding for the construction management of two large shovel ready projects (Central Park 
Playground and Restroom and East Hillsdale Playground Renovations), additional funding to allow the Department to 
replace both play structures at Parkside Aquatic Park and setting aside seed money for planned future projects. The 
proposed FY 2026-2030 CIP budget allocations are in Attachment 1 to this report. 
Revenue
This CIP process also projects revenue for the Parks and Recreation Revenue Fund, which is the primary funding source for 
parks and facility improvements. Staff projected revenues for year one of the five-year plan based on the number of 
residential projects in the building permit process. As it is difficult to project beyond one year, due to changes in the 
market or funding challenges for developers, staff used a seven-year average to project the revenue for years two through 
five. The Department is projecting $15 million in revenue over the next five years. While the department is in a good 
position to fund projects over the next two years, the five-year outlook is challenging, as the improvements for the Dr. 
Martin Luther King Jr. Center are estimated to cost over $20 million over the next five years. We are looking to supplement 
the funding for this project with grants, which would allow us to use the Parks and Recreation Revenue Fund in more park 
locations. In addition to the Parks and Recreation Revenue Fund, projects are also funded with Measure S, Grants, the 
City’s General Fund and Tree Planting Fund. 
Prioritizing Projects
Staff uses a variety of prioritization methods when recommending projects to Council for funding. Included are the 
following methods: 
•
Available funding and funding restrictions. For example, park-in-lieu fees can only fund park projects. 
•
Previously identified Council and community priorities. 
•
The amount of investment already made into a project. 
•
The availability and time limitations on grants or other matching funds. 
•
The location of the project – ensures that all areas of the city receive investment in nearby infrastructure. 
•
Maintenance deficiencies.

These projects are classified in six categories: Priority 1 is currently critical; Priority 2 is potentially critical; Priority 3 is 
necessary but not yet critical; Priority 4 is recommended; Priority 5 is appearance; and Priority 6 is where the 
infrastructure does not meet current codes or standards but can wait for other nearby projects. Due to current resource 
limitations, the City is only able to focus on Priority 1 projects. 
Project Funding Recommendations. 
Funding for Ongoing Projects 
•
Staff has prioritized two projects for additional funding for construction management, to allow the department’s 
Senior Project Manager to focus on developing future projects: 

Project #610027 Central Park Playground 

Project #610017 East Hillsdale Park
•
King Pool Assessments and Improvements – Public Works Staff has recommended $900,000 to continue 
developing a plan to upgrade the Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. Community Center to a resiliency center.
 
Funding for New Projects 
•
Parkside Aquatic Play Area – Staff has recommended an additional $600,000 in the next fiscal year to bring the 
project budget total to $1 million. This would allow the replacement of structures at both play areas. 
•
Ryder Park Water Feature Filtration System – Staff has recommended $50,000 to scope and prepare project 
budget and documentation. 
•
Japanese Garden Repairs – Staff has recommended $350,000 in the next fiscal year with additional funding in the 
following year for the replacement of the fence, roof and pond repairs. 
•
Childcare Facility Improvements – Staff has recommended $150,000 to evaluate the best way to upgrade current 
facilities to support the City’s need for licensed childcare. 
These and other projects are included in the CIP document (Attachment 1). There are several projects that have been 
identified but are not being recommended for funding at this time. These projects are on the unfunded list (Attachment 2). 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Projects will only proceed if funds are available.

This Five-Year Capital Improvement Plan is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative 
activity that will not result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section
