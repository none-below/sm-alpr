SUBJECT:
Electrify San Mateo: Sustainable Building Strategy - Feedback
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an informational report on the “Electrify San Mateo: Sustainable Building Strategy” project and provide feedback 
on policy pathways.
BACKGROUND:
Building energy use represents 38% of communitywide greenhouse gas emissions in San Mateo. Building electrification 
refers to the transition from gas equipment to electric equipment for space and water heating, clothes drying, cooking and 
more. While natural gas is a fossil fuel, Peninsula Clean Energy (PCE) provides carbon-free electricity communitywide. 
Electrifying existing buildings is critical to reducing communitywide greenhouse gas emissions and is a key strategy in 
attaining the objectives contained in City’s adopted Climate Action Plan. To continue to advance existing building 
electrification beyond reach codes and to meet the City Council decarbonization priority, City staff began working with 
Rincon Consultants to develop the Electrify San Mateo: Sustainability Building Strategy. This project aims to create an 
actionable roadmap with steps for the City of San Mateo to electrify buildings based on effectiveness and equity criteria, 
cost-effectiveness, best available science and data, and community feedback. The project team completed a building 
inventory and market segmentation analysis that was presented at the September 13, 2023 Sustainability and 
Infrastructure Commission meeting. After completing Phase I of community engagement, the project culminated in the 
identification of five policy pathways that were presented at the January 16, 2024 City Council meeting. City Council 
directed the project team to conduct community engagement on all five policy pathways to determine overall feasibility 
and support from the community. This report provides an overview of the project, a summary of community engagement, 
and analysis of the five policy pathways that will be considered by City Council.
Building Electrification Background
Electrification benefits include the added health benefit of reducing gas use in the home. Commonly used appliances that 
burn gas, including stoves, heating systems, and water heaters, emit hazardous air pollutants inside a home when 
improperly ventilated, or when leaks occur. These can include benzene, nitrogen oxides, sulfur oxides, carbon monoxide, 
formaldehyde, particulate matter and other harmful pollutants that increase the risk of respiratory disease such as 
asthma. Additionally, gas infrastructure carries an inherent safety risk due to the volatile nature of gas, including risk of 
methane leakage, damage during earthquakes, accidental explosions, or fires. 
Building electrification is also an essential approach to driving the state of California toward its ambitious climate goal of 
carbon neutrality by 2045. Additionally, at the regional level the Bay Area Air District (BAAD) adopted zero nitrogen oxides 
(NOx) standards that will prohibit the sale and installation of gas water heaters starting in 2027 and gas furnaces in 2029 in 
the nine-county Bay Area. These standards are the most ambitious in the country and outpace the state’s goal of banning 
the sale of new gas furnaces and water heaters throughout the state starting in 2030.

--- page 9 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
As part of the FY 2022-2023 goal setting session, the City Council directed staff to present to them policy options to 
decarbonize and electrify existing buildings and eliminate natural gas use by 2030. The City’s Climate Action Plan also 
identifies electrifying existing buildings as a key strategy to reduce communitywide greenhouse gas emissions. The City has 
shown great leadership in advancing building electrification through the adoption of local amendments to the state building 
code also known as reach codes. In 2020, the City Council adopted a reach code requiring all new residential and office 
buildings be all-electric starting on January 1, 2021. In 2022, the City Council adopted a reach code that expanded the all-
electric requirement to all new buildings and requiring electric-readiness and electric appliances for specific home remodels 
that started on January 1, 2023. In 2024, due to a federal court decision, the City suspended the enforcement of its reach 
codes that prohibit the installation of new gas infrastructure. The Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals in California Restaurant 
Association v. City of Berkeley determined that a local ordinance that generally prohibited the installation of natural gas 
infrastructure was preempted by federal law, namely the Energy Policy and Conservation Act (EPCA). In January 2025, the 
San Mateo City Council adopted an Energy Performance Reach Code for new construction that requires energy compliance 
margins for new buildings and repealed the unenforceable all-electric requirement.
Community Engagement
The project team conducted two phases of project outreach. The first phase of community engagement focused on 
educating the community about the costs and benefits of building electrification and about the project’s general goals. 
Outreach also focused on gathering feedback on the equity and effectiveness criteria the project team would use to 
evaluate policy pathways. The top concerns were costs, power outages and grid resilience, and the growth of the grid to 
accommodate future electric buildings. The top values were how the climate crisis is a priority issue, the need for the City 
to act now, and the availability of electric equipment incentives and rebates. 
The second phase of community engagement aimed to get specific feedback on the five policy pathways as directed by 
City Council. The project team conducted presentations at a variety of in-person and virtual community and City 
Commission meetings, engaged with commercial property owners, and conducted an online survey. Throughout both 
phases of engagement, the project team heard from individuals with strong opinions on building electrification policy. 
While many community members continue to be highly supportive of local action, others are firmly opposed to additional 
local policy. Detailed community feedback and survey results for each project phase are provided in the Community 
Engagement Summary attachment. Detailed community feedback as related to each policy pathway is provided in the 
Policy Pathways Analysis attachment. 
Policy Pathways
The project team gathered feedback on five existing building electrification policy pathways. These policy pathways are 
meant to be considered as a menu of options to achieve the City’s climate goals, while considering implementation and 
cost challenges for property owners across the City. Most local governments considering a building decarbonization 
strategy combine multiple strategies together to produce the most comprehensive, and equitable policy result. The 
following provides a high-level summary of the policy pathways. The Policy Pathways Analysis attachment provides 
detailed discussion of each policy pathway including advantages and challenges of policy implementation, greenhouse gas 
emission reduction impact, cost effectiveness and equity considerations.
1.
Business as usual: City should continue with adopting reach codes for new construction and focus on outreach 
and incentives. In the past, the City adopted local building code amendments to require more sustainable new 
construction than the State code. This approach has a limited impact because new construction accounts for a 
fraction of San Mateo’s development, however, this approach is already in place and thus received community 
support to date. There are no policy-related additional costs to building owners unless they voluntarily decide 
to electrify.
 
2.
Reach codes for renovations: This approach would include the evaluation of additional reach codes to 
encourage and/or require electrification during renovations. New reach codes would vary depending on the 
updated State codes. One example of a reach code during renovations is requiring the installation of a heat

--- page 10 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
pump when an air conditioner is installed. Reach codes during renovations help encourage electrification at an 
opportune time when a property owner is already considering home improvements. The upfront costs of 
electrification improvements can vary depending on the project and unique circumstances of your home. Cost-
effectiveness studies must be completed to validate any adopted reach codes. Past studies have shown utility 
savings over time make electric equipment upgrades cost-effective compared to gas equipment replacements. 
3.
Require electric equipment upon replacement ahead of BAAD Zero NOx requirements: The Bay Area Air District 
(BAAD) adopted zero nitrogen oxides (NOx) standards that will prohibit the sale and installation of gas water 
heaters starting in 2027 and gas furnaces in 2029 in the nine-county Bay Area. A local ordinance ahead of 
these milestones would require the replacement of gas appliances with zero NOx versions when they fail or are 
voluntarily upgraded. This is an ambitious policy that would have a broad reach and only a few local 
governments have adopted such a requirement.
4.
Building Performance Standards for commercial buildings: This pathway would establish a new set of policy 
standards for commercial buildings aimed at increasing energy efficiency, and eventually, electrifying 
commercial buildings as a phased approach. This is a well-established policy mechanism that has been 
adopted by many local governments across the country. This approach can be customizable and lead to 
incremental reductions in energy use but not necessarily electrification. The development of such a local 
program would necessitate new staff time.
5.
Electric first policy for City facilities: This policy establishes a mechanism for local government renovations to 
go electric first. The policy ensures financial and feasibility data is being collected for municipal facilities that 
will allow the City to compare costs to electrify versus replacing with gas equipment. This policy has a limited 
reach as it only applies to municipal buildings. It demonstrates City leadership on hard-to-electrify existing 
buildings that mirror the commercial sector.
Staff seeks Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission feedback on these five policy pathways. Feedback will be shared 
with City Council to aid in their discussion of this project, which is tentatively scheduled for June 2025.

Subject: Electrify San Mateo: Existing Building Electrification Policy Pathways Analysis
In the City of San Mateo (City), the residential and commercial built environment make 
up 38 percent of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. To achieve emissions reduction 
targets consistent with state goals and the City’s Climate Action Plan, the City must 
decarbonize its residential and commercial buildings. This is strengthened by the 2022 
City Council priority to “Establish policies to decarbonize existing buildings and 
infrastructure and eliminate methane gas use by 2030.” The decarbonization or 
electrification of buildings refers to the transition from gas equipment to carbon-free 
electric equipment for space and water heating, clothes drying, cooking and 
more. Decarbonization can also focus on increasing energy efficiency and the use of 
renewable energy. In San Mateo, Peninsula Clean Energy (PCE) provides electricity that 
is 50% renewable and virtually 100% carbon free and is committed to delivering 100% 
renewable and carbon free electricity by 2025. The City has already passed a New 
Construction Reach Code aimed at decarbonizing new buildings, however, electrifying 
existing buildings offers the largest opportunity and most cost-effective strategy for GHG 
emission reductions. 
In May 2023, the City, with the help of Rincon Consultants Inc. (Rincon), began 
developing the Building for the Future: City of San Mateo Sustainable Buildings Strategy 
project as a part of the larger Electrify San Mateo initiative. The goal of this project is to 
develop a long-term strategy document to electrify San Mateo’s existing building stock, 
including policies and actions for both residential and nonresidential buildings. This report 
provides Rincon’s final policy findings which are based on the cumulative technical 
analysis and community feedback completed to date. 
Project Timeline and Overview 
The City of San Mateo and Rincon began the initial research for this project in the Summer 
of 2023. The initial analysis included building segmentation analysis and an electrification 
cost study. Rincon also conducted research on the current best practices and a wide 
range of feasible policy options being deployed by other cities around the country. The 
project timeline is shown below in Figure 1.

Community Outreach (Phase I)
During the first phase of community outreach, the City aimed to understand the goals 
and concerns of the community as they relate to San Mateo’s buildings and 
decarbonization. Through the initial round of engagement, the City identified 
affordability, health and safety, resilience, and climate as the primary goals of the 
community. The largest concerns related to building electrification were the potential for 
higher costs, increased energy bills, and a difficult installation process. Based on this 
feedback and direction from City Council, the initial policy scan list was narrowed down 
to five potential policy directions.

Continue adopted reach codes and focus on outreach and incentives (Business as

Community Outreach (Phase II)
In the second phase of community outreach (see Engagement Summary Memo for

second online feedback survey. Feedback received from Phase II of outreach is 
incorporated into an analysis of each policy pathway in the following sections. 
Addressing Community Concerns
Throughout the community engagement process, there have been polarized responses 
to building electrification. While most constituents care about addressing the Climate 
Crisis and agree that the City should pursue existing building electrification in some form, 
many residents also expressed concerns. The primary concerns identified during Phase I 
and Phase II engagement included: 
•
Upfront costs of electric equipment 
•
On-bill or operational costs of electric equipment
•
Installation and electrical infrastructure challenges
•
Grid reliability and resilience
•
Lack of education surrounding electrification (addressed in Policy Option 1)
Each of the pathways balance the City’s climate goals and the roughly half of 
respondents who strongly support building electrificaiton with the concerns voiced during 
the engagement process in different ways. Each primary community concern is 
addressed below and further covered in the policy summaries. 
Upfront Costs of Electrical Equipment
For many building owners, making the switch from natural gas to electric appliances 
results in higher upfront costs without additional incentives or rebates. While rebates can 
cover up to 100% of these costs, rebates are not always available. However, not installing 
electric equipment when it is most cost effective can have significant cost impacts in the 
future. Therefore, the City has identified flexible policies that allow building owners to

While most electric appliance upgrades are relatively simple, some buildings present 
difficult edge cases. Some buildings may not have sufficient space or electrical 
infrastructure to easily upgrade. Upgrading electric panels and service lines can take 
months to complete. While all-building owners are encouraged to make a plan for

Grid Reliability and Resilience
Grid reliability and resilience concerns arose due to the perception that increased 
electrical use would put strain on the grid and that electricity was less reliable than gas. 
However, it has been nearly 20 years since California had a power outage due to lack of 
electricity.2 Furthermore, the utilities, the CPUC, and the CEC, are all planning for 
increased electricity usage over time. The CEC now assumes a 27% increase in peak 
demand by 2035 due to electrification of buildings and vehicles and is expanding the 
grid to meet that demand. Furthermore, neither gas water heaters nor most furnaces 
work without electricity, meaning gas appliances are no more resilient to power outages 
than their electric upgrades. Therefore, the policies identified below focus on these 
appliances.
Policy Findings Summary
To analyze the feasibility and benefits of each policy pathway, Rincon provided a 
description of each pathway, pros and cons, precedent legislation in other cities and the 
state, GHG reductions potential, community feedback, cost-effectiveness, and equity 
considerations. Based on the detailed analysis in the Policy Analysis section, the following 
summary highlights the major takeaways of the five policy pathways. 
1.
Business as usual: The City could continue with adopting reach codes for new 
construction and expand outreach, education, and incentives. In the past, the City 
adopted local building code amendments to require more sustainable new 
construction than the State code. To continue to surpass State code, the City could 
consider other new construction reach codes including an energy performance or 
Zero NOx reach code. This approach has a limited impact because new construction 
accounts for a fraction of San Mateo’s development. However, this approach is 
already in place, cost saving up front and over time, and received community 
support based on our engagement efforts. There are no policy-related additional 
costs to building owners unless they voluntarily decide to electrify. A Zero NOx 
approach would save an estimated 1,770 MT CO2e per year by 2030 while a 
performance approach would save 20 MT CO2e per year by 2030. 
2.
Reach codes for renovations: This approach would include the evaluation of additional 
reach codes to encourage and/or require electrification during renovations or at time 
of replacement for select appliances (air conditioners). New reach codes would vary 
depending on the updated State codes. One example of a reach code during

Policy Option 4: Commercial Building Performance Standards 
Description 
Option 4 introduces new policy standards for commercial buildings (Building 
Performance Standards) aimed at increasing energy efficiency, and eventually, 
decarbonizing commercial buildings as a phased efficiency-focused approach. In 
comparison to relatively homogonous residential buildings, commercial buildings can be 
highly variable in their systems and needs. Commercial building heating and cooling 
systems are also frequently oversized, meaning replacing existing gas equipment in a like 
for like manner with electric equipment is not economical. Process loads also vary 
significantly across commercial building types. For these reasons, decarbonizing 
commercial buildings necessitates a flexible approach where building managers can first 
make strides towards understanding their buildings and cost-effective opportunities 
before committing to a specific approach. Typically, a BPS Program is implemented in 
phases where buildings first report energy use for several years (benchmarking), then 
perform a retro-commissioning process to identify opportunities before finally reducing 
emissions below an established threshold (performance standard). 
•
Phase I: Benchmarking (Years 1-2): This initial phase establishes a building’s energy 
performance baseline by comparing its energy use intensity (EUI) to similar buildings. 
Covered Buildings (buildings and properties with buildings totaling the specified size 
threshold or larger) report their annual energy usage in Energy Star Portfolio Manager. 
The data collected in the benchmarking phase improves building manager and City 
understanding of building energy usage and will inform GHG-emissions performance 
standards. Benchmarking also identifies underperforming facilities and prioritizes 
improvements, with studies showing benchmarked buildings achieve average annual 
energy savings of 2.4 percent.12
•
Phase II: Retro-commissioning (Years 3-4): Retro-commissioning is a process through

Cost Effectiveness and Equity Considerations 
The cost of decarbonizing buildings under a Building Performance Standard (BPS) policy 
varies significantly based on factors such as building age, equipment types, and energy 
end uses. This variability necessitates a flexible, performance-based approach, allowing 
owners to prioritize cost-effective solutions tailored to their assets. The BPS framework’s 
phased implementation mitigates financial burdens:
•
Benchmarking identifies inefficiencies (e.g., leaks, malfunctioning systems) and 
establishes a baseline for improvement. Benchmarking reporting costs approximately 
$3,000 annually.
•
Retro-commissioning pinpoints low-cost operational optimizations (e.g., HVAC 
adjustments, controls recalibration), which typically yield 16% energy savings at an 
average cost of $0.30/sq. ft., often with payback periods under two years.
•
Performance standards are deferred for 4+ years post-benchmarking, enabling 
strategic capital planning for equipment replacements and system upgrades.
Electrification of commercial building electric retrofits will likely have to be paired with a

Policy Option 5: Municipal Electric First Policy 
Description 
This policy establishes a mechanism for local government renovations to go ‘electric first’. 
This means that new appliance purchases, renovations, and replacements should 
prioritize using all-electric equipment. This policy would also allow for technical and cost 
infeasibility on a project-level when an all-electric alternative is either not available or 
deemed infeasible due to cost. The main purpose of the policy is to position the City as 
the leader in decarbonization within the community as well as prepare City facilities for 
upcoming requirements. The City would also learn best practices through this approach 
which it can share with the community and electrified city facilities would serve as 
demonstration buildings to highlight the comfort, health, and safety benefits of going all-
electric. 
Policy Pros: 
•
Demonstrates City leadership in building electrification
•
Availability of significant local government incentives to electrify through Peninsula 
Clean Energy’s Gov BE and Willdan GK12 program 
•
Existing resources available through County of San Mateo including staff report, 
model code language, and case studies
•
Prepares City for future requirements while incentives are available
•
Lower operating costs

Limited reach as policy only covers municipal buildings

buildings to convert to all-electric which could be financially infeasible. One idea

reduce their emissions compared to their own historical emissions rather than meet a 
City or building type average.
2) Converting to electric appliances also creates a possible space issue as electric rooms 
are increasingly taking up more space and old buildings may not have room for it. 
Relocating equipment due to space limitations poses increased engineering and 
financial burdens. 
3) For new construction that must meet the recent Title 24 requirements, can there be a 
vintage threshold to avoid unnecessary financial and time burden for buildings that 
are already well-performing. 
4) For landlords with complicated tenant and lease structures, landlords would need City 
support to navigate conversations with tenants and financial responsibility.

RECOMMENDATION:

BACKGROUND:
The Climate Action Plan (CAP) includes strategies and measures to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The CAP 
contains implementation measures in the following categories: building electrification, renewable energy, energy 
efficiency, municipal energy efficiency, clean transportation fuels, sustainable transportation, solid waste, water and 
wastewater, and off-road equipment. In 2023, the Climate Action Plan technical update was completed to align with the 
General Plan. The updated CAP was adopted in March 2024 in conjunction with the General Plan.
Annual Progress Report Key Highlights
To track CAP implementation, staff provides annual progress reports to the Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission 
and City Council on the status of the different strategies. Attachment 1 to this report provides both qualitative and 
quantitative data to show how progress has been made on each of the CAP measures over the 2024 calendar year.
Some key highlights of implementation of CAP implementation are as follows:

Reach Codes: In January 2025, the San Mateo City Council adopted an Energy Performance Reach Code for new 
construction that requires energy compliance margins for new buildings and repealed its unenforceable all-electric 
requirement. The City’s reach code was approved by the California Energy Commission on April 10, 2025 and will 
be effective through December 31, 2025.

Green City Fleet: City staff continue to evaluate replacement of equipment and vehicles with electric options. 
Currently, the City fleet includes 15 electric units. Installation of charging infrastructure at City facilities is needed 
to support electric fleet units. 
o
City Hall: With the support of the Peninsula Clean Energy’s Public EV Fleets program, the City is working to 
install 12 Level 2 charging ports at City Hall. The City's design was reviewed and approved by PG&E. 
Through PG&E's Electric Vehicle Infrastructure Rule 29 program, PG&E will install to-the-meter 
infrastructure at a nominal cost and the City is responsible for the behind-the-meter infrastructure, 
including the design, installation, and permitting of EV chargers. City staff plan to bid and complete project 
installation in 2025. This project is funded by PCE’s Member Agency Grant and PCE’s EV Ready Program.

Corporation Yard: In August 2024, staff initiated engagement with the PG&E EV Fleet Program to install

where the garage entrance and exit driveways cross the sidewalk. Improvements included protected 
walkways, wayfinding signage, audio and visual warning devices, and convex mirrors for better visibility

residents and businesses. The City of San Mateo continues to provide rebates for rain barrels and electric leaf 
blowers. Through these programs, community members save money while reducing their carbon footprint. Of 
note, Peninsula Clean Energy saw growth in heat pump HVAC rebate and the City issued a record number of 
electric leaf blower rebates for 2024.

Electrify San Mateo: The Electrify San Mateo effort aims to identify existing building electrification policies to help 
meet the City’s climate goals. The project team made significant progress on collecting community input on the 
different policy pathways that were reviewed by City Council in January 2024. Outreach continues in 2025 with 
community workshops and an information gathering survey.  More information on this project is provided as a 
standalone item to the Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission at this May 14, 2025 meeting.

Building Electrification Reach Codes for Existing Buildings
•
In November 2022, the City Council adopted reach codes impacting residential building upgrades 
to encourage electric-readiness and require electric equipment. These reach codes were 
effective January 1, 2023 – July 2024 and are summarized as follows:
o
Electric readiness panel capacity: Requires panel replacement and panel upgrade 
projects to include panel capacity and breaker space for future electrification of building 
systems for all residential buildings.
o
Electric-readiness outlets installed: Requires kitchen and laundry area renovations 
include the installation of an outlet for the use of future electric appliances in single 
family homes and duplexes.
o
Heat pump HVAC: Requires the installation of heat pump HVAC system when new air 
conditioning is installed ore replaced in single family homes and duplexes.
o
Gas infrastructure: Prohibits the extension of fuel gas infrastructure for new uses such 
as new fireplaces, fire pits, grills and pools in residential buildings.
o
Heat pump water heater: Requires heat pump water heater installation in addition and 
alteration projects that include water heater replacement in single family homes and 
duplexes.
•
As stated above, in July 2024, due to a federal court decision, the City suspended the enforcement 
of its reach codes that prohibit the installation of new gas infrastructure. In January 2025, the City 
Council repealed the reach codes that could no longer be enforced due to the federal court 
decision. The electric-readiness requirements for existing buildings continue to be in effect as they 
do not prohibit the installation of new gas infrastructure.

to the Caltrain station at 28th Avenue. The project scope was extended to Kyne Street in January

•
The City Council adopted the “Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance” modeled after the 
County of San Mateo’s ordinance. The ordinance requires food facilities distribute disposable 
items that are fiber-based and compostable. The ordinance went into effect in October 2022.

all 657 facilities of the requirements and had direct one-on-one contact with 67% of those

purchase of compostable single-use foodware and provided funding to 28 food facilities for

complaint and introduces the consultant team. The consultant team contacts the food facility to 
ensure they understand the Ordinance requirements and offer assistance if needed. The team will 
email the food facility three times and then attempt an on-site visit. If contact cannot be made, 
the County will try to reach the food facility. A complaint is considered "resolved" when the

manufactured small off-road engines (SORE) such as those found in leaf blowers, lawn mowers
