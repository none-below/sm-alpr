AB 650 (Papan)
•
AB 650 strengthens the state’s housing planning process by requiring HCD to develop a 
standardized reporting format for Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH) programs. It also 
moves up the timeline for determining Regional Housing Needs Allocations (RHNA) with 
Councils of Governments (COGs) to 30 months before housing element deadlines, giving 
jurisdictions 6 more months for planning. Additionally, the bill mandates that HCD provide clear, 
detailed guidance to help jurisdictions address noncompliance findings and achieve certification 
more efficiently.

Legislative Platform Background and 
Purpose 
Creates a Helpful Guide for Communicating with Various Stakeholders
•
Communicates City’s policies, priorities, and positions to the public, and to state and regional governments.
Allows the City to Have a Gameplan 
•
Allows staff and legislative advocate to quickly triage and assess legislative matters upon introduction and as they advance through the 
process.
•
In 2023-24 legislative session there were 5,564 legislative and budgetary measures introduced.
•
2,628 having some level of impact on public agencies at time of introduction.
Examples Include:
•
Drafting and resubmitting advocacy letters and modifying advocacy letters based on amendments 
•
Testifying in legislative and budgetary committees
•
Attending meetings with state legislative, regulatory and administrative agencies 
•
Negotiating amendments/ comments/ policy on legislative, regulatory or administrative actions. 
•
Seeking funding for key programs and city initiatives   
•
Meeting with stakeholders involved in the state legislative, regulatory and administrative proposal.

SB 358 (Becker)
•
This bill would eliminate the exemption in current law that permits a local agency to retain vehicle traffic impact fees 
at their current rate upon a finding that vehicular traffic trips would not generate fewer automotive trips. Instead, the 
bill requires that the vehicle traffic impact fees be reduced by 50% for projects within a half mile of an existing or 
planned transit stop if certain conditions are met. Further, the bill would specify that the housing development 
provides no more than one onsite parking space for zero- to 2-bedroom units, and 2 onsite parking spaces for 3 or 
more-bedroom units, regardless of local parking ordinances.

AB 647 (Gonzalez)
AB 647 significantly undermines local control by mandating ministerial approval of up to eight-unit 
housing projects, including on lots currently zoned for single-family use. It forces cities and counties 
to approve qualifying applications within 60 days or risk automatic approval, burdening already 
resource-constrained planning departments. The bill strips local governments of the ability to 
impose key development standards such as parking requirements, height limits, setbacks, and floor 
area ratios, even in traditionally single-family neighborhoods. By preempting local zoning and 
linking compliance to state enforcement actions, AB 647 exposes local agencies to greater legal 
risk while limiting their ability to plan growth in a way that fits community needs.

SB 79 (Weiner)
SB 79 severely limits local control by mandating high-density residential development near transit stops, 
overriding local zoning, height limits, and planning priorities. It requires cities to ministerially approve 
qualifying projects without CEQA review, regardless of neighborhood context or infrastructure constraints. 
Local governments that deny compliant projects face automatic penalties under the Housing 
Accountability Act, even in cases where legitimate local concerns exist. This bill not only imposes rigid, 
one-size-fits-all standards statewide but also dramatically increases legal and administrative risks for cities 
already working to meet housing goals.

SB 707 (Durazo)
SB 707 significantly expands Brown Act requirements for local governments, mandating two-way 
telephonic or audiovisual participation, multilingual agenda translations, and expanded definitions of 
“meetings” to include informal discussions. It imposes a new 4/5ths vote threshold for special closed 
sessions to evaluate or discipline public employees or officials, severely limiting local flexibility to 
address urgent personnel matters. Additional mandates, such as disclosing estimated fiscal impacts of 
closed session actions and stricter teleconferencing standards, will increase legal risk, administrative 
complexity, and operational costs. Collectively, these changes burden local governments with costly, 
rigid procedures that undermine effective governance.

SB 63 (Weiner)
•
SB 63 allows San Mateo County to opt into a new regional Transportation Revenue Measure District by 
July 31, 2025, subjecting all cities within the county to a regional sales tax if voters approve. While 
participation could bring significant new funding for transit services like Caltrain and other city-level 
transportation needs, it would also shift decision-making power over those revenues to a regional board, 
reducing direct local control. Separately, the bill expands San Mateo County’s authority to pursue its own 
local transportation taxes in smaller 1/8% increments, offering cities an alternative path to fund critical 
infrastructure without ceding authority to a regional entity.
o
