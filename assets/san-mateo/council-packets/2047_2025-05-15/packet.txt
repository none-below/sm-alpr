in the public and nonprofit sectors, with a deep commitment to sustainable community development, environmental justice, 
and equitable infrastructure planning. Currently, I serve as Executive Director of Canopy (canopy.org), a Palo Alto–based 
urban forestry nonprofit dedicated to growing healthy urban forests in underserved neighborhoods and advancing 
environmental equity through tree planting, education, and community engagement. I oversee a $2 million budget, a 20-
person staff, and a 14-member board of directors, and work closely with government agencies, community partners, and 
residents to implement greening initiatives that support long-term health and resilience. Previously, I was Chief 
Communications Officer at TreePeople, Los Angeles’ largest environmental nonprofit, where I led rebranding, digital 
learning, and policy communications that helped drive climate resilience and green infrastructure solutions across 
Southern California. I also served as a Peace Corps Response Volunteer in Ukraine, supporting public health and 
advocacy organizations to build local leadership and sustainability practices. Throughout my career—including senior 
leadership roles at UCLA and early experience as a journalist covering infrastructure and education—I have developed 
expertise in strategic planning, civic engagement, and translating complex policy into accessible narratives. With a 
Master’s in Public Administration and a track record of cross-sector collaboration, I am passionate about bringing 
inclusive, community-centered approaches to sustainability and infrastructure planning in San Mateo. 
I want to serve on this board/commission because:  I believe that nature-based solutions—like urban trees and green 
infrastructure—are essential, yet too often overlooked components of sustainability and climate adaptation planning. In my 
work leading Canopy, I’ve seen firsthand how strategic investments in urban forestry can reduce heat, improve public 
health, enhance biodiversity, and build community resilience. Yet despite their proven benefits, these solutions rarely have 
a seat at the table when decisions about infrastructure and land use are made. As San Mateo continues to grow and adapt 
to the challenges of climate change, we have a critical opportunity to reimagine our built environment—not just for people, 
but for the ecosystems we rely on. That means planning for wider tree wells that can sustain large, protective trees; 
installing permeable surfaces that allow us to capture and use stormwater more effectively; and integrating reflective 
materials that mitigate urban heat. I want to serve on this commission to advocate for these practical, scalable strategies 
and to help ensure that sustainability is not an afterthought, but a foundational principle in how we design the future of our 
city.
