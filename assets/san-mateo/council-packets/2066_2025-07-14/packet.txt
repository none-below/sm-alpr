BACKGROUND
In 2019, the Age Friendly Task Force was formed to 
prepare San Mateo’s application to the AARP, under 
the guidance of CAFÉ, which had a contract with the 
County to assist cities with their applications
The San Mateo Application to the AARP was submitted 
to the AARP on September 24, 2020. 
By submitting the application San Mateo committed to 
actively engage with residents and local advocates to 
make San Mateo an Age-Friendly place to live. 
The AARP required that San Mateo submit an Action 
Plan within two years of acceptance into the program.
San Mateo submitted its 2022-2025 Action Plan to 
the AARP in November 2022.

PROJECT 1 
 
SAFE WALKING ROUTES FOR SENIORS - 
AND EVERYBODY
This project aims at accelerating the repair of sidewalks that 
represent tripping and falling hazards for seniors caused by 
sidewalks that are lifted, cracked, blocked, or otherwise 
damaged. Unsafe sidewalks are a hazard and a deterrent to 
walking by residents of all ages, not just seniors.
The Age-Friendly Task Force under the leadership of Vince 
Siminitus developed a map showing where seniors are most 
likely to be walking.  
The map was provided to the Public Works Department which 
is incorporating it in its larger effort to assess and improve 
sidewalk safety in San Mateo.
  It is also being used by the Police Department in its 
enforcement of sidewalk parking restrictions.

PROJECT 4 
AGE FRIENDLY 
BUSINESS 
RECOGNITION/ 
CERTIFICATION
Businesses that adapt their services and physical 
locations to be age-friendly can expand their 
customer base, and revenue, while helping older 
adults remain active and live independently. 
Beginning in 2022, the Age Friendly Task Force, 
under the leadership of Vince Siminitus, designed this 
program, including  developing the concept, 
proposing the Age-Friendly criteria, drafting the 
application and proposing a workflow.
In 2023 the City decided to take the lead on 
implementing a pilot for this program.

❖The City developed an on-line version of the application which is 
posted it on the City's website and invites business to apply to the 
program.  The goal is to enroll an initial 20 businesses.
❖Age Friendly Coalition volunteers visit businesses, explain the 
program and invite owners to apply.  
❖A volunteer team, trained and led by Vince Siminitus, then verifies 
applicants’ compliance with the age friendly criteria. 
❖To date, 11 businesses have submitted applications and all were 
verified to meet criteria. Several more have indicated their intent 
to apply. 
❖The businesses that have achieved certification are shown on the 
City’s website.

WHERE ARE WE IN THE PROGRAM CYCLE?
The Age-Friendly Process and Program Cycle

Members of the AARP Network of Age-Friendly States and Communities
commit to an assessment process and cycle of continuous improvement,
the steps of which typically require the member community to:
1. Establish a way — such as through a commission, advisory panel or
focus groups — to include older residents in all stages of the age-friendly
planning and implementation process

2. Conduct a community needs assessment (AARP can provide survey
examples, templates and an online tool in English and Spanish)

3. Develop an action and evaluation plan based on the assessment results
4. Submit the plan for review by AARP

5. Implement and work toward the goals of the plan

6. Share solutions, successes and best practices with AARP

7. Assess the plan’s impact and submit progress reports

8. Repeat!

Safe Routes for Seniors – Project Updates
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update from Public Work staff on implementation of pedestrian improvements identified in the Age-Friendly 
San Mateo, Three-Year Action Plan 2022-2025. 
BACKGROUND:
The World Health Organization’s Age-Friendly Cities and Communities Network and AARP’s Network of Age-Friendly States 
and Communities promote local policies and programs that support aging populations. Guided by these frameworks, the 
City of San Mateo’s Age-Friendly San Mateo Three-Year Action Plan (2022–2025) identifies eight domains of livability that 
serve as a foundation for assessing and enhancing key aspects of community life, including the physical environment, 
municipal services, community activities, and social norms. 
This informational item focuses on the “Transportation” domain, specifically the prioritization of safe walking routes for 
older adults. 
In 2021, the Age-Friendly Task Force used public data to identify commonly used walking routes for older adults and 
partnered with the Public Works Department to help prioritize sidewalk repairs along those corridors. Priority routes were 
identified based on proximity to senior housing, medical facilities, shopping centers, and recreational areas. According to 
Google Maps, these mapped routes span approximately 25 miles, or 12.5% of the City’s 400-mile street network. Notably, 
more than 16% of the City’s senior population resides along these routes. 
It’s important to note that sidewalk maintenance is typically the responsibility of adjacent property owners, as outlined in 
the City of San Mateo Municipal Code. However, Public Works staff have been supporting residents by performing 
sidewalk cutting repairs, a cost-effective alternative to full sidewalk replacement, to help address trip hazards in the 
interim. 
As part of the City’s 2023 Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) project, which focuses on improving living 
conditions for low- and moderate-income residents, over 5,000 sidewalk repairs were completed in the North Shoreview 
and North Central neighborhoods. Many of these locations overlap with senior walking routes identified in the Action Plan. 
In addition, Public Works routinely implements pedestrian safety improvements as part of its annual street improvement 
projects. These enhancements include: 
• Rectangular Rapid Flashing Beacons (RRFBs): Pedestrian-activated warning devices that increase safety at crossings 
• Enhanced pedestrian crosswalks to promote increased visibility 
• ADA-compliant curb ramps

Between 2022 and 2025, Public Works completed 9 projects involving improvements on 61 street segments. Of those, 13 
segments were directly located along the senior-priority pathways identified in the Three-Year Action Plan. In total, Public 
Works completed 55 pedestrian improvements, including the features listed above. 
Looking ahead, Public Works will continue aligning pedestrian upgrades with annual roadway resurfacing projects 
wherever feasible. For the current and future improvements, there are a total of 55 street segments, of which 21 street 
segments are located directly on the senior pathways and include 138 different pedestrian improvements. 
Additionally, staff are evaluating dedicated sidewalk improvement efforts along Age-Friendly priority routes, funded 
through ongoing CDBG initiatives and the City’s annual sidewalk repair program.

SUBJECT:
General Plan 2040 and Housing Element update from Community Development Department. 
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive informational presentation from Community Development Department regarding the General Plan 2040 and 
Housing Element.
BACKGROUND:
The Community Development Department will provide an informational presentation to the Senior Advisory Commission 
on the City’s 2040 General Plan and Housing Element updates. These long-term planning documents guide land use, 
housing, transportation, and community services, and are critical to shaping the city’s future growth and livability. 
As of December 9, 2024, City Council adopted amendments to the Municipal Code expanding the scope of the Senior 
Advisory Commission. Under the revised ordinance, the Commission is now tasked with advising on citywide issues, 
programs, and projects that may affect older adults—including housing, transportation, accessibility, and public health. 
This broader advisory role aligns with the City’s Age Friendly Action Plan and ensures that senior perspectives are included 
in major planning efforts. 
Receiving updates such as this presentation allows the Commission to stay informed on city initiatives and provide 
meaningful input on policies that impact the quality of life for older residents. While the Commission is not an approval 
body for the General Plan or Housing Element, its advisory input plays an important role in supporting inclusive and age-
friendly policy development.
