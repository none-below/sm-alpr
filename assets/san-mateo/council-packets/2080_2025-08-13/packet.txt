19th Avenue / Fashion Island Blvd Multimodal Improvement Project - Informational Presentation
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an informational update about the project and provide feedback or recommendations.
BACKGROUND:
The 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal Improvement Project (Project), is a comprehensive effort to 
enhance safety, accessibility, and operational efficiency along this key east/west corridor for all roadway users, including 
bicyclists, pedestrians, and motorists. The project extends approximately 1.3 miles, from Pacific Boulevard to Mariners 
Island Boulevard.
This Project is the result of combining two previously separate initiatives: the Fashion Island Class IV Bikeway Project and 
the Norfolk Street and Fashion Island Boulevard Intersection Reconstruction Project. The bikeway component was 
originally identified as a priority improvement through the 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Smart Corridor and 
Mobility Hub Study, which recommended enhanced bicycle infrastructure to improve multimodal connectivity. Separately, 
the intersection reconstruction project was funded with the goal of addressing vehicular congestion and improving traffic 
flow at a key junction.
To maximize efficiency and deliver a more integrated solution, the two efforts have been consolidated into a single, 
corridor-wide Project. The updated Project scope and name reflect this unified approach, which seeks to fulfill the original 
multimodal, safety, and congestion relief objectives in a cohesive and coordinated manner.
This multi-agency initiative, sponsored by the City with the San Mateo County Transportation Authority (TA) as the lead 
implementation agency, has an estimated cost of $27 million, funded through various local and federal sources. 
The Project is in the initial design stage and includes the following key improvements:
•
Vehicle Congestion Relief: Significant congestion has been identified in the eastbound direction along the 19th 
Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard corridor, particularly during the afternoon peak period. In response, the City 
conducted a comprehensive corridor analysis in 2021 to evaluate potential improvements aimed at relieving 
congestion and improving traffic operations.
As part of this analysis, the Norfolk Street intersection was identified as a primary bottleneck in the eastbound 
direction. The study concluded that in order to address this constraint, the intersection would need to be 
reconfigured to provide an additional eastbound through lane to accommodate the observed vehicle demand.

While this can be partially addressed by modifying the existing median just west of the intersection to create space 
for another lane, the analysis also determined that a bottleneck would remain at the Seal Slough bridge, which 
currently provides only one eastbound lane and two westbound lanes.
To support this finding, a recently completed traffic count confirmed that eastbound traffic volumes during the 
afternoon peak are approximately 50% higher than westbound volumes in the morning, despite the eastbound 
direction having only a single travel lane. The 2021 corridor study evaluated and recommended a lane 
reconfiguration over the Seal Slough bridge—from two westbound and one eastbound lane to one westbound and 
two eastbound lanes—to better align with actual traffic patterns.
As part of this Project, the City would implement intersection modifications, including the lane configuration 
changes as well as modified signal timing at Norfolk Street, and the bridge lane reconfiguration to two eastbound 
lanes and one westbound lane. These improvements are intended to reduce corridor congestion, address long-
standing traffic concerns, and improve traffic flow during peak periods.
•
Protected Bikeway Facility: The Project includes a Class IV protected bike lane along the entire length of the 
corridor. Class IV facilities are physically separated from vehicle traffic using concrete medians, curbs, posts, or 
other barriers. These facilities improve safety and comfort by minimizing conflicts between people biking and 
motor vehicles. The corridor is identified as a medium-high priority project in the City’s Bicycle Master Plan.
A two-way protected bikeway is proposed on the south side of 19th Avenue from Pacific Boulevard to the YMCA 
parking lot, helping riders avoid SR-92 off- and on-ramp traffic on the west end of the corridor. From the YMCA to 
Mariners Island Boulevard, the design shifts to one-way protected bike lanes on each side of the street, which are 
more familiar to most road users and provide better connections to surrounding bike networks.
•
Pedestrian Enhancements: The Project includes ADA-compliant curb ramps, high-visibility and raised crosswalks, 
pedestrian-scale lighting, and protected intersections designed to improve safety and visibility while reducing 
conflicts between people walking, biking, and driving.
•
Signal Modifications: Five intersections along the corridor will be upgraded with modern signal hardware and new 
timing plans to support all modes. Two of these signals fall under Caltrans jurisdiction, including the 19th Avenue / 
Delaware Street / SR-92 Eastbound On- and Off-ramps and Fashion Island Blvd / US-101 Southbound On- and Off-
Ramps. Signal upgrades will improve operations and safety while accommodating new bike and pedestrian 
infrastructure.
•
Parking Constraints and Corridor Widening: Between Pacific Boulevard and Grant Street, the existing roadway 
width cannot accommodate all proposed uses, including protected bikeways, travel lanes, and on-street parking. 
To address this, the project proposes widening the roadway on the north side using previously unused right-of-
way, enabling multimodal improvements while maintaining parking access for adjacent users.
Currently, there are approximately 64 on-street parking spaces in this segment. The widening will allow 
preservation of about 43 spaces. As the design process continues, the project team will be reviewing the potential 
to retain additional parking spaces in this area. While the widening cost is significant, it is justified to support 
nearby businesses, residents, and other corridor users. 
On the east side of the Seal Slough Bridge, underutilized parking will be reduced from 25 to 11 spaces to 
accommodate expanded bikeways and improved intersection design in line with project goals.
•
Stormwater and Maintenance Considerations: The design also includes green infrastructure features for 
stormwater management and has been developed in coordination with Public Works maintenance staff to ensure 
long-term maintainability of new facilities.

FUNDING AND SCHEDULE
The total estimated cost of the 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal Improvement Project, including 
planning, design, environmental clearance, and construction, is around $27 million. Of this amount, the City of San Mateo 
has contributed approximately $500,000 to date, with the remaining funding anticipated to come from a combination of 
local and federal sources. 
The construction phase of the project is estimated to cost around $25 million, of which approximately $6.5 million in 
construction funding has been secured to date. This includes $3.85 million in federal funds obtained on the Project’s behalf 
by the TA. These federal funds are being used to satisfy the local match requirement typically met through City 
contributions, significantly reducing the City's financial obligation for this Project. To help close the remaining funding gap 
for construction, the City is actively working with the TA to secure additional funding through the TA’s Highway Program, 
which may provide up to 75% of the total project cost in grant funding. Even with this maximum contribution, the Project 
may still face a funding shortfall of approximately $1 million.
City staff continues to pursue all available funding sources to ensure the project remains fully funded. At the same time, it 
is critical that the City meets all deadlines and requirements associated with the federal funds already secured for the 
project. Failure to meet these deadlines could result in the loss of this funding, which would be extremely difficult to 
replace given the scale and regional significance of the project. Without these federal funds, the project would face a 
significant risk of delay or cancellation due to a substantial funding shortfall.
Given the accelerated schedule required to keep the project on track for continued funding eligibility, the project team is 
actively developing a delivery strategy that prioritizes timing and funding requirements. While the current schedule is 
achievable, it will require a highly streamlined approach. As such, the team may need to forego certain typical project 
milestones or administrative steps, such as extended design reviews or phased bidding, in order to maintain the aggressive 
timeline. Staff is coordinating closely with funding and regulatory partners to ensure that the necessary approvals and 
documentation are completed without delay.
COMMUNITY OUTREACH & STAKEHOLDER COORDINATION:
As part of the formal design phase for the 19th Avenue / Fashion Island Boulevard Multimodal Improvement Project, the 
City has planned two community meetings to gather public input and provide project updates. In addition to these 
community meetings, there will be further opportunities for input during this Commission meeting and a future City 
Council meeting, ensuring multiple avenues for community engagement throughout the project process.
The first community meeting was held on July 15, 2025, following completion of the 35% design milestone. This milestone 
represents an appropriate time to seek community feedback, as many project components have been refined since the 
planning phase, but detailed design work has not yet commenced, allowing for adjustments based on input received. This 
meeting marks the first formal opportunity for public engagement during the design phase.
To increase accessibility and participation, an online survey was launched concurrently with the community meeting and 
remained open from June 25 through July 23. Outreach to promote the meeting and survey included the distribution of 
approximately 10,000 mailers to residents and businesses within the project area. Additionally, nearly 50 A-frame posters 
were installed throughout the corridor and in surrounding neighborhoods to raise awareness at the pedestrian and 
bicyclist level. Social media messaging was also utilized to further expand outreach and encourage community 
participation.
The online survey received over 150 responses, a strong level of engagement given the survey contained 17 questions and 
required approximately 10 minutes to complete. The in-person meeting was attended by more than 50 community 
members. Feedback gathered will inform further design refinements ahead of the second community meeting.
At the July 15th community workshop, a live poll showed attendees were split between prioritizing bicycle and pedestrian 
improvements and addressing congestion relief. While many arrived with concerns about congestion, discussions around 
the proposed solutions helped address those concerns. However, there was general interest in reviewing the traffic

analysis before forming a final opinion. Many attendees expressed strong support for the bike and pedestrian 
improvements, though some questioned the proposed transition between one-way and two-way facilities at the YMCA 
parking lot intersection, preferring a location near Grant Street/Ginnever Street for better connectivity to existing bike

In the online survey, respondents slightly favored bike and pedestrian safety improvements over congestion relief. There 
was broad support for key elements of the project, including the lane reconfiguration over the Seal Slough Bridge. The

received support, though responses were more mixed compared to other elements in the survey, indicating some 
community hesitation.
In addition to community engagement, staff have coordinated with other key stakeholders to gather input on the project. 
Staff have held detailed discussions with SamTrans, as well as the Police and Fire Departments, to ensure the project 
design reflects the operational needs of each agency. Input from Police and Fire will be incorporated into the design, 
including considerations for emergency vehicle access, signal preemption, and traffic control. Similarly, feedback from 
SamTrans regarding transit operations and stop configurations will be integrated. Public Works maintenance teams have 
also reviewed the draft plans and provided input on maintenance access, street sweeping routes, and infrastructure 
durability, all of which will be factored into the final design.
Community and stakeholder engagement will continue throughout the upcoming phases of the project to ensure the 
design remains responsive to input and operational needs. A study session with the City Council is scheduled for 
September 15.

General Plan 2040 Policies and Actions 
•
Policy C 1.1: Sustainable Transportation. Reduce vehicle miles traveled (VMT) and greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from transportation by 
increasing mode share options for sustainable travel modes, such as walking, bicycling, and public transit. 
•
Policy C 1.2: Complete Streets. Apply complete streets design standards to future projects in the public right-of-way and on private property. 
Complete streets are streets designed to facilitate safe, comfortable, and efficient travel for all users regardless of age or ability or whether they 
are walking, bicycling, taking transit, or driving, and should include landscaping and shade trees as well as green streets stormwater infrastructure 
to reduce runoff and pollution. 
•
Policy C 1.3: Vision Zero. Use a safe systems approach for transportation planning, street design, operations, emergency response, and 
maintenance that proactively identifies opportunities to improve safety where conflicts between users exist to eliminate traffic fatalities and 
serious injuries in our roadways. 
•
Policy C 1.4: Prioritize Pedestrian and Bicycle Mobility Needs. Prioritize local pedestrian and bicycle projects that enhance mobility, connectivity, 
and safety when designing roadway and intersection improvements. 
•
Policy C 6.2: Circulation Improvement Plan. Maintain a transportation network that will accommodate future growth, reduce VMT per capita, 
and equitably implement complete streets. 
•
Action C 5.13: Safe Routes to Transit. Prioritize bicycle and pedestrian improvement projects that provide safe and equitable access to transit 
stops.
