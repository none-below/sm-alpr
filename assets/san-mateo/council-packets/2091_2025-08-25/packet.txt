LIBRARY BOARD OF TRUSTEES 
June 23, 2025 
 
 Meeting Minutes 
 
1. CALL TO ORDER  
Wendy McCall, Vice President of the Library Board of Trustees, called the meeting to 
order at 5:30 p.m.  President Sheila Canzian was unable to attend this meeting. 
 
2. PLEDGE OF ALLEGIANCE & ROLL CALL 
Ms. McCall led those in attendance with the Pledge of Allegiance. Executive Assistant 
Dena Gomez conducted roll call and present were Library Board Trustees Wendy 
McCall, Richard Wysong, Annie Tsai and Austin Volz, and Youth Commissioner Erin 
Finn.  Trustee Sheila Canzian had previously notified the Board that she was unable 
to attend this meeting. 
 
3. CONSENT CALENDAR ADOPTION  
A motion to approve the consent calendar was made by Ms. Tsai, seconded by Mr. 
Wysong, and the motion passed 4-0. 
 
4. PUBLIC COMMENT 
There was not any public comment. 
 
5. OLD BUSINESS 
Marina Branch Library Restoration Project Update:  City of San Mateo Public Works 
Engineering Manager Jimmie Vo presented a PowerPoint presentation on the progress 
of the Marina Branch Library Restoration Project to the Library Board.  Construction 
is expected to take place from August 2025 through January 2027.  The project 
includes foundation and structural repairs; mechanical, electrical and plumbing 
upgrades; accessibility improvements; exterior and landscaping improvements; a 
newly updated interior, and a relocated deck.  At least three weeks prior to the 
closure date, notice will be posted online and residents within a 1500 foot radius of 
the Marina Library will receive a notice in the mail.  The Board asked how deep the 
foundation piles will be due to the possibility of loud noise in the neighborhood.  Mr. 
Vo will follow up with more information on the process that will be used.  The Board 
also recommended soundproofing in the teen space.  City Librarian Rukshana Singh 
said that due to the budget, another building will not be used as a temporary library 
during the closure.  A storage locker is being considered for holds, and Ms. Singh is 
going to visit the Shoreview Recreation Center next week and explore the possibility 
that it can be a location for picking up and returning books.  Marina Library staff will 
work at Hillsdale Library or the Main Library during the closure. 
 
6. NEW BUSINESS 
A. Annual Rotation of President and Vice President Roles:  The Library Board of 
Trustees conducted their annual rotation for the president and vice president roles 
for Fiscal Year 2025-2026.  A motion was made by Mr. Volz for Ms. McCall to 
rotate into the president role and Mr. Wysong to rotate into the vice president role, 
the motion was seconded by Ms. Tsai, and the motion passed 4-0.

B. Welcome to the Newly Appointed Youth Commissioners: The Library Board of 
Trustees and Ms. Singh welcomed newly appointed Youth Commissioner Erin 
Finn.  Ms. Finn shared that she loves the Main Library and that it is one of the few 
quiet spaces that is available to her in the community.  She joined the Library 
Board as a youth commissioner to help serve the community and learn more 
about the inside workings of the library.  Newly appointed Youth Commissioner 
Oliver Baker was not present. 
 
7. REPORTS AND ANNOUNCEMENTS 
 
A.  City Librarian Rukshana Singh: 
• 
The Peninsula Library System received a Measure K grant from County 
Supervisor Jackie Speier’s office for $50,000.  The goal of the grant is to bring 
people of all ages and backgrounds together in a trusted environment where 
they can explore, learn, and connect.  The hope is that these programs will 
help seniors in particular overcome loneliness and social isolation.  An ad hoc 
committee is being formed to figure out a plan for the grant. 
• 
The library recently received a City Leadership Award from the San Mateo 
Foster City School District for the children’s team’s collaboration with the 
Education Foundation to host a Readathon event at the library.  Ms. Tsai 
added that she served on the Education Foundation for two years, and this 
year the students logged over 2 million minutes of reading during the 
Readathon and raised over $120,000. 
• 
Library staff are assessing improvements we can make to the business center 
using the additional funds from the Franklin Templeton grant.  The business 
center is busy every day with people working and studying and they would like 
to create additional seating. 
• 
There will be industrial umbrellas installed on the children’s patio.  Creating 
some shade is the first step in making the patio a more usable space. 
• 
The Library Card Design Contest has ended.  Staff voted for three winning 
designs.  Winners have been notified and will attend the reveal and prize 
ceremony on September 3.  In addition, we will have a SF Giants card design 
available within the next couple of weeks. 
• 
The oral board interviews for the Deputy City Librarian position will be held on 
July 1, and the department interviews will be a couple of weeks after that.  Ms. 
Singh hopes to have the position filled by the end of July. 
 
B. Board Members: 
• 
Mr. Wysong watched a movie about America’s redwood forests titled Giants 
Rising in the Oak Room on Saturday, June 21.  After the movie, there was a 
panel discussion with speakers from Sempervirens Fund and Citizens Climate 
Lobby, who spoke about how their work impacts our redwood forests and 
opportunities to get involved locally.  
• 
Mr. Volz is resigning from the Library Board and tonight is his last meeting.  
He will be joining the Library Foundation as a board member.  The Library 
Board of Trustees thanked Mr. Volz for his service on the Library Board.

San Mateo Public Library 
Collection Development Policy  
[Revision Adopted by the Library Board of Trustees, 05/28/2024] 
 
Purpose of the Collection Development Policy 
The collection development policy is intended to provide the community and library staff 
documentation which informs and supports the development of the library collection. This 
document will provide guidance to San Mateo Public Library staff and inform the public of 
the principles upon which the library makes decisions regarding the development and 
maintenance of the collection. While this policy will not replace the judgment of librarians, it 
serves to assist them in making decisions when choosing from available materials. 
 
Library Mission Statement  
San Mateo Public Library builds community by inspiring imagination, discovery and reading. 
San Mateo is a resilient, innovative, and inclusive community that is knowledgeable and 
empowered, embracing the future while honoring its history. 
 
Scope of the Main Library 
The role of the main library is to serve as an information and resource center where the 
public has access to an in-depth collection of up-to-date and accurate information.  The 
Library promotes on-site and remote reference, and information services to assist users in 
locating information on subjects ranging from practical questions to in-depth research.  The 
Library’s popular materials collection features current, high-interest materials, both fiction 
and non-fiction, in a variety of formats for all ages.  
 
Scope of the Branches 
The Branch Libraries provide high quality, personalized library services, programs, and books 
and materials to the neighborhoods adjacent to the Hillsdale and Marina Libraries. The 
interests and needs of the actual and potential users of the branch are continually evaluated 
so that each library has a collection reflecting the community that it serves. While each branch 
serves the basic information needs of its neighborhood, it does not duplicate the in-depth 
sources or special collections of the Main Library. Budget and space limit the branch collection 
to materials of high interest to their communities. 
Materials Selection 
Library materials are chosen to serve the information, education, cultural, and recreational 
needs of the community. The Library selects materials on a large variety of subjects and in 
different formats, responding to the wide range of ages, ethnicities, languages, educational 
backgrounds, interests, and reading skills of library patrons. Materials are available in a 
variety of formats, quantities, and titles.   
Collection development decisions are based on staff judgement and expertise, knowledge of 
what is already in the collection, and reviews in professional journals. Selection criteria 
includes but is not limited to past and anticipated public demand; contemporary significance

and timeliness; literary or artistic merit; the need for and availability of information or materials 
in the subject area; authoritativeness of the author or publisher; favorable reviews in standard 
selection sources; cost and budgetary constraints; and shelving space available in the library. 
Selections are made to build and maintain a balanced collection and to include a variety of 
viewpoints and opinions to meet community needs.  
 
Responsibility for Selection 
Ultimate responsibility for materials selection rests with the City Librarian who operates within 
the framework of policies determined by the Library Board of Trustees. Actual selection and 
collection management activities are shared among professional staff that perform this 
obligation consistent with this policy and established procedures. 
 
Suggestions for Purchase 
Library patrons are encouraged to suggest materials for the library to purchase. To do so, 
patrons should complete a ‘Suggest a Purchase’ form via the library website. Suggestions 
from the public will be evaluated by selectors based on the Library’s criteria for selection. All 
suggestions will be considered although not all suggestions may be added to the collection. 
This includes self-published works by local authors or otherwise. 
 
Donations & Gifts 
The Library welcomes the donation of library materials for its collection. The Library reserves 
the right to determine whether or not donated titles will be added to the collection. Materials 
that meet the Library’s selection criteria may be added to the collection. Donated titles that 
are not added are routed to the Friends of the Library who raise funds through their book 
sales. The revenue from the book sales is administered by the Library Foundation which 
awards grants to the Library for new materials. The Library Board also accepts monetary 
donations for the Library’s collection and to the Memorial Fund for the purchase of library 
materials in memory of or in honor of designated persons. 
  
The Collections  
The Library provides print collections in the form of circulating fiction and nonfiction books, 
large-print books, reference books, and periodicals; non-print collections composed of CDs, 
DVDs and audiobooks; and an electronic-resource collection in the form of databases, e-
books, streaming music, video and audiobooks, and various other services made available 
online. Materials in the collection are supplemented through access to the materials of the 
Peninsula Library System’s member libraries and by obtaining materials via interlibrary loan. 
Collections are developed in three major areas - for children, teens, and adults. The library 
supports the following international languages – Spanish, Chinese, Farsi and Japanese.  
 
Children’s Collections: A robust collection of print and media materials for children, items 
are provided in multiple formats for a wide range of interests, ages, and reading levels. 
Although the library strives to provide basic curriculum support, we do not attempt to 
purchase in sufficient quantity for required reading or other district-wide projects.  
 
Teen Collection: A collection of books and magazines for teens, with awareness of the 
variety of interests, backgrounds, reading skills, and development levels within this age 
group. This book collection is predominantly fiction selected for recreational reading. The 
nonfiction titles in the collection are on subjects that meet the developmental needs of teens 
as well as titles of contemporary interest to this age group.

Collection Management 
Collection maintenance and evaluation is important to ensure the materials, both print and 
digital, in the Library are balanced and responsive to community needs. The Library strives 
to maintain an up-to-date, useful collection. Statistical tools such as circulation reports help 
librarians to determine how the collection is being used, and community demographic 
information will be used to help keep abreast of changing community needs.  During the 
process of evaluation, the librarian will have the opportunity to identify subjects where 
materials are needed, missing titles that should be replaced, older editions of titles which 
should be updated, and subjects, authors, and titles that are no longer in demand in the 
community. Titles and items are withdrawn from the collection through periodic and 
systematic review by librarians. Materials may be withdrawn because they are worn, 
obsolete, or seldom used; superseded by a newer edition or better work on the subject; or 
physically damaged or in poor condition.  
 
Maintenance of the library's collection through constant re-evaluation by the library staff 
ensures its usefulness and relevancy to the community. This evaluation depends heavily on 
the staff's professional expertise in assessing the needs of the community and the content of 
the collection. Those materials determined to no longer be of value are withdrawn from the 
collection. Withdrawn items may be disposed of through book sales, giveaways, or 
recycling.

Public Library does not censor library materials of any nature for any member of the 
community. Library staff and patrons may not impose their views, values, and biases upon

San Mateo Public Library – Naming Policy 
(Draft for Board Review) 
Purpose: 
The San Mateo Public Library’s Naming Policy is intended to encourage private support 
and honor outstanding service in a manner that aligns with the Library’s mission and 
values. By offering naming opportunities for library facilities, spaces, collections, or 
programs, the Library can recognize significant financial contributions or extraordinary 
service by individuals or organizations. This policy ensures that naming decisions are 
made thoughtfully and transparently, upholding public trust and the Library’s 
commitment to intellectual freedom, inclusivity, and community service. 
Naming Criteria: 
Naming rights may be considered under the following circumstances, subject to approval 
by the Library Board of Trustees: 
• 
Significant Monetary Contribution: An individual, family, business, or foundation 
has made a major financial donation to the Library. The contribution should be a 
substantial portion of the project or facility cost or a level deemed significant by 
the Board. Specific minimum gift amounts or funding percentages will be 
determined by the Board for each naming opportunity (e.g., naming a new branch, 
room, or special collection). 
• 
Extraordinary Service or Leadership: The naming may honor individuals who have 
demonstrably served the Library or the San Mateo community with distinction. 
This could include long-time library supporters, staff, trustees, or civic leaders who 
have made exceptional contributions to education, literacy, or public service. Such 
honorees must reflect the Library’s values and have a positive reputation in the 
community. 
• 
Alignment with Library Values: All naming proposals must be consistent with the 
Library’s image and values. The proposed honoree’s character and reputation 
should be beyond reproach. The Library will not approve naming rights that could 
imply endorsement of political or religious positions, or that could in any way 
compromise the Library’s integrity, neutrality, or public trust. Corporate or 
commercial names may be considered only if the corporate entity’s primary 
mission and branding are in harmony with the public service mission of the Library. 
In general, the Library’s own name will remain preeminent – for example, a branch 
building will still be identified as a San Mateo Public Library facility even if a donor’s 
name is added to that location or space. 
17 of 21

--- page 18 ---
• 
Scope of Naming: Naming opportunities can include buildings, distinct areas or 
rooms within library facilities, outdoor spaces, specialized collections, 
furnishings or equipment, and endowed programs or positions. The Board will 
maintain an up-to-date inventory of available naming opportunities and associated 
contribution levels. Core library services or broad functions (like “Circulation Desk” 
or “Children’s Room”) will not typically carry donor names to avoid confusion in 
public service operations. The naming will normally be in the form of “Name of 
Donor/Honoree + Function/Location” (for instance, “Jane Doe Community 
Room”). 
• 
Duration of Name: Unless otherwise agreed, the naming rights will extend for the 
useful life of the facility or program that is named. Permanent naming (in 
perpetuity) is generally discouraged. If a facility is significantly renovated, replaced, 
or its use changes, the Library reserves the right to review the naming (see 
Revocation Policy below). Time-limited naming agreements (e.g., a 20-year term for 
naming a branch) may be utilized, especially for corporate donors or when a 
donation supports a defined time period of operations. 
Approval Process: 
Naming proposals undergo a standardized review and approval process to ensure 
fairness and consistency: 
1. Proposal Submission: A naming opportunity may arise from a donor’s offer or from 
a recommendation to honor someone. In either case, Library staff (or the San Mateo 
Public Library Foundation, if involved in fundraising) will prepare a written proposal. 
The proposal documents the details of the honoree/donor, the nature and amount of 
the contribution or service, and the specific space or program proposed for naming. 
It should also address how the proposal meets the criteria of this policy. 
2. Administrative Review: The City Librarian (Library Director) and designated senior 
staff will review each naming proposal. This internal review verifies that the naming 
criteria are met and that there are no conflicts of interest or other concerns. The City 
Librarian may also consult with City administration or legal counsel for guidance, 
especially if the proposal involves City-owned buildings or significant legal 
obligations. 
3. Recommendation to the Board: If the proposal passes staff review, the City 
Librarian will forward it to the Library Board of Trustees with a formal 
recommendation. The Board (or a Board committee appointed for this purpose) will

--- page 19 ---
study the proposal, ensuring due diligence on the contributor’s background and the 
long-term implications for the Library. 
4. Board Decision: The Library Board of Trustees holds authority for approval of all 
naming rights. The Board will discuss the proposal in a public meeting and vote on 
it. A majority vote is required for approval. The Board may approve the naming as 
proposed, deny it, or approve it with modifications or conditions (such as adjusting 
the duration of the naming, or requiring a plaque of a certain style). If the naming 
involves a major facility or a significant public asset, the Board’s action may be 
shared with the San Mateo City Council for information or concurrence as needed 
under City policies (the Board will coordinate any such requirements in advance). 
5. Formal Agreement and Implementation: Upon Board approval, the Library will 
enter into a Naming Agreement with the donor or honoree (or their 
representatives). This written agreement will outline the terms of the naming – 
including the exact name to be used, duration (if applicable), any specific promises 
such as endowment management or maintenance responsibilities, and reference to 
the revocation clause (see below). Once signed by all parties, the Library will 
proceed to implement the naming: ordering and installing signage or plaques 
consistent with Library design standards, updating brochures or websites, and 
planning any public announcement or dedication ceremony with the honoree. The 
Library Director will ensure that appropriate records are kept of all naming 
agreements and that the Library’s facilities maps and literature reflect the new 
naming. 
Throughout this process, transparency and community input are valued. All approved 
naming decisions will be announced publicly, and the Library welcomes community 
engagement in these significant recognitions. 
Revocation & Renaming Policy: 
The Library Board of Trustees reserves the right to revoke or revise a naming designation 
under certain circumstances. While such actions will be rare and undertaken with 
sensitivity, the Library must protect its interests and uphold its values. Grounds for 
revocation or renaming include: 
• 
Failure to Fulfill Contribution: If a naming was granted in recognition of a pledge or 
ongoing donation (for example, a multi-year pledge or an endowment) and the donor 
does not fulfill the agreed contribution, the Board may rescind the naming. The 
Naming Agreement will typically specify a time frame for the donation and that 
naming is contingent upon full receipt of funds.

--- page 20 ---
• 
Facility or Program Changes: If the named area of the Library undergoes major 
changes – such as renovation, repurposing, or demolition – the Library may decide 
to rename the space. In such cases, the Library will attempt to continue honoring 
the original honoree in another appropriate way (e.g., a plaque in a new building 
acknowledging the original name and contribution). However, the original naming 
rights for that physical space or program will not automatically transfer to a 
significantly changed or new facility without Board review. 
• 
Reevaluation for New Donors: When a named facility reaches the end of its 
“useful life” or is slated for a major renovation or reconstruction, it may become 
an opportunity for new fundraising. The Library may choose to offer new naming 
rights as part of a capital campaign. In such cases, the existing name might be 
retired to allow a new donor’s name, while still recognizing the original donor’s 
historical contribution in a appropriate manner. Any such plans will be handled with 
sensitivity and communicated to the original donor or honoree when possible. 
• 
Expiration of Term: For time-limited naming agreements, the naming designation 
will sunset at the end of the agreed term unless renewed. The Board may choose 
to offer the naming opportunity to a new donor at that time, especially if additional 
funding is needed for renovations or operations. The Library will, when feasible, 
inform the original donor/honoree as the end of term approaches and acknowledge 
their contribution in an appropriate manner after the name is removed. 
• 
Changed Circumstances / Reputational Concerns: If the individual or 
organization for whom a space is named falls into disrepute or acts in conflict 
with the Library’s mission, the Library Board has the authority to remove the name. 
This could involve situations where new information comes to light about the 
honoree that would have made them ineligible under the naming criteria (e.g., 
involvement in illegal activities or actions contrary to the Library’s ethical 
standards). The Board would carefully deliberate in consultation with legal counsel 
before taking such action. 
• 
Other Extraordinary Situations: In rare cases, other factors may necessitate a 
change. For example, if a broader facility reorganization or a merger of library 
systems leads to rebranding, the Board will strive to honor existing naming 
agreements but may find it necessary to alter them in dialogue with donors. 
Process for Revocation or Renaming: 
When considering the removal or change of an existing name, the Board will follow a 
careful procedure:

• 
Evaluation: The City Librarian will present a report to the Board detailing the 
situation and recommending a course of action. This report will reference the 
original naming agreement and state the reasons a change is being considered (e.g., 
non-payment, facility closure, etc.). 
• 
Notice to Honoree/Donor: If appropriate, the Library will notify the original honoree 
or donor (or their surviving family/contacts) about the situation. They may be given 
an opportunity to respond or remedy the issue (for instance, to complete a donation 
if still possible). 
• 
Board Decision: The Board will review the case and may hold a vote to revoke or 
change the naming. Revocation of a naming rights agreement requires a majority 
vote of the Board. The decision will be recorded in the Board’s official minutes. 
• 
Follow-Through: The Library will remove signage, plaques, or references to the 
name in a manner that seeks to minimize disruption. If appropriate, an explanatory 
plaque or notice may be placed temporarily to inform the public of the change 
(especially if the naming was highly visible). The Library will also update all materials 
(directories, websites, etc.) to reflect the change. 
• 
In cases of revocation, the Library may choose to offer alternate recognition 
(especially if the revocation was due to factors like facility closure rather than any 
fault of the honoree). For example, the Library might install a plaque in a lobby 
honoring past donors and include the removed name there, to continue 
acknowledging their contribution historically. The overriding principle is to balance 
gratitude to contributors with the Library’s duty to uphold its mission and public

All naming agreements shall include language that clearly establishes the Board’s right to 
revoke naming rights in these circumstances. In the event of a revoked naming, the Library 
generally will not return any donated funds already expended for their intended purpose. 
The Board may, at its discretion, consider alternative recognition for the original donor if a 
name is removed (for example, inclusion on an honor wall or other commemorative 
display). 
 
This Naming Policy is adopted by the Library Board of Trustees to guide recognition of 
donors and distinguished individuals. It shall be reviewed periodically (at least every five 
years) and updated as needed to remain consistent with the Library’s goals and community 
expectations.
