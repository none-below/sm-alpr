BACKGROUND:
The City/County Association of Governments of San Mateo County (C/CAG) and the San Mateo County Transportation 
Authority (SMCTA) are working together to update the Countywide Transportation Plan (CTP), which was previously 
developed ten years ago (2015-2017). The CTP creates a vision for the future of transportation in San Mateo County, sets 
priorities, and guides decision-making at the SMCTA and C/CAG as they plan, fund, and help deliver local and regional 
transportation improvements. With this update, the two agencies are focused on the following outcomes: 
•
Anticipate Changes: Identify pilot programs to adapt to changing policies, technologies, and travel patterns. 
•
Advance Equity: Establish a shared definition of equity among county agencies and set expectations for how the 
definition will be used in transportation funding decisions. 
•
Measure Progress: Select a short list of metrics to gauge the "health" of our transportation system and commit to 
measuring and transparently reporting progress. 
•
Strategize Funding: Position San Mateo County to leverage funding at the local, regional, state, and federal level. 
•
Create a Practical Plan for our Partners: Present the CTP update in a highly usable format and develop webtools to 
make planning easier for our city, town, county, community organization, and agency partners. 
PLANNING EFFORT 
The CTP update kicked off in Summer 2025 with a virtual working session that assembled staff from C/CAG, SMCTA, and 
the Metropolitan Transportation Commission (MTC) to share their goals for the CTP update. The consultant team spent 
the summer months conducting a review of existing transportation conditions, policies, and programs in San Mateo 
County including a review of community feedback collected as part of 15 recent countywide planning efforts. Staff and 
community input as well as observed conditions were used to prepare the blueprint deck being shared today and to lay 
the foundation for the upcoming CTP tasks: developing updated vision, goals, and policies followed by updated strategies 
and actions and a call for projects. 
The CTP offers a 10,000-foot view of what is currently happening and what we want to be happening in the county in the 
future. It is supplemented by agency, mode, and topic-specific plans like the Caltrain Business Plan, the Comprehensive 
Bicycle and Pedestrian Plan, and the Countywide Local Road Safety Plan. This update will continue to serve as a guide 
rather than a highly detailed or overly prescriptive planning document. Critically, it will offer direction for more detailed 
plans or local city and town efforts as they respond to a changing transportation landscape. For instance, using the three

walking or biking, and enhancing safety for all modes of travel. Other types of influences identified for consideration 
including how transportation systems adapt to emerging climate threats like extreme heat and flooding, the shifting 
funding landscape, or the reality of an older and aging population in San Mateo County. 
The CTP will tackle these emerging topics and others in the next phase of work. At this juncture, C/CAG and SMCTA are 
collecting input on what those existing and emerging challenges are, where C/CAG, SMCTA, and other county or regional 
agencies could provide more technical assistance, and where current planning processes are or are not working. As is 
always the case, there are more project ideas and program requests than there are financial or staff resources to support, 
and the CTP is the opportunity to identify where to focus money and energy over the next couple decades. Where do we 
want to be in 2050 and what do we need to do today to get there? 
A presentation will be made by a staff representative from SMCTA and/or C/CAG to provide a progress report on the plan 
and hear Commission and community concerns on multiple topics that will guide the development of the CTP. 
No action is required by the Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission, although feedback is encouraged.

improvements, with people wanting to use Caltrain (63% preferred vs. 38% current) and bus
(35% preferred vs. 17% current) if conditions improved. Slide 23 reveals that safety concerns
are the primary barrier to active transportation. | recommend prioritizing pedestrian & bike
infrastructure improvements within 0.5 mile of every Caltrain station and major SamTrans stops
(ECR) before other investments. The feedback on slide 23 specifically calls for "low-stress bike
and walk facilities on major corridors" and "intersection improvements."

2. Quantifiable Mode Shift Targets Slide 14 shows the baseline: 70.2% drive alone, 3.5% ride
transit, 0.4% bike. The plan does not include specific mode shift targets when it could and
should: e.g., "reduce drive-alone commuting from 70% to 50% by 2035, increase transit to 15%,
biking to 5%." This creates accountability and a framework for project prioritization. Without
measurable goals, the outcomes listed on slide 10 could better be called "aspirations"

3. Address the El Camino Real Paradox Slide 17 identifies El Camino Real as a congestion
concern while slide 23 shows it's a desired location for "low-stress bike and walk facilities." |
recommend the plan explicitly prioritize complete street transformation of ECR segments,
starting with Grand Boulevard Initiative areas (referenced on slide 25). This single intervention
would have dramatic positive effects for tens of thousands of daily trips in the county.

4. Transit Frequency as Non-Negotiable Slide 21 clearly shows people want "more frequent
and reliable service" and "more affordable fare options." Rather than treating this as
aspirational, the plan should identify dedicated funding mechanisms to improve upon SamTrans
ECR route's 15-minute frequencies. Slide 26 acknowledges the "transit funding deficit"—the
plan must address how to close this gap rather than simply noting it exists.

5. Backbone Bike Network Acceleration The backbone bike network shown on slide 22
represents essential infrastructure connecting the entire county. This network should have an
explicit completion timeline (e.g., 50% by 2030, 100% by 2035) with identified funding sources.

6. Equity in Project Prioritization While slide 10 lists equity as a key outcome, the plan should
establish explicit criteria for how the C/CAG Equity Framework (slide 12) will influence project
selection and funding allocation—particularly for communities with high transit dependency and
those identified as underserved.

7. Leverage Existing Momentum Slide 7-8 showcase recent successes (Caltrain
electrification, 101 Express Lanes, active transportation projects). The plan should analyze what
specifically made these projects be deemed successful in order to replicate successes for the
next generation of infrastructure.

BACKGROUND:
History
The City of San Mateo (City) encompasses seven major artificial and natural drainage areas, including: (1) North Shoreview 
District; (2) San Mateo Creek; (3) East 3rd Avenue; (4) 16th Avenue Drain; (5) 19th Avenue Drain; (6) Laurel Creek; and (7) 
direct drainage to Marina Lagoon, see Attachment 1. The latter four drainage areas discharge to Marina Lagoon, a large 
storage and pumping facility created from Seal Slough.
Major sources of flood risk within the City include: (1) coastal flooding associated with high tides in San Francisco Bay and 
(2) interior flooding resulting from stormwater runoff during significant storm events. Prior to the most recent New Year’s 
Eve 2022 storm event, historic storms with documented serious flooding occurred in December 1950, April 1958, January 
1982, January 1983, February 1986, and February 1998.
To address stormwater conveyance and flood risk, the City’s first comprehensive Storm Drain Master Plan was published in 
1966. This plan provided recommendations for major drainage facilities within the City, while the design of local storm 
drains was placed under the purview of developers as land was improved within the watershed area. In the decades 
following publication of the 1966 plan, developers and the City’s Public Works Department made incremental 
improvements to the storm drain system. However, environmental laws enacted in the late 1980s and early 1990s shifted 
stormwater management objectives from a sole focus on flood control to also include pollution prevention. Within this 
evolving regulatory framework, the City developed the most recent Storm Drain Master Plan in 2004.
The 2004 Storm Drain Master Plan included an evaluation of the City’s storm drainage system as a whole, including pipes, 
channels, and pump stations, as well as recommendations for capital improvements and maintenance. While several 
recommended projects were constructed, primarily focused on levee improvements and coastal flood risk reduction, the 
City’s efforts related to storm drain infrastructure upgrades and interior flooding risk were largely limited to maintenance 
and repair of the existing system due to a lack of dedicated funding.
During the New Year’s Eve 2022 storm event, more than 4.5 inches of precipitation fell over a 12-hour period, representing 
an approximately 200-year storm event with a 1-in-200 (0.5 %) annual chance of occurrence. As a result, the City’s 
75 of 131

stormwater conveyance system was overwhelmed, which included overtopping of San Mateo Creek and flooding in 
multiple neighborhoods. In addition, mechanical failures at the Marina Lagoon Pump Station prevented pumps from 
activating as intended, contributing to additional interior flooding around the Marina Lagoon and in upstream drainage 
areas.
The New Year’s Eve 2022 storm marked the beginning of an unprecedented series of nine atmospheric rivers and a “bomb 
cyclone” occurring between late December 2022 and March 2023, which resulted in states of emergency being declared 
by both the Governor and the President.
Stormwater Fee
Prior to the 2022–2023 storm events, the City was already evaluating funding options to finance flood control capital 
improvements, including upgrades to the storm drain infrastructure. A draft Stormwater Funding Analysis Report was 
published in January 2021 and proposed establishing an enterprise fund supported by a user fee. Implementation of the 
enterprise fund would be subject to voter approval requirements under Proposition 218.
In November 2021, a survey was mailed to property owners to gauge support for a stormwater fee. The survey achieved a 
16.6% response rate and indicated neutral support for a $120 per year fee for flood protection and stormwater 
infrastructure purposes. A follow-up survey mailed in late March 2023 showed increased support, with more than 50% of 
respondents favoring an average fee of $96 per year for flood protection and stormwater infrastructure purposes.
In November 2023, property owners were sent ballots to vote on the Community Flood and Storm Protection Fee (Storm 
Fee), with 51.78% of returned ballots supporting adoption of the fee. The City Council formally adopted the Storm Fee on 
January 16, 2024, and directed levy of the fee beginning in Fiscal Year 2024/25 on County tax rolls. Based on a median per-
parcel contribution of $96 per year, the Storm Fee is expected to generate approximately $4 million annually for 
stormwater maintenance and capital improvements. This new revenue stream has enabled the City to make 
advancements in strengthening its stormwater program, most notably by initiating the reevaluation and update of the 
City’s outdated 2004 Stormwater Master Plan and by increasing its preventative maintenance efforts. 
Project Initiation and Scope
In January 2024, the City issued a Request for Proposals (RFP) for a Storm Drain Master Plan Update. Two consultants 
submitted proposals, and Schaaf & Wheeler (S&W) was ultimately selected to perform the Storm Drain Master Plan 
Update scope.
The full scope of the Storm Drain Master Plan Update (Master Plan Update) was awarded to S&W on June 17, 2024, with 
the primary objective of developing a prioritized list of stormwater capital improvement projects (CIPs) to extend system 
serviceability for future generations in San Mateo.
In preparation for developing the prioritized project list, S&W was tasked with conducting a condition assessment of 
existing infrastructure and available data, developing a stormwater and climate model, and establishing specific criteria for 
project ranking. These components are discussed in further detail in this staff report.
Condition Assessment and Data Acquisition
As part of the Master Plan Update, S&W reviewed readily available land use, topographic, geologic, geographic, and storm 
drain system data. These data were used to develop the following:
•
A Citywide digital elevation model (DEM) utilizing 2017 San Mateo County LiDAR (light detection and ranging) data, 
2022 LiDAR data in the vicinity of the Coyote Pump Station, and 2024 Marina Lagoon bathymetric data. The DEM 
was used to develop hydrologic and hydraulic models.
•
Topographic models of major storage facilities, including the two dams at the upstream end of Laurel Creek, the 
regional Bay Meadows detention pond, and the Poplar Pump Station forebay.

Model Development and Capacity Evaluation
A rainfall-runoff model was developed for the City to represent the storm drainage pipe system, open channels, ponds and 
lagoons, and pump stations that provide essential conveyance and storage capacity for stormwater runoff. The modeled 
existing system includes approximately 8,500 nodes, more than 148 miles of pipes, and 11.2 miles of open channels, 
including creeks, ditches, and Marina Lagoon.
The model utilizes a range of storm durations and storm events, which are used to estimate runoff and evaluate system 
performance within the stormwater network. Evaluation of the system performance is based on either a 10- or 100-year 
level-of-service standard, depending upon the criticality of the stormwater asset. A 10-year storm event has a 10 percent 
annual probability of occurrence, and a 100-year event has a one percent annual probability of occurrence. Level-of-
service standards are used to determine when capital improvement projects (CIPs) may be necessary to correct system 
capacity deficiencies. 
A 10-year storm was used as the level-of-service standard for evaluating the gravity pipe systems and smaller pump 
stations, consistent with best practices as well as neighboring jurisdictions. Attachment 2 shows the extent of flooding 
under existing conditions for a 10-year storm event. A 100-year storm event was used as the level-of-service standard for 
evaluating the Marina Lagoon, creeks, and major pump stations. Other storm events that were evaluated but were not 
used as a level-of-service standard included the 2- and 25-year storms. 
City staff also identified 33 known, recurring stormwater system deficiencies based on field observations and resident 
reports. The hydraulic models were used to evaluate the causes of these deficiencies and to identify additional areas likely 
to experience significant flooding during design storm events.
CIP Development
The development of CIPs focused primarily on addressing the most severe and frequently recurring flooding issues 
identified by City staff and confirmed by the stormwater models. These locations are characterized by documented 
flooding even during small storms, and model results indicate that high-intensity rainfall during a 10-year event is likely to 
cause system overflows, creating risks such as property damage, prolonged disruption to traffic and emergency services, 
and potential threats to life safety. The model results for the design storm were used to evaluate system deficiencies and, 
in combination with established CIP criteria, to prioritize projects.

The 2018 Marina Lagoon sedimentation assessment evaluated changes in lagoon bathymetry relative to a 1996 survey at 
both winter and summer operating levels. For this analysis, updated bathymetric data collected as part of the Stormwater 
Master Plan Update scope was compared with the 1996 baseline. The comparison indicates that the majority of sediment 
accumulation has occurred below the lagoon’s usable flood storage elevation during storm events, with limited 
encroachment into the portion of the lagoon relied upon for flood management. Based on observed sedimentation rates 
and current distribution, continued accumulation at similar levels would result in an estimated approximately 1 percent 
reduction in usable flood storage over a 10-year period. 
While sedimentation patterns may evolve over time, current data does not indicate an immediate risk to the lagoon’s 
usable flood storage or to the operational performance of the Marina Lagoon Pump Station. Staff will continue to monitor 
sediment conditions as part of ongoing system evaluation.

Evaluation of Climate Change Impacts
Climate change considerations for stormwater improvements in San Mateo fall into two distinct but interconnected 
categories: increased precipitation resulting from changing climate patterns and sea level rise affecting coastal reaches. 
These considerations are reviewed as part of the Stormwater Master Plan Update to help ensure that proposed projects 
remain resilient and adaptable. 
The storm drain model evaluates two climate change scenarios:
•
2050 Scenario: 10-year storm depth increased uniformly by 10%, with 100-year tide elevated by 0.8 ft 
•
2100 Scenario: 10-year storm depth increased uniformly by 30%, with 100-year tide elevated by 3.1 ft 
The City’s stormwater system model uses a combined analysis that accounts for potential climate change impacts. This 
includes the Environmental Protection Agency’s Stormwater Management Model Climate Adjustment Tool, which provides 
location-specific factors for 2035 and 2060 based on global climate models, and CalAdapt, which offers additional 
estimates of future climate impacts in California, including extreme precipitation events.
Sea level rise estimates are consistent with the California Ocean Protection Council’s (OPC’s) “Intermediate” sea level rise 
scenarios. A high-level shoreline feature susceptibility evaluation was additionally performed using the OPC’s “High” sea 
level rise scenario, which corresponds to 1.2 ft and 6.6 ft of sea level rise by 2050 and 2100, respectively. 
The existing conditions model has been run for the 2050 climate change conditions, while the CIP model, which assumes 
recommended projects are completed, was run for both the 2050 and 2100 climate change conditions. In other words, the 
climate change model was not used to increase the size of drainage network elements for the baseline recommended 
projects. Instead, it was applied to highlight locations where the Capital Improvement Program (CIP) may require 
additional climate change considerations during design and to identify areas where future projects may be needed as the 
CIP is updated. The model identifies several locations at risk for additional, significant flooding due to the impacts of 
climate change. 
As an order-of-magnitude estimate, incorporating climate change considerations for interior flooding projects could add 
approximately $8 million in project value to modified existing projects and $26 million in construction of entirely new 
projects, with a total cost of $34 million. New projects include improvements and expansions of existing culverts and 
crossings at the 16th Avenue Channel and Laurel Creek, upgrades to levees along San Mateo Creek, and a variety of gravity 
system capacity enhancements through the installation of new piping in several areas of San Mateo, specifically along 9th 
Avenue, 19th Avenue, Dore Avenue, and Pacific Avenue. Table 3 shows the combined total CIP cost for existing and climate 
change conditions.

Adaptation Strategies
The full scope of a sea level rise vulnerability assessment, including evaluation of the City’s vulnerability to rising shallow 
groundwater or development of comprehensive coastal protection measures, including erosion control, shoreline 
armoring, flood walls, or levees, are beyond the scope of the Stormwater Master Plan Update. However, the City is 
separately developing a Climate Adaptation Plan which meets the San Francisco Bay Conservation and Development 
Commission (BCDC) Regional Shoreline Adaptation Plan (RSAP) Guidelines. This Climate Adaptation Plan will further 
evaluate the City’s vulnerability to climate change and will help to identify adaptation strategies. The findings from the 
Master Plan Update will be used to inform the Climate Adaptation Plan. 
In addition, the City is a member jurisdiction of the San Mateo County Flood and Sea Level Rise Resiliency District 
(OneShoreline), established in 2020 to protect residents and businesses from the impacts of sea level rise. OneShoreline is 
currently evaluating projects that would provide resilient mitigation measures to address sea level rise. Currently, 
OneShoreline is developing countywide guidance to support climate-resilient public infrastructure planning and design.
Key Master Plan Findings
1.
Gravity Pipe Condition Improvement Projects
The CCTV inspections revealed that while most of the observed pipes were found to be in good condition, 
approximately 28% of the inspected system is rated in poor structural condition (rating 4–5) and will generally 
require immediate attention, and in most cases, replacement. If the CCTV inspections are taken as a 
representative sample, the City’s metal pipes, which make up approximately 2-3% of the system, are beginning to 
corrode and fail with age, and should be prioritized for repair or replacement. Approximately 61% of the City’s 
storm drain system has an unknown material type, so the actual length of metal pipes is unknown. Additional field 
verification is necessary to understand the full scope of system repair and replacement needs. 
2.
Marina Lagoon Pump Station Improvements
In recent years, staff has implemented maintenance improvements at the Marina Lagoon Pump Station to help 
extend its useful life. While the pump station’s reliability can be sustained for additional years through routine 
maintenance, the facility is nearing the end of its service life and continues to experience periodic equipment 
failures, with several major components having required repair or replacement. In addition, though the Marina 
Lagoon Pump Station can accommodate a 100-year design storm, its capacity is not sufficient to address 100-year 
tide levels or 2050 and 2100 sea level rise scenarios. 
The City is currently undertaking, separate from the Master Plan Update, a physical modeling assessment for 
Marina Lagoon which will help to evaluate performance at low lagoon levels.
3.
Pump Station Capacity
Most pump stations currently meet their assigned design storm criteria. When considering climate change 
impacts, the existing pump stations remain sufficient to handle increased precipitation only if the associated pump 
station priority projects are fully implemented, including the long-term upgrades to the Marina Lagoon Pump 
Station, which will add two additional pumps to increase capacity. Furthermore, an additional tide gate and pump 
station may need to be constructed at the mouth of San Mateo Creek to control tidal influences.

Climate Change Effects
Focusing on projects within the City’s direct control, the climate change-related impacts for San Mateo are 
generally smaller in scale compared to overall system stormwater deficiencies. Estimated costs for climate change 
resiliency projects are approximately $33 million (not including Marina Lagoon Pump Station upgrades), compared 
with roughly $232 million for full non-climate system improvements. With careful planning and pursuit of outside 
funding opportunities, the City can make meaningful improvements to enhance climate change resilience.
However, there are climate change issues beyond the intended scope of this Stormwater Master Plan update that 
will require further evaluation and analysis. Because climate change impacts occur at a regional scale and do not 
align with individual city boundaries, multi-agency coordination is necessary to adequately evaluate impacts and 
identify appropriate improvements. Among the regional-level challenges, the two most prominent relate to 
shoreline protection and channel protection along San Mateo Creek. Both issues would require large-scale system 
improvements, potentially in the form of levee reconstruction along coastal reaches, low-lying interior areas, and 
levees along the entire stretch of San Mateo Creek. 
Given the scale of these potential improvements, and the limited scope of the Stormwater Master Plan update, 
which is intended to focus on more localized improvements within available resource, further multi-agency 
coordination and study will be necessary to address these issues effectively.

Funding:
The Storm Fee generates approximately $4.0 million in annual revenue for stormwater-related activities. Currently, the 
City has $600,000 budgeted in annual operation and maintenance costs, but this is forecasted to increase with increased 
vegetation and overall drainage system management needs.  On an annual basis, the remainder would be available for 
potential capital improvement projects. 
Given the scale of improvements identified in the Master Plan Update, the current stormwater fee is insufficient to fully 
fund the recommended projects within the proposed implementation timeframe, which advises for completion of “Very 
High” priority projects within 10 years. This funding gap is driven primarily by the significant capital investment required 
for the long-term investment in upgrading the Marina Lagoon Pump Station, which represents a cost of approximately 
$125 million.

Public Engagement
In December 2025, during meetings with the Marina Lagoon Ad Hoc Committee and as part of the annual Wet Weather 
Update presented to the City Council, staff highlighted ongoing community outreach efforts associated with the 
Stormwater Master Plan. As part of these efforts, staff has developed an interactive reporting map tool for public use. The 
mapping tool provides users with the ability to report past flooding events in and around their neighborhoods and 
citywide. It also allows users to upload photos and short videos of rainfall events. This tool is not intended for reporting 
flood emergencies. 
The data and information uploaded by community members allow the design team to examine past flooding events and 
validate the storm model for accuracy. Furthermore, the tool can provide crucial information about flooding areas that are 
not captured in the stormwater model, which may indicate a need for, or an opportunity to explore maintenance 
improvements that were previously unknown.
Staff anticipates releasing the tool by the end of January and plan to keep it live for several months in support of the 
Master Plan Update. Staff will consider the option of keeping the tool live beyond this period, to be used on an ongoing 
basis. 
The Stormwater Master Plan draft is anticipated to be available for public review and comment in late February. 
Community feedback is an important part of the planning process, and residents are encouraged to provide public 
comments to help inform final refinements and ensure the plan reflects local priorities and concerns.
Public Works staff will host two community meetings in Spring 2026 to present and discuss the draft updated Stormwater 
Master Plan. These meetings are intended to provide community members with an opportunity to learn more about the 
City’s stormwater system, understand planned improvements and priorities, and ask questions directly to City staff. 
Community input will help inform implementation of future stormwater projects and programs.

Key Findings
1. CCTV inspections show most pipes are in good condition, but additional field 
verification may be necessary to understand the full scope of system repair and 
replacement needs.  
2. Although recent maintenance has extended the Marina Lagoon Pump Station’s life, it 
is nearing the end of its service life with insufficient capacity for future tide and sea 
level rise conditions.
3. Most pump stations meet current design standards, but accommodating future 
climate impacts will require full implementation of priority upgrades, including added 
pumps at Marina Lagoon and potentially a new tide gate and pump station at San 
Mateo Creek.
