SUBJECT:
Humboldt Bike Lanes Alternative - Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive a presentation from staff on efforts to explore potential alternatives to a portion of the Humboldt Street bike 
lanes and provide direction/recommendation for City Council consideration.
BACKGROUND:
IIn 2020, the City Council adopted the Bicycle Master Plan (BMP), which identifies projects and programs organized around 
five goals: Connectivity, Safety and Comfort, Community, Equity, and Ridership. All BMP projects were subsequently 
prioritized, resulting in more than 100 planned improvements. The Humboldt Street bike lane segment ranked among the 
top three priorities citywide. The following timeline summarizes how the Humboldt Street bike lanes were constructed, 
community response to the project, and how it led to additional outreach looking into the feasibility of an alternative 
route:
•
July 2020 – The City received FY 2020–21 federal Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) funding from the 
U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development. A portion of these funds was allocated for street paving and 
bicycle/pedestrian infrastructure improvements titled the North Central Bike Lanes Project (Project). 
•
July 2021 – Design completed for the North Central Bike Lanes Project.
•
November 2021 – Construction began on the paving and pedestrian improvements portion of the North Central 
Bike Lanes Project.
•
February 2022 – City Council authorized installation of the bike lanes on Humboldt Street as part of the North 
Central Bike Lanes Project. The Project included, but was not limited to, approximately 1.3 miles of Class II bicycle 
lanes on Humboldt Street between 5th Avenue and Peninsula Avenue and required the removal of approximately 
170 parking spaces on Humboldt Street.
•
July 2022 – Construction of the North Central Bike Lanes Project was completed.
•
August 2022 – March 2023 – Residents reported that the parking loss associated with the new bike lanes was 
having a significant negative impact on the North Central neighborhood, especially those residents living directly 
on Humboldt Street.
•
May 2023 – City Council appropriated funds for a new initiative, the North Central Complete Streets Plan, to 
address circulation and parking impacts resulting from the North Central Bike Lanes Project.
•
June 2023 – May 2024 – Through outreach for the North Central Complete Streets Plan and other community 
contacts, staff continued to receive feedback highlighting ongoing parking loss impacts caused by the 2022 bike 
lane installation on Humboldt Street.
At the June 17, 2024, City Council meeting, residents reiterated on-going concerns about difficulty finding parking in the 
North Central neighborhood that was exacerbated by the Project. City Council directed staff to begin investigating

additional ways to address community concerns. In response, staff hosted two workshops:  In October 2024, staff met with 
the smaller North Central Lived Experience Group, which was formed as part of the North Central Complete Streets Plan 
(NCCSP), to discuss the bike lanes on Humboldt Street and explore potential alternatives that might help alleviate impacts. 
In December 2024, staff held a larger community workshop at the Martin Luther King Jr. Center, where more than 110 
residents attended in person to provide feedback on the bike lanes on Humboldt Street, post installation.
PHASE I DIRECTION FROM CITY COUNCIL
On February 3, 2025, the City Council held a Study Session where staff presented the feedback from the October and 
December 2024 meetings as well as the NCCSP and sought Council direction on how to address community impacts 
resulting from the Project. Staff presented the five (5) following (paraphrased) alternatives for Council consideration: 
•
Alternative #1 - Keep As-Is: Retains the Class II bike lanes on Humboldt Street and do not restore any parking. 
•
Alternative #2 - Bicycle Boulevard on Humboldt Street: Removal of the existing Class II bike lanes on Humboldt 
Street, replacement with a bike boulevard with level 2 and 3 traffic calming devices, and restoration of 
approximately 170 parking spaces on Humboldt Street from Peninsula Avenue to 5th Avenue. 
•
Alternative #3 - Partial Removal of Bike Lanes, and Establishment of a Bike Boulevard on a Parallel Street: This 
alternative was broken down into a short-term plan and a long-term plan.
o
Humboldt Street Parking Restoration (2nd Avenue to Indian Avenue Only): In the short-term restore 
approximately 100 parking spaces on Humboldt Street from 2nd Avenue to Indian Avenue (and establish a 
north-south street parallel to Humboldt Street as a pilot bike boulevard between 2nd Avenue and Indian 
Avenue. 
o
Pilot Parallel Bike Boulevard: Implementation of a pilot bike boulevard on a different street (Idaho Street 
or Fremont Street) after community vetting. 
o
Long-Term Plan: In the long-term perform a connectivity study in the North Central neighborhood to 
reanalyze destinations within the community and see how to improve bike connectivity north of Indian 
Avenue and south of 2nd Avenue and further within the North Central neighborhood. 
•
Alternative #4 - Restore Parking with No Bike Infrastructure: Remove bike lanes and restore the approximately 
170 parking spaces on Humboldt Street from Peninsula Avenue to 5th Avenue. 
•
Alternative #5 - Streetlighting Upgrades: Upgrade existing cobrahead streetlight fixtures to brighter fixtures to 
help address community concerns about low lighting while assessing negative effects of additional light on 
residential properties. 
City Council directed staff to further explore the feasibility and community support surrounding Alternatives 3 and 5. In 
particular, the City Council was interested in piloting the streetlighting upgrades associated with Alternative 5, and they 
were interested in engaging the community further, especially the residents who live on Fremont and Idaho Street, to 
understand whether the community was in support of Alternative 3. 
PHASE II ENGAGEMENT
Phase II evaluated the feasibility of Alternatives 3 and 5 through an extensive and inclusive community engagement 
process. The strategy centered on three public workshops and one pilot streetlighting demonstration, all designed to 
gather direct public input. To ensure broad participation, staff implemented a multi-faceted outreach campaign that 
included:
•
Partnering with trusted local networks to amplify awareness
•
Mailing over 35,000 notices to residents and stakeholders
•
Distributing more than 1,000 flyers along the corridor and at City facilities
•
Conducting four citywide surveys

This inclusive, multilingual, and multi-channel approach ensured meaningful participation from a broad cross-section of 
the community throughout Phase II. Below is a summary of each of the three workshops and the streetlighting pilot 
efforts. 
•
Workshop No. 1 - Initial Feedback 
The first community workshop focused on gathering input on current travel patterns and experiences along 
Humboldt Street, Idaho Street, and Fremont Street. A total of 56 participants signed-in at the registration table, 
with several additional attendees who did not sign in but completed surveys. The session began with a staff 
presentation outlining the project goals and City Council direction, followed by facilitated small-group discussions 
that followed a structured set of questions to understand the impacts of the existing Humboldt Street bike lanes 
and current usage of the three corridors.  Discussion groups reported key findings back to identify common 
themes.
Summary of Meeting: Overall, the first community workshop was energetic and well attended, with robust 
participation throughout the evening with approximately 60 attendees. A total of 58 in-person surveys were 
completed, and 155 post-meeting online surveys were received, resulting in 213 responses overall. Participants 
repeatedly emphasized that the removal of on-street parking to accommodate the existing Humboldt Street bike 
lanes has created a significant burden on residents and surrounding neighborhoods. Streetlighting improvements 
along the Humboldt corridor were very well received, with widespread agreement that upgraded lighting would 
substantially enhance safety and visibility for all users. There was no clear consensus on whether Idaho Street or 
Fremont Street would be the preferred location for a bike boulevard. Strong concerns were raised about 
implementing a bike boulevard on either street due to their narrow roadway widths, existing vehicle speeds, traffic 
congestion, and limited visibility. Several attendees proposed Amphlett Boulevard as a viable alternative route 
that should be considered for bicycle facilities. Overall, the feedback highlighted a strong community desire to 
address parking impacts, improve safety through better lighting, and carefully evaluate any new bicycle route to 
avoid shifting problems to already constrained local streets.
Workshop No. 2 - Draft Concept Designs
Building on the feedback from Workshop No. 1, the second workshop presented draft concept designs that 
addressed the community’s priorities of not removing more parking beyond what is required under State 
daylighting laws, meaningful speed reduction, and enhanced safety for all users. The draft concepts featured two 
parallel bicycle boulevard alternatives to the existing Humboldt Street facility, both running from Indian Avenue to

2nd Avenue: Idaho Street Bike Boulevard and Fremont Street Bike Boulevard. Each option incorporated traffic-
calming measures designed to lower vehicle speeds and improved safety while preserving on-street parking. 
Participants engaged in group discussions guided by a knowledgeable facilitator and used visual aids. Dedicated 
scribes captured detailed feedback. The structured format allowed residents to examine the proposed treatments 
in detail, ask technical questions, and provide targeted input on each corridor, setting the stage for final 
refinement of the preferred alternative.
Summary of Meeting: The second workshop, attended by 38 in-person residents and 9 virtual participants with 
more than 70 survey respondents, centered on the proposed traffic-calming measures for both the Idaho Street 
and Fremont Street bike boulevard alternatives. Attendees showed strong interest in examining the specific 
treatments presented for each corridor. Staff noted specific participant preferences for these treatments, based 
upon feedback received at the meeting and through the survey following the meeting, for incorporation into the 
bike boulevard concept designs: 
•
For the Idaho Street alternative, participants immediately and consistently expressed a clear preference 
for speed humps over rumble strips. 
•
The six proposed intersection safety treatments—developed in direct response to Workshop No. 1 
feedback emphasizing intersection safety on Idaho Street—were very well received. 
•
High-visibility crosswalks and the introduction of four-way stops at key locations were also strongly 
supported for both alternatives. 
•
Feedback on curb extensions was mixed; several residents noted that they can make turning movements 
more difficult for larger vehicles, while traffic circles were viewed favorably as an alternative calming tool. 
Overall, the proposed calming measures for both alternatives received positive feedback, with participants 
acknowledging that the designs are projected to reduce operating speeds from the current 25 mph to 
approximately 15–18 mph. However, skepticism remained about whether those speed reductions would be fully 
achieved once constructed. Concerns about driveway visibility when vehicles back out were raised during both the 
question-and-answer period and table discussions. There was notable support for creating a bike boulevard on 
either Idaho Street or Fremont Street, with many citing the anticipated reduction in vehicle speeds and volumes as 
a significant benefit. At the same time, there was equally strong sentiment against removing the existing bike lanes 
on Humboldt Street, with many participants stating they would not support any alternative that eliminates the 
current bicycle facility.
Workshop No. 3 - Final Concept Design
This third and final workshop incorporated all feedback received from Workshop No. 2 and presented refined final 
concept designs for both the Idaho Street and Fremont Street bike boulevard alternatives. The primary objective 
was to obtain clear feedback from the community on their preferred outcome: 
•
Implementing the Idaho Street bike boulevard; 
•
Implementing the Fremont Street bike boulevard; or 
•
Retaining the existing Class II bike lanes on Humboldt Street in their current configuration
Staff clarified to participants in attendance as well as in the survey that the bike boulevard alternatives proposed 
for Idaho Street and Fremont Street did not involve removal of parking on these streets (beyond what may be 
required by State daylighting laws), as this was a point of confusion for some community members. 
The session included a concise, high-level staff presentation that highlighted the most significant changes made to 
the bike boulevard concept designs in response to Workshop No. 2 feedback, summarized results from the 
streetlighting pilot demonstration, and strongly encouraged participants to complete a focused four-question 
survey designed to capture the community’s final preferences for the future of bicycle facilities on Humboldt 
Street. The streamlined format and clear choice presented in the survey allowed staff to gather decisive input to 
bring to the Commission and City Council. As this was the final survey of Phase II, staff significantly expanded 
distribution beyond the in-person, virtual, and online options by mailing approximately 4,500 prepaid-postage

Summary of Meeting: The third and final workshop was attended by 41 residents in person and 10 virtually. 
Driveway visibility emerged as a recurring concern, with several participants noting increased bicycle usage on the

In-Person Survey Results
Staff received a total of 40 surveys at the workshop with 34 from residents of the North Central 
neighborhood. When asked directly which of the three alternatives they support, 35% of North Central 
respondents oppose keeping the existing bike lanes on Humboldt Street, with 65% in support of retaining 
them. Among residents who live directly on Humboldt Street, the split is more drastic: 0% oppose keeping 
and 100% support keeping the bike lanes, noting that two in-person respondents lived on Humboldt 
Street. The priority ranking indicates a preference across all groups to keep the existing bike lanes on 
Humboldt Street compared to the other alternatives. This demonstrates consistent neighborhood-wide 
sentiment to maintain the current Humboldt Street configuration.
•
Online Results
Staff received a total of 219 online surveys, with 99 survey responses from residents of the North Central 
neighborhood. When asked directly which of the three alternatives they support, 27% of North Central 
respondents oppose keeping the existing bike lanes on Humboldt Street, with 73% supporting retaining 
them. Among residents who live directly on Humboldt Street, the split is nearly identical: 26% oppose and 
74% support keeping the bike lanes. The priority ranking indicates a preference across all groups to keep 
the existing bike lanes on Humboldt Street compared to the other alternatives. This demonstrates 
consistent neighborhood-wide online sentiment for maintaining the current Humboldt Street 
configuration. These results are consistent with the in-person survey results. Tables with the detailed 
survey results are provided in Attachment 3 (Table 3 and 4).

Streetlight Pilot
The City conducted a streetlighting pilot between 9/17 and 10/15/25. The streetlighting pilot was designed to 
gather direct community feedback on appropriate lighting levels and fixture styles for Humboldt Street and for a 
potential bike boulevard alternative. Two demonstration locations were established:
•
Humboldt Street: Three cobrahead fixtures were installed side-by-side for comparison — the existing standard 
cobrahead, a medium-intensity version, and a high-intensity version.
•
Idaho Street: Two pedestrian-scale fixtures were displayed — the existing pedestrian-scale standard and a 
high-intensity version. (A medium-intensity pedestrian-scale option was not included because the City does 
not currently have an approved standard for that output level.)
Residents were able to view the fixtures at night and provide input on preferred brightness, glare, color 
temperature, and overall suitability for each corridor. Feedback from this pilot was summarized and presented at 
Workshop No. 3 to help inform final lighting recommendations.
Survey Summary: Staff received a total of 71 survey responses. Of these, 63% preferred the high-brightness option

School Engagement
On January 23, 2025, the San Mateo-Foster City School District Board unanimously adopted a resolution strongly 
supporting retention of the existing Class II bike lanes on Humboldt Street. The resolution describes the Humboldt 
corridor as one of the most vital bicycle segments in the City of San Mateo and states that removal of the lanes 
would pose a threat to safety. On February 1, 2025, staff also received a letter from the Parent Teacher 
Association (PTA) of the San Mateo City School District (SMFCSD) stating their position to keep the bike lanes on 
Humboldt. 
During Phase II outreach, staff met separately with administrators and faculty from San Mateo High School and 
Fiesta Gardens International School. Both schools emphasized that safety must remain the highest priority in any 
final decision. Fiesta Gardens International School representatives acknowledged the parking loss caused by the 
Humboldt bike lanes and noted that the already-constrained on-street parking supply in the surrounding 
neighborhood indirectly affects staff, families, and visitors. They welcomed any additional speed-calming measures 
on Indian Avenue, which experiences heavy vehicle, pedestrian, and bicycle traffic before and after school.
Phase II Engagement Summary
Overall, the engagement efforts made for this entire process were comprehensive and consistent. The feedback received 
was mixed, but there are some common themes, as listed below: 
•
Support for measures to slow speeds and volumes in neighborhood streets are welcomed, but not at the trade-off 
of removing parking or existing bicycle infrastructure. 
•
Streetlighting upgrades are well supported as the community perceives it as having a safety benefit. 
•
When looking at the overall priority ranking survey results during and after workshop #3, there is consistent 
support for retaining the bike lanes on Humboldt Street by in-person and online survey respondents. Mailed 
survey responses indicated residents living on Humboldt and Idaho Streets preferred the Fremont Street bike 
boulevard over retaining the Humboldt Street bike lanes.

Potential Idaho Street Alternative
The final bicycle facility concept for Idaho Street, as presented to the community, proposes a potential bike boulevard 
alternative along Idaho Street between Indian Avenue and 2nd Avenue. The potential alternative route is approximately 
4,000 feet long, compared to the existing 3,200-foot bike facility on Humboldt Street over the same general alignment. 
This alternative incorporates traffic calming and safety improvements along the entire corridor. Safety enhancements are 
provided at seven intersections and include two new traffic circles at Monte Diablo Avenue and Indian Avenue and high-
visibility crosswalks at all seven intersections. Additionally, eight speed humps are proposed along Idaho Street, with a 
total of ten-speed humps throughout the connecting route. Combined, these measures are projected to reduce prevailing 
vehicle speeds from approximately 25 mph to 15–18 mph and lower peak-hour traffic volumes from 130–289 vehicles per 
hour to 31–50 vehicles per hour. The final proposed concept can be found in Attachment 1 – Final Idaho Street Alternative. 
•
Cost Estimate: $1.37M: This cost includes restoration of Humboldt Street from Indian Avenue to 2nd Avenue to 
before bike lanes were installed, the design work needed to bring the 15% concept designs to construction, 
construction and inspection services and a contingency. It should be noted that safety enhancements are 
proposed at the intersection of Indian Avenue and Humboldt Street as part of a funded and planned project (Local 
Roadway Safety Plan Priority Package 1) and therefore not included in the cost estimate.
Potential Fremont Street Alternative
The final bicycle facility concept for Fremont Street, as presented to the community, proposes a bike boulevard along 
Fremont Street between Indian Avenue and 2nd Avenue. The existing bike facility on Humboldt Street is approximately 
3,200 feet long; the new proposed route along Fremont Street extends roughly 4,700 feet before reconnecting to the 
existing Class II bike lane on Humboldt Street. This alternative includes safety improvements at ten intersections. Proposed 
treatments include high-visibility crosswalks, all-way stops, centerline hardening, and curb extensions at Monte Diablo 
Avenue. In total, ten speed cushions are proposed, along with high-visibility crosswalks at Cypress Avenue, Tilton Avenue, 
both Monte Diablo Avenue intersections, Santa Inez Avenue, and Indian Avenue. Additionally, the proposal converts 2nd 
Avenue between S. Grant Avenue and Fremont Street to westbound-only motor vehicle traffic, allowing contraflow (two-
way) bicycle travel on that segment. Combined, these measures are projected to reduce existing vehicle speeds on 
Fremont Street from 23–26 mph to 15–18 mph and lower peak-hour traffic volumes from 100–200 vehicles per hour to 
50–120 vehicles per hour. The final proposed concept can be found in Attachment 2 – Final Fremont Street Alternative. 
•
Cost Estimate: $1.32M:  This cost includes restoration of Humboldt Street from Indian Avenue to 2nd Avenue to 
before bike lanes were installed, the design work needed to bring the 15% concept designs to construction, 
construction and inspection services and a contingency. It should be noted that safety enhancements are 
proposed at the intersection of Indian Avenue and Humboldt Street as part of a funded and planned project (Local 
Roadway Safety Plan Priority Package 1) and therefore not included in the cost estimate.

Staff have received feedback from the public as early as October 2024 and during the Phase II engagement effort, 
about the creation of a permit parking program on or near Humboldt Street to help alleviate the parking issue in 
the community. A summary of staff’s initial findings on this topic is below:

o
Policy Limitations: The City’s current policy allows a Residential Permit Parking Program (RPPP) in areas 
within residential neighborhoods where on-street parking is impacted by parked cars from non-residents, 
or parking generators such as a school, business, commercial district or commercial use. Accordingly, all of 
Humboldt Street would not be eligible for an RPPP unless the current policy must be amended, or a North 
Central neighborhood-specific permit parking program is developed.  
o
Limited On-Street Supply: From the feedback gathered from the community pre- and post-Project, staff 
estimates there are on average 2-3+ vehicles per household, for which there is insufficient on-street 
parking supply to accommodate. Some community members in North Central currently use on-street 
facilities to park commercial business vehicles, which would not be permitted under the current RPPP 
policy. For the program to be effective, a per-house limit on issued permits would need to be determined. 
An RPPP may not meet all of the needs of households relying solely on on-street parking to satisfy their 
needs. It should be noted that most properties also have some form of dedicated on-site parking.
o
Capital, Maintenance, and Upkeep: If an RPPP is established in North Central around Humboldt Street, it 
could be one of the City’s largest RPPP areas (depending on the program area) and require an estimated 
initial capital investment from $10,000 - $40,000+ to establish signage throughout the community (various 
depending on program area). On-going costs will include permit issuance, permit renewal, enforcement, 
and sign maintenance which is yet to be defined.

Other Humboldt Projects
Outside of the Humboldt Bike Lane Alternative effort, the City currently has two other active safety improvement 
projects on Humboldt Street and identified additional traffic calming measures that could be implemented.
o
Poplar/Humboldt Traffic Signal Improvement: This project was completed in late September 2025 and 
installed protected left turns at the Poplar Avenue and Humboldt Street intersection. This project was 
listed as the top priority project out of 10 in the City’s adopted Local Roadway Safety Plan (LRSP). 
o
Local Roadway Safety Plan Priority Package 1: This project was awarded approximately $1 million in 
federal funding to implement Priority Projects 1, 2, 4, and 6 from the City’s adopted LRSP. Priority Project 4 
specifically includes safety improvements at three intersections along Humboldt Street (Humboldt 
Street/Indian Avenue, Humboldt Street/E. Santa Inez Avenue, and Humboldt Street/Tilton Avenue). 
Improvements include, but are not limited to, all-way stop control, intersection lighting upgrades, raised 
crosswalks, curb extensions, and enhanced sight-distance improvements. The project will start the design 
phase in early 2026. 
o
Other Humboldt Street Calming Measures: Based on consistent community feedback in support of 
additional traffic calming measures, staff explored whether additional safety enhancements along 
Humboldt Street between Peninsula Avenue and 2nd Avenue could be implemented.  Staff identified the 
option of installing two raised crosswalks between Peninsula Avenue and Poplar Avenue, one at the 
existing crossing at the San Mateo Superior Court and the other at the existing crossing at College Way. 
When combined with all other ongoing Humboldt Street projects, these additional measures are projected 
to reduce prevailing vehicle speeds from the current 24–31 mph to approximately 22–26 mph. The two 
proposed raise crosswalks are estimated to cost approximately $130,000, for which there is currently not 
any identified funding. 
POTENTIAL ALTERNATIVES 
Staff is providing a range of potential alternatives for the Commission to consider in making a recommendation to Council 
on addressing community concerns about safety and the loss of parking on Humboldt Street, as described below.  These 
alternatives take into consideration the feedback from community engagement process, including the consistent and 
widespread support for additional traffic calming/safety measures and enhanced lighting and the more mixed feedback on 
keeping/removing the Humboldt Street bike lanes vs. installing bike boulevards on Idaho or Fremont Streets.   
•
Alternative #1 - Keep As-Is: 
This alternative retains the Class II bike lanes on Humboldt Street and does not restore any parking. This option 
maintains existing bike infrastructure, which allows for consistent North-South bike travel from 5th Avenue to 
Peninsula Avenue on Humboldt Street. This option does not address the comments from the community about the 
need to increase parking supply. 
o
Cost Estimate: $0 
•
Alternative #2 – Keep-As-Is with Additional Humboldt Street Calming Measures + Streetlighting Upgrades + 
Explore Permit Parking Program: 
This alternative retains the existing Class II bike lanes on Humboldt Street (no parking restoration) and includes the 
following additional improvements not provided in the LRSP Priority 1 project: streetlighting upgrades along the 
entire Humboldt Street corridor from Peninsula Avenue to 5th Avenue and implementation of enhanced traffic-
calming measures on Humboldt Street beyond the scope of the LRSP Priority Package 1 improvements. More 
specifically, these improvements include the installation of two raised crosswalks between Peninsula Avenue and 
Poplar Avenue.  As part of this alternative, staff will further explore the establishment of a permit parking program 
tailored to the North Central neighborhood. 
o
Cost Estimate: $225,000 (Includes design, construction and inspection services, but does not include costs

Dear Sustainability Committee,

I am writing to express my strong support for keeping the existing bike lanes on Humboldt
Street and my opposition to any proposal to relocate them to Idaho or Fremont Streets. The
current configuration on Humboldt offers clear advantages in terms of fiscal responsibility,
rider safety, and transportation efficiency, - considerations that are essential for prudent city
planning and responsible governance.

1. Avoiding Unnecessary Taxpayer Costs

Relocating established bike lanes is an expensive undertaking. The costs of removing the
existing infrastructure, redesigning intersections, reconstructing curb lines, repainting traffic
control markings, and installing new signage add up quickly. These are dollars that taxpayers
have already invested in a safe, functional network on Humboldt Street. Redirecting additional
funds to rebuild something that is already working well would not only be wasteful but would
also divert resources from other critical needs such as street maintenance, pedestrian safety
improvements, or public transit enhancements.

2. Proven Bicycle Safety on Humboldt Street

Humboldt Street currently provides a consistent, predictable route for cyclists, with traffic
patterns and street design already adapted for shared use. Cyclists and drivers alike have
learned these patterns over time, and this predictability is a cornerstone of road safety.
Relocating the lanes to Idaho or Fremont—streets that may have higher traffic volumes, more
driveways, or less visibility - could introduce unnecessary risks. Keeping infrastructure where
it already functions safely is the most reliable way to protect vulnerable road users.

3. Efficiency and Direct Access to Key Destinations

Humboldt Street offers direct and efficient access to popular destinations and neighborhood
connectors. The bike lane’s current alignment makes it easier for cyclists to reach schools,
parks, businesses, and transit corridors without detours or complicated routing. Shifting the
lanes to Idaho or Fremont would disrupt these established patterns and lengthen travel times
for residents who rely on biking as an affordable, sustainable mode of transportation. The
city’s active transportation network is only as strong as its most practical, intuitive routes - and
Humboldt Street is one of them.

For these reasons, I urge the Committee to maintain the bike lanes on Humboldt Street. This
decision would support fiscal stewardship, uphold community safety, and retain the
convenient and efficient bicycle network that many residents depend on daily.

Thank you for your consideration and for your ongoing work to make our city a safe,
sustainable, and accessible place for all.

Despite much resident uproar, Sunnyvale will eliminate parking along Hollenbeck Avenue.

At its most recent meeting, Dec. 2, the Sunnyvale City Council opted to take the recommendation of the city’s Bicycle and Pedestrian Advisory
Committee (BPAC),

The topic came up as part of'a study issue to explore buffered bike lanes between Hollenbeck Avenue and Danforth Drive. The item went through
several rounds of public outreach and two BPAC meetings.

Angela Obeso, transportation and traffic manager, told the council that city employees are exploring traffic calming measures in light of resident input.
Many said safety along Hollenbeck Avenue would be better served by traffic calming than by eliminating parking.

The city was only focused on feasibility, she added.

“There wasn’t discussion of an existing safety concern or safety condition, and so there was no extra scope included in here to look into any specific
safety concern,” Obeso said.

The council considered two options: one that eliminated parking with two buffered bike lanes and another that eliminated parking on the east side of the

RESOLUTION NO. 10/24-25

RESOLUTION OF THE BOARD OF EDUCATION
OF THE SAN MATEO-FOSTER CITY SCHOOL DISTRICT
IN SUPPORT OF THE PRESERVATION OF SAFE BICYCLE LANES
ON HUMBOLDT STREET IN SAN MATEO

WHEREAS, it is of utmost importance that we address the critical need to maintain safe and
accessible bicycle infrastructure within our community.

Meanwhile, ensuring our students' and families' safety and well-being is paramount to creating a
sustainable and secure environment for all individuals.

WHEREAS, the Humboldt Street bike lanes, stretching 1.6 miles, stand as one of the longest and
most vital segments of bicycle infrastructure in the City of San Mateo. These lanes serve four
schools directly, benefitting the entire school district community, and provide a crucial link to
downtown San Mateo and public transportation.

WHEREAS, the strategic location of these bike lanes makes them an important component of the
city's bicycle network. This network enables regional connectivity to safe bicycle overpasses over
Highway 101, connects the east and west sides of San Mateo, and provides bike routes to Foster
City and Burlingame. It plays a vital role in ensuring the safety and mobility of cyclists throughout
the region.

WHEREAS, we express our concerns regarding pedestrian safety along Humboldt Street and in the
North Central neighborhood. We firmly believe in slowing down vehicle traffic without compromising
existing bicycle infrastructure.

WHEREAS, Safe Routes to Schools offers students the opportunity to commute to school safely,
minimizing reliance on cars and reducing overall vehicle congestion. We believe that the City of San
Mateo possesses an array of effective tools that can be implemented to decrease vehicle speeds
and enhance the safety of our entire community.

WHEREAS, we strongly recommend that the City of San Mateo take immediate action to preserve
the safe infrastructure of the Humboldt Street bike lanes, which were diligently installed in 2022.
The recent City of San Mateo data reveals that these lanes are a crucial transportation route for
over 10,000 monthly bicycle trips.

WHEREAS, efforts to remove the Humboldt Street bike lanes and impose a shared space with
vehicle traffic would be detrimental to the objectives of Safe Routes to School and pose a significant
threat to the overall safety and well-being of all individuals utilizing these routes.

NOW THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, we urge the San Mateo City Council to maintain the
integrity of the Humboldt Street bike lanes for the benefit of our community. Your attention to this
matter and subsequent support in preserving these vital transportation routes will undoubtedly
enhance safety and accessibility for all residents.

The use of large aggregated datasets has suggested that there is a correlation between the amount of bicycle facilities and the rate of bicycle ridership. One of the first
types of this type of study, conducted by Arthur Nelson and David Allen (1997) used a before and after analysis of 18 cities to look at the relationship between the
installation of bicycle facilities and their effect on bicycle commuting. They also looked at a myriad of other variables including mean temperature, number of rainy
days, percent of students and number of bicycle pathways against bicycle commute rates. Their results found that, “each mile of bikeway per 100,000 residents is
associated with a 0.069 percent increase in commuters using bicycles, holding other factors constant” (Nelson and Allen 1997)

A study by Nebiyou Tilahun, David Levinson, Kevin J. Krizek found that individuals were willing to choose different types of infrastructure at the cost of travel time.
By having survey participants select their preferences from pictures with and without bicycle lanes, the researchers found that individuals were willing to add more

2008 ~ Jennifer Dill and John Gliebe (doi.org/10.15760/trec.151)

In their study, 164 adults showed that individuals placed high importance on directness of their route and avoiding streets with heavy vehicular traffic. In some cases,
the study participants would go out of their way to utilize low traffic streets over higher trafficked streets with bicycle lanes, which supports the Krizek study (2009).
Additionally, there was a clear difference between route choice and gender, which supports the findings that Emond, et al (2009) found in their stated preference
study.

It is shown that streets with speeds reduced to less than 20 mph have fewer injuries than streets that have not reduced their speeds (Grundy et al. 2009).
Coincidentally, these low speed streets are the same streets that Dill and Gliebe highlight as preferred by cyclists.

Individuals will have to choose between traveling on high-traffic streets with bicycle lanes, which are direct, or traveling on roads with no facilities that are not direct

The Bike Lane is coming of Age

The Germans invented the motorcar, the bicycle and, most importantly, the simple bike lane to separate the two. By 1927, the City of Magdeburg had its municipal
architect, Dr. Henneking, work with the Cycle Path Association on a working design. And the Radweg-Netzwerk or Separated Bike Lane Network was born (and
‘network’ is the key here).

“The creation of specially conceived paths for bicycles is the only real solution to the annually escalating problem of creating safe traffic conditions for cycles and
pedestrians.” (Dr. Henneking, Magdeburg]

Some Germans thought the primary and sinister purpose of the lanes was that cars could go faster. The Dutch used and improved on the idea of separate facilities.
They aimed to get people out of cars and onto bicycles — as they saw a congestion problem and needed a healthier and safer way to keep people moving. The
Imperial British, however — always the warriors — thought it was wiser to have motorists and cyclists fight it out for themselves, so they invented “vehicular cycling”
or “driving your bike” and “sharing the road”.

One could say that the Dutch policies were people-centric. The German policies might have been more car-centric. The British policies, however, seemed
dangerously knucklehead-centric.

World's Best Cities for Cycling (2010)

To this very day you find a mix of these British, German, or Dutch philosophies in any city and city hall in the world:

1. “Sharing the Road” means there is no separation or safety for vulnerable road users. You got yourself a car-centric city.
2. Separating slow road users from people driving because it’s better for the driving experience.

3. It is better to separate vulnerable road users from people driving because it’s better for the vulnerable road users and society.

1819: “The Dandy Horse”

In 1817, the Draisine was invented in Karlsruhe, Germany. It became a hit in Europe, and only 2 years later, America picked up and adopted it as well. But in
American cities, pedestrians complained, and the boom ended when municipalities basically outlawed them instead of providing infrastructure. This first bike boom
was beaten down by municipal codes.

1868: “The Boneshaker” and the “Penny-Farthing”

Shortly after the first pedalcycles were invented in France and the U-K., they came to America. But it was the same story as before: there was no unique
infrastructure provided, pedestrians complained, and city codes beat down this biking boom as well. This is because these vehicles weren’t ready for All-Ages-and-
Abilities (AAA).

1890: “The Safety-Bicycle”

The first chain-based transmission system made the bicycle more accessible for everyone. An economic depression in 1896 pushed bicycles to the forefront. The
Good Roads Movement also came about during this era. Of course, with the advent of the car, the American Automobile Association (the other AAA) took over the
effort and started to push bicycles off the roads.

This is also the era of Katherine T. “Kittie” Knox and the racist League of American Wheelmen or League of American Bicyclists, which banned black
membership between the years of 1884 and 1999.

1965: “The Schwinn Sting-Ray”

The Schwinn Company provided this bike boom, which resulted from good product engineering. Schwinn built a cool bicycle for children. When these children grew
up, they took their love for bicycles with them into adulthood, buying Schwinn derailleur bicycles. At the height of the boom, in 1972, 1973, and 1974, more bicycles
than automobiles were sold in the U.S. The boom was prolonged by the desire to have inexpensive transportation for recreation and exercise and the interest

in reducing pollution. Eventually, the oil crisis ended, and the fossil fuel and automobile industries returned to their old advertising strength.

But coming back to the theme of “bicycle culture” here, to this very day, you will find mostly older men arguing for more bicycle infrastructure and mostly older
women arguing against it. The reason can be found in this bike boom, which was again stopped by a lack of infrastructure. That is undoubtedly one reason why
America’s “bicycle culture” is heavily tilted towards older men — which are the generation that grew up in the 1970s with the Schwinn bike — and maybe their sons.
Their daughters, however, are mostly excluded.

congested cities have famously joined the ranks of cities with “bicycle culture.” All they had to do was to add bicycle lanes. We are talking about Paris, Seville,
Berlin, London, and even Dublin, which are joining now. All are working on providing the necessary real bike lane network, quickly leading to the culture and
