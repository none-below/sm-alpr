SUBJECT:
Bermuda Drive Bridge Replacement Project – Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with Bureau Veritas North America, Inc. in the amount of $887,000 for inspection and materials 
testing services for the Bermuda Drive Bridge Replacement Project (Project); establish a contingency reserve of $89,000; 
and authorize the Public Works Director to execute the agreement in substantially the form presented and issue change 
orders as allowed by the agreement documents within the contingency amount.
BACKGROUND:
In 2008, the City applied for preliminary engineering funds through the Highway Bridge Replacement and Rehabilitation 
Program (HBRRP) to evaluate the condition of the Bermuda Drive bridge over Borel Creek and address structural 
deficiencies identified by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). Following approval of HBRRP funding in 
2010, the City entered into an agreement with Biggs Cardosa Associates, Inc. (BCA) for preliminary engineering. Structural 
and seismic evaluations, which were completed in 2011-2012, recommended a full bridge replacement rather than a 
retrofit. Caltrans subsequently approved the revised project scope in 2017.
 
On May 21, 2018, City Council approved a professional services agreement for final design and environmental 
documentation with BCA. Design was completed in early 2025. The Project will reconstruct the deficient bridge to provide 
a safe and reliable route consistent with current design standards.
 
Due to limited in-house staffing resources to oversee construction and ensure compliance with federal and Caltrans 
requirements, the City issued separate Requests for Proposals (RFPs) in September 2025 – one for inspection and materials 
testing services, and another for engineering design services during construction (ESDC). These services are necessary to 
provide daily inspection, materials testing, and engineering oversight throughout construction. Proposals were evaluated 
based on qualifications and relevant experience. The ESDC agreement was approved by City Council on December 1, 2025.
 
To provide field inspection and materials testing support, staff recommends awarding an agreement to Bureau Veritas 
North America, Inc., the most qualified firm selected from the City’s September 2025 RFP. Based on their relevant bridge 
construction management experience and proposed scope, staff determined the firm is well suited to deliver the required 
construction management and inspection services for this project.
BUDGET IMPACT:
The project is funded through a combination of local and federal funds under the Bermuda Drive Bridge Replacement 
Project (462250) and Federal Project No. BRLS-5102(049). These funds will be used to award this agreement, as well as to 
establish the contingency reserve.

BACKGROUND:
In June 2013, City Council approved an agreement with Tyler Technologies, Inc. (Tyler Technologies) to purchase permit
and project tracking software (EnerGov) with related services and support to convert the City’s paper-based permit 
tracking system to an electronic system. The term of this agreement for Tyler Technologies’ services automatically renews 
each year unless terminated by either party. The continuing fees for Tyler Technologies’ services, including licensing and 
maintenance/support, are thereafter renewed, and invoiced on an annual basis. 
When originally established, the City hosted the software in City owned on-premises servers located at City Hall, requiring 
continuous software and hardware maintenance, and monitoring and upgrades by City IT staff.  As part of the next 
generation of software and security upgrades, a transition to a cloud-based service became necessary. On August 19, 
2024, the City Council approved Amendment No. 2 to the agreement with Tyler Technologies to transition to a cloud-
based platform for file storage, support, and security, and included an increased annual service maintenance/support fee. 
This transition to the cloud-based system has freed up in-house server space, reduced City overhead administrative 
burdens and provided for an improved user experience with enhanced cybersecurity protection and monitoring 
functionality.
This Amendment is coming before the Council to authorize the full cost of this agreement for the five-year term, as 
required by the City’s purchasing policy. The annual cost of this software was previously covered by Community 
Development’s annual budget, however with the annual cost now exceeding $100,000, it requires City Council approval. 
When this amendment was approved by Council on August 19, 2024, it authorized the transition to the cloud-based 
services and an increase in the annual service and maintenance fee for the first year, but not years 2-5 of the Agreement.  
This amendment to the Agreement with Tyler Technologies will cover the annual service maintenance/support fee 
increases for the current year (FY25-26) and the remaining three years of the five-year term of the agreement. All other 
agreement terms are the same as those approved by City Council on August 19, 2024.

BACKGROUND:
In May 2016, the City purchased the Cartegraph Operations Management System (Cartegraph OMS) and contracted with 
Cartegraph Systems, LLC (Cartegraph) to implement and host the system. Cartegraph has since been acquired by OpenGov, 
Inc. (OpenGov). The selection was made by a multi-departmental committee that included staff from Public Works, IT, 
Parks and Recreation, Police, City Clerk’s Office, and the City Manager’s Office. This committee unanimously agreed that 
Cartegraph OMS would best serve the City’s operational field work needs. Staff have since moved all work order 
management for streets and traffic, the sewer collection system, parks and recreation, and facilities to Cartegraph OMS, 
and continue to use the system for these operational needs. 
On May 6, 2019, April 4, 2022, and May 5, 2025, Council approved the three-year renewals of Cartegraph OMS with 
OpenGov based on the continued success of Cartegraph OMS and the continued need to serve City operations 
management functions. As part of the contract renewal in 2019, the City purchased an additional product called the 
Integration Toolkit, which allows the application programming interface (API) access to OpenGov for third party 
applications. During the City’s current Customer Relationship Management (CRM) implementation process with Catalis, it 
was identified that the more applicable product the City requires is the Asset Management Systems Integration, which is a 
separate OpenGov product. 
The proposed Amendment No. 1 updates the software services included in the Order Form of the Agreement to replace 
the Integration Toolkit with the Asset Management Systems Integration product. There is no additional cost to the City for 
this exchange.
BUDGET IMPACT:

funded by the Information Technology and Public Works departments with 25 percent of the total cost funded through 
Information Technology’s annual operating budget in the General Fund and 75 percent funded by Public Works’s operating 
budgets in the Sewer (50 percent), Solid Waste (25 percent), and Stormwater (25 percent) funds.

SUBJECT:
Excess Workers' Compensation Insurance Policy for CY 2026, and CY 2027 – Approval
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve the purchase of excess workers' compensation insurance from Midwest Employers' Casualty Company in an 
amount projected to be $574,924 ($287,462 annually) for the two-year period commencing January 1, 2026, and ending 
December 31, 2027.
BACKGROUND:
Excess workers' compensation coverage protects the self-insured employer financially for workers’ compensation claims 
that exceed the specified dollar limit. Currently, the City’s dollar limit (or retention) for workers’ compensation claims is 
$1,000,000. The City has been insured for excess workers' compensation coverage with Safety National Casualty 
Corporation (“Safety National”) since 2009. Such policy will expire on January 1, 2026. 
As the City’s broker, Keenan & Associates solicited quotes from three insurance companies for excess workers’ 
compensation coverage.  Safety National and Midwest Employers' Casualty Company (“Midwest”) were the only two 
companies that responded and provided their terms of coverage. 
Safety National provided 2 options: 
1) 1-Year Term at $1,000,000 Retention/$500,000 Corridor Retention/$25,000,000 Limit: Premium $324,445 with a rate 
guarantee for CY 2027/28. 
2) 1-Year Term at $1,250,000 Retention/Statutory Limit: Premium $219,751. 
Midwest provided 4 options: 
1) 1-Year Term at $1,000,000 Retention/Statutory Limit: Premium $287,462. 
2) 2-Year Term at $1,000,000 Retention/Statutory Limit: Premium $574,924 ($287,462 annually). 
3) 1-Year Term at $1,250,000 Retention/Statutory Limit: Premium $264,862. 
4) 2-Year Term at $1,250,000 Retention/Statutory Limit: Premium $529,724 ($264,862 annually). 
Keenan & Associates recommended we keep retention at $1,000,000 because by selecting option 3 or 4 there is potential 
risk of paying an additional $250,000 on a claim compared to only saving approximately $22,600 on the annual premium. 
Based on the analysis of available options, it was determined that the option offered by Safety National comes at a much 
higher cost, $324,445 versus $287,462 quoted by Midwest. Furthermore, Midwest provided more favorable terms 
regarding corridor retention and policy limit as outlined above.

$574,924 ($287,462 annually) with Midwest. Staff requests for approval of the purchase pursuant to Municipal Code

BUDGET IMPACT:
The premium quoted by Midwest Employers' Casualty Company for CY 2026 and 2027 is calculated at a rate of $0.3218 per

rate. Despite the reduction in rate, our annual premium of $287,462 represents an increase of 7.23%, which is due to 
increased payroll costs, going from $72,338,127 in actual payroll costs in CY 2024 to $89,329,485 in estimated payroll costs 
in CY 2026. There are sufficient funds budgeted in the Workers' Compensation Fund for this expense.

BACKGROUND:
Staff has previously updated Council on wet weather preparation activities beginning at the August 21, 2023, City Council 
meeting and have transitioned to provide annual updates with the most recent one from the November 18th, 2024.  The 
initial wet weather preparation update presented at the August 21, 2023, City Council meeting identified four (4) distinct 
timeline categories and twenty-three (23) individual initiatives and tasks related to stormwater management and 
maintenance improvements. The full list of these requested action items is provided in Attachment 1, with the majority 
now completed. This wet weather update will focus on the remaining incomplete initiatives, on-going annual maintenance 
progress, and other new activities related to stormwater management. 
Incomplete Initiatives:
•
Develop Marina Lagoon Alternatives Analysis for Pumping Below 95 feet
o
Staff reviewed Schaaf & Wheeler’s identified alternatives for the Marina Lagoon pumps and elected to 
conduct a physical hydraulic model (which entails building a scale model of the pump station) to assess 
flow patterns, identify potential improvements to optimize pump efficiency and performance, and 
determine whether pumping below elevation 95.0 feet is advisable.
o
Staff released a Request for Proposals (RFP) and selected Northwest Hydraulic Consultants, Inc., to 
perform the physical hydraulic modeling study, with Council awarding the agreement at the December 1, 
2025, City Council meeting. Staff anticipates the study will be complete by July 2026. 
•
Storm Drain Master Plan Update
o
Staff and the Master Plan consultant (Schaaf & Wheeler) have completed data acquisition and model 
development for the Master Plan and are currently focusing on reviewing climate change projections, 
developing criteria for project prioritization, and reviewing the preliminary capital project list.
o
Public Works staff will provide an update on the Stormwater Master Plan to the Sustainability and 
Infrastructure Commission and the City Council on January 14 and 26, 2026, respectively. The draft Master 
Plan is expected to be released for public review between February and April 2026, with formal Council 
adoption anticipated in June 2026.  Staff will work to incorporate initial capital improvement projects in 
the 2026-2027 Capital Improvement Program.
•
Marina Lagoon Spot Dredging 
o
Staff previously advertised the project on June 8, 2025, and received a single bid that was approximately 
30% higher than the original engineer’s estimate. The bid was formally rejected, and City Council directed 
staff to negotiate with the sole bidder in an effort to reduce costs. Unfortunately, the only viable

SUBJECT:
Economic Development Work Plan 2026-2029 — Adoption
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt Economic Development Work Plan 2026–2029. 
BACKGROUND:
On November 4, 2024, the City Council approved a framework for a three-year Economic Development Work Plan, to 
guide implementation, foster partnerships and strengthen and diversify the City’s economic base. The Work Plan affirms 
the City’s commitment to building a more welcoming, adaptable, inclusive, and resilient business and visitor community. A 
thriving local economy is essential to community vibrancy and long-term fiscal stability. Following Council direction to 
proceed with developing this Work Plan, the City adopted its 2025 Strategic Plan, which identifies Economic Development 
as one of the ten key Initiatives to focus on over the next five years.
The proposed 2026-2029 Economic Development Workplan (Attachment 1) builds on the Council approved framework and 
is designed to be flexible, data-driven, and responsive to changing market conditions.
The proposed Economic Development Work Plan is informed by: 
•
Analysis of San Mateo’s economic strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats (SWOT);
•
Alignment with General Plan 2040 goals related to economic development;
•
Engagement with stakeholders including the San Mateo Area Chamber of Commerce, Downtown San Mateo 
Association (DSMA), developers, business owners, and residents;
•
A collaborative cross-departmental staff review; and
•
The City’s Strategic Plan.
PURPOSE
The Economic Development Work Plan provides a clear framework to guide staff in advancing San Mateo’s economic 
priorities by building on the City’s strengths, expanding opportunities, and addressing emerging challenges. Through 
partnerships with the Downtown San Mateo Association, Chamber of Commerce, SAMCEDA, The SF Peninsula, workforce 
organizations, and neighborhood-serving business districts, the Plan supports long-term economic sustainability and 
community vibrancy.
FINDINGS
Following Council’s approval of the initial economic development framework, staff conducted extensive outreach with 
neighborhood businesses, the Chamber of Commerce, Downtown San Mateo Association, commercial brokers, community 
representatives, and internal cross-departmental staff to identify San Mateo’s key strengths, weaknesses, opportunities

and threats (SWOT). The following is a summary of the key findings:
•
Strength - Vibrant Downtown and Active Business Districts – The City has a walkable urban core and a diverse mix 
of businesses and community amenities that attract residents and visitors.
•
Strength - Strategic Central Location and Strong Transit Access – The City’s mid-peninsula location, three Caltrain

•
Strength - Diverse and Dynamic Business Base – The City’s innovative and inclusive business environment

shifting retail trends, broader economic uncertainty, and increased competition from neighboring cities may 
impact the City’s ability to retain businesses, sustain workforce, and support long-term economic vitality.

Overview of the Current Economic Landscape
San Mateo’s economy is anchored by a diverse mix of industries, high household 
incomes, and a strong retail and culinary scene. Its central Peninsula location and 
unmatched connectivity to regional markets provide a competitive advantage. However, 
pressures from online retail, rising costs, and the shift to remote work require adaptive 
strategies to sustain long-term competitiveness.
The City spans approximately 11 square miles (excluding water areas) and features 
four prominent retail districts: Downtown, Hillsdale Shopping Center, Bay Meadows, and 
Bridgepointe Shopping Center.
Downtown San Mateo
Adjacent to Central Park, Downtown offers a vibrant mix of shops, 
restaurants, offices, housing, and a Century 12 movie theater. Well-known for 
its wide variety of global cuisines, Downtown is a pedestrian-friendly 
destination located along the Caltrain line and serves as a lively cultural and 
commercial center for the community and peninsula.
Hillsdale Shopping Center
A major regional mall featuring more than 100 stores, including anchors such 
as Trader Joe’s, Barnes & Noble, Macy’s, Nordstrom, and others, along with 
a diverse range of dining options. Conveniently accessible via Caltrain, 
Hillsdale is a key hub of retail activity on the Peninsula. Its latest expansion, 
North Block, adds additional shops like West Elm, Cinepolis, restaurants, and 
provides public gathering spaces for events and entertainment.
Bay Meadows
A modern, transit-oriented neighborhood blending residential, office, retail, 
and open space. Bay Meadows features restaurants, cafes, and services 
designed to serve both residents and visitors, with a strong emphasis on 
walkability, sustainability, and access to Caltrain. It is directly across Hillsdale 
Shopping Center. Major office users are Roblox, Survey Monkey, and 
Guidewire. 
Bridgepointe Shopping Center
A major retail destination offering a wide selection of national retailers, 
specialty stores, and dining options. Its location near Highway 92 provides 
convenient regional access, attracting shoppers from Foster City, Halfmoon 
and East Bay.

--- page 89 ---
In addition to its four primary retail districts, San Mateo offers a variety of neighborhood 
shopping centers and retail nodes—such as 25th Avenue, Laurie Meadows, Laurelwood 
Shopping Center, Shoreview Shopping Center, Bel Mateo Plaza, Crystal Springs 
Village, 17th Avenue, and clusters along El Camino Real—that primarily serve the daily 
needs of surrounding neighborhoods.
Beyond retail, San Mateo has a diverse employment base anchored by fintech, gaming, 
healthcare, life sciences, and startups. Major employers—including Sony Interactive, 
Sutter Health, Kaiser, Franklin Templeton, and Roblox—contribute stability, innovation, 
and regional visibility. Retaining these industries is critical to sustaining jobs, tax 
revenues, and long-term economic resilience. Looking forward, San Mateo is also well-
positioned to attract complementary sectors such as AI/data services, expanded life 
sciences, and cleantech, which align with broader Bay Area strengths and support 
future growth.
San Mateo’s competitive advantages include its walkable urban fabric, vibrant 
downtown, unique transit access with three Caltrain stations, strong civic engagement, 
and high-performing schools. At the same time, the City faces challenges common 
across the Bay Area: high operating costs, a perception of overregulation, and ongoing 
housing affordability pressures. Still, there are opportunities to diversify retail offerings, 
recruit innovative growth-stage companies, and expand cultural programming with 
support from the Downtown Association and Chamber.
San Mateo is also dealing with bigger trends that threaten local businesses, like more 
people shopping online instead of in stores, the long-term shift to remote work and 
smaller office needs, uncertainty around interest rates, and stronger competition from 
nearby cities and global markets. Finally, the ups and downs of the national and world 
economy make planning harder, showing why flexible and resilient strategies are so 
important.

which includes staff’s proposed work plan for each initiative for the upcoming fiscal year. The first step of the process was 
the City Council Blue Sky Workshop on February 22, 2025. At the workshop, the City Council reviewed the adopted Fiscal 
Year 2024-25 Priorities, removed completed and operationalized items, reorganized the projected completion timeline for 
existing priorities, and proposed new priorities to add to the list for Fiscal Year 2025-26. On May 5, 2025, City Council 
reviewed and formally adopted the Fiscal Year 2025-26 City Council Priority List. Fiscal Year 2025-26 started on July 1, 
2025, and ends on June 30, 2026. This report focuses on the progress made from July 1 through October 10, 2025, and 
estimated progress through the end of the fiscal year.

steps for advancing these initiatives. The following are some examples:

completion expected toward the end of 2026.

SUBJECT:
City Council Special Meeting Start Time — Direction 
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction regarding the preferred start time for City Council special meetings.
BACKGROUND:
The City Council conducts its official business through regular and special meetings, as established by the City Charter, 
State law, and the Council’s Rules and Procedures. Section 2.09 of the Charter provides that regular meetings are held on 
the first and third Monday of each month and may not begin earlier than 6:30 p.m., unless adjourned to a specific date 
and time. In practice, Regular City Council Meetings typically begin at 7:00 p.m.
Special meetings, authorized under Charter Section 2.11 and State law, may be scheduled outside of the regular meeting 
calendar and may be called by the Mayor or City Council with at least 24 hours’ notice, compared to the 72-hour notice 
requirement for regular meetings. The Council commonly holds special meetings immediately before regular meetings to 
allow time for focused discussion. These special meetings are often held as study sessions or closed sessions. Study 
sessions provide additional time for complex or high-priority matters where staff seeks policy direction rather than formal 
action. Although no votes are taken, study sessions serve as a key forum for the Council to ask questions, explore policy 
options, and give direction that guides subsequent staff work.
The City Council also holds special meetings for other purposes, such as advisory body interviews, goal setting workshops, 
public employee evaluations, and other non-routine matters. For the purposes of this report, the direction being 
requested is limited to special meetings scheduled in conjunction with Regular City Council Meetings.
Staff reviewed the start times for City Council special meetings over the past three years to identify patterns in scheduling 
and meeting duration. Special meetings typically include closed session items, study sessions, or other time-sensitive 
discussions held prior to the regular meeting.
•
2025:
A total of 19 special meetings began at 5:30 p.m., while four (4) began at 6:00 p.m. or 6:30 p.m.
Meetings held after 6:00 p.m. were generally short in duration and included only closed session items and/or one 
brief study session.
•
2024:
A total of 11 special meetings began at 5:30 p.m., eight (8) began at 5:00 p.m., and one (1) began at 6:00 p.m.
Meetings scheduled at 5:00 p.m. typically included closed session items and/or longer study sessions that required 
additional time.

2023:
A total of 17 special meetings began at 5:30 p.m., one (1) began at 5:00 p.m. due to a larger number of items, and 
seven (7) began at 6:00 p.m.
The meetings held at 6:00 p.m. primarily consisted of closed session items or short informational updates. This review 
shows that earlier start times (5:00-5:30 p.m.) have historically been used when the agenda includes multiple or lengthy 
items, while later start times (6:00 p.m. or later) have been used when only brief or limited discussion items are 
anticipated.

going forward.

If a standard start time is preferred, what time should be designated for special meetings (including study 
sessions).
