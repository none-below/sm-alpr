SUBJECT:
Potential Ballot Measures for November 2026 — Survey Results and Council Direction
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive a presentation from the City’s consultant, True North Research, concerning the potential Revenue Measures 
Polling Survey results and provide staff with direction on whether to proceed with informing and educating the community 
about one of the potential ballot measures in preparation for the November 2026 election.
BACKGROUND:
At the June 16, 2025 City Council meeting to adopt the FY 25-26 Citywide budget, the City Council adopted a General Fund 
budget that anticipated a budget deficit of $12 million. City staff has revised its estimated budget deficit to $14 million 
since the adoption of the budget because the State’s adopted budget only included 67 percent of the total Vehicle License 
Fee (VLF) backfill owed to the City in FY 25-26. Due to this estimated budget deficit and the anticipated budget deficits 
projected over the next 10 years as part of the General Fund 10-year forecast, the City has implemented a Fiscal 
Sustainability Plan. As part of this plan, staff continues to review opportunities to reduce General Fund expenditures, 
improve cost recovery and cost allocation, pursue grant opportunities, and strategize Citywide economic development. 
Another key component of this plan is to review revenue enhancement options such as a November 2026 ballot measure 
to bring in new revenues to both sustain city services at their current levels by offsetting the projected $5 -$7 million per 
year structural operating deficits and to fund the estimated $350 million in capital improvements and infrastructure needs 
for which there is insufficient funding. 
City staff reviewed potential ballot measure options with the Revenue Measure Ad Hoc Committee on August 6, 2025. 
Then staff reviewed the potential ballot measure options with the full Council on August 16, 2025. Council expressed a 
desire to better understand San Mateo voter sentiments regarding a 1/4 cent transactions and use (“sales”) tax and a 
General Obligation Bond, and polling was conducted through a statistically reliable survey in November 2026. 
Revenue Measure Options 
Transactions and Use Tax (sales tax) 
The City can add a transactions and use tax, also known as a district or local add-on sales tax, on top of the regular sales 
tax. A sales tax applies to merchandise that is delivered within a jurisdiction and does not apply to grocery items or 
prescription medicines. San Mateo voters approved Measure S, a sales tax, in November 2015, which extended Measure L, 
a 1/4 cent sales tax. Since the passage of Measure RR in November 2020 to support Caltrain operations, the sales tax rate 
in the City of San Mateo has been 9.625 percent. The State of California imposes a limit of two percent above the 
statewide base sales tax rate (7.25%). Since 0.625 percent of the City’s current sales tax is exempt from this limit, the City 
still has 1/4 cent of capacity left before reaching the limit. If another entity places a sales tax measure on the ballot before 
the City uses its cap, the City may no longer have the option to add a 1/4 cent sales tax for local funding that stays within 
the City limits. In order to pass, this measure would require a simple majority (50% plus 1) voter approval, and the

General Obligation Bond 
Another specific type of revenue enhancement that the City could pursue is a General Obligation (GO) bond. A GO bond 
requires 2/3 voter approval and once approved authorizes the City to sell bonds based on a levy or tax on real and 
personal property within the City limits. For a GO bond, the voters approve a maximum bond issuance amount and the use 
of the bond proceeds, but not a specific tax rate or annual payment. Revenues from a GO bond can only be used for 
approved infrastructure improvements and not for operating expenses. Thus, this revenue source is appropriate for 
addressing the City’s unfunded capital improvement needs. The City’s municipal advisor, UFI, analyzed three scenarios for 
potential GO bond issuances. In each scenario they developed what the tax rate per $100,000 of assessed value would be 
for a parcel, based on an estimated total amount of bond proceeds being approved. Table 1 outlines the estimated tax 
rates and bond proceeds. 
As shown, property owners would pay approximately $30 per $100,000 of assessed valuation on their property per year if 
the City received approval to bond $325 million, close to the amount currently estimated in unfunded infrastructure 
needs.
Survey Methodology and Objectives
Between November 10 and November 15, 2025, True North Research administered the survey to a stratified random 
sample of 1,012 registered voters in the City of San Mateo who are likely to participate in the November 2026 election. 
The survey followed a mixed-method design that employed multiple recruiting methods (email, text, and telephone) and 
multiple data collection methods (telephone and online). The average interview lasted 17 minutes and the survey is 
statistically reliable. 
One of the objectives of the study was to determine how support for a local revenue measure may vary depending on the 
type of financial mechanism employed: bond or sales tax. To reliably estimate support for the different types of measures, 
a split-sample methodology was employed such that half of voters were administered a survey that focused on a bond 
measure and the other half were asked questions regarding a potential sales tax measure. All 1,012 respondents received 
common questions that applied to both types of measures.
Key Findings - Is a revenue measure feasible for 2026? 
Voters have a high regard for the quality of life in San Mateo (86% rate it as excellent or good) and are generally satisfied 
with the City’s provision of services (74% very or somewhat satisfied). How these sentiments translate into support for a

Sales Tax 
A majority of voters initially exhibited support for a 1/4 cent sales tax to fund San Mateo city services, such as fire 
protection, paramedic, and 911 emergency response, police and crime prevention, keeping local streets, sidewalks, parks, 
infrastructure, and community facilities safe, clean, and well maintained, and addressing homelessness. With just the 
information conveyed in the 75-word ballot statement, 53% of voters indicated they would support the sales tax at the 
Initial Ballot Test (3% above the requisite simple majority threshold). Additionally, support for the sales tax increased over 
the course of the interview in response to the projects that could be funded and the arguments tested, ending the survey 
6% above the threshold required for passage at the Final Ballot Test. 
General Obligation Bond 
The survey results were less promising with respect to the feasibility of a $325 million bond to pave and maintain local 
streets, fix potholes, improve intersections for traffic flow/safety, repair/replace aging infrastructure, upgrade community 
parks and playfields, and repair/restore community facilities including fixing deteriorating roofs, plumbing, sewer, and 
electrical systems. Although 59% of respondents initially supported the bond, the threshold required for passage of a bond 
is higher (two-thirds). Support for the bond also declined over the course of the survey in response to tax rate information 
and negative arguments, landing at 54% at the Final Ballot Test. 
Key Findings Summary 
The results of this survey indicate that a general sales tax may be feasible for the November 2026 ballot provided that it 
focuses on voters’ priorities and is accompanied by robust voter/opinion leader engagement, education, and 
communication. The results of the survey indicate that a general obligation bond is not feasible given that it needs two-
thirds voter approval and landed 13 percentage points below this level at the Final Ballot Test. Figure 2 below provides the 
Final Ballot Test percentages for both the bond and sales tax. Additionally, True North Research’s Baseline Revenue

Options for City Council Consideration 
1.
Should the City staff proceed with education and outreach on the sales tax measure and return to Council for a 
decision whether to place it on the November 2026 ballot? 
2.
Should the City proceed with education and outreach on the general obligation bond and return to Council for a 
decision whether to place it on the November 2026 ballot? 
3.
Should the City proceed with education and outreach on the sales tax measure and general obligation bond and return 
to Council for a decision whether to place one or both on the November 2026 ballot? 
4.
Should the City not proceed with education and outreach on either the sales tax measure or the general obligation 
bond and no longer pursue a revenue measure for the November 2026 ballot? 
Next steps 
If staff receives City Council direction to move forward with option 1, 2, or 3, staff will engage in an educational outreach 
effort to inform the community about the potential November 2026 ballot measures from January through July 2026. This 
outreach will include meeting with dozens of community stakeholder organizations and providing educational information 
to the community. Informational outreach to the full community will include communitywide mailers and digital 
advertising that is linked to a City dedicated informational website. State election law requires that the City no longer 
spend City resources or staff time on outreach to the community once Council makes a final determination that an item 
will be placed on the ballot, which is anticipated to be decided in July 2026.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
Incorporated in 1894, the City of San Mateo encompasses 15.9 square miles in the San Francisco
Bay Area and is currently home to an estimated 103,352 residents.1 One of only two charter cit-
ies in San Mateo County, the City is governed by a five-member City Council, while the City’s
daily operations are managed by a dedicated team of employees that provide a full suite of ser-
vices to residents and the local business community.
As San Mateo has grown, so too have the demands placed upon its facilities, infrastructure, and
services. Unfortunately, the City’s revenue streams have not kept pace with the growing
demands and escalating costs, leading to shortfalls in the funding required to adequately
address its various infrastructure systems—including streets and roads, sewer and stormdrains,
parks, and community facilities—as well as municipal services. In total, the City has an estimated
$350,000,000 in capital improvements and infrastructure needs for which it does not have a
funding source, as well as projected structural deficits in the revenues needed to sustain city ser-
vices at their current levels. 
MOTIVATION FOR RESEARCH    The primary purpose of this study was to produce an
unbiased, statistically reliable evaluation of voters' interest in supporting a revenue measure to
fund priority repairs, upgrades, and improvements to the City’s infrastructure systems, as well
as associated city services. Additionally, should the City decide to move forward with a revenue
measure, the survey can guide how best to structure the measure so it is consistent with the
community's priorities and expressed needs. Specifically, the study was designed to:
•
Gauge current, baseline support for a revenue measure to fund infrastructure, facilities, and 
city services;
•
Identify the types of projects and services voters are most interested in funding, should a 
measure pass;
•
Expose voters to arguments in favor of, and against, the proposed revenue measure to 
assess how information affects support for the measure; and
•
Estimate support for the measure once voters are presented with the types of information
they will likely be exposed to during an election cycle.
It is important to note at the outset that voters’ opinions about tax measures are often some-
what fluid, especially when the amount of information they initially have about a measure is lim-
ited. How voters think and feel about a measure today may not be the same way they think and
feel once they have had a chance to hear more information about the measure during the elec-
tion cycle. Accordingly, to accurately assess the feasibility of establishing a local revenue mea-
sure, it was important that in addition to measuring current opinions about the measure
(Questions 5 & 7), the survey expose respondents to the types of information voters are likely to
encounter during an election cycle—including arguments in favor (Question 12) and opposed
(Question 15) to the measure—and gauge how this information ultimately impacts their voting
decision (Questions 16 & 17).

K E Y  F I N D I N G S
As noted in the Introduction, this study was designed to provide the City of San Mateo with a sta-
tistically reliable understanding of voters’ interest in funding priority repairs and improvements
to the City’s infrastructure systems as well as general municipal services with a local revenue
measure (bond or sales tax). Whereas subsequent sections of this report are devoted to convey-
ing the detailed results of the survey, in this section we attempt to ‘see the forest through the
trees’ and note how the collective results of the survey answer some of the key questions that
motivated the research. The following conclusions are based on True North’s interpretations of
the survey results and the firm’s collective experience conducting revenue measure studies for
public agencies throughout the State.
Is a revenue measure 
feasible for 2026?
Voters have a high regard for the quality of life in San Mateo (86% rate it
as excellent or good) and are generally satisfied with the City’s provision
of services (74% very or somewhat satisfied). How these sentiments
translate into support for a local revenue measure—and the feasibility of
the measure if placed on a ballot in 2026—depends on the type of mea-
sure being considered.
A majority of voters initially exhibited support for a 1/4 cent sales tax to
fund San Mateo city services, such as fire protection, paramedic, and 911
emergency response, police and crime prevention, keeping local streets,
sidewalks, parks, infrastructure, and community facilities safe, clean,
and well-maintained, and addressing homelessness. With just the infor-
mation conveyed in the 75-word ballot statement, 53% of voters indi-
cated they would support the sales tax at the Initial Ballot Test (3% above
the requisite simple majority threshold). Additionally, support for the
sales tax increased over the course of the interview in response to the
projects that could be funded and the arguments tested, ending the sur-
vey 6% above the threshold required for passage at the Final Ballot Test.
The survey results were less promising with respect to the feasibility of a
$323 million bond to pave and maintain local streets, fix potholes,
improve intersections for traffic flow/safety, repair/replace aging infra-
structure, upgrade community parks and playfields, and repair/restore
community facilities including fixing deteriorating roofs, plumbing,
sewer, and electrical systems. Although 59% of respondents initially sup-
ported the bond, the threshold required for passage of a bond is higher
(two-thirds). Support for the bond also declined over the course of the
survey in response to tax rate information and negative arguments, land-
ing at 54% at the Final Ballot Test.
The results of this survey indicate that a general sales tax may be feasi-
ble for the November 2026 ballot provided that it focuses on voters’ pri-
orities and is accompanied by robust voter/opinion leader engagement,
education, and communication (more on this to follow).

Which projects and ser-

A general tax is “any tax imposed for general governmental purposes”2
and is distinguished from a special tax in that the funds raised by a gen-
eral tax are not earmarked for a specific purpose(s). Thus, a general tax
provides a municipality with a great deal of flexibility with respect to
what is funded by the measure on a year-to-year basis.
Although the San Mateo City Council would have the discretion to decide
how to spend the revenues, the survey results indicate that voters are
most interested in using the proceeds to keep streets, sidewalks, parks,
infrastructure, and community facilities safe, clean, and well-maintained
(86% strongly or somewhat favor), fix potholes (85%), provide quick
responses to 911 emergencies (85%), pave and maintain local streets
and roads (84%), and provide fire protection and paramedic services
(83%). Moreover, a majority of respondents strongly favored funding
each of the above five service areas with proceeds from the measure.

information campaign 
affect support for the 
proposed measure?
As noted in the body of this report, individuals’ opinions about revenue
measures are often not rigid, especially when the amount of information
presented to the public on a measure has been limited. Thus, in addition
to measuring current support for a measure, one of the goals of this
study was to explore how the introduction of additional information
about a measure may affect voters’ opinions about the proposal.
It is clear from the survey results that some voters’ opinions about the
proposed sales tax measure are somewhat sensitive to the nature—and
amount—of information that they have about the measure. Information
about the specific projects and services that could be funded by the
sales tax, as well as arguments in favor of the measure, were found by
many voters to be compelling reasons to support the measure—effec-
tively boosting support for the measure to 58% at the Interim Ballot Test.

ments, and there is a risk that voters could be swayed by divisive and
hyper-partisan campaigning during the 2026 election cycle. Accordingly,
one of the keys to building and sustaining support for a measure will be

I N I T I A L  B A L L O T  T E S T
As noted in the Introduction, the primary objective of this study was to estimate voter support
for two potential ballot measures—a $323 million bond to pave and maintain local streets, fix
potholes, improve intersections for traffic flow/safety, repair/replace aging infrastructure,
upgrade community parks and playfields, and repair/restore community facilities including fix-
ing deteriorating roofs, plumbing, sewer, and electrical systems, and a quarter-cent general
sales tax to fund San Mateo city services, such as fire protection, paramedic, and 911 emer-
gency response, police and crime prevention, keeping local streets, sidewalks, parks, infrastruc-
ture, and community facilities safe, clean, and well-maintained, and addressing homelessness.
To this end, questions 5 (bond) and 7 (sales tax) were designed to take an early assessment of
support for the respective measures. A split sample approach was employed to randomly assign
respondents to one version of the measure. Once selected for a particular version (e.g., sales
tax), the respondent continued with that version for the remainder of the interview.
The motivation for placing questions 5 and 7 up-front in the survey is twofold. First, voter sup-
port for a measure can often depend on the amount of information they have about a measure.
At this point in the survey, the respondent has not been provided information about the pro-
posed measure beyond what is presented in the ballot language. This situation is analogous to a
voter casting a ballot with limited knowledge about the measure, such as what might occur in
the absence of an effective education campaign. Questions 5 and 7, also known as the Initial Bal-
lot Tests, are thus a good gauge of voter support for the proposed measure as it is today, on the
natural. Because the Initial Ballot Test provides an estimate of ‘natural’ support for a measure, it
also serves a second purpose in that it provides a useful baseline from which to judge the impact
of various information items conveyed later in the survey on voter support for the measure.
SUPPORT BY MEASURE TYPE   Figure 8 on the next page presents the results of the Initial
Ballot Test for the two versions of the measure. Overall, 59% of likely November 2026 voters indi-
cated they would support the proposed $323 million bond, with 26% opposed and 15% either
unsure or unwilling to share their opinion. Support for the bond was approximately eight points
below the two-thirds supermajority (66.7%) required for passage.
A majority of respondents (53%) indicated they would support the one-quarter cent sales tax

T A X  T H R E S H O L D  F O R  B O N D
Naturally, voter support for a revenue measure is often contingent on the cost of the measure.
The higher the tax rate, all other things being equal, the less likely a voter is to support the mea-
sure. One of the goals of this study was thus to gauge the impact that changes in the tax rate for
the bond can be expected to have on voter support for the proposed measure.3
Questions 9 and 10 were designed to do just that. Respondents were first instructed that the
amount each homeowner will pay if the bond measure passes depends on the assessed value of
their home—not the market value. Voters were then presented with the highest tax rate ($30 per
$100,000 assessed valuation) and asked if they would support the proposed measure at that
rate. If a respondent did not answer ‘definitely yes’, they were asked whether they would support
the measure at the lower tax rate. The three tax rates tested using this methodology and the per-
centage of respondents who indicated they would vote in favor of the measure at each rate are
shown in Figure 11.
Question 9   The amount homeowners will pay if the bond passes depends on the assessed value
of their home - not the current market value of the home. If you heard that the annual property
taxes on your home would increase: _____ per 100,000 dollars of assessed valuation, would you
vote yes or no on the bond measure? 
FIGURE 11  TAX THRESHOLD: BOND
The most obvious pattern revealed in Figure 11 is that some voters are price sensitive when it
comes to their support for the proposed bond measure. As the cost of the measure to their
household increases, support for the bond decreases. At the highest tax rate tested ($30 per
$100,000 of assessed valuation), 45% of voters indicated that they would support the bond.
Incremental reductions in the tax rate resulted in incremental increases in support for the mea-
sure, with 55% of voters indicating that they would support the bond at the lowest tax rate tested
($15 per $100,000 of assessed valuation).

sionally overestimate their current assessed valuation and/or have difficulty translating the tax

rate information for the bond. In addition to presenting the rate as described above, voters were

are presented in Figure 12 below.
Voters responded more positively when the cost of the bond measure was expressed as an
annual total for the median homeowner when compared with a rate per $100,000 of assessed
valuation. At a tax rate of $30 per $100,000 of assessed valuation, 45% of voters indicated that
they would support the proposed bond measure. When that rate was translated to an annual cost
for the median homeowner (approximately $225 per year), 53% of those surveyed indicated that
they would support the bond, 36% would oppose it, and 11% were unsure or unwilling to share

P O S I T I V E  A R G U M E N T S
If the City chooses to place a measure on an upcoming ballot, voters will be exposed to various
arguments about the proposal in the ensuing months. Proponents of the measure will present
arguments to try to persuade voters to support the measure, just as opponents may present
arguments to achieve the opposite goal. For this study to be a reliable gauge of voter support for
a revenue measure, it is important that the survey simulate the type of discussion and debate
that will occur prior to the vote taking place and identify how this information ultimately shapes
voters’ opinions about the measure.
The objective of Question 12 was thus to present respondents with arguments in favor of the
proposed measure and identify whether they felt the arguments were convincing reasons to sup-
port it. Arguments in opposition to the measure were also presented and are discussed later in
this report (see Negative Arguments on page 28). Within each series, specific arguments were
administered in random order to avoid a systematic position bias. Arguments that were pre-
sented only to those who received the sales tax measure are preceded by [Sales Tax] in the label.

M E T H O D O L O G Y
The following sections outline the methodology used in the study, as well as the motivation for
using certain techniques.
QUESTIONNAIRE DEVELOPMENT   Dr. McLarney of True North Research worked closely
with the City of San Mateo to develop a questionnaire that covered the topics of interest and
avoided possible sources of systematic measurement error, including position-order effects,
wording effects, response-category effects, scaling effects, and priming. Several questions
included multiple individual items. Because asking the items in a set order can lead to a system-
atic position bias in responses, items were asked in random order for each respondent.
Some questions asked in this study were presented only to a subset of respondents. For exam-
ple, only individuals who did not support the bond measure (or were unsure) at the Initial Ballot
Test (Question 5) were asked Question 6 regarding their reasons for not supporting the mea-
sure. A split-sample approach was also used throughout the survey so that respondents received
questions related to one version of the measure (bond or sales tax) based on their being
assigned to Sample A or Sample B. In some cases, two versions of a project or argument were
tested to identify how wording or framing differences impact perception of the item. In such
cases, half of each sample (within Sample A or B) received the item with version 1 wording (e.g.,
Question 15, item A1) and the other half received version 2 (e.g., Question 15, item A2). The
questionnaire included with this report (see Questionnaire & Toplines on page 38) identifies the
skip instructions that were used during the interview to ensure that each respondent received
the appropriate questions.
PROGRAMMING & PRE-TEST   Prior to fielding the survey, the questionnaire was CATI
(Computer Assisted Telephone Interviewing) programmed to assist interviewers when conduct-
ing telephone interviews. The CATI program automatically navigates skip patterns, randomizes
the appropriate question items, and alerts the interviewer to keypunching mistakes should they
occur. The survey was also programmed into a passcode-protected online survey application to
allow online participation for sampled voters. The integrity of the questionnaire was pre-tested
internally by True North and by dialing into voter households in the City prior to formally begin-
ning the survey.
SAMPLE   The survey was administered to a stratified and clustered random sample of regis-
tered voters in the City who are likely to participate in the November 2026 general election, with
a subset who are also likely to participate in the lower-turnout June 2026 primary. Consistent
with the profile of this universe, the sample was stratified into clusters, each representing a com-
bination of age, gender, and household party type. Individuals were then randomly selected
based on their profile into an appropriate cluster. This method ensures that if a person of a par-
ticular profile refuses to participate, they are replaced by an individual who shares their same
profile.
To reliably estimate support for a bond measure as well as a sales tax measure, a split-sample
methodology was employed such that 506 voters were administered a survey that focused on a
bond, whereas a separate 506 voters were asked questions regarding a sales tax measure. All
respondents received questions that were common to both types of measures. The split-sample

approach was employed because it is the most reliable method of estimating voter support for
alternative tax measures. Prior research (and actual election results) have consistently shown
that attempting to estimate support for multiple tax measures (e.g., bond and sales tax) with the
same respondent during the course of an interview will lead to an artificially low estimate of sup-
port for whichever measure is introduced second—and it also has a tendency to cause confusion.
To avoid these sources of measurement error, it is important that each respondent be asked
their opinions regarding one of the alternatives, not both.
STATISTICAL MARGIN OF ERROR   By using the probability-based sampling design
noted above, True North ensured that the final sample was representative of voters in the City
who are likely to participate in the November 2026 general election. The results of the survey
can thus be used to estimate the opinions of all voters likely to participate in said election.
Because not all voters participated in the study, however, the results have what is known as a sta-
tistical margin of error due to sampling. The margin of error refers to the difference between
what was found in the survey of voters for a particular question and what would have been found
if all of the estimated 42,415 likely November 2026 voters identified in the City had been sur-
veyed for the study.
Figure 18 provides a graphic plot of the maximum margin of error in this study. The maximum
margin of error for a dichotomous percentage result occurs when the answers are evenly split
such that 50% provide one response and 50% provide the alternative response. For questions
answered by all 1,012 respondents, the maximum margin of error is ± 3.0%. For questions spe-
cific to one type of revenue measure (bond or sales tax), the maximum margin of error is
approximately ± 4.3%.

Within this report, figures and tables show how responses to certain questions varied by sub-
groups such as age, gender, and partisan affiliation. Figure 18 is thus useful for understanding
how the maximum margin of error for a percentage estimate will grow as the number of individ-
uals asked a question (or in a particular subgroup) shrinks. Because the margin of error grows
exponentially as the sample size decreases, the reader should use caution when generalizing
and interpreting the results for small subgroups.
RECRUITING & DATA COLLECTION   The survey followed a mixed-method design that
employed multiple recruiting methods (telephone, text, and email) and multiple data collection
methods (telephone and online). Telephone interviews averaged 17 minutes in length and were
conducted during weekday evenings (5:30PM to 9PM) and on weekends (10AM to 5PM). It is stan-
dard practice not to call during the day on weekdays because most working adults are unavail-
able and thus calling during those hours would likely bias the sample.
Voters recruited via email and text were assigned a unique passcode to ensure that only voters
who received an invitation could access the online survey site, and that each voter could com-
plete the survey only one time. A total of 1,012 surveys were completed between November 10
and November 15, 2025.
DATA PROCESSING   Data processing consisted of checking the data for errors or inconsis-
tencies, coding and recoding responses, weighting, and preparing frequency analyses and cross-
tabulations.
ROUNDING    Numbers that end in 0.5 or higher are rounded up to the nearest whole num-
ber, whereas numbers that end in 0.4 or lower are rounded down to the nearest whole number.
These same rounding rules are also applied, when needed, to arrive at numbers that include a
decimal place in constructing figures and tables. Occasionally, these rounding rules lead to small
discrepancies in the first decimal place when comparing tables and charts for a given question.
