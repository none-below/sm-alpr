Fiscal Year 2025-26 Mid-Year Budget — Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive the Fiscal Year 2025-26 mid-year budget update.
BACKGROUND:
This agenda report provides a financial update on the City’s major funds: the General Fund and the Sewer Fund. However, 
the main focus is on the General Fund, the City's primary operating fund, which accounts for major tax revenues and funds 
essential city services. The report summarizes the City’s mid-year fiscal status by analyzing anticipated revenues and 
expenditures based on data through December 2025 and comparing the projections to the FY 2025-26 budget. The mid-
year projections incorporate FY 2024-25 results, year-to-date actuals, and new data which was not available when the 
budget was originally developed. 
GENERAL FUND
General Fund Revenues 
With over half of the fiscal year’s actual transactions analyzed, the City’s General Fund revenues are now projected to 
slightly exceed the initial budgeted projections. The adopted budget projected total revenues of $175.8 million. Based on 
year-to-date results through December 2025, total revenues are now expected to reach $176.8 million, an increase of 
approximately $1.0 million. Table A below shows the currently projected General Fund revenues for FY 25-26. 
 
 
Table A – FY 25-26 General Fund Revenue Projections
 
The favorable budget variance is primarily due to higher-than-expected real property transfer tax, partially offset by an 
estimated decline in sales tax revenues and the decrease in Vehicle License In-Lieu Fee revenues since the City only 
received 67% of its VLF shortfall. The following analysis provides insight into the primary contributing factors to the overall 
revenue forecast.

PROPERTY TAX - $89.4M ADOPTED | $87.2M PROJECTED 
 
Property tax is the largest and most stable tax revenue for the City, but it is not expected to meet the $89.4 million budget 
projection for FY 2025-26 due to two main factors.
 
Property Tax In Lieu of Vehicle License Fee (VLF) Shortfall
The first and most significant reason property tax is not expected to meet the adopted budget is because the original 
budget assumed the State would pay 100 percent of the VLF shortfall from FY 23-24 due in FY 25-26. Since the State only 
provided the City with 67 percent of this VLF shortfall, the property tax projection is $2.2 million lower.
 
As discussed in previous meetings on the budget, VLF is funded by ERAF and property tax from non-basic-aid school 
districts. Of the 23 school districts in San Mateo County, only five school districts remain as non-basic-aid schools. This has 
led to the persistent VLF shortfalls that require the County to file an annual claim with the State to backfill the prior fiscal 
year’s VLF shortfalls, resulting in a two-year delay in the VLF shortfall payments. 
 
The Countywide VLF shortfall has grown as more school districts shift from non-basic-aid to basic-aid status. In FY 2021-22 
the Countywide shortfall was $32.4 million and by FY 2024-25 it had grown to $119.2 million. As a result, the City’s share 
of the Countywide VLF shortfalls also grew from $1.9 million in FY 2021-22 to $6.6 million in FY 2023-24 and $6.9 million in 
FY 2024-25 as outlined in Table B.

Although the City has not received data from the County on the anticipated FY 25-26 shortfall, the Countywide VLF 
shortfall is expected to continue to grow as the remaining five school districts transition to basic-aid status. The main 
concern with VLF, whether the State will backfill the VLF shortfall or not, has become a more acute issue since the State 
reduced the County’s reimbursement of VLF shortfall funding by 67 percent this year. If the State refuses to pay its VLF 
shortfall obligations, currently there is no other funding source available to the County and cities to fill the funding gap.
 
The City is working with the County and the other cities within the County to develop new solutions to address this issue, 
such as educating communities about the anticipated funding loss and looking at potential legislative fixes. The City, the 
County, and all other cities within the County also filed a lawsuit against the State for withholding VLF shortfall funds.  Staff 
will continue to monitor these efforts and to analyze long-term effects on revenues if discussions do not go well as VLF 
revenues continue to be a major component of the City’s property tax revenue and the VLF shortfall is going to continue to 
grow.  
 
High-Interest Rates
Another issue to keep in mind is interest rates and their effect on property taxes. Over the past 10 years, the City’s real 
property assessed values (AV) have grown annually on average of 6.9 percent. For FY 2025-26, projected AV growth is 3.8 
percent, indicating a slowdown due to high interest rates. County Assessor’s data confirmed that secured property tax 
growth will align with the AV growth. However, if high interest rates persist, future property tax growth may be 
constrained.

SALES TAX - $27.6M ADOPTED | $27.4M PROJECTED 
 Sales tax, the second largest tax revenue source for the General Fund, is particularly susceptible to economic cycles. At 
mid-year, sales tax revenue is projected to remain flat at $27.4 million compared to the previous year’s total of $27.2 
million. This is $.2 million less than projected with less growth than anticipated.
 
Staff developed this updated estimate based on a summary of calendar year 2024 data provided by HdL Companies, the 
City’s sales tax consultant, and quarterly updates through September 2025. The data shows that the City’s sales tax in 
calendar year 2024 was down $.5 million from the previous year. San Mateo’s sales tax base relies heavily on General 
Consumer Goods (GCG), Restaurants, Fuel and Service Stations and Autos in addition to receipts from the County pool. 
GCG held steady in 2024 and into 2025 and Restaurants showed some increase, but the other three categories showed a 
decrease. Autos and Transportation sales appeared to dip in 2024 and the first two quarters of 2025 and Fuel and Service 
Station sales tax declined due to lower gas prices and reduced gas consumption. 
 
In addition, County pool receipts to San Mateo declined in 2024 and the first two quarters of 2025. Online sales tax paid by 
residents within the County is first accumulated at the County Pool and then allocated to cities based on their local share 
of the overall sales tax revenues. If the City’s sales tax from businesses in town is higher in relation to other cities, its 
portion of the County pool will be higher and similarly, if its sales tax is lower in relation to other cities, its share of the 
pool declines. Thus, the reduction in regular sales tax in 2024 and the first two quarters of 2025 reduced the City’s 
allocation from the County Pool for these online sales. 
 
The City’s most recent report for the third quarter of calendar year 2025 shows growth in sales tax revenues, but City staff 
is not including this in our most recent estimate since the growth was based on one-time activities, according to our 
consultant. Staff will continue to monitor this trend and adjust the forecasts as needed.
 
REAL PROPERTY TRANSFER TAX - $8.6M ADOPTED | $11.0M PROJECTED 
 
Real property transfer tax (RPTT), the third largest tax revenue for the General Fund, is highly volatile and subject to 
economic cycles as shown in Table C. At mid-year, regular RPTT (0.5 percent) is projected to increase to $9 million due to 
higher than projected housing activity. At the same time, Measure CC RPTT, the additional 1 percent RPTT that applies to 
properties sold or transferred with a value of $10 million or more, is performing better than budgeted with $1.9 million 
received as of mid-year. Although there is potential for more revenue in the Measure CC category, staff is conservatively 
not projecting any additional funding since no specific sales have been identified at this time. Total RPTT at mid-year is 
projected to be $11.0 million for FY 2025-26.

OTHER GENERAL FUND REVENUES
 
Other General Fund revenues are projected to be on track with budgeted amounts, except one. Investment earnings are 
projected to outperform the original budget by $1.0 million due to favorable investment yields in a high-interest rate 
market. 
Overall, General Fund revenues exhibit resilience and are projected to exceed the original budget by approximately $1 
million in FY 2025-26. Although the increase in revenues is good news, staff is not recommending any budget adjustments 
at this time due to the limited dollar amount.
General Fund Expenditures
 
The adopted FY 2025-26 budget included $187.8 million in General Fund total expenditures (including transfers out). Based 
on January 2026 year-to-date transactions, total expenditures are projected to be $183.7 million, a favorable budget 
variance of $4.1 million.  
Table D – FY 25-26 General Fund Expenditure Projections
The majority of the favorable budget variance currently stems from salary and benefits savings achieved through vacancy 
savings and other levers within the financial sustainability plan. Since local government expenditure budgets 
(appropriations) serve as the legal level of budgetary control, it is common that some level of savings will be realized in any 
fiscal year. Although departmental budgets were analyzed for both underfunded operating needs and anticipated 
budgetary savings, the focus was on ensuring budget adequacy for General Fund operations for the remainder of the fiscal 
year.  
 
General Fund Balance
With the mid-year updates, the General Fund is projected to generate $176.8 million in total revenues and incur $183.7 
million in total expenditures (including transfers out), resulting in a $6.9 million deficit for FY 2025-26.
 
Table E – Estimated General Fund Deficit
 
Due to budget savings from personnel costs and improved revenue projections in certain categories, the projected deficit 
is smaller than the $12.0 million deficit in the adopted budget. The General Fund, though, still ended with a $3 million 
budget shortfall last year, and at mid-year, is projecting another $6.9 million deficit. At the start of FY 2025-26, the General 
Fund beginning balance was $119.5 million (per audited FY 2024-25 financial report). The projected General Fund ending

SEWER FUND
The Sewer Fund has maintained sufficient liquidity to support ongoing operations and critical capital improvement 
projects. The approved 3 percent annual sewer rate increase (effective FY 2023-24 through FY 2027-28) provides necessary 
funding to complete the construction of the new wastewater treatment plant and improve the City’s sewer collection 
system, which will address regulatory requirements and minimize the potential for sanitary sewer overflows. At the same 
time this funding will support ongoing operations of the City’s sewer enterprise and provide a robust debt coverage ratio 
to support the Fund’s ongoing financing of the Clean Water Program.
 
For FY 2025-26, the Sewer Fund budget includes $95.7 million in total revenues, $64.6 million in total expenses, and $29.6 
million of new funding added to the Sewer Fund’s capital budget for capital improvement projects (CIP). In addition, the 
City Council approved two capital project appropriations since July bringing the CIP budget to $31.4 million.
 
Based on a review of the Sewer Fund as of the end of January, revenues are expected to be slightly above budget
