SUBJECT:
Clean Water Program and Wastewater Treatment Plant Upgrade and Expansion — Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an informational update on the Clean Water Program and the Wastewater Treatment Plant Upgrade and 
Expansion project.
BACKGROUND:
The City is currently under a Regional Water Quality Control Board Cease and Desist Order requiring improvements to its 
sewer collection system and Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) to eliminate sanitary sewer overflows and 
unauthorized discharges of untreated or partially treated wastewater to San Francisco Bay. The Clean Water Program 
(CWP) is a $1 billion program that started in 2015 to comply with the Cease and Desist Order. The CWP includes numerous 
capital improvement projects that have either been completed, are currently under construction, or are soon starting 
construction. The projects include improvements to sewers, manholes, and pump stations, the new Underground Flow 
Equalization System (located at the San Mateo County Event Center), and improvements at the WWTP. The WWTP 
Upgrade and Expansion Project (WWTP Project) is the largest of these CWP projects, which will result in new liquid 
treatment facilities for processing wastewater required to meet regulations. 
Since the CWP projects were chartered, CWP’s project budgets, including the WWTP Project, considered the costs 
associated with administrative services, program/project controls, project management, preliminary evaluations, 
engineering services, procurement, construction contracts, construction management, permits, outreach, risk 
contingencies, and escalation. A strategic funding and financing plan to deliver the CWP was executed and included raising 
sewer rates over several years, issuing bonds, and pursuing loans. The CWP successfully received three low-interest rate 
loans from State and/or Federal programs. These loans were able to reduce the fiscal impact of the City’s investment in 
the sewer infrastructure on the San Mateo rate payers. In June 2023, Council approved annual 3 percent inflationary 
sewer rate increase for five years, starting in Fiscal Year 2023-24; without the low-interest rate loans, sewer rate increases 
would have been higher. 
Currently, staff anticipates completing the overall Clean Water Program at the end of this calendar year. In 2017, the 
CWP’s project budgets were baselined and the CWP’s estimate at completion was $991 million. In February 2025, Council 
received an updated $1.028 billion CWP estimate at completion, based on the best available information at the time. 
Between February 2025 and June 2025 when Council adopted the 2025/26 capital budget, the estimate at completion 
went down to $1.025 billion.  Due to risks, changes, delays, and unknowns identified over the past year, the CWP’s current 
estimate at completion has increased to approximately $1.035 billion. The new budget represents an overall increase of 
4.4% from the original program budget in 2017 and can partially be attributed to the Covid 19 pandemic, a period of 
historic inflation, and significant material shortages due to supply chain interruptions, but overall is still a reasonable 
increase for a program of this size. This increased estimate does not require an adjustment to the currently approved 5-

In February 2025, staff also provided Council with details of the WWTP Project, which included construction progress 
updates, causes for delays and budget increases, and updated schedules. At that time, the WWTP Project budget and 
estimate at completion was $597 million. Due to risks, changes, delays, and unknowns identified over the past year, the 
WWTP Project’s current estimate at completion has increased by approximately $5.9 million and is now $603 million, 
which is still subject to change. Construction has been challenging while still progressing forward. Since February 2025, the 
Project has required several significant changes, including changes related to SCADA monitoring system programming, 
force main configuration at the headworks, flow equalization conversion, chlorine contact basin lighting, tower crane 
overrun, stormwater pollution prevention, power backup for critical controls as well as the associated soft costs for 
inspection, construction management, insurance, and engineering services. As a result of the realized and potential change 
orders, as well as schedule challenges, construction expenditures are expected to exceed the previously anticipated 
estimate at completion for the Project. 
To ensure there is sufficient funding to complete the WWTP Project, staff will be proposing a $5.9 million budget increase 
in the coming Fiscal Year 2026-27 Capital Improvement Program to fund the completion of the WWTP Project; this 
increase will align the budget committed to the project account with the WWTP Project’s estimate at completion of $603 
million. This $5.9 million increase includes costs associated with the construction contract ($3.85 million) and extended 
soft costs ($2.05 million). The $3.85 million budget request associated with the construction contract is inclusive of known 
changes and risks, potential delay costs, potential future risks and changes, and contingencies. The $2.05 million budget 
increases associated with the extended soft costs include project management, construction management, specialty 
inspections, owner-controlled insurance program, and engineering services during construction. The $5.9 million budget 
increase is based on information known today and is subject to change as the project progresses closer to completion and 
as the project team further evaluates potential costs such as those related to project delay.  Staff anticipates the WWTP 
project will be complete by June 2026.  
The program also anticipates a potential net budget increase for the collection system projects of $2.51 million that is 
attributed mostly to the increased cost of mechanical vault repairs at the Underground Flow Equalization System and 
resolution of standby generator issues at the Dale Avenue Pump Station, as well as projected cost increases for the 
remaining sewer collection system rehabilitation projects. A net increase in the cost estimated at $1.24 million is 
attributed to the Immediate Action Projects (IAP) Package III project due to unforeseen subsurface conditions and some 
closeout costs for the IAP Package II project, which are outside the main WWTP Project.
Based on the project cash flow estimates for the remainder of the current Fiscal Year 2025-26, there is sufficient budget 
and existing authorized construction contract contingencies to keep the projects progressing. Staff anticipates returning to 
Council after the Fiscal Year 2026-27 budget is adopted with requests to increase the construction contract contingencies 
as needed to complete the various projects in the CWP. The planned $9.65 million budget request is inclusive of the 
anticipated construction contract contingency amounts; however, waiting to make this request in the new fiscal year will 
allow the construction management team to further review all the potential construction contract changes with the intent 
to mitigate as much of the risk as possible and hopefully reduce the overall request. 
As the Clean Water Program draws closer to completion, staff is looking to the post-CWP program of capital needs, 
including anticipated improvements to the solids processing facilities (portion of the old WWTP that will remain in service) 
as well as developing an updated Sewer Master Plan that will evaluate future condition or capacity needs within the 
collection system, including the many sewer pump stations, after completion of all of the CWP projects.  
BUDGET IMPACT:
The budget information included in the update will be incorporated into the City's overall future Fiscal Year 2026-31 
Capital Improvement Program, which will be presented to Council for approval in June.
