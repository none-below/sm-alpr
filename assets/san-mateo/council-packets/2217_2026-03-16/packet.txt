SUBJECT:
City Council SMART Activities Priority List — Direction
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction on the draft Fiscal Year 2026-27 SMART Activities Priority List.
BACKGROUND:
The City Council goal setting process includes a three-step deliberation process resulting in the Adopted City Council 
Priority List for each fiscal year. The first step in the deliberation process is the Blue Sky Workshop, which was held on 
February 7, 2026. During this time, the City Council received a status update on its 2025/2026 Fiscal Year Priority List, 
which contained 41 priorities within three timeframe categories. The categories represent expected completion within one 
fiscal year, 2-5 fiscal years, or 5 or more years. 
With the adoption of the City’s first Strategic Plan on September 15, 2025, the Blue Sky goal setting workshop folded in the 
City’s Strategic Plan into the City Council goal setting process. The Strategic Plan offers a comprehensive framework for 
City Council to utilize when making decisions for both long-term and immediate priorities. It enables the City to move in an 
intentional, strategic direction over a longer timeframe. This is critically important to ensure resources are used to carry 
out City Council priorities with the utmost efficiency and impact. 
The City is now transitioning away from the three timeframe categories for the Council priorities and implementing 
“SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound) Activities” that represent the City Council’s 
priorities. The intention of the SMART Activities is to provide intentional, achievable actions that can be completed within 
the upcoming fiscal year. This allows staff to better report to Council and the community on progress made. If progress is 
unlikely in the upcoming fiscal year, it’s important to not lose sight of these actions, so staff also created “2-5 Year 
Objectives” that will be revisited in the future. 
Staff made recommendations to remove completed priorities, convert incomplete priorities to SMART Activities, and move 
priorities into the 2-5 Year Objectives category based on progress to date and work remaining. At the workshop, Council 
reviewed the 41 priorities and directed staff to remove, convert to SMART Activities, or move to the 2-5 Year Category. 
In addition, the Council also heard a report on staff’s existing work plan items that are unrelated to formalized Council 
priorities but are a part of each department’s daily work. Further, each Councilmember presented three proposed new 
priorities to be integrated into the list of Adopted City Council priorities for the new fiscal year and deliberated on whether 
they should be retained. 
The March 16, 2026 meeting marks the second step in the process when the Council will be reviewing and providing final

Dear Mayor Loraine and Members of the City Council, 
 
We are writing in support of the proposed strategic priority #54 for the City of San Mateo in 2026. 
 
The issue of increasing vehicle speeds and severe crashes involving bicycles and pedestrians, including 
recent pedestrian fatalities, is an area of great concern for the Central Neighborhood Association 
(CNA).  We continue to advocate for speed humps, LPI - leading pedestrian interval - 6 second delays, 
(RRFB) - rectangular rapid flashing beacons at crosswalks, and other traffic calming measures.  Such 
measures benefit from an ease of installation and low expense in order to make safe streets for children, 
seniors, dog walkers and all of our residents of San Mateo. 
 
Our experience in the Central Neighborhood is that speed humps are very important traffic mitigation 
solutions, as proven on Fremont Street and Ellsworth Street.  We need these speed humps on more 
streets in our neighborhood.  The current process for requesting a speed hump is too restrictive and 
should not be dependent on a high response rate from residents to mailers in order to address safety 
issues quickly.  Also, In order to qualify for a speed hump, speeds must be registered at 10 mph over the 
posted 25 mph limit.  Driving at 35 mph or higher in our residential neighborhood is a very high bar 
required in order to qualify for a speed hump.  This is a very dangerous requirement that limits protection 
of pedestrians and bicyclists. We need a less complicated and faster process to qualify for traffic 
calming. 
 
CNA actively promotes neighborhood safety. We advocated for a safer crossing of 4th Avenue at Grant 
Street which resulted in the installation of bollards on 4th and more recently, due to increased traffic, we 
were able to have the City install rectangular rapid flashing beacons (RRFB) for pedestrians who cross 
4th Avenue at this intersection.  We were recently able to successfully ask the City to implement a 6 
second (LPI) leading pedestrian interval at 9th at Delaware and 9th at B Street in the Downtown.  This 
makes crossing safer for our residents and, especially, students who attend the nearby Sunnybrae 
Elementary School and Borel Middle School.  We advocate for sidewalk repairs and green pedestrian
