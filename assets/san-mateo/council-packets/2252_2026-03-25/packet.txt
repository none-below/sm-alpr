RECOMMENDATION:
Recommend to the City Council adoption of the proposed Fiscal Year 2026-27 fee schedule updates for Parks and 
Recreation facilities and miscellaneous fees as provided in Attachment 1 of the Administrative Report.
BACKGROUND:
Each year the City reviews the fee schedule and adjusts where deemed appropriate. Generally, fees are adjusted based on 
the Consumer Price Index (CPI) for the previous year. The Finance Department identified the official CPI for this fiscal year 
to be 2.5%. Fees have all been rounded to whole dollar amounts after calculating the CPI increase. To support the 
department’s financial sustainability goal language has been added to facilitate more robust sponsorship program for City 
sponsored events. The approved fee schedule is available on the City’s website. The recommended fee changes for the 
Parks and Recreation Department are detailed in Attachment 1.
Below is a summary of the significantly recommended fee changes and language updates by category.
Park In Lieu Fees / Impact Fees: 
Park In Lieu fees/ Impact fees for residential construction have increased in the city due to a 4.7% increase in median sales 
price of homes in the City of San Mateo.
Recreation (including facility rental fees):
One new fee for the Recreation Division for department sponsored special events sponsors, booth and food vendors. 
These fees will vary depending on the size of the event and will be at the discretion of the department head.
Arbor:
Arbor fees have been moved from calendar year increase to fiscal year to align with other building and planning permit 
fees.
Golf: 
The following per round fees have each been increased in excess of CPI to align analysis of the local market.

S E R V I N G  T H E  P A R K S  A N D  R E C R E A T I O N  I N D U S T R Y
1
The following shall serve as the policy guidance for establishing a Recreation Activities Cost Recovery Plan, 
developing annual activity budgets, and establishing fees to be charged for recreation activities.
Policy Statement
The City of San Mateo Parks and Recreation Department Financial Sustainability Strategy intends to create 
organizational resilience by way of logical, intentional, and thoughtful guidelines for investment and spending 
decisions. The strategy encourages revenue generation strategies and practices that are fair, equitable, and 
responsible.
In alignment with the City of San Mateo’s Strive San Mateo General Plan 2040 Parks and Recreation Department 
goal COS-9 - Provide stable and adequate operational and capital funding for the parks and recreation system, the 
Department’s financial sustainability plan seeks to balance community investment with financial discipline to 
insure long-term sustainability of the Department. This policy guides the allocation of City resources to allow for 
the most equitable benefit to the community.
Subsidy/Cost Recovery
Cost recovery refers to offsetting the costs (expenses) of delivering services by way of revenues generated from 
fees and charges, sponsorships, donations, grants, and other alternative revenue streams. Alternatively, subsidy 
represent a tax source into the City. It is granted by a governmental entity; typically to remove some type of 
burden and often considered to be in the overall interest of the public. It is also given to promote a social good or 
an economic policy.
For example, a cost recovery level of 75% simply means that for each dollar spent on a service, 75-cents are 
generated from a revenue source (i.e., fees) with the remaining 25-cents covered by subsidies (i.e., taxes).
Service Categories
The development of categories which include like services are important when it comes to justifiable and equitable 
allocation of subsidy, cost recovery levels, and assignment of budget and general ledger lines to account for a 
category’s fiscal performance. 
The benefits of this type of approach are two-fold. First, it is inefficient for the Department to determine cost 
recovery expectations by each individual service including facility, activity, or event. Secondly, categorizing by 
“type of service” or “likeness of service” discourages attempts to determine fees and charges (and therefore cost 
recovery decisions) based upon special interests, age-based services, or individual values.

San Mateo Parks and Recreation provides many services annually to the community. The following Service 
Categories represent the Department’s service menu and include Service Category definitions as well as example 
services. 
Upon completion of a 12-month fiscal period, the City identified subsidy levels for each of the service categories. 
Those subsidy percentages, or investment choices, are listed next to each of the service categories.)
1. Parks and Facilities - Access to parks, park amenities, and recreation facilities for all community members. 
Examples: City of San Mateo Parks such as Casanova, Beresford Park, and Trinta.(95-100%)
2. Human Services - Services designed to support independence and community connection by providing or 
linking resources for those in need. Examples: Senior Transportation Services, Senior Lunch Services, 
Community Lectures. (90-100%)
3. City-Wide Events - Large-scale events appealing to a broad community segment regardless of age, ability, or 
family composition. These intensive events typically occur annually. Examples: Summer Concert Series, Fourth 
of July, Movies in the Park, Winter Wonderland. (75-100%)
4. Child Care - In-house childcare facilitated by City staff. Examples: Summer Camps, Afterschool Care, Care on 
No-School Days. (40-70%)
5. Beginner Activities - Classes, clinics, workshops, and other led/instructed activities aimed at acquiring or 
learning a new skill. Examples: Beginning Dance, Fitness, Swim Lessons. (25-50%)
6. Drop-In Activities - Self-directed activities that do not require registration or instruction but include 
supervision or oversight by staff and/or volunteers. Examples: Rec Lap Swim, Open Gym, Games, Crafts, Senior 
Movies.(25-50%)
7. Camps Contracted - Camps operated by third parties during school breaks, providing specialized enrichment 
for children. Examples: Science/Tech Camps, Musical Theater Camps, Dance Camps, Athletic Camps, Art Camps. 
(0-20%)
8. Competitive Activities - Organized competitive leagues without guided instruction. Examples: Adult Softball, 
Adult Volleyball.(0-20%)
9. Advanced Activities - Classes, clinics, workshops, and other led/instructed activities designed to advance or 
master a skill. Examples: Advanced Athletic Classes, Advanced Martial Art, Advanced Art. (0-20%)
10. Rentals - Reservable outdoor/indoor spaces and facilities available for exclusive use by individuals or groups. 
Examples: Recreation/Community Center Rooms, Sports Fields, Picnic Areas. (0-20%)
11. Private/Semi-Private Activities - Individualized classes, clinics, workshops, and other led/instructed activities 
offered in a private or semi-private setting to meet specific needs, interests or skill sets. Examples: Private 
Music Lessons, Tech Tutoring, Private Swim Lessons. (0-20%)
Note: Service Categories listed above are in order from those perceived to be Common Good Services (#1) to those 
seen as providing a more Exclusive Benefit Services (#11) as ranked by Department staff as well as San Mateo Parks 
and Recreation Commission representatives.

RECOMMENDATION:
Review and comment on Parks and Recreation’s Proposed Five-Year Capital Improvement Plan for FY 2027-2031.
BACKGROUND:
As part of the Capital Improvement Program (CIP) budget development process, the Parks and Recreation Department 
prepare funding requests that capture five-year capital project needs. Total project costs are developed and matched with 
appropriate funding sources, such as the General Fund or Measure S that can be used for any purpose and special 
revenues such as those restricted for park and recreation development purposes. New and ongoing capital project 
requests are prioritized and recommended for funding through review and analysis by the City Manager, Assistant City 
Manager, Public Works Director, Parks and Recreation Director, and Finance Director. The prioritization process includes 
evaluating the criticality of a project regarding public health and safety, maintaining the viability of a City asset or facility, 
whether projects are already underway with existing investments, and/or whether a project can be phased to spread costs 
over a longer period. The primary focus of the five-year CIP budget development process is balancing available General 
Fund, special funding measures, and Parks and Recreation Revenue Fund funding with project budget needs, while 
determining if future deficits require additional revenue sources. For reference, previous Capital Improvement Plans are 
available on the City’s website. 
 
Prioritizing Projects. Staff uses a variety of prioritization methods when recommending projects to Council for funding. 
Included are the following methods: 
▪ Available funding and funding restrictions. For example, park-in-lieu fees can only fund park and recreation projects. 
▪ Previously identified Council and community priorities. 
▪ The amount of investment already made into a project. 
▪ The availability and time limitations on grants or other matching funds. 
▪ The location of the project – ensures that all areas of the city receive investment in nearby infrastructure. 
▪ Maintenance deficiencies. These projects are classified in six categories: Priority 1 is currently critical; Priority 2 is 
potentially critical; Priority 3 is necessary but not yet critical; Priority 4 is recommended; Priority 5 is appearance; and 
Priority 6 is where the infrastructure does not meet current codes or standards but can wait for other nearby projects. 
Revenue. The CIP process also projects revenue for the Parks and Recreation Revenue Fund, which is the primary funding 
source for parks and recreation facility improvements. Due to changes to State Law, almost Park and Recreation Revenue 
Funds are now collected at the time of occupancy. Staff projected revenues for the next one to two years will be close to 
zero and department will use funds that have previously been collected to bridge this gap in revenue collection. The City is
