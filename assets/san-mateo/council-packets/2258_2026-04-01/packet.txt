BACKGROUND:
On March 2, 2026, the City Council formally adopted the 2026 City of San Mateo Legislative Platform. The Platform was 
developed through a coordinated, cross-departmental process led by the City Manager's Office in partnership with CPPG, 
the City's state legislative consultant. The Platform reflects input from multiple City departments including Community 
Development, Planning and Housing, Public Works, Parks and Recreation, Economic Development and Sustainability, the 
Police Department and Homelessness Services, and the San Mateo Consolidated Fire Department. Additionally, elements 
of key City-guiding documents including the City's Housing Element, Strategic Plan, General Plan 2040, adopted equity and 
inclusion policies, and prior City Council resolutions were also included.
The 2026 Legislative Platform is organized around major policy subject areas, including Community Development and 
Housing; Transportation and Public Works; Parks, Recreation, and Community Services; Governmental Transparency and 
Public Meeting Access; Utilities, Infrastructure, and Climate Adaptation; Administration and Fiscal Sustainability; Public 
Safety and Homelessness Services; and Fire and Emergency Medical Services. Each section contains guiding principles 
intended to preserve local authority, promote fiscal sustainability, advance equity and inclusivity, protect public safety, 
and support the City's ability to deliver essential services to the community.
The Platform is intended to function as a living document, reviewed and updated periodically to reflect evolving City 
priorities, changes in state policy, and emerging legislative issues. Adoption does not limit the City Council's authority to 
act on matters outside the Platform; rather, it provides an organizing framework that supports consistent, timely, and 
informed engagement with state legislative and regulatory developments.
The City Council Legislative Subcommittee meets periodically throughout the legislative year to review and provide 
direction on active state legislation affecting San Mateo.
Legislative Calendar Context
The Legislature is currently in session, with Spring Recess beginning upon adjournment on March 25, 2026. The Legislature 
reconvenes on April 6, 2026. Key upcoming milestones include the Governor's May Revision of the state budget (mid-
May), the Legislature's deadline to pass the primary budget bill (June 15), the Governor's deadline to sign the primary 
budget bill (June 30), and the start of Summer Recess on July 2, 2026.

Howard Jarvis Taxpayers Association Initiative: Impact on Local Tax Authority
The Howard Jarvis Taxpayers Association (HJTA) has submitted a statewide ballot initiative (AG File No. 25-0006) that, if 
approved by voters, would significantly curtail local tax authority in California. HJTA has claimed submission of over one 
million signatures, with 886,000 valid signatures required to qualify. The Secretary of State is expected to announce 
qualification status in mid-April 2026. If the measure qualifies, the Legislature is expected to engage HJTA in negotiations 
toward a legislative resolution, likely through the state budget process.
The initiative would affect local tax authority in the following ways:
Transfer Tax Authority: Under current law, charter cities may set their own transfer tax rates above the standard 
documentary tax rate. For example, the City of San Mateo currently assesses rates of 0.5% and 1.5%. Under the initiative, 
charter city transfer taxes would be permanently eliminated, leaving only the standard documentary split of 0.055%.
Special Tax Vote Threshold: Under current law, a simple majority through citizen initiative is sufficient to enact a special 
tax, as upheld by recent court rulings. Under the initiative, a two-thirds supermajority would be required for all special 
taxes, with no exceptions.
Existing Parcel Taxes: Parcel taxes currently valid under prior court decisions approving simple majority votes would be 
invalidated on December 31 of the second year following statewide passage, resulting in an estimated loss of 
approximately $300 million annually statewide.
Local Democratic Control: The initiative would override locally voter-approved measures. As an example, San Mateo's 
Measure CC was approved by 71.8% of voters but could be invalidated under the initiative's provisions.
LEGISLATIVE POSITIONS — DEFINITIONS
Support: A Support position indicates to the Legislature, regulatory agencies, and other stakeholders that the City is in 
favor of the proposed legislation in question.
Sponsor: A Sponsor position indicates to the Legislature, regulatory agencies, and other stakeholders that the City is not 
only in strong support of the legislation in question but is publicly leading the advocacy effort. This position is only used 
when a Member of the Legislature has agreed to author a specific legislative proposal on behalf of the City.
Oppose Unless Amended: An Oppose Unless Amended position indicates to the Legislature, regulatory agencies, and 
other stakeholders that the City is against the proposed legislation in question, unless requested changes are adopted to 
address the City's policy-based concerns.
Oppose: An Oppose position indicates to the Legislature, regulatory agencies, and other stakeholders that the City is 
against the proposed legislation in question. This position is often used only when amendments or other policy 
compromises cannot be reached.
Neutral: A Neutral position indicates to the Legislature, regulatory agencies, and other stakeholders that the City has 
formally removed a prior adopted position due to changes made to the legislation or proposal. A Neutral position 
therefore indicates that the City is impartial on the legislation in question.
Watch: A Watch position is an internal designation indicating that the City is closely monitoring and evaluating the 
legislation in question. Should factors change, the City reserves the right to adopt a formal external position.

SB 922 (Laird) — Vehicles: Local Agency Charges: Use of Streets or Highways: As amended March 11, 2026, this bill would 
clarify the decision in Rogers v. City of Redlands (2025), in which the Court of Appeal held that embedding road repair 
costs into solid waste rates violated the Vehicle Code's prohibition on local charges for the privilege of using streets or 
highways. SB 922 would clarify that this prohibition applies specifically to weight-based charges, and would explicitly affirm 
that local agencies may impose fees, charges, or surcharges to recover the costs of street maintenance and repair, as well 
as other related public service costs, without such charges being characterized as prohibited taxes or charges for the use of 
streets. The bill also removes outdated statutory references and makes technical corrections.
Status: Senate Floor
Recommended Position: Support
Platform Statement: Support legislation and ballot measures that protect the City's fiscal health, responsibilities, legal 
authority, and operations; uphold existing City policies, prior actions, or current City Council strategic priorities; and 
enhance programs, services, or infrastructure that benefit San Mateo residents.

Senate Committee on Local Government Analysis of SB 922
•
First, it seeks to restore local agencies’ ability to include road repair costs in their waste hauling fees, which 
the Rogers decision prohibited.  
•
Second, it seeks to limit the prohibition on fees for the privilege of using roads to weight fees so that other 
charges do not face legal challenges. 
•
States that fees, charges, or surcharges for local agencies to recover costs of street maintenance, repair, 
and other costs to provide public services do not count as a tax, permit fee, or other charge for the privilege 
of using its streets or highways.  
•
Explicitly allows local agencies to impose such a fee, charge, or surcharge.  The measure also allows local 
agencies to impose taxes, permit fees, or other charges for the privilege of using its streets or highways, 
provided they are not based on weight.

AB 1557 (Papan):Vehicles: electric bicycles. 
This bill would redefine the e-bicycle classes, changing the speed limit to 16 mph for both class 1 and class 2 
bicycles while keeping the power limit for the motor at 750 watts. Electric bicycles manufactured before 
January 1, 2027, meeting existing standards, would retain their classification. The bill also imposes 
restrictions on manufacturers and retailers, prohibiting them from selling electric bicycles with motors 
exceeding 750 watts or equipping class 1 and class 2 bikes with motors over 250 watts that enable speeds 
over 16 mph. Violations would result in civil penalties up to $15,000 for a first offense and $50,000 for
