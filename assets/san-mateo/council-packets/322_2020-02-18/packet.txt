3. US-101/Peninsula Avenue Interchange Project Update 
Public Works Director Brad Underwood and Senior Engineer Bethany Lopez, provided an update on the US-
101/Peninsula Avenue Interchange Project presenting a project overview, traffic analysis, design concepts, right-
of-way issues and asked for direction on potential next steps. Council asked questions of staff.  
Public Comment - Geno Caccia, business and building owner on N. Amphlett, stated the potentially affected 
business have been kept in the dark and it appears the City is not fully committed to getting this project done. 
Anthony Caccia stated less than a week notification that the City was looking at this project was not sufficient. 
Lynn Lenardon stated she was 45 years old when this process started and now she is 61 and they still don’t know 
what is going on; the shadow of this happening has kept the property values artificially low. Tom Carney stated 
traffic movement has changed in the past 25 years and needs to be looked at with fresh data. Austin 
Reinders stated not to just focus on commuters needs and traffic, but also consider the businesses. Joanne 
Bennett stated concerns with impacts on businesses and the need not to eliminate any parking, noting that there 
are residents as well as businesses in the affected area. Diana Petit stated a need for traffic study and noted 
there are three generations living in the area. Rick Swartz, owner of Nini's Coffee Shop, N. Idaho Street, stated 
concern that it is still not known if this project is going to happen or if it is still up in the air.  
Mayor Goethals acknowledged the impacts from being in limbo on this project has been for businesses and 
resident. He clarified that this is not just a traffic issue, the core issue is safety. This is one of those very difficult 
decisions about what is the greater good, acknowledging a change like this comes at a big cost to people in the 
room and how many people will stand to benefit from the change also has to be weighed.  
Council Member Bonilla concurred with Goethals statement and highlighted the negative impacts from the 
current ramps from Highway 101. He noted it’s not just about automobile traffic, that more people are riding 
bikes and walking, and he concurs with the need for the first step to study feasibility of the whole project. 
Council Member Rodriguez stated this project is less about traffic and more about public safety and is a serious 
decision. He noted that Vision Zero effort is to eliminate conditions that cause traffic deaths and injuries, and this 
interchange has serious issues. He stated that he is in favor of moving forward with the study. He is concerned 
with the lack of communication and pledged to ensure to step up the efforts to keep stakeholders informed.  
Council Member Lee stated priority order for her is life, liberty, then property and she is very concerned with the 
number of accidents there and noted she personally witnessed a child struck by a car. She is interested in moving 
forward in a way that minimizes impact to the area. At the present time, she is seeking more information on the 
project and the property processes before supporting. 
Council Member Papan noted for the record that a project this big taking this long is not unusual, public projects 
take a long time to make happen – just working out funding sources takes a long time. She acknowledged the 
City needs to be better on communicating on this process and expressed concerned about the changes in the 
neighborhood and with the process.

SUBJECT:
1 Transit Center – Lease Amendment 
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve the second amendment to the lease with Rise Wood Fire, LLC, authorize the City Manager to execute the 
amendment in substantially the form presented, and authorize the City Manager to approve future revisions to the 
approved lease provided that the revisions to the approved tenant improvements are valued at less than $50,000 or 
changes to the site plan comprise less than 50 square feet. 
BACKGROUND:
On April 15, 2019, Council approved a long-term lease with Rise Wood Fire, LLC (also known as Rise Pizza), with an initial 
fifteen-year lease with two additional five-year options, landlord assistance of $130,000 towards the costs of tenant 
improvements, and six-months of rent abatement. On September 16, 2019, Council approved the first amendment to the 
lease, which increased the landlord assistance to $275,000, extended the rent abatement to eighteen months, and 
clarified the tenant’s responsibility to pay prevailing wage for the implementation of the tenant improvements.
Staff is recommending that Council approve a second amendment to the lease in order to provide for minor revisions to 
the approved detailed floor plan for the outdoor patio and coffee kiosk area (included as Attachment 1). The revision 
minimally increases the patio area in order to extend the patio to align with the existing posts for the Transit Center 
canopy so that they can be used as part of the framing for the patio. The City’s facilities staff have reviewed the proposal 
and do not believe it will not compromise the structural integrity of the posts.
In addition to this change to the patio dimensions, Rise has identified a few other changes to its proposed tenant 
improvements from what was described in the first lease amendment in Exhibit D.  The changes requested are as follows:
Adding up to 156 square feet to the patio area
Increasing the size of the bar/breakfast kiosk to up to 160 square feet. 
Adding a 570 square foot covered structure over patio (including skylights, lighting and fire sprinklers)
Changing the materials of the patio walls from glass to polycarbonate (or other similar materials)
 
Staff is also seeking authority from Council to allow the City Manager to approve future adjustments in the detailed floor 
plan valuing less than $50,000 in tenant improvement costs, changes under 50 square feet, or substitutions in construction 
materials for other similar materials. These parameters do not substantially change the project while allowing Rise Pizza 
flexibility to make minor changes expeditiously. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for approving the second amendment to the lease with Rise Pizza.

SUBJECT:
Investment Advisory Services - Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with Chandler Asset Management for Investment Advisory Services and authorize the Finance 
Director to execute the agreement in substantially the form presented.
BACKGROUND:
Currently, the Finance Director/City Treasurer, with the assistance of staff, manages approximately 78% of the City's cash 
and investment portfolio, which has averaged to be $205 million during the past twelve months.  The remaining 22% of 
the portfolio is managed on a discretionary basis by a qualified investment advisory firm, with the City maintaining control 
of its investment program by establishing the guidelines and policies to which the adviser must adhere.  
In April 2015, the City entered into a three-year agreement with two one-year extension options with PFM Asset 
Management, the City's current investment advisory firm. The City has exercised the two extensions, the second of which 
ends on March 31, 2020.  
On October 14, 2019, the Finance Department issued a Request for Proposals on the City's website and Public Purchase, an 
online platform that provides bid notifications for governmental agencies in the procurement process.  Proposals from five 
firms were received by the November 8, 2019 deadline.  Members of the City’s Investment Advisory Committee, the 
Treasury and Debt Manager, and the Finance Director reviewed the five proposals to assess the quality of the proposal, 
professional experience, staff qualifications, and cost. As such, of the five firms that submitted a proposal, two firms 
advanced to the second phase of the selection process and participated in the interviews on January 9, 2020.  The City's 
interview panel consisted of the Chair of the Investment Advisory Committee, the City's Finance Director, and the Treasury 
and Debt Manager.
Careful review and comparison of each of the firms was conducted based on the responses to the criteria outlined in the 
RFP.  The two firms invited to interview were PFM Asset Management and Chandler Asset Management.  The interview 
process provided the opportunity for the firms to further present their experience, services, and investment philosophies.  
The interview panel unanimously agreed to recommend Chandler Asset Management as the vendor of choice.  
The professional services agreement, included as Attachment 1, is for Chandler Asset Management to provide investment 
advisory services as detailed in the scope of work for three years, beginning April 1, 2020, and ending March 31, 2023, with 
the City's option to extend the agreement annually for two additional years.  More information on the firm's investing and 
compliance procedures, philosophy, and services can be found in Chandler's interview presentation, which is included as 
Attachment 2.

SUBJECT:
San Mateo County Event Center Pari-Mutuel Tax Revenue Memorandum of Understanding – Termination 
RECOMMENDATION:
Authorize the City Manager to send written notice to terminate the Memorandum of Understanding with the San Mateo 
Count Event Center, in which the City provides certain municipal services to the Event Center at no cost in exchange for 
pari-mutuel tax revenue.
BACKGROUND:
In August 2018, the City of San Mateo (City) and the San Mateo County Event Center (Event Center) entered into a 
Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) under which the Event Center agreed to remit to City a tax payment in the amount 
of 0.33% of pari-mutuel wagers placed at the Jockey Club to the City. In exchange, the City would provide the Event Center 
with “ordinary and traditional municipal services” at no charge.  These services have included police, fire (including fire 
inspection), traffic control, and sewer.  
This arrangement was initially favorable to the City, as the pari-mutuel revenue received exceeded the cost of providing 
services.  More recently, however, pari-mutuel revenue has been on the decline, while the cost of providing City services 
has increased.  This increase has been especially impactful for sewer services as rates have risen to support the needs of 
the Clean Water Program.  As such, staff expects that the cost to provide services will exceed revenues received in 2019-
20.  With the cost and revenue trends not expected to change over time, staff believes it is in the City’s best interests to 
terminate the MOU with the Event Center.
The MOU states that either party may terminate for any reason with 120 days’ written notice. Staff recommends providing 
the Event Center written notice at this time so as to coordinate with the end of the fiscal year.
BUDGET IMPACT:
Termination of the MOU with the Event Center will forgo approximately $150,000 in revenue, but will position the City to 
invoice the Event Center for all of the ordinary and traditional municipal services beginning in fiscal year 2020-21.  This will 
allow the City to fully recover the cost of providing services to the Event Center.

not result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(5).)

The City Attorney’s Office determined that the City is not legally able to charge a reduced fee for projects involving a 
“change of use” and has determined the parking in-lieu fee is an impact fee subject to the restrictions of the state’s 
Mitigation Fee Act. This requires that all projects pay their share of impact fees.  Fees must be the same for all projects so 
that fees collected from one project do not subsidize fees required for others.   Due to this determination, and to support 
City Council’s desire to reduce the impact of parking in-lieu fees for projects converting existing buildings to new uses, staff 
has researched additional options. 
An area designated as the Primary Benefit Zone (PBZ) within the CPID was created in the late 1980s and is depicted on the 
map included in Attachment 1.  Each property within the larger CPID boundary was charged a minimum uniform amount 
for payment of bonds utilized to build parking structures.  Properties within the PBZ paid an additional parking deficiency 
assessment. In 1989, an amendment to section 27.64.100 of the Zoning Code revised the fee structure within the PBZ to 
provide a credit of 10 parking spaces for projects that convert existing building spaces to accommodate new uses.  Since 
the inception of the assessment, staff estimates that properties have contributed enough funds through the assessment to 
have covered the cost for 10 parking spaces. This supports the credit of 10 parking spaces that are given to projects within 
the PBZ.
In practice, when a project identified as a “change of use” is proposed within the Primary Benefit Zone, the required 
parking is calculated based on the CPID parking requirements contained in the Zoning Code. If the additional parking

Public comment for this topic has additionally included requests for the City to consider a reduction in parking

measures are considered in the traffic impact analysis for each project, and the estimated trip generation rates are 
reduced based on the estimated reduction from TDM measures. However, parking requirements for projects is not 
currently altered by the TDM measures. Parking reduction for TDM measures is allowed in some cities, including 
Burlingame, Palo Alto, and Mountain View. Evaluating potential parking requirement reductions for projects 
implementing certain TDM measures may identify Municipal Code and Zoning Code amendments required to 
implement this process.

SUBJECT:
Transportation Analysis Policy Development - Introduction
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive information on the transportation policy development effort to comply with Senate Bill 743, changing traffic 
impact analysis from level of service to vehicle miles traveled methodology, and provide direction to staff.
BACKGROUND:
The purpose of this agenda report is to familiarize City Council with the changes to the California Environmental Quality 
Act (CEQA) as a result of the passage of California Senate Bill 743 (Steinberg, 2013) (SB 743). This report is also intended to 
provide the Council with the scope of work and schedule for the City’s work to comply with SB 743 by the mandated date 
of July 1, 2020, and to provide an opportunity to discuss and provide comments. A study session is scheduled with the 
Planning Commission on February 25, 2020 to review this information.  
Transportation analysis conducted by the City under CEQA has historically evaluated Level of Service (LOS) and vehicle 
delay at intersections to assess localized traffic impacts as a result of planned development. The City transportation 
policies used for the CEQA analysis are contained in the Circulation Element of the current General Plan 2030. Specifically, 
Policy C2.1 identifies the City’s standard as “Maintain a Level of Service no worse than mid LOS D, average delay of 45.0 
seconds, as the acceptable Level of Service for all intersections within the City.” The potential transportation impact that 
might be caused by proposed land development is evaluated by conducting Transportation Impact Analyses (TIA).  TIAs 
assess future conditions of the roadway network both with and without the project while incorporating future growth 
estimates. The TIAs compare the estimated incremental impact from the project to the City-adopted policies and, if the 
project impacts exceed City policy thresholds, traffic mitigation measures can also be evaluated and appropriate impact 
fees can be assessed to the project.
Under SB 743, the state is seeking to “…balance the needs of congestion management with statewide goals related to infill 
development, promotion of public health through active transportation, and reduction of greenhouse gas emissions.” To 
this end, SB 743 eliminates LOS, vehicle delay, or other similar measures of vehicular capacity or congestion as the metric 
to assess significant transportation impacts under CEQA. 
To align transportation analysis with state goals for climate change and active transportation, the Governor’s Office of 
Policy and Research (OPR) developed guidelines for new metrics to utilize in CEQA transportation analyses. OPR published 
a final technical advisory, based on stakeholder engagement and feedback, during December 2018 that identifies Vehicle 
Miles Traveled (VMT) as the required metric for transportation analysis under CEQA. 
VMT analysis identifies how projects affect the broader multimodal transportation network (e.g., car, transit, bicycle, 
pedestrian), accessibility, and emissions. Analyses of VMT include all modes of transportation, and resulting mitigation

--- page 184 ---

measures focus on transportation demand management (TDM) measures rather than improvements to traffic operations 
or infrastructure to accommodate additional traffic.  Common TDM measures include improved pedestrian or bicycle 
facilities, expanded transit options, and on-site amenities to reduce trips. As such, the mitigation measures to reduce VMT 
associated with a project increase access to active transportation modes and reduce greenhouse gas emissions attributed 
to transportation.  In contrast, use of local LOS and the assessment of traffic impacts near a proposed project only 
addresses the impacts to the capacity of the transportation network and does not necessarily include all modes of 
transportation, accessibility, or emissions.
To comply with SB 743, jurisdictions are required to adopt CEQA transportation analysis policies based on VMT no later 
than July 1, 2020. SB 743 does not prevent jurisdictions from continuing to analyze delay or LOS as part of General Plans, 
fee programs, or general traffic monitoring.
City staff, in collaboration with Kittelson & Associates, Inc., will be evaluating methodologies and potential thresholds that 
meet the needs of the City and its community while complying with the requirements of SB 743.  The staff presentation at 
this meeting will include information regarding:

SB 743 Legislation and OPR Guidelines,

How VMT is calculated,

Examples of what other cities and agencies in California are doing to comply with SB 743,

How the traffic impact study guidelines will be modified to include a blend of both traditional LOS-based metrics and 
VMT,

How TDM measures can be used to mitigate VMT impacts and trip reduction,

What types of tools may be developed to simplify this process, and

How this work ties in with the ongoing General Plan Update and the City travel demand model update.
Staff intends to present to the Planning Commission during late spring to discuss the proposed VMT policy and obtain 
feedback and comments. Following the presentation to the Planning Commission, staff will present the proposed policy to 
City Council during June 2020 for review and adoption. The resulting VMT policy will be coordinated with the General Plan 
2040 work currently underway, and the new policy will align with the goals and policies of the updated Circulation Element 
contained therein.
