SUBJECT:
San Mateo Public Library Foundation Operating Lease - Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an amendment to the Property Lease and Operating Agreement with the San Mateo Public Library Foundation to 
extend the lease for an additional year through March 31, 2021; and authorize the City Librarian to execute the 
amendment in substantially the form presented.
BACKGROUND:
The San Mateo Public Library Foundation (SMPLF) leases office space in the Main Library to accommodate Foundation 
employees who perform fund development duties in support of the Library Foundation’s mission.  The Commercial 
Property Lease and Operating Agreement between the City of San Mateo and San Mateo Public Library Foundation was 
signed on December 14, 2007.  Subsequent lease extensions through March 31, 2020 have been approved by the City 
Council.  This Amendment represents a one-year extension of the lease agreement through March 31, 2021.
In fiscal year 2018-19, the Library Foundation awarded $300,000 for support of programs and collections. The successful 
2018 Author Gala raised funds for space enhancements in the Children’s Library. The 2019 Gala raised funds to support 
and expand the Library's successful Technology Device Lending Program.  
BUDGET IMPACT:
In consideration of the financial support the SMPLF fulfills on behalf of the San Mateo Public Library, the rental amount of 
the lease will continue to be set at the nominal rate of $12 per annum.

This lease is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is a government fiscal activity which does not involve any 
commitment to any specific project which may result in a potentially significant physical impact on the environment.

SUBJECT:
Library Café Property Lease – Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an amendment to the Property Lease Agreement with Rafat Haddad of 3 Bees Café for a two-year lease renewal 
extension through May 14, 2022; and authorize the City Librarian to execute the amendment in substantially the form 
presented. 
BACKGROUND:
When the new Main Library opened in 2006, a café space was provided on the 3rd floor due to feedback gathered during 
many community outreach meetings to plan and design the new library building. Between 2006 and 2015, two vendors 
operated café establishments in the space, the first exercising their option to terminate their lease in 2008 and the second 
operating from 2008 until 2015, at which point their lease was not renewed by the City.
There was a strong desire on the part of the public and staff to maintain a café presence, so the Library launched a third 
RFP for café operation in February 2016. At the conclusion of the selection process, Rafat Haddad of 3 Bees Coffee was 
selected as the new café operator. Mr. Haddad has owned and operated 3 Bees Coffee at 224 E. 3rd Avenue in downtown 
San Mateo for the past 16 years. The business enjoys a good reputation and loyal clientele, and Mr. Haddad has extended 
his commitment to good customer service and product quality to the Library café as well. The existing lease term is for two 
years with two two-year renewal options, with the first two-year renewal extension expiring on May 14, 2020. The City 
and Lessee wish at this time to exercise the second two-year renewal option, extending the lease to May 14, 2022.
BUDGET IMPACT:
The City receives 10% of gross sales for months in which the gross sales exceed $10,000. Rent is waived if the gross sales 
do not exceed $10,000 to support the sustainability of the café service in the Library. The estimated annual revenue to the 
City will be within the range of $6,000 -$12,000.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This lease is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is a government fiscal activity which does not involve any 
commitment to any specific project which may result in a potentially significant physical impact on the environment. 
(CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(4).)

SUBJECT:
California Transportation Commission Local Streets and Roads – Project List Fiscal Year 2020-21
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to submit a list of projects proposed to be funded with the Road Repair and Rehabilitation Account, 
Senate Bill 1 funding, to the California Transportation Commission for Fiscal Year 2020-21.
BACKGROUND:
On April 28, 2017, the Governor signed Senate Bill 1 (SB1), which is known as the Road Repair and Accountability Act of 
2017 to address basic road maintenance, rehabilitation, and critical safety needs on both the state highway system and the 
local streets and roads system.  SB 1 increases per-gallon fuel excise taxes, diesel fuel sales taxes, and vehicle registration 
fees, and it also provides for inflationary adjustments to tax rates in future years.
Beginning November 1, 2017, the State Controller has deposited various portions of this new funding into the newly 
created Road Maintenance and Rehabilitation Account (RMRA).  A percentage of this new RMRA funding has been 
apportioned by formula to eligible cities and counties pursuant to Streets and Highways Code Section 2032 (h) for road 
maintenance, rehabilitation, and critical safety projects on the local streets and roads system.
Prior to receiving an apportionment of RMRA funds from the State Controller in a fiscal year, a local agency must submit to 
the California Transportation Commission (CTC), by May 1st of each year, a list of projects proposed to be constructed with 
these funds.  The estimated amount of the apportionment for San Mateo for fiscal year 2020-21 is approximately 
$1,926,000, subject to fluctuations in actual tax and fee revenues.
Staff recommends seeking RMRA fund apportionment for pavement rehabilitation projects for the following street 
segments for fiscal year 2020-21:

Barneson Avenue, from Alameda de las Pulgas to Shafter Street

Fashion Island Boulevard, from Fashion Island Bridge to Mariner’s Island Boulevard

Huron Avenue, from Norfolk Street to Monte Diablo Avenue

Roberta Drive, from Norfolk Street to Hamlett Street

2nd Avenue, from Norfolk Street to Quebec Street

19th Avenue, from Fashion Island Boulevard to End of Road

Nash Drive, from Cottage Grove Avenue to Cottage Grove Avenue

Ottawa Street, from Lorraine Avenue to York Avenue

5th Avenue, from El Camino Real to Virginia Avenue

2nd Avenue, from Delaware Street to Fremont Street

WHEREAS, the City wants to assist the District in providing protection for school 
children who are required to cross heavily traveled streets in the City; and
WHEREAS, some protection can be afforded by placing crossing guards at specific

WHEREAS, the District wishes to cooperate in providing protection for school pupils

1.

employees of the District for all purposes.
2.
The City agrees to reimburse District for wages and salaries paid by the District to 
the crossing guards for daily administrative costs incurred in providing the crossing guards so 
long as this reimbursement does not exceed that figure agreed to pursuant to paragraph 6 below.
3.
Not later than thirty (30) days after the close of each calendar quarter, the District 
shall bill the City for reimbursement of the costs described in paragraph 2 above, and the City 
shall promptly pay the bill.
4.
The City and the District shall meet during the month of March of each year 
during the term of this agreement to allocate the City and District portions of the crossing guard 
expense.  
5.
At any time during the term of this agreement, the District may request the City to 
delete or add one or more locations from or to the list attached as Exhibit A hereto.  If the City 
determines, pursuant to California Traffic Manual section 10-08.2, that a location either should 
or should not have a crossing guard, the parties may amend this Agreement to add or delete a 
location from Exhibit A.  If a location is added, the District shall promptly provide a crossing 
guard at that location and bill the City appropriately for the location pursuant to this agreement.

not exceed three (3) years for any of the following reasons: (1) where the position is created

have layoff rights, bumping rights, severance payments or reemployment rights. This provision

SUBJECT: Classification Updates - Departments of Public Works, Information Technology, and Parks and Recreation
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to approve the 1) job specifications and salaries for new classifications of Fleet Services Supervisor, 
Geographic Information Systems (GIS) Technician I-II, and Deputy Director of Parks and Recreation; 2) reallocation of the 
vacant Public Works Supervisor to Fleet Services Supervisor and of the vacant Park Planning Administrator to Deputy 
Director of Parks and Recreation; and 3) reclassification of the Database Specialist to GIS Technician I-II.
BACKGROUND:
Per San Mateo Municipal Code Section 2.57.030(d), classification updates become effective upon approval by the City 
Council.
Create a Fleet Services Supervisor classification in the Department of Public Works
The Facilities and Fleet Services Manager oversees the maintenance of all City buildings and the procurement and 
maintenance of City’s fleet of approximately 260 vehicles as well as other major equipment. Currently, there is no 
supervisory position to support fleet services and manage fleet-related matters. Maintenance of the fleet is outsourced to 
First Vehicle Services with a services contract of approximately $1 million per year. Unfortunately, First Vehicle has 
experienced chronic staffing problems that have impacted its ability to successfully meet the fleet service and 
maintenance needs of the City.
The Department of Public Works has evaluated the cost effectiveness of self-performing fleet maintenance and believes 
that fleet maintenance work can be self-performed more cost effectively and efficiently. Therefore, the Department is 
requesting to reallocate a vacant Public Works Supervisor position to create a Fleet Services Supervisor position 
responsible for the management of daily fleet maintenance operations and meeting City departments’ needs for 
appropriate fleet, equipment, and services. The recommended approval of new classifications performing fleet 
maintenance work and reporting to the Fleet Services Supervisor will be provided to the City Council in a future 
Administrative Report. The salary for Fleet Services Supervisor will match the monthly salary range for Building 
Maintenance Supervisor at $8,299.23 – $9,894.92.
Create a GIS Technician I-II classification and reclassify a Database Specialist in the Department of Information 
Technology
As City business needs and use of technology have evolved, so have the City of San Mateo’s Geographic Information 
Systems (GIS) needs. The City does not have a classification focused clearly on GIS Technician work. The GIS Technician 
duties are currently being performed by a Database Specialist in the Department of Information Technology, which is a 
classification more aligned with public works and general data entry and lacks expectations of GIS-specific qualifications 
and duties.
33 of 82

--- page 34 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
In order to maintain high-quality GIS service levels, the Department of Information Technology requests to create a 
flexibly-staffed GIS Technician I-II classification responsible for a variety of technical duties necessary to provide GIS 
support for City departments and programs. The Department requests to reclassify Database Specialist Phyllis Chan to GIS 
Technician I-II. Ms. Chan meets the minimum qualifications and can provide proper support for GIS systems and data while 
maintaining a continuity of service level to departmental colleagues. The salary for GIS Technician I-II would match the 
monthly salary range for Information Systems Support Technician I-II at $6,026.86 – $7,189.18 for GIS Technician I, and 
$6,629.53 – $7,902.57 for GIS Technician II.
Create a Deputy Director of Parks and Recreation classification in the Department of Parks and Recreation
It is recommended that a new classification of Deputy Director of Parks and Recreation be created. This position would 
allow for more structured succession planning upon the retirement of the Director of Parks and Recreation. A 
determination of how long to retain the Deputy Director position once an appointment to the Director position is made 
will be determined as part of a broader departmental assessment.  In the interim, it is recommended that the vacant Parks 
Planning Administrator position be reallocated to the Deputy classification. Duties and responsibilities normally assigned 
to the Park Planning Administrator will be covered through a combination of per-diem and contract resources. The 
monthly salary range for the Deputy Director of Parks and Recreation will be 20% below the Director of Parks and 
Recreation at $14,149.77 – $15,600.12.
BUDGET IMPACT:
The Public Works Supervisor is funded by the Sewer Enterprise Fund, while the requested Fleet Services Supervisor will be 
funded out of the Fleet and Building Maintenance Fund, which is mostly supported by the General Fund. The Fleet Services 
Supervisor carries an annual cost of approximately $165,000. For the remainder of the current fiscal year, the cost of the 
reallocation from the Public Works Supervisor to the Fleet Services Supervisor will be absorbed in the Fleet and Building 
Maintenance Fund. The associated salary and benefits for the Fleet Services Supervisor will be incorporated into the 2020-
22 Business Plan.
The proposed reclassification from a Database Specialist to a GIS Technician I is keeping the cost distribution the same: 
75% in the General Fund and 25% in the Construction Services Fund. The GIS Technician I carries the same cost as the 
Database Specialist, and therefore no supplemental budget appropriation is required. However, since the GIS Technician I 
is a flexibly-staffed position, if/when the incumbent progresses to a GIS Technician II, the associated salary and benefits 
will be incorporated into the budget, and the annual financial impact is approximately $9,000.
The reallocation of a Park Planning Administrator to a Deputy Director of Parks and Recreation has a financial impact of 
approximately $51,000 in the General Fund for the full fiscal year. However, as the Park Planning Administrator has not 
been filled, the vacancy savings are sufficient to fund for the reallocation request, and no supplemental appropriation is 
required for the current year. The associated salary and benefits for the Deputy Director of Parks and Recreation will be 
incorporated into the 2020-22 Business Plan.

SUBJECT:
City Council Strategic Plan - Adoption 
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt the City Council Strategic Plan for Fiscal Year 2020-21.
BACKGROUND:
The City Council goal setting process includes a three-step deliberation process resulting in the adoption of a Strategic Plan 
that allows for multi-year resource planning. The first step of the process was a City Council workshop, held on January 25, 
2020. At this workshop, the City Council reviewed the adopted fiscal year 2019-20 strategic plan and made suggested 
revisions to the list of objectives that will be tracked over the next fiscal year. 
As part of the discussion, the Council reviewed and adjusted the assigned priority for each objective in the strategic plan. 
An “A” priority item indicates items that are or will be resourced with funding and staff. “B” priority items are the goals 
that will be next on the list to address when resources are available. A “C” priority indicates the items Council is not yet 
ready to address but want to track for future resourcing. 
The second step of the process was held at the March 2nd City Council meeting. The City Council reviewed the draft 
Strategic Plan to ensure staff accurately captured the discussion at the City Council workshop and allowed for public 
comment on the document. The City Council revised the draft Strategic Plan by including an additional objective related to 
increasing childcare supply and making modifications to the assigned priorities, required resources, and work plans in the 
draft plan. 
Additionally, staff raised concerns during the March 2nd Study Session regarding staff capacity to implement three of the 
“A” priority objectives that require a significant amount of outreach to the business community. The three objectives 
include exploring a smoking ban in commercial areas, the adoption of San Mateo County’s Disposable Food Service Ware 
regulations, and the establishment of Property-Based Improvement Districts (PBID). Staff believes all three of these 
objectives can be addressed next fiscal year provided that the PBID work is limited to major stakeholder outreach and the 
outreach for the Food Service Ware regulations and the smoking ban are sequenced so that they are not being 
implemented concurrently.  
Following the March 2nd meeting, staff amended the draft Strategic Plan to reflect the feedback and comments given by 
the City Council. The revised Strategic Plan is included as Attachment 1. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact from this agenda item.  Resource requirements for the adopted Strategic Plan will be 
incorporated into the 2020-22 Business Plan.

SUBJECT:
Parking Demand and Objective Standards – Ordinance Introduction
RECOMMENDATION:
Introduce an Ordinance to amend the San Mateo Municipal Code Chapter 27.64.100 "Parking Assessment and Special 
Districts" with an interim measure to allow project-specific parking demand studies and to include objective standards for 
review of parking in-lieu fee payment requests within the Central Parking and Improvement District.
BACKGROUND:
Within the Central Parking and Improvement District (CPID) the City offers developers an option to pay a fee in lieu of 
providing sufficient parking as required by the City’s Municipal Code Section 11.62. The funds generated from the parking 
in-lieu fee are utilized to support the availability of public parking in the CPID through constructing public parking 
improvements identified in the City’s capital improvement plan and budget. City Council approved an increase in the 
parking in-lieu fee to $50,000 per space on March 2, 2020. The increased fee will become effective on May 1, 2020, 60 
days following the March 2, 2020 approval by City Council.
Currently, required parking for proposed development projects located in the CPID is calculated utilizing ratios in the City’s 
Municipal Code Chapter 27.64.100 Section (a)(1). The ratios specify parking requirements for different uses within the 
CPID, including office, residential, retail, and restaurant. Public comment received regarding the parking in-lieu fee 
increase included concern that the parking required through the City’s Zoning Code may be in excess of actual parking 
demand. As a result, City Council directed staff to (1) conduct a comprehensive parking study to assess current parking 
requirements in the CPID, (2) evaluate the feasibility of utilizing parking demand studies to determine project parking 
requirements, and (3) consider whether reducing parking requirements for developments is appropriate if transportation 
demand management (TDM) measures are implemented.
The comprehensive parking study and potential Zoning Code amendments resulting from the study are a long-term effort, 
and staff will come back to City Council with a timeline for completion when the project is scoped and a consultant has 
been selected. To address the time between when the new parking in-lieu fee increase is effective and when the 
comprehensive parking study is complete, staff has proposed an interim amendment to the City’s Municipal Code allowing 
for a project-specific parking demand study instead of calculating required parking based on the codified parking ratios.  A 
parking demand study evaluates similar projects in similar locations for each proposed project and assesses the parking 
supply and demand for those existing facilities. Based on the findings from similarly situated projects, the parking demand 
for a proposed project in the CPID would be established and proposed as part of the project application. A project would 
then be required to provide parking per the demand study analysis through provision of parking on-site and/or payment of 
parking in-lieu fees. 
Recent discussions at the Planning Commission have included the parking in-lieu fee, the evaluation of development

projects within the CPID and the Limited Parking Zone (“LPZ”; see Attachment 3) contained therein, and developer 
requests to pay the parking in-lieu fee rather than provide additional parking.  As established within the City’s 2009 
Downtown Area Plan, there is less parking permitted on-site within the LPZ than the other portions of the CPID area.  
Consequently, it is less likely new and redevelopment projects within this area will be able to satisfy required parking on-
site and will therefore be required to pay the parking in-lieu fee for the majority of required parking. 
Projects located outside of the LPZ that are not subject to this parking maximum are anticipated to have the ability to 
provide a larger proportion of parking on-site. However, requiring the provision of all required parking on-site at each 
project site does not support the General Plan and Downtown Area Plan goals of a pedestrian-friendly environment in the 
downtown core given the potential for increased vehicle trips to individual project sites. Staff proposes that projects 
provide at minimum 25% of the required parking on-site in this portion of the CPID (outside of the LPZ).  This would allow a 
portion of required on-site parking to be provided onsite to reduce demand on public facilities. However, in portions of the 
CPID that are adjacent to residential districts or located near public parking facilities that are near or at capacity, staff 
proposes projects provide at least 50% of the required parking on-site to reduce potential impact on the City’s residential 
neighborhoods. 
Staff proposes objective standards, adopted by ordinance, to evaluate requests to pay the parking in-lieu fee in each of 
these areas. As such, staff proposes language to clarify the application of the CPID parking in-lieu fee for development 
projects within the CPID depending on whether or not they are also located within the LPZ.

Projects located within the Limited Parking Zone – Proposed development projects may request to pay the parking 
in-lieu fee for any parking that is not accommodated on-site. 

Projects located outside of the Limited Parking Zone – Proposed development projects must provide, at minimum, 
25% of the required parking on-site and may request to pay for the remainder of parking through parking in-lieu 
fees. However, if public off-street parking occupancy within one-quarter mile of the proposed project location 
exceeds 85% at peak hours per a parking study, or the proposed project location is adjacent to areas zoned R-1, R-
2, R-3, R-4, or R-5, a minimum of 50% of required parking must be accommodated on-site.
If approved, the interim provision for conducting a project-specific parking demand study and the objective standards for 
evaluation of parking in-lieu requests would go into effect 30 days following the second reading of the ordinance.

SUBJECT:
Revenue Enhancement Ballot Measure(s) Polling 
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction for polling on one or more revenue enhancement ballot measure for the November 2020 ballot.
BACKGROUND:
The City's General Fund Long-Term Financial Plan anticipates that projected expenditures will exceed projected revenues 
for most of the ten-year planning period. As a result of the anticipated long-term deficit and absent any action by the City 
Council, the entirety of the City’s General Fund unassigned reserves, which are reserves that are above and beyond the 
City’s 25% operating reserve and housing set aside, will be completely drawn down by the end of the ten-year forecast. 
As part of the comprehensive effort to address this structural imbalance, staff is reviewing the City's operating budget for 
efficiencies and aligning resources with the cost to maintain current service levels. In addition, the City Council has been 
considering three revenue enhancement options since October 2018 - Transient Occupancy Tax, Business Tax, and Real 
Property Document Transfer Tax. 
Transient Occupancy Tax
The City's Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) rate is currently 12% and was last increased from 10% to 12% in 2009. Six 
municipalities (Belmont, Colma, Daly City, Foster City, San Carlos, South San Francisco) in San Mateo County increased 
their TOT rate during the last election in November 2018, which reset the prevailing TOT rate in San Mateo County to 
between 12% and 14%. Every 1% increase in the City's TOT rate would generate approximately $0.7 million in additional 
revenue. The survey included as Attachment 1 compares neighboring agencies from San Mateo County and Santa Clara 
County, including their current TOT rate and whether they are considering a TOT revenue measure for the November 2020 
election.
Business Tax
The City's Business Tax rate was last modified in 1977. Of the nearly 10,000 businesses that pay a business tax, over 7,000 
pay a business tax that is based on gross receipts, while the remainder pay a flat rate. Positioning the impact of a potential 
business tax increase to the same order of magnitude of the potential TOT increase ($1.4 million increase in revenues) 
would require the respective business tax tiers based on gross receipts to increase by 25%. Small businesses (those 
reporting $100,000 or less in gross receipts per year) account for 72% of the total number of businesses that pay the tax 
based on gross receipts but only 5% of the total gross receipts-related business tax revenue. As an example, the first 
business tax tier, which applies to businesses with gross receipts of $30,000 per year or less, would increase from $31 to 
$39 per year. The proposed increase in business tax would have an average impact on small businesses of an additional 
$14 per year.

CITY OF SAN MATEO
In contrast, large businesses account for 28% of the total number of businesses that pay the tax based on gross receipts, 
while also accounting for 95% of the total gross receipt-related business tax revenues. The proposed increase in business 
tax would have an average impact on large businesses of an additional $665 per year.
Real Property Document Transfer Tax
The City’s Real Property Document Transfer Tax (Property Transfer Tax) was established in 1967 and last modified in 1977 
to a rate of 0.5% of sales price. If the Property Transfer Tax were structured such that properties sold at a price of $5 
million or less would be assessed at the current rate of 0.5%, and properties sold at a price of greater than $5 million 
would be assessed at a rate of 1.0%, based on the property sales in fiscal year 2018-19, the City would receive 
approximately $6.4 million in additional Property Transfer Tax revenue. A number of other charter cities in the San 
Francisco Bay Area have recently modified their property transfer tax rates to a tiered structure, including San Francisco, 
Berkeley, Oakland, and Richmond, as reflected in Attachment 2. 
Other 2020 Ballot Measures
The November 2020 election will likely present several other tax-related ballot measures that could impact how voters 
consider local initiatives.  One of the most impactful measures is the California Tax on Commercial and Industrial 
Properties for Education and Local Government Funding Initiative, which has already qualified for the November 2020 
ballot. The initiative is more commonly known as the “split roll”, and its passage would result in commercial and industrial 
properties being taxed based on market value rather than purchase price.  This measure fundamentally changes 
California’s Proposition 13, which has been in place since 1978, and is expected to garner a lot of attention during election 
season. 
In addition, Senate Bill 278 (SB 278) proposes a 1% sales tax that would fund a wide array of transportation projects and 
improvements. The bill passed out of the State Senate at the end of January as a “spot bill”, which means it has 
placeholder text.  More details are expected to be available in advance of the Assembly hearings that will happen in April 
2020. SB 278, as an “urgency bill”, will require a 2/3 majority in order to appear on the ballot in the same year in which it is 
approved. Depending on the outcome SB 278, Caltrain may consider a 1/8 cent sales tax on the November 2020 ballot to 
provide stable funding and service increases for Caltrain. 
If the City Council were to place multiple revenue enhancement measures on the ballot, the likelihood of success would be 
low due to the number and magnitude of the other tax measures. As such, staff recommends that the revenue 
enhancement poll focus on Transient Occupancy Tax because it is the one potential option that would not have a direct 
impact on residents.  Based on staff discussions with our polling consultant, a TOT measure is the most likely to be 
successful in this environment, and as such, staff recommends moving forward with polling on a TOT increase to 14%.  
Staff also recommends that potential changes to the Business Tax and the Real Property Tax be revisited in early 2022 for 
Council consideration for the November 2022 ballot.
 
Upon final approval from the City Council to move forward with the revenue enhancement poll, the City’s polling 
consultant will follow a similar methodology to that of the recent community survey that was completed last month to 
ensure statistically valid survey results. Staff will present the survey results to the Council in mid-April to determine next 
steps.

The cost for revenue enhancement polling services are approximately $26,000.  These services were contemplated in the 
fiscal year 2019-20 operating budget for the Finance Department, and as such, no supplemental budget appropriation is 
required.

SUBJECT:
Proclamation of a Local Health Emergency
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to proclaim a Local Health Emergency related to the 2019 Novel Coronavirus (COVID-19).
BACKGROUND:
Both the State of California and the County of San Mateo have issued emergency declarations regarding the Novel 
Coronavirus (COVID-19) epidemic. Persuant to Section 2.78.030 of the City's Municipal Code, the Director of Emergency 
Services (City Manager) is empowered "to request the City Council to proclaim the existence or threatened existence of a 
local emergency." 
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) has stated that COVID-19 is a serious health threat, at least partly 
due to the fact that so much about the virus us currently unknown. The exact modes of transmission, the factors 
facilitating human-to-human transmission, the groups at the highest risk of serious illness, and the fatality rate all still 
factors under investigation. 
Based on the guidance of the CDC and the County Health Officer, the City of San Mateo is taking unprecedented steps to 
support mitigation efforts related to COVID-19. For example, public facilities such as the library and recreation centers 
have been temporarily closed, and other City functions are being adjusted significantly to accommodate best practices 
such as social distancing to help prevent the spread of this virus. 
Declaring a local emergency regarding COVID-19 aligns the City of San Mateo with the County of San Mateo and the State 
of California in terms of having a declaration of emergency in place. It also provides the Director of Emergency Services 
greater latitude to "obtain vital supplies, equipment, and other such properties found lacking and needed for the 
protection of life and property" (Municipal Code Section 2.78.040(b)) and to mobilize our workforce to most effectively 
address the emergency (Municipal Code Section 2.78.040(d)).  Finally, this declaration could strengthen the City's eligibility 
for recovering extraordinary costs related to the COVID-19 outbreak response. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no direct budget impact from declaring a local health emergency. As noted, doing so could position the City to 
achieve some level of cost recovery for extraordinary costs related to the response to this outbreak.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This Resolution is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative activity that will not result 
in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(5).)

RESOLUTION NO. ____ (2020)
A RESOLUTION OF THE CITY COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF SAN MATEO
PROCLAIMING THE EXISTENCE OF A LOCAL EMERGENCY
WHEREAS, section 27.78.030 of the San Mateo Municipal Code empowers the City Council to 
proclaim a local emergency when there are actual or threatened conditions of disaster or extreme peril 
within the City; and
WHEREAS, the City Council has been requested by the Director of Emergency Services of the 
City to proclaim the existence of a local emergency therein; and
WHEREAS, actual or threatened conditions of extreme peril to the safety of persons and 
property have arisen within the City of San Mateo caused by the spread of 2019 Novel Coronavirus 
throughout the Bay Area; and
WHEREAS, the conditions of extreme peril warrant and necessitate the proclamation of the 
existence of a local emergency. 
NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, by the City Council of the City of San Mateo, that it hereby 
proclaims that a local emergency now exists throughout the City. 
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that during the existence of this local emergency, the powers, 
functions, and duties of the Director of Emergency Services and the Emergency Organization of this 
City shall be those prescribed by state law, ordinances, and resolutions of this City and by the City of 
San Mateo Emergency Plan.
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that that the Director of Emergency Services of the City of San Mateo 
acting as the executive officer for the San Mateo Consolidated Fire Department, a Joint Powers 
Authority providing fire and emergency services within the City of San Mateo is hereby authorized to 
execute for and on behalf of the City of San Mateo, and on behalf of the San Mateo Consolidated Fire, 
any agreement or application for, and to file the agreement with, the California Governor's Office of 
Emergency Services for the purposes of obtaining federal financial assistance
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that the local emergency shall be deemed to continue to exist until 
its termination is proclaimed by the City Council of the City of San Mateo.
