13. Future Development Projects – Community Benefit
Kohar Kojayan, Community Development Department Director, provided a presentation on the need for a 
community benefits or public improvements program to provide clarity for development projects. She outlined 
existing fees and requirements, historical development project benefits, what projects are currently affected. She 
requested direction on what method of evaluation should be used to determine the amount of community benefits 
and what public improvements/community benefits should be prioritized above those already covered under impact 
fees. Council asked questions of staff. 
Public Comment via eComment – Lisa Diaz Nash stated the top three community benefits should be affordable 
housing above the density bonus; contribution to transportation; and childcare in the project. Pat Calihan, 
Prometheus Real Estate Group, is in support of providing clarity and detail to the benefit process. Jordan Grimes 
stated that concerns for the overall community be prioritized over concerns of the immediate neighborhood. Adam 
Loraine stated priority should be given to bike, pedestrian and active transportation. Jon Stone, Prometheus Real 
Estate Group, is in support of anything clarifying the community benefits process. Marcus Gilmour, Lane Partners, is 
a developer in support of the recommendations of staff. Brian Myers, California Coastal Properties, developer of 
Passage, outlined community benefits already included in the project, and noted a focus on circulation improvement 
is needed for cut through traffic.
Council asked clarifying questions and discussion ensued. City Council was unanimously in support of using an 
independent third party to do an economic feasibility analysis to identify the value of the benefit being provided, and 
priority of public improvements in excess of base city requirements should be based on community/neighborhood 
input and circulation improvements near the project site. Kojayan noted that these will be priority guidelines and a 
formal adoption of community benefits will be incorporated in the General Plan update process.

Public Assistance Cost Recovery – Designation of Authority for Expense Reimbursement
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to designate the City Manager and Finance Director as the agents of the City of San Mateo for the 
purposes of obtaining federal and/or state financial assistance for expenses incurred in response to COVID-19.
BACKGROUND:
In response to the growing and evolving threat of the COVID-19 pandemic, the following actions have been taken at the 
local, state, and federal level: 
March 3, 2020 - The County of San Mateo County Manager's Office issued a Public Health Emergency Proclamation, and 
along with the San Mateo County Health Officer, declared a local health emergency, which was ratified by the County of 
San Mateo Board of Supervisors on March 10, 2020. 
March 4, 2020, The Governor of the State of California declared a State of Emergency. 
March 13, 2020 - The President of the United States declared a national emergency and on March 22, 2020, approved a 
Presidential Major Disaster Declaration (FEMA-DR-4482-CA).
March 16, 2020 - The City Council of the City of San Mateo proclaimed a local emergency. 
In accordance with Section 502 of the Robert T. Stafford Disaster Relief and Emergency Assistance Act (Stafford Act), 
federal funding is available to state, tribal, and eligible local governments and certain private nonprofit organizations on a 
cost-sharing, reimbursement basis for emergency protective measures in response to the COVID-19 pandemic, including, 
but not limited to: 
1.
Management, control, and reduction of immediate threats to public health and safety: 
a.
Emergency Operation Center costs; (the City of San Mateo opened multi-agency Emergency Operations 
Center on March 16, 2020)
b.
Training specific to the declared event;
c.
Disinfection of eligible public facilities;
d.
Technical assistance on emergency management and control of immediate threats to public health and 
safety. 
2.
Emergency medical care; 
3.
Purchase and distribution of food, water, ice, medicine, and other consumable supplies, including personal 
protective equipment (PPE) and hazardous material suits;

4.
Security and law enforcement 
5.
Communications of general health and safety information to the public; and 
6.
Eligible overtime costs. 
The City of San Mateo incurred costs related to emergency protective measures during this period and expects to submit a 
“Request for Public Assistance” to the California Governor’s Office of Emergency Services (CalOES) in accordance with 
forthcoming guidance from CalOES. If the City of San Mateo qualifies for funding, then the Council must adopt a resolution 
designating an agent or agents for the purpose of responding to and coordinating with CalOES for “all matters pertaining 
to such state disaster assistance”. The proposed resolution (Attachment 1) designates the City Manager and Finance 
Director as the City’s agents (see Exhibit A to Attachment 1).
Further, this resolution will be valid for three years from the date signed and would allow the City of San Mateo to seek 
cost recovery for eligible disaster expenses incurred by any future Declared Disasters during this three-year period.
BUDGET IMPACT:
Though the action of approving the Designation of Applicant's Agent Resolution for Non-State Agencies has no direct 
impact to the City's budget, reimbursement of costs incurred by the City for its efforts toward emergency protective 
measures from the COVID-19 pandemic will potentially provide some relief from the financial impact to the City. 
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This designation is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative activity that will not 
result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(5).)

Cal OES Form 130 Instructions
A Designation of Applicant’s Agent Resolution for Non-State Agencies is required of all Applicants to be eligible to 
receive funding.  A new resolution must be submitted if a previously submitted Resolution is older than three (3) years 
from the last date of approval, is invalid or has not been submitted.  
When completing the Cal OES Form 130, Applicants should fill in the blanks on page 1. The blanks are to be filled in as
follows:
Resolution Section:
Governing Body: This is the group responsible for appointing and approving the Authorized Agents.  
Examples include:  Board of Directors, City Council, Board of Supervisors, Board of Education, etc.
Name of Applicant: The public entity established under the laws of the State of California.  Examples include:  School 
District, Office of Education, City, County or Non-profit agency that has applied for the grant, such as: City of San Diego,
Sacramento County, Burbank Unified School District, Napa County Office of Education, University Southern California.
Authorized Agent:  These are the individuals that are authorized by the Governing Body to engage with the Federal Emergency 
Management Agency and the Governor’s Office of Emergency Services regarding grants applied for by the Applicant. There are
two ways of completing this section:
1.
Titles Only: If the Governing Body so chooses, the titles of the Authorized Agents would be entered here, not
their names. This allows the document to remain valid (for 3 years) if an Authorized Agent leaves the position
and is replaced by another individual in the same title. If “Titles Only” is the chosen method, this document
must be accompanied by a cover letter naming the Authorized Agents by name and title. This cover letter can
be completed by any authorized person within the agency and does not require the Governing Body’s signature.
2.
Names and Titles:  If the Governing Body so chooses, the names and titles of the Authorized Agents would be
listed. A new Cal OES Form 130 will be required if any of the Authorized Agents are replaced, leave the position
listed on the document or their title changes.
Governing Body Representative: These are the names and titles of the approving Board Members.
Examples include:  Chairman of the Board, Director, Superintendent, etc.  The names and titles cannot be one of the
designated Authorized Agents, and a minimum of two or more approving board members need to be listed.
Certification Section:
Name and Title: This is the individual that was in attendance and recorded the Resolution creation and approval.
Examples include: City Clerk, Secretary to the Board of Directors, County Clerk, etc. This person cannot be one of the
designated Authorized Agents or Approving Board Member (if a person holds two positions such as City Manager and 
Secretary to the Board and the City Manager is to be listed as an Authorized Agent, then the same person holding the
Secretary position would sign the document as Secretary to the Board (not City Manager) to eliminate “Self
Certification.”

SUBJECT:
Community Development Block Grant Funds – Supplemental Appropriation 
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to appropriate $40,000 from available Community Development Block Grant entitlement funds from 
prior years into the 2019-20 operating budget.
BACKGROUND:
Each year, the City of San Mateo receives an allocation of Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) funds from the 
Department of Housing and Urban Development. These grant funds, plus program income from old Housing Rehabilitation 
Loan repayments, are programmed to provide programs and services to assist low and moderate income neighborhoods 
and households. This year, due to a combination of increased revenues and program savings, there is an estimated surplus 
of approximately $40,000 that can be reprogrammed immediately or carried over to the next fiscal year.
In light of the COVID-19 emergency, staff is recommending that these funds be directed to assist Samaritan House, our 
Core Service Agency, now rather than waiting until next fiscal year. Samaritan House has seen a substantial increase in 
requests for assistance, which has required them to increase staffing to address the urgent needs. These funds will be used 
to support increased staffing costs to meet the growing requests for assistance. 
Samaritan House already receives funds through the CDBG Community Funding Program, so these surplus funds can be 
used to amend the existing agreement in order to expedite getting resources to the agency. The new agreement amount 
will be $56,500, which does not require Council approval and will be executed by the City Manager.
BUDGET IMPACT:
Approval to appropriate $40,000 of CDBG entitlement funds from prior years into the 2019-20 operating budget has no 
net fiscal impact, as these were available funds that would have otherwise been programmed into the 2020-21 operating 
budget.

SUBJECT:
Fiscal Year 2019-20 Budget Appropriation
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to transfer and appropriate $3,500,000 from the General Fund's available fund balance to the Workers’ 
Compensation Fund's current year budget, $150,000 from the General Fund’s available fund balance to the Public Works 
current year operating budget, and $700,000 from the Construction Services Fund’s available fund balance to the General 
Fund. 
BACKGROUND:
Workers’ Compensation Fund
Claims expenses in the Workers’ Compensation Fund have been well above historical averages in the past few years, with 
last fiscal year at a record high. Even with the current year budget adjusted to reflect the increase in claims expenses, 
claims expenses are once again tracking higher than budget. While work has been underway to settle outstanding claims, 
an actuarial study conducted earlier this year shows that an additional $2.2 million is needed beyond what had been 
budgeted to set liability reserves at the marginally acceptable confidence level per the City’s policy. The status of the 
Workers’ Compensation Fund was presented to Council during the mid-year financial update, with staff noting that a 
budget appropriation would be requested later in the fiscal year as more data becomes available. The original estimate 
stood at $3.2 million and would utilize the surplus generated by the General Fund in the prior fiscal year.
After reviewing claims expenses year-to-date, and combined with the adjustment needed for liability reserves, the total 
amount of funding requested for the Workers’ Compensation Fund is $3.5 million. With the revised appropriation, the 
current forecast will continue to show the fund remaining in negative balance for the next three years before moving back 
into positive balance. While there is sufficient General Fund surplus to cover for the additional funding beyond what was 
originally estimated, the purpose in not requesting a higher amount of appropriation is to smooth out the impact to the 
General Fund in the long-term, so that operations are not negatively impacted and services are maintained. Concurrently, 
work will continue in settling outstanding claims and mitigating new claims to ensure the health of the Workers’ 
Compensation Fund. 
Public Works Operating Budget
From the beginning of fiscal year 2019-20 through March, Public Works staff continued to process a high number of 
developer planning applications, building permits, and encroachment/right-of-way permits. To keep up with this demand 
and maintain review response times, the Department dedicated additional Engineering Services staff time to reviewing 
permit activity. Although much of this time was charged to planning applications and building permits, staff time to review 
encroachment permits and other right of way activities are charged to the General Fund. The Public Works Department is 
requesting an appropriation from the General Fund of $150,000, some of which is offset by additional revenues from 
encroachment permits, to cover additional salary costs in FY 2019-20.

Construction Services Fund
Effective the current fiscal year beginning July 1, 2019, the Construction Services Fund will pay the General Fund $400,000 
for the space occupied by construction services staff in the Community Development Department (CDD) in City Hall. This 
practice is consistent with neighboring municipalities and generally accepted accounting principles. CDD’s occupancy cost 
is calculated on a cost per square foot basis with fixed annual increase over a period of five years, which reflects market 
rates for commercial office space leases..  In addition, the labor distribution for certain CDD staff members has been 
modified to more closely align with their core duties in the Construction Services Fund. Based on an estimate of where 
Construction Services Fund expenditures are projected to be by the end of the fiscal year, an additional $700,000 in budget 
appropriations are needed to address the above changes, which have been incorporated into the proposed budget for 
fiscal year 2020-21 and the long-term forecast for both the General Fund and the Construction Services Fund.

SUBJECT:
San Mateo - Foster City Public Financing Authority Joint Exercise of Powers Agreement - Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to amend the San Mateo - Foster City Public Financing Authority Joint Exercise of Powers Agreement 
with regard to the assignment of roles for the Treasurer-Auditor-Controller and the Clerk; amend insurance language; and 
outline payment changes for the extended roles.
BACKGROUND:
The City of San Mateo and the Estero Municipal Improvement District/City of Foster City formed the San Mateo – Foster 
City Public Financing Authority (PFA) in February 2016 for the purpose of financing capital improvements for the member 
agencies. In 2019, the PFA issued $270 million bonds to finance the member agencies’ respective shares for the Clean 
Water Program. Last month, the PFA issued $46 million in lease revenue bonds to finance the City of San Mateo’s North 
Shoreview Flood Improvement Project and various street rehabilitation/reconstruction projects.
A joint exercise of powers agreement outlines the PFA’s governance and operating structure. Staff from Foster City and the 
San Mateo are jointly proposing several amendments to that agreement. First, the agreement currently states that the 
member agencies’ City Clerks shall serve as Secretary to the PFA Board on a rotating two-year term. The agreement also 
states the member agencies’ Finance Directors shall serve as the PFA’s Treasurer-Auditor-Controller on a rotating four-
year term. Both terms are scheduled to end in June 2020. Foster City staff has expressed a desire to have the City of San 
Mateo’s City Clerk and Finance Director continue serving as the Secretary and Treasurer-Auditor-Controller and City of San 
Mateo staff is able and willing to do so. The proposed amendments would allow for this arrangement, at the option and 
expense of the City of Foster City.
Secondly, staff is proposing to change the insurance requirements for PFA Board and Officers. Currently the agreement 
requires the PFA to maintain errors and omissions liability with a minimum of $1 million in coverage on a “claims made” 
basis. The proposed amendment would change that requirement to $1 million on an “annual aggregate” basis and reflects 
the availability of errors and omissions liability coverage in the insurance market.  
If the amendments are approved by the respective city councils of each member agency, and subsequently by the PFA 
Board, the amended Joint Powers Agreement (JPA) will be sent to the Secretary of State.
BUDGET IMPACT:
The City Clerk of the City of San Mateo has served as Secretary of the PFA since inception, as the EMID Clerk requested San 
Mateo to serve as PFA Secretary for the partial year and subsequent two-year term. The fiscal year ending June 30, 2020 
marks the end of the City’s Finance Director’s term as Treasurer-Auditor-Controller and the City Clerk’s term as Secretary. 
Upon approval of the amended JPA, in fiscal year 2020-21, EMID will pay $14,362.49 to the City of San Mateo for the first

SUBJECT:
Microfiche, Microfilm, and Paper Records Digitization and Ongoing Document Scanning Services - Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an agreement with BMI Imaging Systems, Inc. for microfilm, paper records digitization, and ongoing document 
scanning services in the amount not to exceed $400,000, and authorize the Community Development Director to execute 
the agreement in substantially the form presented.
BACKGROUND:
A substantial portion of the City’s property and building permit records currently exist only in microfiche format, with an 
estimate of 600,000 images and approximately 50 rolls of microfilm that may constitute up to 47,000 property records 
images. The Community Development Department (CDD) also currently has 1,350 boxes of planning applications and other 
documents, dating as far back as the 1970s, in storage at Corodata’s facility in San Jose. All of these records are considered 
“permanent records” in accordance with the City’s Records Management Policy approved by the City Council in October 
2015. Accordingly, not only is it incumbent upon the City to preserve these records for City use, it is also the City’s 
responsibility to provide these records to the public in a timely manner in response to Public Records Act (PRA) requests.
Although microfilm and microfiche were the best technology available at the time of initial conversion of paper documents 
into compact forms in the 1980s and early 1990s, these records are now antiquated, degrading in quality, and are 
cumbersome to use in terms of locating and viewing records. Additionally, the City has only one operable microfiche 
reader and does not plan to acquire more as the existing ‘micro’ readers are quickly becoming obsolete. Similarly, with 
thousands of paper records in offsite storage, the process for recall, delivery, and viewing is a slow and inefficient way for 
staff or public to access those records. The reality is that today’s digital environment demands improvement in the quality, 
expediency of delivery, and ease of use when it comes to accessing these documents. 
In 2014, the Council approved management audit recommendations that included digitizing the microfiche and the 
prioritization of electronic document storage, access, and processing. CDD has since been focused on improving the 
customer service experience and access to information by implementing new processes and services through the 
advancement of technology with the goal of becoming more efficient, flexible, transparent, and adaptable. Getting these 
records preserved in a digital format will go a long way toward achieving those goals by providing a higher quality image 
and easier methods to access and review these public records in person and remotely. 
It will also assist staff in quickly accessing property records for a variety of purposes, which can take up to several days 
using our current system. This is especially problematic when it comes to time-sensitive records requests, such as files 
related to PRAs and associated legal deadlines. These documents will now be scanned into digital records and prepared 
with a wide variety of metadata that will allow for a much faster search and access to the records needed. It will also 
substantially reduce or eliminate the need for and costs of physical storage and retrieval.

Request for Proposals and Responses 
A Request for Proposals (RFP) for Microfiche, Microfilm, and Paper Records Digitization and Ongoing Document Scanning 
Services was released on November 14, 2019.  The RFP described the three different elements of the City’s needs: 1) 
microfiche and microfilm digitizing, 2) ongoing document scanning for new building permits and code enforcement 
documents, and 3) scanning existing documents held in storage. The RFP also detailed the City’s technical requisites for the 
indexing, imaging, data delivery, quality control, and document transfer and security. 
The City received seven responses to the RFP by the deadline of December 11, 2019. Staff completed an initial eligibility 
review, disqualifying one that did not comply with a requisite that all documents must stay within the State of California. 
The remaining six proposals were reviewed and evaluated by a staff committee from CDD and Information Technology, 
resulting in interviews with two of the responding companies, BMI Imaging Systems, Inc. and Hines EDM, Inc. The fee 
schedules of the two were the most comparable of all respondents and they were the only two companies who provided 
an estimated total budget. 
There were additional service considerations that BMI included that specifically addressed City interests. A few of the 
City’s priority interests were the single pick-up of all microfiche rather than multiple batch pick-ups, the ability to retrieve 
scanned documents quickly, and the use of innovative technology, in this case Digital ReeL. Digital ReeL would allow the 
City an ongoing, near real-time way to access, review online, and print scanned documents during the project rather than 
waiting for a completed scanned batch to be returned and the corresponding upload to occur. This would allow staff to 
access and provide digital records for customers almost immediately for micro records, and within 24-48 hours for paper 
records. This is well within legal timelines associated with public records requests and is responsive to customer needs.    
Digital ReeL also allows for the City to directly make certain corrective inputs to the data as needed rather than submit 
required corrections to the vendor and wait for their completion. This will help facilitate a more complete and accurate set 
of data in the cumulative, final upload to SIRE, the City’s current electronic document management system. An additional 
benefit in selecting BMI is their up-to-date knowledge of the City’s documents and systems, as they are the current vendor 
for the ongoing scanning of new building permit documents and have provided this service for many years. With the 
pricing, intuitional knowledge, and innovative technologies proposed, BMI is expected to provide faster and more efficient 
service with better access to the digital documents during the project as well as after completion.
BMI is also aware of the City’s activity with regards to identifying a new Electronic Document Management System (EDMS) 
to replace the SIRE system and has indicated their willingness to work with the City if any changes in data configuration 
during the project may be desired. Upon the completion of reference checks for both vendors and committee 
concurrence, BMI Imaging Systems is the recommended service provider.
The attached Agreement contains a Scope of Work, Project Schedule, and Fee Schedule and Budget, as well as the 
required insurance documents. Should there be a significant delay in starting the project due to the impacts of the COVID-
19 virus, the schedule will be reviewed with the vendor and adjusted accordingly.

Pre-Production Activities

Microfiche, Microfilm & Document Digitization 
 
BMI’s document processing facilities are fully-equipped with scanning hardware/software 
resources that will allow us to address all of the City’s materials without the need for 
subcontracting.  All of these resources are designed and manufactured to yield high-quality, 
accurate data deliverables in a high-volume production environment.  Included below is 
information regarding the digitization capabilities that enable BMI to address the City’s various 
material/record types: 
 
Microfilm and Microfiche 
 
BMI will utilize a combination of Mekel Mach 7 and 10 scanners with Mekel's Quantum 
scanning software for the digitization of microfiche and microfilm-based records, respectively.  
All scans will be performed at a 300 DPI resolution to a single-page PDF file format (TIF and 
JPG are other options).  Mekel’s Quantum software controls the operation of the scanner and 
provides extensive image enhancement and verification capabilities to support the production 
and quality review/control processes. 
 
During the post-digitization image quality review process, inspections will be performed to 
verify successful image capture.  BMI’s image inspection procedure is based on “ANSI/ASQC 
Z1.4-2008 Sampling Procedures and Tables for Inspection by Attributes”, using a 0.10% AQL 
using MIL STD 105E switching rules.  This procedure dictates that we inspect a certain number 
of images from each microfiche and, if the quality/completeness of an image is deemed to be 
unacceptable, the inspection level is escalated to review a larger sample which, ultimately, may 
result in the microfiche being re-scanned using alternate settings. 
 
IMPORTANT NOTE:  To facilitate on-line access to the digitized microfiche while the 
conversion project is ongoing (and for 60 days following completion), BMI will provide the City 
with the ability to utilize its hosted Digital ReeL web-portal.  Digital ReeL, a BMI-
developed/hosted microfiche archiving and retrieval application, will provide staff with a high-
fidelity electronic replica of each microfiche for its image quality control review purposes. 
 
Small and Large-format Paper Records 
 
BMI will utilize best-in-class Kodak, Canon and Opex production scanners for the auto-feeding 
and digitization of all small-format paper documents at a 300 DPI resolution to a single-page 
PDF file format (TIF and JPG are other options).  The image files will be processed through an 
automated “cleanup” (de-skew, reorient, blank page removal etc.) and saved in the required 
format (black/white, grayscale or color).  During the scanning process, the document index 
values associated with any barcode sheets inserted during the document preparation will be 
captured and the barcode sheet images will be purged.

Quality Control at Scan Time 
 
BMI will work with the City to define and document the quality control requirements for each 
project element.  At a minimum, BMI employs the following procedures/tactics to ensure a high 
level of quality and accuracy during the scanning process: 
 
x Color scanning for all small-format pages (required format is generated post-scan) 
x Duplex scanning for all small-format pages (blank pages are deleted post-scan) 
x Kofax Virtual ReScan (VRS) image cleanup processing during scanning 
x Ultrasonic double-feed detection to detect double-feeds during auto-feeding capture 
x Manual sheet feeding for very poor quality or fragile originals 
x Review of 100% of images during the scanning processes 
x Post-scan thumbnail image review to detect cropping or skewing issues 
 
In general, the image acceptance criteria are readability (legibility of lettering and graphics), 
contrast and position.  If it is determined that an image does not meet the established acceptance 
criteria, the document will be rescanned using manual controls.  If the physical document 
ultimately cannot be scanned to an acceptable/proper quality level because it is a poor quality 
original, it will be scanned to the highest quality possible and the associated image will be 
marked as “Poor Quality Original” following BMI’s receipt of approval from the City. 
 
Optical Character Recognition (OCR) 
 
Following the scanning process, BMI can employ multiple technologies or “engines” to perform 
automated optical character recognition (or OCR) processing on captured images.  Our most 
commonly utilized tool is the Nuance OCR engine, which can be deployed as a Unity process 
flow component.  Nuance supports a multi-engine OCR voting capability for improved 
recognition results.  The OCR results for each document can be provided as a separate/related 
text file, included within the PDF file (“searchable PDF”) or, if supported by the SIRE XML 
import module, included as a component of the XML file itself. 
 
Web-based Application for Quality Control

microfilm and document digitization projects, BMI will provide the City with on-line access to 
the digitized records via its innovative, hosted Digital ReeL (DR) record retrieval/QC review 
solution while the conversion project is in-progress.  DR will meet the City’s stated requirement 
for a secure, web-based application for the City to view and monitor any quality control issues 
and the related corrections.

RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to approve the: 1) job specifications and salaries for the new classifications of Communications 
Manager and Housing and Neighborhood Services Manager; 2) reclassifications of the Communications and Public 
Relations Analyst to Communications Manager in City Manager’s Office, and the Housing Supervisor to Housing and

BACKGROUND:
Per San Mateo Municipal Code Section 2.57.030(d), classification updates become effective upon approval by the City 
Council. 
Create a Communications Manager and reclassify a Communications and Public Relations Analyst in City Manager’s 
Office
The City created the Communications and Public Relations Analyst position to hire for staff with expertise in the field to 
work on a departmental level to facilitate public outreach and messaging. As there began to be communications positions 
across multiple departments, it became clear that there was a need for a Communications Manager that serves as the 
primary coordinator of all Citywide communications, sets the policies and procedures to be followed by other 
departments, and ensures consistency in messaging. The need for this has become even more acute during this current 
COVID-19 crisis. Communications and Public Relations Analyst Samantha Weigel has been playing this role for an extended 
period of time, even prior to the crisis.
Ms. Weigel meets the minimum qualifications for the Communications Manager classification and the City Manager 
requests to reclassify Weigel’s position to Communications Manager. In maintaining the City’s internal salary relationships, 
the monthly salary range for Communications Manager will be set at approximately 10% above the Communications and 
Public Relations Analyst classification at $9,915.84 - $11,827.82. This salary is in line with the existing division head level 
classes of Accounting Manager and Budget Manager.
Create a Housing and Neighborhood Services Manager and reclassify a Housing Supervisor in Community Development 
Department
Given the focus on housing within the county and the various programs requiring collaboration and complex analysis, it is 
necessary to create a manager classification over the housing function responsible for complex staff work in administering 
and leading the direct use of the City’s housing funds, providing leadership and recommendations on innovative housing 
initiatives and programs, overseeing the City’s Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) program, and coordinating 
the statutory requirements for administration of this complex program providing much needed funding and improvements

Recommended Action

It is recommended that the City Council review and approve the attached draft Resolution
denouncing stigmatization, hateful racism and xenophobia during the COVID-19 pandemic against
particular groups of people, notably Asian Americans, immigrants and health care workers.

Background

The COVID-19 pandemic has dramatically increased stress, feelings of isolation and depression
among the global population. Fear and uncertainty around COVID-19, coupled with hateful
political rhetoric at the federal level, has led to a sharp increase in violence, hateful acts of racism
and stigmatization of particular groups of people, notably Asian Americans. Stigmatization is
common during disease outbreaks and began early on with COVID-19 when it emerged in China. It
continues to threaten our overall resilience.

The World Health Organization (WHO) has indicated that attaching locations or ethnicity to the
disease is dangerous. The official name for the disease was deliberately chosen to avoid
stigmatization — the “co” stands for Corona, “vi” for virus and “d” for disease, and 19 for the
emergence of the disease in 2019. It is not a “Chinese virus,” “Asian virus” or “Wuhan virus.” The
President of the United States, however, has referred to COVID-19 as the “Chinese virus” and
many other leaders and community figures have furthered this ignorance in their own
communications.

Unsurprisingly, discrimination against Asian Americans has followed suit, documented by
increases in hate crimes and other forms of blatant racism. The Stop AAPI Hate reporting center
has gathered over 1,000 reports of anti-Asian discrimination in just over one week. Established by
San Francisco State University’s Asian American Studies Department, Chinese for Affirmative
Action (CAA) and the Asian Pacific Policy & Planning Council (A3PCON), it continues to receive
about 100 incident reports a day. These incidents range from shunning and verbal harassment to
workplace discrimination and physical assault. A large percentage involves spitting and coughing at
Asian Americans. These reports reveal the virulent anger and hostility now directed at Asian
Americans.

Stigmatization represents a serious threat to our collective emergency response to the pandemic. It
furthers dangerous and false information about how COVID-19 spreads, misinforms people’s
perception of risk and repeats mistakes in our own history during past emergencies when racist and
xenophobic sentiment targeted vulnerable groups. According to the Centers for Disease Control and
Prevention (CDC), stigma associated with COVID-19 may currently be affecting persons of Asian
descent, people who have traveled, and emergency responders or healthcare professionals. Indeed,
reports in the news and social media, even within our own city, validate the CDC. Asian Americans
have increasingly taken to social media to voice their experiences, with the topics now widely

RE:  
 
City Funding through SMC Strong Fund for Small Business Assistance 
 
The California Shelter in Place (SIP) order implemented to curtail the spread of COVID-19 has impacted 
many throughout the state, including local small businesses which have been forced to reduce staff or 
services as a result of lower demand and strained resources. San Mateo County, in partnership with 
Silicon Valley Community Foundation and San Mateo County Economic Development Association 
(SAMCEDA), created the San Mateo County Strong Fund (SMCSF).  
 
On March 24, 2020, the San Mateo County Board of Supervisors approved a $3 million donation of 
Measure K funds to seed “San Mateo County Strong,” a COVID-19 recovery fund established in 
partnership with Silicon Valley Community Foundation (SVCF) and the San Mateo County Economic 
Development Association (SAMCEDA) to support the following groups: 
 
 
$1 million for individuals and families in San Mateo County needing assistance with basic needs 
due to COVID 19 
 
$1 million for non-profit organizations that provide services and support to the most vulnerable 
San Mateo County residents for operating support 
 
$1 million for San Mateo County small businesses affected by COVID-19 
 
County of San Mateo, SAMCEDA, and San Mateo Credit Union Partnership 
 
Specific to small business support, the goal of the SMCSF is to provide emergency grants through the 
San Mateo Credit Union (SMCU) to support small, local businesses in their efforts to provide payroll for 
employees, maintain operations, meet ongoing obligations and survive the economic impacts of 
California’s Shelter in Place order. 
 
The County, SAMCEDA and SMCU have been working closely to develop and implement an online 
process that will accept applications for SMCSF small business grants and utilize SMCU’s underwriting 
resources to screen applications for eligibility and completeness. Once eligibility is confirmed, SMCU will 
provide the County with application status reports. 
 
The initial $1 million in funding from the County for small business grants will ONLY provide 100 
businesses across San Mateo County with an average grant of $10,000. The money will go almost 
immediately. Which is why we are aggressively fundraising from San Mateo County’s major employers, 
major foundations, and all residents wanting to support their specific communities small businesses.

Partnership Potential with San Mateo County’s Cities and Towns: 
 
The County, SAMCEDA, and SMCU are structuring the small business grant process to handle the 
distribution of grants on a countywide basis and on a geo-targeted basis if a donor wishes to target 
specific funding for a city or sub-region of the county. The ability for the process to allow for donors to 
target funding geographically has already attracted the participation and interest of several cities: 
 
 
The City of Redwood City approved $300,000 to support eligible businesses in Redwood City. 
 
The City of San Carlos approved $112,500 to support eligible businesses in San Carlos. 
 
The City of Burlingame is considering a $500,000 contribution. 
 
I have also provided updates to the East Palo Alto City Council, Pacifica City Council, San Bruno 
City Council, and I am scheduled for upcoming city council presentations to the San Mateo, 
Belmont and Daly City Councils. 
By contributing city or town funding through SMCU, a local jurisdiction can leverage the infrastructure 
being created by the County, SAMCEDA and SMCU to get small business grants into the hands of  
small business owners quickly. This SMCSF eligibility criteria will help to ensure that grants are being 
awarded to businesses that stand a chance of being able to resume full operations after California’s 
Shelter in Place order is lifted. 
SAMCEDA is happy to work with any City Manager or Mayor to discuss how to participate in the SMCSF’s 
small business grant program. SAMCEDA is also happy to present or be available for questions if this 
partnership concept is brought before a city or town council for discussion. 
 
Partnership Potential with San Mateo County’s Major Foundations and Residents: 
 
As stated above, the County, SAMCEDA, and SMCU are structuring the small business grant process to 
handle the distribution of grants on a countywide basis and on a geo-targeted basis if a donor wishes to 
target specific funding for a city or sub-region of the county. 
An example could be that a city uses their SMCSF contribution and “challenges” local foundations or 
residents to match or exceed the city’s dollars to deepen the impact of public support for the small 
business grant program. 
 
 
 
IMPORTANT FINANCIAL PROTOCOLS FOR  
FUNDING SMC STRONG FUND SMALL BUSINESS GRANTS 
 
Funds to support small business grants from cities, foundations or individuals MUST be donated to the  
San Mateo Credit Union – NOT the Silicon Valley Community Foundation. 
 
 
Internal Revenue Service rules do not allow The Silicon Valley Community Foundation to make direct grant 
payments to small business.  
 
This is why SAMCEDA and the County Manager’s Office have been working with SMCU to set up the small 
business grant process through SMCU. 
 
Any funds not donated directly to SMCU will be significantly delayed in helping San Mateo County’s 
small businesses who need the money right away.
