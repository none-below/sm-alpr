SUBJECT:
Emergency Ordinance Imposing a Moratorium on Evictions for Nonpayment of Rent by Tenants Impacted by the COVID-19 
Pandemic
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive a report from the City Attorney on San Mateo County’s emergency regulations imposing a moratorium on 
evictions for nonpayment of rent by residential tenants impacted by the COVID-19 Pandemic and on federal and state 
actions taken to address foreclosures and adopt an Emergency Ordinance making minor amendments to the previously 
adopted ordinance imposing a moratorium on evictions for nonpayment of rent by small business tenants impacted by the 
COVID-19 Pandemic.
BACKGROUND:
On March 16, 2020, the City Council adopted a resolution proclaiming a local health emergency related to the novel 
Coronavirus pandemic.  The City Council also directed staff to prepare and bring back for council consideration an 
emergency ordinance to protect tenants from eviction for nonpayment of rent related to the economic impacts of the 
pandemic.  
On March 23, 2020, the City Council adopted an emergency ordinance imposing a moratorium to protect small business 
tenants impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic.  The City Council was aware that the San Mateo County Board of Supervisors 
was considering the adoption of an emergency ordinance to protect residential tenants on March 24, and, for this reason, 
the council did not include residential tenants in the ordinance it adopted.  
During its consideration of the eviction ordinance, the Council expressed its concern about the potential for widespread 
foreclosures due to the financial impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on San Mateo homeowners.  The Council also directed 
the city attorney to investigate what actions, if any, the council could take to address this problem.
On March 24, 2020, the Board of Supervisors passed emergency regulations to protect residential tenants impacted by the 
COVID-19 pandemic from evictions.  
On March 30, staff will present to the City Council (1) a report on the emergency eviction regulations adopted by the Board 
of Supervisors, (2) a report on actions taken by the state and federal government on foreclosures, and (3) a proposed 
ordinance to make certain technical and clarifying amendments to the small business eviction ordinance adopted by the 
Council on March 23. 
The County’s Emergency Regulations
With the exception of a narrow class of evictions, the emergency regulations approved by the Board of Supervisors is

substantially consistent with the emergency order issued by the City of Santa Monica that served as the model for the 
ordinance prepared by staff. The county’s ordinance allows for the eviction of tenants even during the emergency period 
when the tenant is being evicted so that the unit may be occupied by the owner, or a member of the owner’s family. In 
staff’s view, evictions for this purpose should be infrequent and few in number.  
On the other hand, the county’s regulations provide tenants with a procedural advantage over the Santa Monica model.  
Under the Santa Monica model, in order to receive the protection of the ordinance, the burden was on the tenant to notify 
the landlord when rent was due and the tenant as unable to pay.  Under the county’s regulations, the landlord is required 
to provide the tenant with notice of the protections in the County’s regulations before the landlord may take any action 
toward evicting the tenant for nonpayment of rent.
Foreclosures
In Mid-March, the Federal Housing Finance Agency (HFA) announced a 60-day stop on foreclosures for federally backed 
mortgages for single-family homes.  One source reports that 60% of mortgages nationwide are backed by the HFA and 
would receive this protection.  
On March 25, Governor Gavin Newsom announced an agreement with five major banks and almost 200 other financial 
institutions on the temporary suspension of foreclosure proceedings and the deferral of mortgage payments.  Under the 
agreement, lenders including Wells Fargo, JP Morgan Chase, Citibank, and US Bank, will stop foreclosures for 60 days, and 
will defer mortgage payments for up to 90 days, and possibly longer.  According to an industry expert, this agreement is 
expected to cover the remaining 40% of California borrowers who did not receive the benefit of the HFA’s action.
In addition, during the County’s deliberations on its eviction regulations, county counsel indicated that its staff had 
researched whether the county could enact a foreclosure moratorium. County counsel concluded that the local 
governments are preempted from taking such action by state and federal law.
Amendments to the City’s Small Business Eviction Ordinance
Staff has identified a number of areas in which the emergency ordinance enacted by the Council on March 23 can be 
improved.  These are largely technical amendments and clarifications. The proposed amendments revise the recitals (the 
“whereas” clauses) to remove references to residential tenancies and concerns with residential evictions, revises section 4 
to clarify that a person who earns income by renting housing can be considered a “small business,” and replaces the CEQA 
determination with a more appropriate exemption.  In addition, the term is amended to provide that the ordinance will 
expire on May 31, 2020.  This amendment is being made to eliminate an argument that the city’s ordinance is preempted 
by state law.  The Governor’s order authorizing local governments to enact eviction moratoria expires on May 31.
BUDGET IMPACT:
Adoption of the proposed emergency ordinance will have no impact on the budget.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This In accordance with California Environmental Quality Act guidelines section 15061(b)(3), adoption of this emergency 
ordinance is exempt from the provisions of the California Environmental Quality Act because there is no possibility that the 
implementation of this ordinance may have significant effects on the environment. This ordinance will apply tenant 
protections to tenants in existing residential units for a limited period of time.

Section 4. A temporary moratorium on eviction for non-payment of rent by small business and nonprofit entity 
tenants impacted by the COVID-19 crisis is imposed as follows:
(a) During the period of local emergency declared in response to COVID-19, no landlord shall endeavor 
to evict a tenant in either of the following situations: (1) for nonpayment of rent if the tenant demonstrates that 
the tenant is unable to pay rent due to financial impacts related to COVID-19 or (2) for a no-fault eviction unless 
necessary for the health and safety of tenants, neighbors, or the landlord. A landlord who knows that a tenant 
cannot pay some or all of the rent temporarily for the reasons set forth above shall not serve a notice pursuant 
to CCP 1161(2), file or prosecute an unlawful detainer action based on a 3-day pay rent or quit notice, or 
otherwise seek to evict for nonpayment of rent. A landlord knows of a tenant’s inability to pay rent within the 
meaning of this Ordinance if the tenant, within 14 days after the date that rent is due, notifies the landlord in 
writing of lost income and inability to pay full rent due to financial impacts related to COVID-19, and provides 
documentation to support the claim. For purposes of this Ordinance, “in writing” includes email or text 
communications to a landlord or the landlord’s representative with whom the tenant has previously 
corresponded by email or text. Any medical or financial information provided to the landlord shall be held in 
confidence, and only used for evaluating the tenant’s claim. Nothing in this Ordinance shall relieve the tenant of 
liability for the unpaid rent, which the landlord may seek after expiration of the local emergency and the tenant 
must pay within six months of the date the local emergency is lifted. A landlord may not charge or collect a late 
fee for rent that is delayed for the reasons stated in this Ordinance; nor may a landlord seek rent that is delayed 
for the reasons stated in this Ordinance through the eviction process.
(b) For purposes of this Ordinance, “financial impacts related to COVID-19” include, but are not limited 
to, loss of income due to any of the following: (1) being sick with COVID-19, or caring for a household or family 
member who is sick with COVID-19; (2) lay-off, loss of hours, or other income reduction resulting from business 
closure or other economic or employer impacts of COVID-19; (3) compliance with a recommendation from a 
government health authority to stay home, self-quarantine, or avoid congregating with others during the state 
of emergency; (4) extraordinary out-of-pocket medical expenses; or (5) child care needs arising from school 
closures related to COVID-19.
(c) For purposes of this Ordinance, “no-fault eviction” refers to any eviction for which the notice to 
terminate tenancy is not based on alleged fault by the tenant, including but not limited to eviction notices 
served pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1161(1) or 1161(5).
(d) For the purposes of this Ordinance a “small business” is a business entity with gross receipts of less 
than $25 million per year and includes persons and entities who earn income through the rental of housing.

terminate tenancy is not based on alleged fault by the tenant, including but not limited to eviction notices 
served pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1161(1) or 1161(5).
(d) For the purposes of this Ordinance a “small business” is a business entity with gross receipts of less 
than $25 million per year and includes persons and entities who earn income through the rental of housing.
(e) Violations of this Ordinance shall be punishable as set forth in Chapter 1.04 of the San Mateo 
Municipal Code. In addition, this Ordinance shall serve a defense in the event that an unlawful detainer action is 
commenced in violation of this Ordinance. In the event of a violation of this Ordinance, an aggrieved Tenant may 
institute a civil proceeding for injunctive relief, money damages of not less than three times actual damages 
(including damages for mental or emotional distress as specified below), and any other relief the Court deems 
appropriate. In the case of an award of damages for mental or emotional distress, said award shall only be 
trebled in the trier of fact finds that the Landlord acted in knowing violation of or in reckless disregard of this 
Ordinance. The prevailing party shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to court order. 
The remedies available under this section shall be in addition to any existing remedies which may be available to 
the tenant under local, state or federal law.
(f) Administrative review. The City Manager shall have the authority to review and grant relief to a 
landlord who experiences undue or excessive hardship as a result of this moratorium. An aggrieved landlord 
shall file a written request for relief explaining the nature of the hardship. Such request shall be accompanied by 
documentation supporting the claimed hardship, such as the property owner's interest in the property, price 
paid or option price, assessed value, tax on the property, mortgage indebtedness, income and expense 
statements for income-producing property, and the like. Decisions of the City Manager shall be appealable the 
City Council in the manner prescribed in Sections 7.16.100-110 of the Municipal Code.
(g) Pursuant Section 2.16 of the City Charter, if this Ordinance has not expired by its terms, it shall 
automatically stand repealed as of the ninety-first day following the date on which it was adopted, but this shall 
not prevent reenactment of the ordinance if the emergency still exists.  This ordinance shall remain in effect 
until May 31, 2020.

COVID-19 Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive update on operational impacts and other City efforts related to COVID-19 and confirm rules and regulations issued 
by the City Manager acting as the Director of Emergency Services in response to declared emergency.
BACKGROUND:
On March 4, 2020, the Governor proclaimed a state of emergency in response to the outbreak of respiratory illness due to 
a novel coronavirus now referred to as COVID-19.  As a consequence of this proclamation the City Manager, acting as the 
City’s Director of Emergency Services, was authorized to make and issue rules and regulations on matters reasonably 
related to the protection of life and property as affected by such emergency.
On March 16th, 2020, the County of San Mateo Health Officer issued an order to "shelter in place", effective March 17, 
2020 at 12:01 am. This order, amongst other things, ceased non-essential activities in the County in an effort to mitigate 
the impacts of COVID-19.  Essential City operations like police, fire, and wastewater treatment services, as well as the 
maintenance of critical infrastructure, are continuing to be provided.  Other City services are being provided on a limited 
basis in conjunction with the Health Officer’s order or to administer City operations.
On March 16th at 3:00 p.m., the City Manager, in his role as Director of Emergency Services, officially opened the 
Emergency Operations Center (EOC) in response to this health crisis.  The EOC is currently in operation and is expected to 
continue to be operational for the foreseeable future.  At the March 16th City Council meeting, the Council declared a local 
health emergency.  In addition to positioning the City for potential mutual aid and cost recovery opportunities, the 
emergency proclamation also provides the City Manager broader authority to be able to expeditiously address issues 
related to the emergency.  With that said, actions taken by the City Manager outside of the general authority of the duties 
provided for in the City Charter are subject to confirmation by Council as soon as practicable.  
On March 23rd, the City Manager provided an update on the operational impact of COVID-19 and the actions that were 
taken under the authority of the emergency proclamation.  The City Council provided confirmation of the actions taken to 
date and two additional actions that were proposed.  At the March 30th meeting, the City Manager seeks Council 
confirmation of the additional actions taken in response to the shelter in place order.  A detailed description of the 
additional emergency actions requiring Council confirmation will be presented at the Council meeting.
