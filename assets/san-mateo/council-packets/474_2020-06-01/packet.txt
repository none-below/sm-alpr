SUBJECT:
Fiscal Year 2020-21 General Obligation Bond - Tax Rate Levy
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to levy the property tax rate of $0.0067 per $100 of assessed value (equates to $6.70 per $100,000 
assessed value) for the voter-approved general obligation bond annual debt service payment.
BACKGROUND:
General obligation bonds, or GO bonds, are typically issued to finance government improvements benefiting the 
community as a whole. For local agencies like the City of San Mateo, they are secured by an annual tax levy on all assessed 
property within a jurisdiction and must be approved by a two-thirds majority of voters. Due to the strength of this security 
pledge, GO bonds are typically the lowest cost form of public financing. 
In 1999, voters approved a $35 million bond issue to finance construction of a new main library and improvements to the 
City’s branch libraries. These bonds were issued in two series: $12 million in 2000 (GO Bonds, Series 2000) and $23 million 
in 2004 (GO Bonds, Series 2004A). In February 2013, City Council authorized the refinancing of these two bond issues. 
General Obligation Refunding Bonds, Series 2013, were issued for $27.295 million and resulted in net present value savings 
of $8.4 million over the life of the remaining outstanding bonds. Correspondingly, starting in 2014, this refinancing 
reduced the annual assessment rate paid by property owners by approximately 20%. 
The assessment is collected annually with property taxes. The City Council must adopt an annual resolution authorizing the 
County Tax Collector to include the assessment on the tax rolls. (Attachment 1) The proposed tax levy for the 2020-21 
fiscal year is $6.70 per $100,000 of assessed value, a 5.6% reduction from the prior year levy of $7.10 per $100,000 of 
assessed value. The recommended reduction in the tax levy is due to the projected growth in the City’s assessed value 
from 2019-20 to 2020-21. The City’s total net taxable assessed valuation for fiscal year 2019-20 increased by 7.6% to 
$28.75 billion, compared to the prior year’s $26.72 billion. The levy rate for 2020-21 assumes a 5% increase in assessed 
value, bringing the total to $30.19 billion. This calculation of the tax levy is sufficient to meet debt service requirements as 
shown in Attachment 2. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
The recommended general obligation bond tax rate of $0.0067 per $100 of assessed value for fiscal year 2020-21 is 
estimated to raise adequate funds, approximately $2.033 million, to meet the annual debt service requirement for the 
General Obligation Refunding Bonds, Series 2013, originally issued for library capital projects.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This action is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is a government fiscal activity which does not involve any 
commitment to any specific project which may result in a potentially significant physical impact on the environment.

SUBJECT:
Police Department Verizon Wireless Expenses for City-Issued Cell Phones, Equipment, and Service – Change Order 
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve a change order to the San Mateo Police Department’s purchase order with Verizon Wireless for an additional 
$40,000 to cover the estimated remaining cost of service through June 2020 for city-issued cell phones, equipment, and 
service, bringing the cumulative amount for FY 2019-2020 to $130,000; and authorize the City Manager to approve costs 
of cumulative service in subsequent Fiscal Years, which are anticipated to exceed $100,000 annually.
BACKGROUND:
San Mateo Police Department oversees the use of over 260 individual telecommunications devices to perform a variety of 
mission-critical tasks, including but not limited to: 
• Basic mobile phone communications and on-call access 
• In-field and remote web access through mobile devices 
• Connection to individual officers’ body-worn cameras 
• Connection to various criminal justice information and intelligence systems 
• Security and surveillance equipment including automated license plate technology and closed-circuit video 
• Remote-work capabilities 
Annual costs for the management of SMPD’s devices through Verizon Wireless, the City of San Mateo’s 
telecommunications provider, borders annually on the $100,000 mark, and in recent years, the annual fiscal-year totals 
have been about $98,000 for FY17-18 and $95,000 for FY18-19. SMPD issued a purchase order for $90,000 at the 
beginning of FY19-20 and estimates that this fiscal year’s costs will rise over the $100,000 mark for the first time. The City 
Council is the approving authority for purchases that exceed $100,000. Expenses vary annually, and employee turnover, as 
well as the department’s expanding need to operate remotely under certain circumstances to leverage technology, are 
factors that can impact the varying annual costs. In addition, FY19-20 saw the upgrade of all officer-connected mobile 
devices to provide exclusive public safety signal pre-emption in the event of a disaster or other public safety emergency. 
Although the expansion of the purchase order requires City Council approval, the overall expenditures anticipated to these 
technology applications are not anticipated to be significantly beyond the department’s budget allocation for this area. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Funding for Verizon Wireless expenses is included in the Police Department’s operating budget. There are sufficient funds 
in the 2019-20 budget to fund this change order.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This action is not a project subject to CEQA, because it can be seen with certainty that it will not cause a physical change in 
the environment. (Public Resources Code Section 21065.)

SUBJECT:
Downtown Business Improvement Area – Resolution of Intent for Annual Renewal
RECOMMENDATION:
Accept the fiscal year 2019-20 Downtown San Mateo Association Annual Report; adopt a Resolution of Intent to Levy the 
Annual Assessments for the Downtown Business Improvement Area; and set a public hearing for the June 15, 2020 
City Council meeting to consider levying the annual assessment.
BACKGROUND:
The Downtown Business Improvement Area (DBIA) was formed in 1986 for the purpose of improving Downtown San 
Mateo and promoting businesses within the area. The Downtown San Mateo Association (DSMA) has served as the DBIA 
administrator since its formation. Each year, as required by the California Street and Highways Code Section 36533, the 
DSMA provides the City Council with an annual report of activities and expenditures for the previous fiscal year and 
proposes assessments rates for the upcoming fiscal year. 
In May 2020, the DSMA submitted its annual report, incorporating a summary of 2019-20 activities and proposed program 
and budget for the next fiscal year 2020-21 (included as Attachment 1).  Before City Council may act on the DSMA’s 
request to renew the DBIA assessments, the request must be considered at two City Council meetings. At first the meeting 
on June 1, 2020, the City Council is asked to accept the DSMA’s annual report and adopt the Resolution of Intent to levy 
the proposed assessment (included as Attachment 2). At the second meeting on June 15, 2020, the City Council conducts a 
public hearing on the proposed assessment and considers adoption. Lew Cohen, owner of B Street Books and the current 
president of the DSMA, will make a presentation on DSMA’s annual accomplishments and answer questions from Council 
during the public hearing.
In the past year, the DSMA had been primarily focused on completing a financial review and pursuing a modification of the 
DBIA fee schedule. Morton & Associates was hired to conduct the financial review and the firm found no irregularities in 
the accounting. In February, the DSMA submitted the completed financial review to the City. The financial review report is 
included in the appendix of the annual report. 
DSMA hired NBS Consulting to study a modification of the assessment fees in November 2019. NBS completed the study in 
February 2020. And, in March, the DSMA had planned to launch a series of public reach out meetings to Downtown 
businesses. However, the outreach efforts were cancelled due to the COVID-19 events and the implementation of the 
shelter-in-place order. 
With COVID-19 creating severe economic injuries to local businesses, DSMA quickly pivoted from studies and reports to 
increased marketing and promotion of Downtown restaurants opened for business with weekly ads in the Daily Journal. 
Additionally, the DSMA increased its social media efforts to promote Downtown businesses on Facebook, Instagram, and

Annual Assessment 
There have not been fee increases or an adjustment for inflation for the Downtown assessment fees since 2009. The 
flatlined revenue has made it difficult for the organization to hire, maintain personnel, grow as an organization, initiate 
new projects, and support Downtown businesses to the greatest extent possible. For the past two years, the DSMA has 
functioned as an all-volunteer team, with City staff providing administrative support. 
At last year’s annual meeting, the DSMA expressed to City Council its desire to seek a modification of assessment fees for 
fiscal year 2020-21. Due to COVID-19 impacts on the economy, the DSMA has decided to postpone pursuing an assessment 
fees increase to help small, independent businesses make ends meet during these difficult times and reduce the cost of 
doing business. Furthermore, the economic damages created by COVID-19 will continue to affect businesses for months 
after shelter-in-place order has ended. Consequently, DSMA is proposing a 50% reduction of business improvement district 
fees for fiscal year 2020-21 at the annual public hearing for the DSMA. A letter requesting Council approval for a fifty-
percent assessment reduction is included as Attachment 3.  
The DSMA’s projected revenue for the next year is approximately $75,000 in estimated DBIA assessments. Its budgeted 
expenditures are $175,000, which will require them to draw from reserves next fiscal year. The DSMA projects a cash 
balance of $230,000 at the start of fiscal year 2020-21, which accounts for an expected $20,000 draw from reserves in the 
current year, and projects a cash balance of $130,000 at the end of fiscal year 2020-21. 
Next Steps

be published in the newspaper announcing the public hearing for no less than seven days prior to the meeting. 
Additionally, DSMA will provide information about the hearing in the e-newsletter to its members.  If written protests are 
received from the owners of business in the area that total 50% or more of the assessments proposed to be levied, no 
further proceedings to levy the assessments will be taken for one year. Any written protests received will be provided at 
the June 15, 2020 Council Meeting.
At the June 15th public hearing, Lew Cohen, owner of B Street Books and the current president of the DSMA, will present 
an overview of their annual report and will be available to address any questions from the City Council and the public prior 
to Council consideration of levying the assessment for the next fiscal year. Per Streets and Highways Code Section 
36535(b), the City Council has the option of modifying the amount of the assessment during the public hearing. Should 
Council approve of the fifty percent reduction in assessment rate, DSMA will send written notification to all of the assessed 
businesses of the new rates starting in July 2020.

Fiscal year 2020 (FY2020) presented itself as the most difficult year for the Downtown San

to operate through the efforts of an all-volunteer board and administrative assistance from City 
Hall.  The DSMA has continued the tireless work of successfully promoting San Mateo’s 
unique and thriving Downtown before and during this event.

As a reminder, each year since 1986, the DSMA has been designated by the City Council to 
manage the Downtown Business Improvement District. Originally activated to serve a largely 
retail-oriented district, the DSMA now manages a district with a much broader range of 
businesses. 
 
Thanks to its regionally central location and proximity to CalTrain, Downtown San Mateo is an 
employment hub and a popular place to meet for both business and leisure purposes. 
Commuters and leisure visitors come from San Francisco, Santa Clara County and the East Bay, 
as well as nearby Peninsula communities.  Downtown is known for its vast abundance and 
variety of restaurants, which comprise more than 20% of district businesses. It is also an 
important node in the Silicon Valley ecosystem, hosting the offices of start-ups and tech-based 
companies large and small. The presence of Mills Medical Center means that Downtown also 
includes many medical professionals and other healthcare uses. While there are traditional 
retailers in the district, the storefronts on most blocks are dominated by food purveyors, along

entities as prescribed by the American Institute of Certified Public
Accountants. In accordance with FASB ASC subtopic 958-205-05-6, the
Organization reports its financial position and operating activities in three
classes of net assets: unrestricted net assets, temporarily restricted net
assets and permanently restricted net assets.

e Unrestricted Net Assets include those assets over which the Board of

operations. Under this category, DSMA maintains an Operating Fund.

also be categorized as unrestricted net assets. DSMA has elected to
report as an increase in unrestricted net assets any restricted revenue
received in the current period for which the restrictions have been met
in the current period.

e Donor Imposed Restrictions include those assets which are subject to
a donor restriction and for which the applicable restriction was not
met as of the end of the current reporting period. They also include
assets which are subject to a non-expiring donor restriction such as
donor contributions to an endowment fund.

Cash _and Cash Equivalents include highly liquid investments and those

Dear Mayor Goethals and Honorable City Council Members:

Since the Downtown business improvement district (BID) was formed in 1986, the San Mateo City Council has appointed
the Downtown San Mateo Association (DSMA) as the manager each year. The City Council reviews the work of the
DSMA and authorizes the collection of assessments from businesses within the district. The DSMA uses the collected
fees to promote, clean, enhance, organize, and advocate on behalf of the Downtown business community.

For the last ten years, the DSMA has not received any assessment increases and revenues have flatlined. The limited
revenue has made it difficult for the organization to grow and operate independently. Therefore, it has been the main
goal of the DSMA to propose a modification of assessment fees for FY 2021 in order to provide greater equity among
businesses and increase revenues in order to better serve businesses.

However, the economic damage of COVID-19 has been extremely difficult for small, independent business owners. Due
to COVID-19 impacts on the economy, the DSMA has decided to temporarily postpone pursuing an assessment fees
increase to help small businesses make ends meet during these difficult times and reduce the cost of doing business.

Furthermore, economic damages already created by COVID19 will continue to affect businesses for months after
shelter-in-place order has ended. Consequently, DSMA is proposing a 50% reduction of business improvement district
fees for FY 2021 at the annual public hearing for the DSMA.

With the reduced fees, DSMA will need to spend down existing reserves in order to continue to provide services such as
Downtown cleanliness, holiday banners, and increased marketing campaign for Downtown businesses online, in print,
and on social media. While the DSMA will need to pursue a modification of the assessment in the future, FY2021 is not
the right time. DSMA can continue to function as a convener, facilitator, and advocate for the Downtown provided that
full revenue collection resumes in fiscal year FY2022.

We hope you will continue to support the DSMA and approve of our request for a one-year assessment reduction.

Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Project – Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve a professional design services agreement with Advanced Mobility Group for Phase 1 of the Downtown Parking 
Garage Pedestrian Safety Project, with an option to amend the agreement to include Phase 2, for a total agreement 
amount not-to-exceed $120,000; and authorize the Public Works Director to execute the agreement in substantially the 
form presented. 
BACKGROUND:
The Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Project includes the design of pedestrian safety improvements at the 
entrances and exits of Central Parking Garage, Main Street Parking Garage, and 2nd and El Camino Parking Garage. 
Pedestrian-friendly improvements may include convex mirrors, beacons, electronic signage, warning lights, video 
pedestrian detection, or a combination thereof to address site-specific safety needs at each facility. 
On February 26, 2020, Public Works issued a Request for Proposal (RFP) to firms for professional design services for 
Phases 1 and 2 of the Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Project. Public Works staff received three (3) proposals 
by the deadline of April 10, 2020 from Advanced Mobility Group (AMG); DESMAN, Inc.; and TJKM Transportation 
Consultants. Staff evaluated each proposal and selected AMG and DESMAN, Inc. to participate in a panel interview 
process. Staff determined that AMG is most qualified to complete the scope of work identified in the RFP.
The work will be divided into two phases to allow for community input. Phase 1 of the project will include a study to 
identify options for pedestrian safety improvements, community engagement, and initial design plans for the work. Phase 
2 of the project will use the input received to prepare the 65%, 95% and 100% plans and specifications, environmental 
document, and bidding and construction support. 
Staff recommends entering into an agreement with AMG to perform the scope for Phase 1 with the option to implement 
Phase 2, for a total not-to-exceed agreement amount of $120,000. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Sufficient funds for this agreement are available in Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Project (#46F001).

 TASK 3: RECOMMEND PEDESTRIAN SAFETY IMPROVEMENTS 
Subtask 3.1 – Develop Recommendations for Pedestrian Safety Improvements 
Upon completion of Task 2, AMG will develop recommended improvements for each entrance and exit 
of the parking garages included in the study. Recommended solutions will be based on our project 
understanding and approach, existing infrastructure and constraints.  
There are several options available to improve pedestrian safety and driver/pedestrian awareness at 
parking garages. It is critical to provide the most feasible solutions for individual locations in 
conjunction with funding availability and ease of implementation. AMG will evaluate feasible 
recommendations for each of the study locations including but not limited to: 
1. Caution Car Coming Electronic Signage: 
Caution Car coming Electronic Signage installed at the 
building exterior, activated by motion detectors will alert 
pedestrians on the sidewalk crossing the parking garage of 
exiting vehicles.  
2. Audible Alarms: 
Audible alarms with a voice message stating "Attention, 
vehicles exiting. Watch for vehicles." in conjunction with the 
Caution Car Coming Electronic Signage will warn distracted pedestrians and drivers of a vehicle 
exiting the garage. 
3. Convex Mirrors:  
Convex mirrors will improve sight visibility of exiting vehicles improving awareness of motorists 
of approaching pedestrians. 
4. In-Roadway Warning Lights (IRWL):  
IRWL are high-intensity LEDs that give advanced 
warning to motorists as they exit the garage. The 
IRWLs flash at a photosensitive epilepsy safe rate. 
MUTCD compliant, IRWL are a proven method of 
increasing driver awareness up to 95%. Highly 
flexible, IRWL are an ideal traffic calming pedestrian 
safety solution at parking garage exits, at-grade 
walkways, and building entrances, however they 
should be installed with a high degree of specificity 
else they may result in maintenance issues, which 
has been experienced by several cities. 
5. Motion Detectors:  
In conjunction with the IRWLs, motion detectors should be installed. When objects approach 
the motion detection device, the IRWLs and other system equipment is energized to operate.

--- page 68 ---

6. Flashing LED Pedestrian Approaching Signs:  
Flashing LED Pedestrian Approaching Signs will help increase motorist yielding. 
The LED sign would be programmed to activate via passive detection motion 
sensors, and flash only when a pedestrian is sensed. 
7. Gated Parking Garage Exit:  
Gated parking garage exits will further improve compliance 
of motorists to exiting stop control at the parking garages 
and alert pedestrians to exiting vehicles.  
8. Restriction of Left-turns into Parking Garages: 
Reducing the number of potential conflicts will ensure further 
safety of pedestrians in the sidewalk. The study locations will 
be evaluated to ascertain if converting parking garages to 
right-in-right-out only is feasible. 
Although good lighting is rarely found to be a factor in vehicle-pedestrian collisions in parking garages 
per Pedestrian and Bicycle Safety in Parking Facilities (2017), published by the Institute of 
Transportation Engineers (ITE), it is an important consideration for parking facilities. Lighting in 
parking garages should not only provide security and visibility, but also uniformity as bright spots in 
certain areas can make lesser lit spots be perceived as darker than they actually are by the human eye. 
This is especially critical at the Central Parking Garage entrance and exit locations since they are under 
the parking garage overpass, which blocks direct sunlight at the exit. 
Based on the aforementioned ITE source, a vast majority of pedestrian injuries were slip and fall and 
trip and fall. Slips, trips, and falls can be addressed through appropriate design and construction 
practices and by providing good lighting and slip-resistant walking surfaces.  
 AMG will consider other innovative solutions to improve pedestrian safety and awareness, including 
repaving driveways with different color/texture than existing sidewalks. During our preliminary field 
observations, we noticed that it was difficult to see the entrances and exits of the parking garages for 
pedestrians on the sidewalk. Different colored/textured pavement will alert distracted pedestrians on 
the sidewalk as they approach parking garage entrances and exits.

below the ridership goal in the agreement. The City has done additional outreach via social media and the City’s website in 
the latter portion of the fiscal year to promote the shuttles and will continue to do so moving into the upcoming year.

SUBJECT:
Trash Capture at Poplar Avenue at the Golf Course Project – Appropriation and Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to accept funding and appropriate $330,000 to the Trash Capture at Poplar Avenue at the Golf Course 
Project; approve a cooperative agreement for Caltrans to contribute a total amount of $330,000 for the design and 
construction of the project; and authorize the Public Works Director to execute the agreement in substantially the form 
presented.
BACKGROUND:
The Trash Capture at Poplar Avenue at the Golf Course Project (Project) is part of the City’s plan to achieve compliance 
with the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency’s San Francisco Bay Regional Water Quality Control Board’s (RWQCB) 
Municipal Regional Stormwater Permit.  The existing permit requires the City to reduce trash and litter discharge by 100%, 
relative to 2009 baseline values, by July 1, 2022. The City demonstrated a reduction of 94% in fiscal year 2018-19 through 
existing trash capture devices, source control measures, creek and shoreline cleanup efforts, and other control 
mechanisms. The Project will provide approximately 12.4% additional trash capture reduction and allow the City to remain 
compliant with permit requirements. 
Caltrans, under its own, separate RWQCB permit is also required to comply with trash capture requirements and has 
shown interest in partnering with the City of San Mateo in mutually beneficial projects, including the trash capture device 
currently under contract for construction at the new Coyote Point Pump Station. The Project will treat large areas of 
Caltrans’ right-of-way on El Camino Real and Highway 101.  Pending final approval of the Project by the RWQCB, Caltrans 
will contribute $330,000 of funding toward the Project, which will be managed and administered by the City of San Mateo.  
There are no matching requirements.  The Project is already fully funded in the City’s CIP program, so the additional 
funding from Caltrans will allow the funds from the City’s solid waste program that were originally planned for the project 
to be utilized for other purposes.
The Trash Capture at Poplar Avenue at the Golf Course Project is estimated to cost approximately $900,000 and is 
anticipated to be completed by Summer 2022.  The final schedule will be dependent on environmental permits that will 
need to be obtained from the appropriate regulatory agencies.  
BUDGET IMPACT:
The Resolution will appropriate $330,000 to the Trash Capture at Poplar Avenue at the Golf Course Project budget (No. 
460059). Any local funding not used for the projects will be returned to their respective funds.

COOPERATIVE  IMPLEMENTATION  AGREEMENT 
 
THIS AGREEMENT, ENTERED INTO EFFECTIVE ON __________________, 2020, is 
between the State of California acting by and through its Department of Transportation, referred 
to herein as CALTRANS and the City of San Mateo, a body politic and a municipal corporation 
(chartered City) of the State of California, referred to herein as AGENCY.  CALTRANS and 
AGENCY are together referred to as PARTIES.   
RECITALS 
1. 
CALTRANS and AGENCY, pursuant to California Streets and Highways Code (SHC) 
Sections 114 and 130, are authorized to enter into a Cooperative Agreement for 
improvements to the State Highway System (SHS) as a watershed stakeholder within 
AGENCY’s jurisdiction. 
2. 
As per Attachment IV of the Caltrans National Pollutant Discharge Elimination Permit 
Order 2012-0011-DWQ (NPDES Permit), Section I.A, CALTRANS and AGENCY are to 
collaboratively implement the NPDES Permit requirements as they have been identified as 
stakeholders in the Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) or contain Significant Trash 
Generating Areas (STGA) for the Poplar at Golf Course Trash Capture Project (hereinafter 
referred to as “PROJECT”).  CALTRANS has agreed to contribute an amount not to exceed 
Three Hundred Thirty Thousand Dollars ($330,000) to AGENCY for AGENCY to construct 
the PROJECT, within the regional area under the jurisdiction of AGENCY to comply with 
the TMDL or to treat STGAs.  The NPDES Permit (including Attachment IV) is located at:  
www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/stormwater/caltrans.shtm 
3. 
AGENCY has agreed to implement the PROJECT subject to the terms and conditions of this 
Agreement including all documents attached hereto that are incorporated herein and hereby 
made a part of this Agreement (collectively referred to as the “AGREEMENT”). 
4. 
AGENCY will be responsible for all management, maintenance and operations, including 
costs of the constructed PROJECT. 
5. 
CALTRANS will be credited with one (1) Compliance Unit (CU) for each Eighty-Eight 
Thousand Dollars ($88,000) paid to AGENCY. A compliance unit is defined as one (1) 
acre of CALTRANS’s Right-of-Way (ROW) from which the runoff is retained, treated, 
and/or otherwise controlled prior to discharge to the relevant reach. The financial 
equivalent as submitted by CALTRANS is One Hundred Seventy-Six Thousand Dollars 
($176,000) per CU. The State Water Resources Control Board (“State Water Board”) is 
encouraging collaborative efforts and Cooperative Implementation Agreements and uses a 
50% discount for CU in dollars contributed to the Cooperative Implementation. This sets 
the CU equivalent at Eighty-Eight Thousand Dollars ($88,000). 
6. 
For NPDES permit compliance, CALTRANS will claim a percentage of PROJECT 
pollutant waste load reductions granted by the San Francisco Bay Regional Water Quality 
Control Board for PROJECT equal to the percentage of total PROJECT cost contributed by 
CALTRANS or the waste load allocation assigned to CALTRANS for the PROJECT 
watershed, whichever is less.  CALTRANS intends to use the project waste load reductions

Project Name 
Poplar at Golf Course Trash Capture Project 
  
  
Scope of Work/Description 
A trash screen and net or gross solids removal device will be constructed at the 
Poplar at Golf Course location in the City of San Mateo.  Approximately 333.3 acres 
of land will be treated by this system, of which 11.2 acres are from Caltrans Right of 
Way (of which 3.3 acres are Significant Trash Generating Area).  These devices will 
screen, separate and trap debris and sediment from stormwater runoff.  These 
projects provide a watershed-based solution to reduce pollutant loadings, trash, and 
sediments from entering the San Francisco Bay, thus enhancing water quality for 
beneficial and recreational uses.

Review and provide feedback on Police Department technology, including proposed use of fixed automated license plate

BACKGROUND:
The Police Department continually seeks to expand and enhance its crime detection and crime solving capabilities by 
leveraging technology. One of the City’s current priorities is the Safe and Secure Neighborhoods Initiative, which supports 
the implementation of advancements in technology to combat crime. This initiative was developed in response to the 
recent uptick in property crimes. 
ALPR Program 
The San Mateo Police Department has utilized automated license plate reader (ALPR) technology to solve crime and 
provide evidence to prosecutors since 2011. The use of ALPR technology has become an industry standard and a best 
practice for criminal investigations. In 2019, the City Council approved the replacement of the department’s six ALPR 
systems in the amount of $108,124.30. This equates to approximately $18,020 per unit. Four (4) devices have been 
installed on police patrol vehicles and two (2) on mobile traffic control trailers. 
ALPR technology is rapidly advancing and equipment is becoming less expensive, making it more readily accessible to 
cities, homeowner and neighborhood associations, shopping centers, and business districts.  Through the San Mateo 
County Police Chiefs and Sheriffs Association, staff became aware of equipment and technology provided by Flock Safety. 
This company originally developed its ALPR equipment as an affordable security device for homeowners’ and 
neighborhood associations. Over the past few years, Flock Safety has further refined and developed its equipment to be 
used by the public sector at the same affordable pricing. Flock System leases a turnkey system, which includes the physical 
equipment and backend software at a cost of approximately $2,000 per unit, per year. The anticipated service life of 
existing ALPR equipment is approximately five (5) years, resulting in an approximately 45% cost savings savings per unit 
over that time period. 
The San Mateo Police Department has four (4) ALPR equipped police patrol vehicles that capture data while moving or 
stopped and two (2) traffic trailers equipped with ALPR that capture data from a stationary position. The traffic trailers can 
be quickly moved from location to location. The Flock Safety ALPR devices are currently designed and limited to operate 
from stationary locations, such as a light pole or a utility pole. Flock ALPR cameras can also be installed on traffic speed 
feedback and changeable message boards for rapid deployment to from location to location. Nearby Cities who have or 
are anticipated to acquire Flock System equipment include: San Ramon, Danville, Foster City, Milpitas, Union City, Newark, 
Benicia, South San Francisco, and Atherton.

Staff is seeking to explore the deployment of Flock ALPR cameras in areas vulnerable to property crimes and gang-related 
activity throughout the City. Strategic deployment of ALPR cameras in fixed locations will allow the Police Department to 
react more quickly to real-time events and research captured data to discover the responsible individuals who have

ALPR Data Retention & Privacy
ALPR data is essentially a picture of a vehicle, its license plate, a date and time stamp as to when the picture was captured, 
and the location of the ALPR device. Identifying information regarding the vehicle’s license is accessed through other 
official law enforcement databases, such as the California Department of Motor Vehicles. Raw, unprocessed ALPR data is 
not stored or retained by SMPD. We utilize the strictest industry standards with respect to how the data is accessed and by 
whom. We have a records sharing agreement with the Northern California Regional Intelligence Center (NCRIC), along with 
all of the other allied County law enforcement agencies, to ensure adherence to these strict standards. Although Flock 
Systems is a private company, they do not have access to identifying information for the ALPR image data they are 
collecting through their devices. This raw ALPR data is retained on their servers for up to 30 days. The raw ALPR data will 
be uploaded directly to the NCRIC in San Francisco within 30 days, where it is stored and managed based on stringent 
federally-constructed policies. Access to the NCRIC data is strictly regulated and is for law enforcement personnel only. 
The ALPR data stored on NCRIC servers is automatically deleted after one year per Federal guidelines, unless it is part of an 
ongoing investigation. Once the ALPR data is deleted, it cannot be retrieved.
 
SMPD maintains security procedures as well as usage and privacy policies to protect the ALPR data collected. SMPD is in 
compliance with the law, and maintains a webpage explaining our commitment to privacy and strict procedures in the use 
of ALPRs. The page includes our Policy and Privacy Impact Assessment in coordination with NCRIC, as well as links to the 
Countywide record sharing and data accessing relationship with NCRIC. SMPD’s compliance was singled out as exemplary 
in the San Mateo County Grand Jury’s 2016-2017 report “A Delicate Balance - Privacy vs. Protection.”
Small Unmanned Aircraft Systems Equipment Program 
Police Departments and first responders across the county are adopting small Unmanned Aircraft Systems (sUAS) in 
support of their public safety and law enforcement missions. With widespread adoption of small Unmanned Aircraft 
Systems, police agencies are discovering the benefits of affordable, reliable, and rapid deployment of flight services 
traditionally limited to manned aircraft. The aerial perspective has always been a coveted tool in search and rescue 
missions, to locate missing persons, find dangerous suspects and fugitives, inspect otherwise hazardous locations or 
objects, investigate crime scenes or traffic collisions, and detect dangers that would otherwise not be seen from the 
ground, all from a safe location.  In recent years, the cost of sUAS have fallen, while the safety and reliability of these 
aircraft has significantly improved to the point sUAS have become a tool of necessity for public safety and law 
enforcement services. 
A sUAS consists of an unmanned aircraft weighing under 55 lbs., the command system, a secure control link, and other 
related safety support equipment. The sUAS aircraft, whether a fixed wing or rotocopter, is intended to navigate in the air 
without an onboard pilot. 
Anticipated Flight Operations 
All sUAS operations will be operated by San Mateo Police personnel who have been trained and certified in the operation 
of the sUAS. Flights will be approved in advance by the sUAS Unit Commander and Pilot, who will both ensure compliance 
with San Mateo Police Department’s sUAS policy. A copy of the proposed policy is included as Attachment 1, and a 
summary of FAA Part 107 flight restrictions area included as Attachment 2.

Flight Operation Rules 
Without a FAA flight waiver, one can generally operate a sUAS under the following Part 107 rules: 
• Unmanned aircraft must weigh less than 55 lbs. 
• The remote pilot in command must have visual line-of-sight (VLOS) personally or with an approved 
visual observer.  
• At all times the small unmanned aircraft must remain close enough to the remote pilot in 
command and the person manipulating the flight controls of the small UAS for those people to be 
capable of seeing the aircraft with vision unaided by any device other than corrective lenses. 
• Small unmanned aircraft may not operate over any persons not directly participating in the 
operation, not under a covered structure, and not inside a covered stationary vehicle. 
• Daylight-only operations, or civil twilight (30 minutes before official sunrise to 30 minutes after 
official sunset, local time) with appropriate anti-collision lighting. 
• Must yield right of way to other aircraft.

SUBJECT:
Open Container and Outdoor Dining in Public Right of Way – Ordinance Introduction 
RECOMMENDATION:
Introduce an Ordinance to amend the San Mateo Municipal Code Section 10.04.080, “Drinking – Public Areas,” to allow for 
alcohol consumption in the public right of way under specific permitted conditions, such as street closures for special 
events or outdoor dining for restaurants. 
BACKGROUND:
Restaurants are one of the many economic sectors impacted by the global pandemic, COVID-19. They are suffering from a 
tremendous loss of revenue due to the inability to retain clientele amidst shelter-in-place and social distancing regulations. 
According to the business outreach calls conducted by staff, restaurants in the City of San Mateo are seeing reductions of 
over 70% of their revenues as a result of the shelter-in-place orders. The City Council stated an interest in wanting to help 
create solutions to assist restaurants during this economically challenging time.  
It is anticipated that when restaurants are allowed to re-open for in-restaurant dining, their capacity will be significantly 
decreased in order to keep tables appropriately socially distant. The City Council is interested in exploring ways to allow 
restaurants to offer full-service outdoor dining on portions of the public right-of-way in order to expand their potential 
seating capacity once the shelter-in-place orders are amended to allow for in-restaurant dining. 
The San Mateo Municipal Code Section 10.04.080, “Drinking – Public Areas,” prohibits open containers of alcohol in the 
public right-of-way, which includes both sidewalks and the street area. This prohibits restaurants from providing alcohol 
service when using these areas. In addition, as written, this section of the Municipal Code does not allow for alcohol 
consumption during street closures for special events, such as the September Nights on B Event, although that provision of 
the Code has not been enforced.
In order to allow for alcohol consumption within the public right of way, staff proposes amending Municipal Code Section 
10.04.080 to allow for alcohol consumption in the public right-of-way under specific permitted conditions, including special 
events or for outdoor restaurant dining. After consultation with the Police Department, the last sentence of Section 
10.04.080 is being removed, as it is no longer necessary. The proposed Ordinance is included as Attachment 1 to this 
report. It is important to note, that even if this Ordinance is adopted, Alcoholic Beverage Control (ABC) permit restrictions, 
which are governed by the State, will continue to apply. Restaurants may need to amend their ABC permits in order to 
legally serve alcohol if they expand their seating areas outdoors.
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no anticipated budget impact for this item.
