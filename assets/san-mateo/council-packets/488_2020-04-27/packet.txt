SUBJECT:
COVID-19 Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive update on operational impacts and other City efforts related to COVID-19 and confirm rules and regulations issued 
by the City Manager acting as the Director of Emergency Services in response to declared emergency.
BACKGROUND:
On March 4, 2020, the Governor proclaimed a state of emergency in response to the outbreak of respiratory illness due to 
a novel coronavirus now referred to as COVID-19. As a consequence of this proclamation the City Manager, acting as the 
City’s Director of Emergency Services, was authorized to make and issue rules and regulations on matters reasonably 
related to the protection of life and property as affected by such emergency. 
On March 16th, 2020, the County of San Mateo Health Officer issued an order to "shelter in place", effective March 17, 
2020 at 12:01 am. This order, amongst other things, ceased non-essential activities in the County in an effort to mitigate 
the impacts of COVID-19. This order has subsequently been extended, with the current order in place through May 3rd.
 
The City Council also declared a local health emergency on March 16th. In addition to positioning the City for potential 
mutual aid and cost recovery opportunities, the emergency proclamation also provides the City Manager broader 
authority to be able to expeditiously address issues related to the emergency. With that said, actions taken by the City 
Manager outside of the general authority of the duties provided for in the City Charter are subject to confirmation by 
Council as soon as practicable. 
Since March 23rd, the City Manager has provided a weekly update on the operational impact of COVID-19 and the actions 
that were taken under the authority of the emergency proclamation. The City Council has subsequently provided 
confirmation of the actions the City Manager has taken to date. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for receiving this informational report.

SUBJECT:
40 Baywood Avenue – Property Donation
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction to staff regarding negotiations for the possible donation of the 40 Baywood Avenue property to the City.
BACKGROUND:
In August 2019, the City Council considered the possible donation of the property at 40 Baywood Avenue. The 
owner of the property, Thelma Lilienthal, approached the City about donating her property, along with an endowment to 
maintain it, to the City as part of her estate.  Staff brought the proposal to City Council for consideration and was given 
direction to negotiate terms with the donor. 
40 Baywood Avenue is comprised of two parcels that together total 3.24 acres and are split between two jurisdictions. The 
parcel that contains the main house, which is a roughly 7,000 square foot building, is located in Hillsborough and is 1.34 
acres. The other parcel, which is the larger of the two at 1.9 acres, is located in San Mateo and primarily contains 
landscaped grounds. The property is surrounded on all sides by other single-family residences.
Since August 2019, staff has continued conversations with Ms. Lilienthal, her Trustee, and her legal team. Ms. Lilienthal 
has changed her initial property donation offer and now wants to leave the property to the City of San Mateo and Town of 
Hillsborough for both jurisdictions to jointly manage.  Before committing to moving forward with the donation, the Council 
needs to consider the proposed terms of the donation, including:
1.
Joint ownership with the Town of Hillsborough
2.
The Trustee’s role in managing the estate endowment
3.
The ability of the property to generate sufficient revenues to cover operating expenses
4.
Potential restrictions on use of the venue
 
BUDGET IMPACT:
It is anticipated that owning and operating this property will not be cost neutral and will require an ongoing commitment 
of funds from the General Fund. Staff will present an estimate of the expense of owning and operating this property at the 
April 27th City Council meeting.

SUBJECT:
Planning Application Processing and Public Outreach During and After the Shelter-in-Place Order
RECOMMENDATION:
Discuss and consider continued processing of planning applications and policy update efforts and shifting to remote 
community engagement tools.
BACKGROUND:
Since the Shelter-in-Place Order was issued on March 16, 2020, staff has ceased acceptance of all new planning 
applications for development projects and has either canceled or postponed all in-person public outreach meetings and 
workshops for policy and code update projects, including the General Plan Update (GPU). Staff has, however, continued to 
remotely process all active planning applications for development projects that were submitted prior to the issuance of 
the Order, has been communicating with potential applicants who are interested in submitting applications, and continued 
processing and issuance of building permits for essential projects.
ANALYSIS
Public engagement and community input, which have historically included in-person meetings, are integral to the planning 
application and policy update processes. For planning applications, public outreach and neighborhood meetings are 
required by City Resolution prior to new submittals and for continued processing of current submittals. Public outreach 
meetings are also integral to policy and code updates given the need for the community at-large to provide input and build 
consensus. It is expected that large-scale gatherings that have traditionally been part of the development and policy 
planning process will not be allowed for an extended amount of time. Furthermore, even when gatherings are allowed, 
many members of the public may be hesitant to attend meetings in-person for the foreseeable future due to public health 
concerns. Therefore, to continue processing of active planning projects and to allow new applications in the near future, 
staff proposes to develop new remote meeting protocols and guidelines for facilitating public outreach and neighborhood 
meetings hosted by both City staff and project applicants.

SUBJECT:
Financial Assistance for Community Child Care Providers 
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive information regarding restrictions on use of child care development impact fee and other options for providing 
financial assistance to childcare providers impacted by COVID-19 pandemic and provide direction to staff.  
BACKGROUND:
At its last meeting, the City Council appropriated $400,000 from the general fund to provide grants to assist small 
businesses impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic.  In the course of its deliberations, council members expressed concern 
about the ongoing economic viability of the community’s childcare providers impacted by state and local “shelter in place” 
orders.  The council directed staff to identify options for providing financial assistance to childcare providers, and, 
specifically, to determine whether the city may use uncommitted child care development impact fees to help existing child 
care providers remain in business. 
Adoption of the City’s Child Care Development Impact Fee
In 2004, the City adopted a childcare development impact fee in accordance with procedures required by state law 
referred to as the Mitigation Fee Act (the “Act”).  The Act requires that, among other things, the City prepare a study 
demonstrating that the amount of the fee bears a reasonable relationship to the increase in need for childcare facilities 
resulting from the development upon which the fee is imposed.  While the City’s fee study discussed the potential for a fee 
imposed on residential development, the City ultimately chose to only impose the fee on commercial development.  The 
fee study calculated construction costs for child care facilities and based the fee amount on those costs.  
The Act also requires that cities identify the proposed use of funds at the time a new fee is established and imposed.  The 
Act prohibits the use of fees for purposes other than those for which the fees were collected.  The administrative report 
submitted at the time the fee was established stated that the funds would be used to fund construction of child care 
facilities – both new construction and tenant improvements.  Therefore, as adopted, the uncommitted childcare fees the 
City has collected to date may only be used to finance the construction of new childcare facilities and may not be used to 
fund the cost of operating existing facilities.
Other Options
At the time this report was drafted, staff was exploring a number of different options to address the needs of existing 
childcare providers.  Staff will prepare and present a detailed description of these options at the April 27 council meeting.
