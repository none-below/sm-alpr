SUBJECT:
Emergency Ordinance – Imposing a Moratorium on Small Business Evictions Related to COVID-19
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive a report from the City Attorney and provide direction on whether to extend the emergency ordinance imposing a 
moratorium on evictions for nonpayment of rent by small business tenants impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic.
BACKGROUND:
On March 23, the City Council adopted an emergency ordinance imposing a moratorium on the eviction of small business 
tenants impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic. On March 30, the City Council adopted an ordinance amending the 
provisions of the moratorium ordinance. The amendment revised the term of the ordinance to align it with the provisions 
of Govenor Newsom's order authorizing cities to adopt eviction moratoria. The governor's order expires on May 31, unless 
extended. The City's ordinance was amended to provide that it would also expire on May 31. An agenda item has been 
scheduled to determine whether the City Council wishes to extend the termination date of the City's eviction moratorium. 
As of the date this memo was prepared there has been no announcement about whether the governor will act to extend 
his previous order. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
The decision on whether to extend the eviction moratorium will have no impact on the City's budget.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This action is not a project subject to CEQA, because it can be seen with certainty that it will not cause a physical change in 
the environment. (Public Resources Code Section 21065.)

SUBJECT:
COVID-19 Update (#8)
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive update on operational impacts and other City efforts related to COVID-19 and confirm rules and regulations issued 
by the City Manager acting as the Director of Emergency Services in response to declared emergency. 
BACKGROUND:
On March 4, 2020, the Governor proclaimed a state of emergency in response to the outbreak of respiratory illness due to 
a novel coronavirus now referred to as COVID-19.  As a consequence of this proclamation the City Manager, acting as the 
City’s Director of Emergency Services, was authorized to make and issue rules and regulations on matters reasonably 
related to the protection of life and property as affected by such emergency.
On March 16th, 2020, the County of San Mateo Health Officer issued an order to "shelter in place", effective March 17, 
2020 at 12:01 am. This order, amongst other things, ceased non-essential activities in the County in an effort to mitigate 
the impacts of COVID-19.  On March 31st, this order was extended through May 3rd.  On April 27th, the Health Officer 
announced that the shelter-in-place order would be extended again through the end of May.
The City Council also declared a local health emergency on March 16th.  In addition to positioning the City for potential 
mutual aid and cost recovery opportunities, the emergency proclamation also provides the City Manager broader 
authority to be able to expeditiously address issues related to the emergency.  With that said, actions taken by the City 
Manager outside of the general authority of the duties provided for in the City Charter are subject to confirmation by 
Council as soon as practicable.  
Since March 23rd, the City Manager has provided a weekly update on the operational impact of COVID-19 and the actions 
that have been taken under the authority of the emergency proclamation.  The City Council has subsequently provided 
confirmation of the actions the City Manager has taken to date.  
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for receiving this informational report.  
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This informational report is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative activity that will 
not result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(5).)

Restaurants
may reopen
in the street

Downtown, 25th Avenue are
two potential spots for dining

By Zachary Clark
DAILY JOURNAL STAFF

San Mateo residents
may soon be able to dine
at restaurants outdoors,
and B Street and 25th
Avenue are two locations
under consideration, offi-
cials announced Monday.

The idea is to poten-
tially close those or other streets to traffic
to allow restaurants to serve customers in a
manner compliant with social distancing
guidelines.

“T think this is one of the only ways to
keep restaurants in business” said Mayor
Joe Goethals.

At least three restaurants in the city have
already expressed strong interest in the
idea, Goethals said at Monday’s meeting,
adding the program might include parklets
or other outdoor seating areas in locations
without restaurants. Goethals also said par-
ticipating restaurants should be permitted
to also sell alcohol outside because “it’s an
important part of keeping them in busi-
ness.”

SUBJECT:
Fiscal Sustainability Plan - Direction
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction to staff on the path forward with the City's Fiscal Sustainability Plan.
BACKGROUND:
The COVID-19 pandemic has had a significant impact on all aspects of life on a global, national, state, and local level. In 
addition to COVID-19's toll on health and wellness, the financial impact has been severe and immediate. The City of San 
Mateo’s tax revenues constitute over 80 percent of its total General Fund revenue, which includes sales tax, transient 
occupancy tax, and real property transfer tax. Due to COVID-19, non-essential businesses have been closed during the 
shelter in place, hotels have been reduced to single-digit occupancy, and the real estate market has been hampered. 
The appropriations included in the proposed 2020-21 budget already reflect preliminary actions taken to address the 
financial impact of COVID-19 based on Council direction, including freezing non-safety vacant positions that are funded or 
indirectly supported by the General Fund, suspending additional discretionary payments to CalPERS to address the City's 
unfunded pension liability, and suspending the housing set-aside. Even with those actions, the City’s proposed 2020-21 
budget has a General Fund deficit of $7.8 million. 
The Fiscal Sustainability Plan (Plan) outlines options to reduce costs that the Council could opt to move forward with to 
address the City's structural deficit and ensure the City is positioned for long-term fiscal sustainability. The options for 
consideration include modifying operations, employee compensation, funding support, revenue enhancement, and other 
items. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
The City’s proposed 2020-21 budget projects a General Fund deficit of $7.8 million. The Fiscal Sustainability Plan is 
designed to identify options to address the City's General Fund structural deficit.
