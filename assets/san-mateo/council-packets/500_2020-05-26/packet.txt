CITY OF SAN MATEO
LIBRARY BOARD OF TRUSTEES
February 24, 2020
Meeting Minutes
1.
CALL TO ORDER 
 The meeting was called to order at 5:35 p.m. by Dr. Lester Chen, President. 
2.
PLEDGE OF ALLEGIANCE & ROLL CALL
Dr. Lester Chen led those in attendance with the Pledge of Allegiance.  Executive Assistant Dena 
Gomez conducted roll call.  Board Members Dr. Lester Chen, Lisa Diaz Nash, Elizabeth De Winter, 
Kathy Shields and Don Hill were present.
3.
CONSENT CALENDAR ADOPTION 
Ms. Diaz Nash advised that there is a typographical error in the meeting minutes of January 27, 
2020. Ms. Gomez stated that she would make this correction. Dr. Chen requested the statistical 
reports for January 2020 be pulled for discussion.  After discussion, Ms. Shields made a motion to 
approve the consent calendar, it was seconded by Ms. Diaz Nash, and the motion passed 5-0.
4.   OLD BUSINESS
A.
Unattended Child Policy: Deputy City Librarian James Moore presented an updated version of 
the Unattended Child Policy that included the Board’s recommended edits, along with 
advisement from the San Mateo City Attorney’s Office. As to the third bulleted policy point, Mr. 
Hill motioned to change the wording to “Caregivers are expected (in place of must) to stay 
within eyesight of the child/children they are responsible for attending to”, Ms. De Winter 
seconded the motion, and the motion passed 5-0. A motion to formally adopt the policy with 
the presented changes was made by Dr. Chen, seconded by Ms. De Winter, and the motion 
passed with four votes in favor and one vote against.
B.
Overdue Fine Elimination: Mr. Moore shared that he met with City Manager Drew Corbett and 
was informed that the City of San Mateo would make up the difference in revenue if the San 
Mateo Public Library adopted a policy of no fines for overdue materials. Therefore, it was not 
necessary to discuss possible areas of the Library’s budget to trim in order to accommodate fine 
elimination. Board members expressed their support of fine elimination; and of the need to 
have clear written communication explaining the difference between overdue fines and fees for 
lost items. Mr. Moore will work with library managers to prepare a draft policy to present to the 
Board for review at a future meeting.
C.
Community Outreach Strategy: Ms. Diaz Nash reviewed a draft document of a community 
outreach strategy that included desired goals, strategy proposals, ideas for implementation, and 
the ideal impact it could accomplish. Board members offered their suggestions and input and, 
since fine elimination was previously chosen to be the first subject presented to the community 
for input, this topic was tabled until a formal vote is taken on fine elimination.
5.   REPORTS
A.
Library Board President:  Dr. Chen did not present a report.
B.
City Librarian: Mr. Moore shared the following:


The Library will be allowing the U.S. Census Bureau to set up in the lobby of the Main Library 
to conduct employment outreach. Additionally, they will be using the community meeting 
rooms to conduct training for census workers, and Library staff will be trained to assist 
patrons with completing the census questionnaire online.

This week, Mr. Moore will be attending the Public Library Association conference in 
Nashville.

National Library Week is April 19-25, and the theme is “Find Your Place at the Library”. 

Mr. Moore shared thoughts about the Library’s mission, and his vision to move in the 
direction of increased active learning and project creation opportunities, do-it-yourself 
clinics, providing space for local writers to hone their skills and showcase their work, and to 
be a hub for entrepreneurship. Ms. Girard added that she would like this included in 
LibraryLink, the Foundation’s publication.

Mateo, the Library’s community outreach van, will be at Central Park on Saturday, April 11 
for Eggstravaganza.
C.
Library Foundation Representative: Foundation President Ann Girard and Executive Director 
Lynne Esselstein shared the following: 

Thank you to everyone, including Library staff, who helped at the Leadership Circle Brunch.

Thank you to Ms. Diaz Nash for the planning work on a forthcoming Collaboration 
Committee event on the U.S. Constitution.

Many sponsorships have already been received for the Author’s Gala on April 18. Gillead 
renewed their $7,500 sponsorship and Zoetic Tapestry Project is a new $7,500 sponsorship.

Mary Holst is stepping down for now from her volunteer position with the Friends of the 
Library. 

A visitor from Franklin Templeton expressed confusion on the difference between the San 
Mateo Public Library (SMPL) versus the San Mateo County Libraries (SMCL). Ms. Esselstein, 
Mr. Moore and other Library staff that were present at this meeting provided information 
on the difference between these two library systems, including that SMPL is the 
biotechnology center for the region. It remains an ongoing issue that the website for SMCL 
is not clear that donations made to SMCL will not benefit SMPL.


Dr. Chen attended a program at the Main Library presented by the African American Library 
Advisory Committee: Renée Lubin-Holmes Quartet in concert.

Chapter 1. 
Purpose and Overview  
This manual describes the way the San Mateo City’s Boards and Commissions do their business. 
It is a compilation of all Board and Commission (hereafter referred to as “Commission” for the 
sake of brevity) rules and procedures that have been formally adopted by Council Resolution. 
These rules are intended to enhance public participation and debate so that the best possible 
decisions can be made for San Mateo. San Mateo has a long and proud tradition of open 
government and civil, intelligent public discourse. These rules, which must be approved by the 
City Council as a body, are subject to review and future modification by the City Council as 
needed. 
Appointed Board and Commission members are expected to understand and comply with these 
Rules and Procedures. Failure to comply with the rules and procedures outlined in this 
document may be cause for removal from the appointed position. 
In most cases, these rules and procedures supersede any existing Board or Commission by‐laws.

Chapter 2. 
Commission Authority 
1. 
Commission Authority, Generally 
A. 
The primary role of City commissions is to increase public input and participation 
in the determination of City policies and some programs.  
B. 
The specific role of a City commission is to review and make recommendations to 
the City Council on matters within its scope of responsibility as defined in the 
Charter or Municipal Code. The commission is basically a citizen’s advisory arm of 
the City Council, although certain commissions may take final actions in certain 
prescribed areas. 
C. 
Most importantly, the commission serves as a vehicle for public input into City 
programs and policies referred to it by the City Council or brought to its attention 
by staff. 
D. 
The commission also serves to reduce the reevaluation of these matters before 
the City Council. 
E. 
On the specific matters referred to it by the City Council, the commission serves as 
the principal reviewing body of the City. 
F. 
Commissions adhere to the City Council’s policies on the programs under their 
scopes of interest. 
G. 
Commissions serve to represent the general interests of the entire City and not 
specific program areas or specific interest groups. 
H. 
Commissions are encouraged to express their views on issues, projects or policies 
brought to them by the City department. 
2. 
Applicability 
The complete scope of responsibilities for each commission is identified either in the 
Charter or in the City’s Municipal Code and will be provided to you by the Departments 
supporting your commission. When not in conflict with the Charter of the City of San 
Mateo, or the Constitution or laws of the State of California, these Commission Rules and 
Procedures (“Rules”) shall be in effect upon adoption by resolution of the Council. 
3. 
Revisions to these Rules 
The Council shall review and revise these Rules as needed.

Chapter 3. 
Duties 
1. 
Duties of the Chair 
A. 
All City commissions elect a presiding officer, known as a chairperson for a one‐
year period. 
B. 
The Chair sets the tone for the entire meeting. 
C. 
To conduct meetings of the Commission as its chairperson. 
1. 
Ensure that consideration of items on the agenda move along without 
delay. 
2. 
Ensure that petitioners, proponents and opponents are heard but not 
allowed to disrupt the meeting.  
3. 
Ensure that decorum is maintained at the meeting. 
4. 
In presiding over matters where the public has provided testimony and/or 
raised questions, the Chair should: 
a. 
Restate the question coming before the Commission.  
b. 
Direct questions or comments requiring a response to staff for a 
response.  
c. 
Ensure that staff and members of the public direct their comments 
to the chair. 
d. 
If necessary, help keep Commissioner questions relevant to the 
matter being considered by the Commission. 
e. 
If necessary, consider calling for a brief recess if orderly conduct of 
the meeting is being disrupted.  
f. 
Announce the decision of the Commission on all subjects. 
5. 
To ensure that each member of the Commission is provided an opportunity 
to completely express their views on items of business, the Chair should 
see that Commissioners ask to be recognized by the Chair before speaking. 
D. 
To correspond on behalf of the Commission provided that opinions expressed on 
behalf of the Commission or City Government are consistent with the consensus of 
the Commission.

Chapter 6. 
Meetings 
1. 
All Commission decisions must be taken at Commission meetings. Before taking action the

2. 
No business may be transacted by the commission at a regular or special meeting unless a

3. 
Commission Meeting Dates 
A. 
If at any time any regular meeting of the Commission falls on a holiday or if it is 
known that a quorum will not be available, the meeting may be rescheduled to the 
earliest convenient time. 
B. 
The Boards and Commissions shall adopt a schedule of quarterly or more frequent 
meetings on an annual basis, which become the regular meeting dates. 
C. 
Regular meeting dates may be amended with the approval of the Commission. 
4. 
Types of Meetings 
A. 
Regular Meetings are those identified in the adopted meeting schedule.  
B. 
Special Meetings are called at a non‐regular meeting date and time. They are 
called by the Chair or Commission with a minimum of 24 hours’ notice, versus 72 
hours’ notice for regular meetings. 
C. 
Study Sessions are special meetings that are held for the purpose of providing 
information to the Commission, particularly on issues that are more complex or 
more time‐consuming than matters typically scheduled on regular Commission 
meetings. At study sessions Commissioners may collectively provide direction to 
City staff. Typically, no action is taken at the study sessions. 
D. 
Closed Sessions at the commission level would be very rare and would only occur 
at the direction of the City Attorney. The procedures for the conduct of the closed 
sessions shall be the same as those for open session meetings, except that the 
public, after allowance for public comment, will be excluded. 
E. 
Emergency Meetings are allowed per the Ralph M. Brown Act when an emergency 
situation involving matters upon which prompt action is necessary due to the 
disruption or threatened disruption of the public’s well‐being.

BACKGROUND
Given, the unprecedented changes that have occurred in everyone’s lives due to the COVID-19 pandemic, I want to 
provide a more in-depth City Librarian report for our first meeting since the emergency shelter-in-place began in March. 
Currently, there a many more questions than answers on how we and other public institutions will operate in the post 
COVID-19 world. We are all now forced to create our own maps as we go along. With this in mind, I want to shed a little 
light on the overall fiscal situation on City of San Mateo, provide some updates on how library staff have quickly adapted in 
order to maintain high-quality services without access to the beautiful facility we normally enjoy, and to provide a brief 
summary our reopening plan, specifically the introduction of curbside pickup service in early June. 
Despite the profoundly challenging situation we all find ourselves in, I have great confidence in the creative, 
compassionate, and adaptive capabilities of human beings, especially library people. We are going through a very difficult 
time and my heart goes out to all those experiencing deep, life-changing losses due to this pandemic. Moreover, as a 
community member and public servant, I embrace my responsibility to not just endure this crisis but to seek opportunities 
for growth and renewal. I believe that in every life crisis there also lies unforeseen opportunities. It is our duty to find the 
“silver lining” in our current dark cloud and emerge from this particular crisis with a renewed since of our priorities and our 
mission. We at the San Mateo Public Library endeavoring to do just that. Thank you.
Summary of Library Reopening Plan
I deeply appreciate the community members who have inquired about the library resuming its mission to provide access to 
life-enriching cultural, literary, and informational resources. Considering the circumstances, it is nice to be missed. Closing 
temporarily in response to COVID-19 safety measures was a difficult but necessary step, and re-starting service while 
keeping staff and the public safe in midst of such uncertainty, is a complex, multifaceted process. I want to assure 
community members that the San Mateo Public Library (SMPL) has planned ahead as far as conceivably possible. We have 
taken decisive steps to map out a reopening plan that begins with offering curbside pickup service to our patrons. 
We recently completed a plan to begin offering curbside pickup service starting the first week of June. The first service we 
will offer is to allow the retrieval of holds by people who have been waiting since the mid-March closure. We will then 
move to the next group of holds that were placed after the closure. Once done with fulfilling those holds, we plan to begin 
allowing patrons to place new holds to pick-up on-demand style. We will start accepting returns again once we are 
properly set-up to receive, quarantine and safely process them. At some point in not so distant future, we look forward to 
allowing some patrons back into the library on a limited basis at first, en route to eventually returning to a state of full
