SUBJECT:
Central Park Fallen Hero's Memorial Design 
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and comment on the preferred design for the Central Park Fallen Hero's Memorial. 
BACKGROUND:
The Central Park Master Plan adopted by the City Council in 2017 identified a location for a Fallen Hero’s Memorial that is 
intended to recognize San Mateo community members who died during an armed conflict while serving in the military as 
well as those members of the San Mateo Police and Fire Departments who died while on duty.  In 2018 an advisory 
Committee was formed by then Council Member Freschet to help advise the City on issues such as memorial design, 
policies, fund raising, etc. Representatives of the Committee include city staff, veterans, retired members of the San Mateo 
Police and Fire Departments, various Commissioners, and both current and former City Council members. The original 
vision of the Committee was that the memorial would list the names of individuals who died by branch of service and/or 
conflict as opposed to a more universal design that honored the service of military members, in general.  
The Committee enlisted the assistance of the architect program and club at the College of San Mateo as a way to generate 
ideas for possible memorial designs.  Students were asked to visit the park and were provided a list of basic design 
parameters that included factors such constructability, suitability for the location, durability over time, design inspiration, 
etc.  Thirteen students submitted entries that were reviewed by the Committee and several were passed along to RRM 
Design Group, for further refinement.  In addition, the Committee invited RRM to develop their own design concept for 
Committee consideration.  RRM returned with two concepts, Meandering Ribbon, which was inspired by several student 
submissions and Remembrance Poppy, an original RRM design that took its inspiration from the red poppy, the national 
emblem of remembrance that was worn following World War I.  (Attachment 2).  The Committee overwhelmingly chose 
the Remembrance Poppy which is the preferred design becoming recommended to the Commission. 
The design consists of seven vertical stainless steel columns, each containing a segment of the poppy that when viewed 
from a certain angle, will unite to form a single image.  Each column will represent a branch of the military (and the San 
Mateo Police and Fire Departments) with engraved names of those who died in service. A concrete or possibly granite 
band will be imbedded into the linear pavers with the specific units motto etched in the concrete.  Flush mounted up-
lighting will illuminate the names.  Site furnishings will include items such as benches, monument signage and a major 
donor recognition plaque.  The Committee has also indicated an interest in integrating brick pavers within some sort of 
edge treatment or pathway that could serve as both an opportunity for families to honor the service of a relative (who 
may not have died in conflict) as well as provide a means of fund raising.  (Attachment 3) 
The Commission is being asked to review the proposed design relative to its overall aesthetic and visual qualities, its 
suitability within the Central Park setting, its durability over time, etc.  As the project moves to the next phase of

Option 1: Meandering Ribbon
DESIGN INSPIRATION
This concept uses a yellow ribbon as a unifying element in a space that has 
loosely defined edges and embraces the trees that give the grove its sense of 
place. Along the ribbon are a series of benches for quiet contemplation.
At the center is a circle in the paving that serves as the gathering place; 
within it are representations of the various military and public service 
branches, with places for flags to be placed on special occasions. Instead of 
providing individual monuments to each, this concept bring all the heroes 
together as one at the heart of the space.
Names of fallen heroes can be etched along the ribbon.
Yellow Ribbon                                   Attachment 2
This concept is inspired by several of the student design submissions. A 
common element to several of these designs was circular, radial 
geometry, with various combinations of walls and benches. One design 
took this further, creating a series of symbolic intertwining ribbons.

Option 2: Remembrance Poppy
DESIGN INSPIRATION
The red poppy, which became a national emblem of remembrance in 
1920, is worn to honor and memorialize those who gave their lives to 
protect our country’s freedom. It’s historical roots date back to World 
War I, when a Lieutenant Colonel was overcome with sorrow after a 
battle in the poppy-covered Flanders Field in Belgium. The Colonel 
wrote a poem to channel his grief, called “In Flanders Fields”. 
This poem caught the eye of a professor at the University of Georgia, 
and she vowed to always wear a red poppy in remembrance of those who 
had been left behind. She lobbied to make the red poppy a national 
memorial symbol, and on September 27, 1920, the red poppy officially 
became the U.S. national emblem of remembrance. 
Red poppies are donned on Memorial Day and also on National Poppy 
Day. The American Legion Auxiliary - female relatives of wartime 
veterans, is the main group that raises money through the distribution 
of crepe-paper poppies. These poppies are handcrafted by veterans and 
provides them wages and a therapeutic outlet.
