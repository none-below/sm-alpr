SUBJECT:
Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Project – Draft Recommendations Review and Feedback
RECOMMENDATION:
Review the draft recommendations memorandum and provide feedback on the recommended pedestrian safety 
improvements for the Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Project.
BACKGROUND:
The Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Project aims to design pedestrian safety improvements at the entrance 
and exits of Central Parking Garage, Main Street Parking Garage, and 2nd and El Camino Parking Garage. In coordination 
with Advanced Mobility Group (AMG), staff conducted an existing conditions assessment to observe pedestrian safety 
concerns at each parking facility. The Existing Conditions Memorandum contains an inventory of existing pedestrian safety 
issues observed from the assessment.
Recommendations to improve pedestrian safety were developed for each parking garage based on the Existing Conditions 
Memorandum. Improvements include, but are not limited to convex mirrors, directional, traffic control, and warning 
signage, lighting, ADA compliant treatments, or a combination thereof. Recommended improvements for each parking 
facility are explained in the Recommendations Memorandum. 
Existing Conditions Assessment
The existing conditions assessment consisted of a walking audit, online community survey, review of traffic data, and a 
nighttime field review. Staff and the consultant team completed the following to observe existing conditions at the parking 
garages.

Walk Audit - AMG and City staff from Public Works and the San Mateo Police Department visited Central, Main 
Street, and 2nd and El Camino parking garages on July 8, 2020 and evaluated existing conditions using a parking lot 
safety and security checklist developed by AMG.

Online Community Survey - Staff launched an online survey from August 3 to August 14, 2020 to gather feedback 
from the community on their experience at the parking garage entrances and exits. Participants were asked to 
consider questions from an annual perspective looking back and forward due to the health orders in place. The 
survey received 274 responses.

Collision Data - AMG analyzed collision data provided by the City, which included vehicle and pedestrian counts 
near the parking garages and collision data from the San Mateo Police Department. 

Nighttime Field Review - AMG staff revisited each parking facility at night to observe pedestrian and driver 
behavior as well as lighting conditions in the evening hours.

Project Background 
City of San Mateo engaged Advanced Mobility Group (AMG) to evaluate existing conditions, identify 
pedestrian safety concerns, and provide recommendations and design plans to improve pedestrian 
safety at entrance and exit locations at three City-owned parking garages within Downtown San 
Mateo. The three parking garages included in the study are Central Parking Garage, Main Street 
Garage and 2nd and El Camino Parking Garage. 
Downtown San Mateo is one of the largest downtowns on the Peninsula. It boasts many historic 
buildings, nearly 150 restaurants, fitness centers, commercial storefronts, and Central Park. Downtown 
San Mateo provides easy walkability and a pedestrian friendly environment to access various 
businesses and residences within the core downtown area. El Camino Real, Railroad Avenue, 1st Avenue 
and 5th Avenue border the downtown area. Given the unique characteristics of Downtown San Mateo, it 
is critical to promote pedestrian safety, especially in parking environments with higher risk of vehicle 
conflicts such as parking garages. 
This memo summarizes pedestrian safety concerns identified as part of the existing conditions 
assessment. Table 1 summarizes the study locations included in this study. Figure 1 illustrates the 
project study area.

Field Observations - General Issues 
General issues from Table 2 are discussed in detail below, 
Damaged/Worn Out Signing & Striping: Existing signing and striping is damaged, old, and/or worn 
out and is not compliant with current California Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (CA 
MUTCD) standards. Damaged signing and striping affects drivers’ awareness of pedestrians, adherence 
to traffic controls, allowing turning movements that are restricted. It also affects pedestrian awareness 
of driveways and vehicles exiting the driveways.  
Dim/Insufficient Lighting: Existing interior and exterior lighting fixtures near the parking garage 
entrance and exit locations provide dim or insufficient lighting. Insufficient lighting affects visibility for 
both pedestrians on the sidewalk and vehicles exiting the driveway. It also affects pedestrians and 
drivers’ sense of security and safety. 
Lack of ADA Compliant Treatments: Under existing conditions, there are limited, or no ADA 
compliant curb ramps and truncated domes installed on the sidewalk at parking garage entrance and 
exit locations. ADA compliant curb ramps and domes alert visually impaired pedestrians of crossing a 
driveway. 
Limited Pedestrian Sight Visibility for Drivers: Currently, there is limited visibility of pedestrians for 
vehicles exiting the parking garage driveway. Additionally, pedestrians on the sidewalk cannot see exiting 
vehicles until they are at the pedestrian crossing area. There are no pedestrian warning (visual or 
audible) feedback devices at parking garage entrance and exit locations to detect pedestrians on the 
sidewalk and alert drivers of potential conflict.  
Limited Vehicle Sight Visibility for Pedestrians: Currently, pedestrians on the sidewalk cannot see 
vehicles exiting the driveway until the vehicles are on the sidewalk. There are no vehicle warning (visual 
or audible) feedback devices to detect vehicles at driveway exits and alert pedestrians of potential 
conflict. Lack of vehicle warning audible devices also creates an unsafe environment for visually 
impaired pedestrians at the driveway crossing as they have limited awareness of vehicles exiting the 
driveway. 
Security Cameras: Security cameras dissuade theft and vandalism and provide security for both 
vehicles and pedestrians using the parking garage. Currently, no security cameras are installed at 
parking garage entrance and exit locations. 
No Signage for Parking Enforcement: Parking enforcement signs such as “Security-Parking Garage 
Closed between 2-5 a.m.” help reduce homeless and loiterers from frequenting the parking garage at 
night and aid with parking enforcement. Currently, no such signage is installed at parking garage 
entrance and exit locations. 
Need for Skid Resistant Colored/Textured Pavement Surface: Skid resistant colored and/or textured 
pavement surface at driveway crossings alert pedestrians of entrance and exit driveways ahead due to 
the difference in color and texture. Additionally, the colored/textured sidewalk surface is skid and trip 
resistant, alerts visually impaired pedestrians of the driveway crossing, and enhances the aesthetics of 
the downtown environment. Currently, parking garage entrance and exit locations lack this treatment.

Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Survey  
As a part of the Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Study, AMG prepared an online 
questionnaire to gather feedback from the community regarding their experience 
walking/bicycling/parking at the study parking garages. The questionnaire was prepared using Google 
Forms survey platform and was posted for two weeks between August 3rd, 2020 and August 14, 2020 on 
the City of San Mateo’s social media platforms. The survey asked respondents to consider questions 
from an annual perspective looking back and forward since the outreach was conducted during the 
pandemic. The survey received 274 responses. This section highlights major findings of the survey. The 
questionnaire is provided in Appendix B and individual responses are summarized in Appendix C. 
Demographics 
Based on the responses received, approximately 47% of the respondents interacted with two or all of 
the parking garages. The survey results showed that Central and Main Street parking garages were 
most utilized by respondents. Approximately 36% of respondents parked at the study parking garages 
on a daily basis and 32% parked on a weekly basis. The survey asked the respondents when they parked 
at or passed by the parking garages. Their response showed that approximately 32% parked or passed 
by the parking garages in the evening between 5 p.m. and 8 p.m. and 28% in the morning between 7 
a.m. and 10 a.m. The majority of respondents (66%) parked their vehicle in the parking garages and 
29% walked by them. 
Significant Findings 
The survey was tailored for two separate demographics – respondents walking/cycling by the parking 
garages (Group A) and walking in and out of the parking garages (Group B). Additionally, the 
respondents were questioned on the challenges faced at the parking garages. Some of the challenges 
in the survey included, but were not limited to, sight visibility, good lighting, security, accessibility, 
cleanliness, and ADA compliant treatments. A holistic overview of the responses showed that most 
respondents from both demographics experienced challenges related to sight visibility, good lighting, 
and security. Key themes and significant findings obtained from the survey are discussed below. 
Sight Visibility 
The majority of Group A respondents chose sight visibility (89%) as one of the primary factors that 
needed improvement compared to 43% of the respondents from Group B. Most comments in the open 
response section of the survey highlighted sight visibility and safety issues for pedestrians walking by 
the parking garages. Feedback indicated vehicles speeding towards the exit, blocking the sidewalk 
while exiting and making restricted turning maneuvers while exiting the parking structures. Safety and 
sight visibility issues were experienced more significantly in the afternoon/evening when traffic 
demand and parking garage usage is higher compared to the morning. 
When asked which improvements respondents would most like to see at or near the parking garage 
entrances and exits, approximately 66% from both demographics opted for visual aids to see oncoming 
vehicles.

Good Lighting 
Approximately 48% and 64% of Group A and Group B respondents, respectively, chose lighting as one 
of the primary factors that needed improvement. When asked if lighting at the parking garages was 
sufficient, 39% of both demographics responded that the parking garages had sufficient lighting closely 
followed by 34% that requested for more lighting. Respondents’ comments regarding lighting aligned 
with the findings from the nighttime field visit conducted by AMG. Central Parking Garage at B Street 
and Ellsworth Avenue west driveway, Main Street Parking Garage both driveways and 2nd & El Camino 
Parking Garage were the primary locations that required more lighting.  
When asked which improvements respondents would most like to see at or near the parking garage 
entrances and exits, approximately 47% from both demographics opted for more lighting. 
Security 
When asked how the respondents felt regarding their safety, 31% of the Group A respondents said they 
felt unsafe compared to 15% of the respondents from Group B. Though this response includes safety 
due to other challenges discussed above, 33% of the respondents  from both demographics also 
mentioned that they have concerns regarding their vehicle’s safety and 44% listed homeless and 
loiterers frequenting the parking garages as one of their concerns. 
Respondents’ comments regarding security indicated security concerns due to homeless and loiterers 
in the parking garage and theft. 
Accessibility  
The survey also noted that 35% of the respondents from Group B do not feel safe walking in and out of 
the parking garages. 23% of the Group B respondents would like to see treatments that would provide 
easy access to enter and exit the parking garage. When asked about the improvements they would like 
to see at the parking garages, the respondents from both demographics opted for a clear walking path 
to the pay stations (26%), more signage to find the pay stations (30%), more signage directing to 
parking garage entrances and exits (15%) and colored/textured sidewalks (26%).  
Cleanliness 
Approximately 35% and 59% of Group A and Group B respondents, respectively, chose cleanliness as 
one of the primary factors that needed improvement. In general, several comments in the open 
responses section mentioned cleanliness as a concern while navigating through and/or by the parking 
garages. 
ADA Improvements 
Approximately 9% and 11% of Group A and Group B respondents, respectively, find the entrances and 
exits difficult to access for individuals with mobility impairments. 7% of Group A respondents would like 
to see ADA compliant treatments compared to 2% of the respondents from Group B.  
In general, most comments requested for signing and striping upgrades, cleanliness, sight visibility 
improvements, pedestrian safety features such as high visibility crosswalks, mirrors, slowing exiting 
vehicle speeds and security at the parking garages.

Next Steps 
Based on the existing conditions assessment, pedestrian safety features including vehicle warning 
electronic signage, audible alarms, convex mirrors, motion detectors, flashing LED pedestrian warning 
signs and high visibility crosswalks will greatly improve conditions at all garages surveyed. Additionally, 
treatments such as different textured/colored sidewalks and clear and visible wayfinding signs in front 
of parking garages will help alert vehicles and pedestrians of potential conflict areas.  
The existing conditions observed, and responses received from the online survey will be utilized to 
develop short-term and long-term recommendations and preliminary cost estimate for safety 
improvements at all parking garages.

Dear Ms. Chan, 
This memorandum summarizes our draft recommendations for the Downtown Parking Garage 
Pedestrian Safety (DPGPS) project. 
Project Background 
As a part of the DPGPS study, AMG conducted a daytime and nighttime field review at the Central, 
Main Street and 2nd & El Camino parking garages entrance and exit locations. Based on the field 
reviews, the team developed an inventory of existing pedestrian safety issues at each study location, 
which was summarized in the Downtown Parking Garage Pedestrian Safety Study ‐ Final Existing 
Conditions Assessment memorandum provided to the City. The safety issues observed at each of the 
parking garages included limited sight visibility for pedestrians on the sidewalk and exiting vehicles 
from the garage exits, lack of pedestrian safety features such as visual and audible feedback devices to 
alert pedestrians and drivers of potential conflicts with each other, Americans with Disabilities Act 
(ADA) compliant treatments such as curb ramps, truncated domes and ADA compliant walking paths 
inside parking garage from ADA parking spaces to pay stations. Other issues observed included but are 
not limited to dim lighting, need for signing and striping upgrades and security. 
Recommendations 
AMG evaluated the issues experienced at each parking garage entrance and exit location and 
developed recommendations to address those specific issues. A recommendations matrix was 
developed to summarize locations and recommended treatments. The recommendations include 
general improvements needed at each study location and additional considerations specific to each 
location. Table 1 summarizes the recommendations for each of the parking garage entrance and exit 
locations.

General Recommendations 
General recommendations from Table 1 are discussed in detail below. 
Signing & Striping Upgrades: Upgrading old and worn out signing and striping is recommended to 
update to current California Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices ( CA MUTCD) standards and to 
improve vehicle awareness of pedestrians, traffic controls, allowed turning movements, and restrictions 
as well as pedestrian awareness of driveways via wayfinding signs. This recommendation also aims to 
improve the consistency of markings and signs by replacing the “No Left‐turn” signs with “Right‐turn 
Only” signs. 
Convex Mirrors: Installing a convex mirror is a cost‐effective way to improve sight visibility at parking 
garage entrance and exit locations where blind spots and potential conflicts between vehicles and 
pedestrians exist. 
Lighting: At locations with dim or insufficient lighting, upgrading interior and exterior lighting is 
recommended for improved visibility for both pedestrians on the sidewalk and vehicles exiting the 
driveway. Additionally, brighter and more uniform lights provide a sense of security and safety. 
ADA Improvements: ADA compliant truncated domes should be provided on the sidewalk at parking 
garage entrance and exit locations to alert visually impaired pedestrians of the driveway crossing. 
Pedestrian Warning Visual Feedback Devices: Flashing LED pedestrian warning visual feedback signs 
along with motion detectors in the sidewalk are recommended at the parking garage exit to detect 
pedestrians on the sidewalk and alert vehicles of potential conflict. This treatment will mitigate the 
issue of limited sight visibility of pedestrians on the sidewalk by vehicles exiting the driveway. 
Vehicle Warning Visual Feedback Devices: Vehicle warning visual feedback signs along with motion 
detectors at the driveway exit are recommended at the parking garage exit to alert pedestrians of 
vehicles exiting the driveway and potential conflict. This treatment will mitigate the issue of limited 
sight visibility of vehicles exiting the driveway by pedestrians on the sidewalk. 
Pedestrian Warning Audible Feedback Devices: Flashing LED pedestrian warning audible feedback 
devices along with motion detectors in the sidewalk are recommended at the parking garage exit to 
detect pedestrians on the sidewalk and alert vehicles of potential conflict. This treatment will mitigate 
the issue of limited sight visibility of pedestrians on the sidewalk by vehicles exiting the driveway. 
Vehicle Warning Audible Feedback Devices: Vehicle warning audible feedback signs along with 
motion detectors at the driveway exit are recommended at the parking garage exit to alert pedestrians 
of vehicles exiting the driveway and potential conflict. This treatment will mitigate the issue of limited 
sight visibility of vehicles exiting the driveway by pedestrians on the sidewalk. This treatment provides 
an additional safety measure for and increases awareness of visually impaired pedestrians of the 
driveway crossing. 
Security Cameras: Installing security cameras at parking garage entrance/exit locations is 
recommended to dissuade theft and vandalism and provide security for both vehicles and pedestrians 
using the parking garage.

SUBJECT:
Temporary Storage Containers in Public Right-of-Way – Feedback and Recommendation
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and recommend City Council adopt a new fee for temporary storage containers in the FY 2020-21 Comprehensive 
Fee Schedule and related Municipal Code amendments.
BACKGROUND:
Storage containers or portable on demand storage (pods) are enclosed containers used to temporarily store household 
items for moving. City staff have received an increasing number of requests from residents to place a temporary storage 
container on-street in front of their residence as part of their moving process.
The City allows storage containers to be placed on private property, e.g. a driveway, without an encroachment permit. 
Residents living in multi-family housing or who do not have a sufficiently sized driveway request to place storage 
containers in the street because placing it on private property is not an available option. Pursuant to the City’s Municipal 
Code Section 17.08.020, storage containers may not be placed in the public right-of-way for more than one hour at a time 
and are not expressly allowed with an encroachment permit as similar obstructions in the public right-of-way are allowed.
In response to the increased resident demand for locating storage containers in the public right-of-way, staff studied 
permit requirements from other jurisdictions to recommend a new fee and simplify the encroachment permit process for 
residents. Further, staff have recommended minor amendments to the City’s Municipal Code to allow these actions with 
an encroachment permit. 
ANALYSIS 
Staff researched cities that permit storage containers in the public right-of-way and reviewed the City’s existing 
encroachment permit application process and fee in an effort to simplify the process for residents.

The City’s encroachment permit fee for dumpsters and storage containers is the second highest of all studied cities 
at $381 plus a $500 deposit. While there is a permit fee in the fee schedule for storage containers, the Municipal 
Code does not allow an encroachment permit to be issued for placing storage containers in the public right of way.

Conditions of Approval: Staff researched other jurisdictions to collect information on requirements for placing a 
storage container in the public right-of-way, such as conditions of approval associated with the permit. With an 
encroachment permit, the City of San Mateo currently requires storage containers in the public right-of-way to be 
placed within a legal parking space with reflectors for a maximum of five days. Conditions from the studied cities 
that were specific to temporary storage container use include the following:
o
Location of the container (e.g. in front of permittee’s residence, within a legal parking space, adjacent to 
the curb);
o
Restrictions on placement (e.g. on the sidewalk, in front of driveways, crosswalks, bus stops, fire hydrants, 
etc., where visibility is impaired for drivers or pedestrians);
o
Maximum number of storage containers per household;
o
Length of time allowed in the public right-of-way;
o
Placement and type of reflectors on the container;
o
Posting the permit or contact information on the container;
o
Maximum container size;
o
Restrictions on posting advertisements on the container; and
o
Delivery, placement, and removal times.

Application Process: The City’s current encroachment permit application process requires a completed 
application, Certificate of Insurance, and detailed site plan. The site plan is a diagram showing the dimensions of 
the proposed work. Staff researched application requirements for similar jurisdictions and found that some cities 
allow a sketch drawing or map mark up to be submitted in place of a site plan. 
RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on evaluation of the City’s current processes and fees and benchmark research, staff recommend the following 
revisions to the fee schedule, encroachment permit conditions of approval, and encroachment permit application process. 
Staff also proposes modifications to the City’s Municipal Code to expressly permit temporary storage containers in the 
public right-of-way with encroachment permit.

Permit Fee: The FY 2020-21 Comprehensive Fee Schedule groups storage containers with dumpsters. Based on 
customer service inquiries and previous encroachment permit applications, residents are more likely to apply for 
storage containers while developers or construction companies familiar with the encroachment permit process 
typically apply for dumpsters in conjunction with a construction project. By creating a separate temporary storage 
container fee, staff can set a fee appropriate for the service provided and staff time necessary for review. 
o
Staff recommends creating a new fee of $181 in the FY 2020-21 Comprehensive Fee Schedule for an 
encroachment permit for temporary storage containers parked in the public right-of-way. Staff estimates 
review and processing of the application will require one hour of time, and the fee is based on cost 
recovery for that time. A deposit of $350 will be required and refunded to the resident upon closing the

Conditions of Approval:  Staff recommends expanding the existing encroachment permit conditions of approval to

o

o

o

o

street space and courtesy notification to the nearest property owner(s) or tenant(s) to the container is

o
May not be placed in front of driveways, bus stops, accessible curb ramps, fire hydrants, colored curb

where visibility is impaired for vehicles, bicyclists, and/or pedestrian traffic;
o
May not be placed in a manner that restricts access to a utility box or utility manhole; and
o
Staff may restrict placement where the container adversely affects public peace and safety.

Application Process: When requesting a storage container, staff recommends requiring a basic sketch or map 
mark-up showing the proposed location, property owner or tenant’s residence, driveways, proximity to street 
corners, container dimensions, and if applicable, fire hydrants, accessible curb ramps, bus stops, and colored curb 
markings. This will streamline the application process for residents in comparison to the fully detailed site plan 
currently required and allow staff to evaluate the application and proposed location based on the recommended 
conditions of approval.

Municipal Code Amendments: The City’s Municipal Code Section 17.08.020 prohibits obstructions in the public 
right-of-way with the exception of certain circumstances allowed with an encroachment permit. Staff proposes to 
amend Municipal Code Section 17.08.020 to expressly allow temporary storage containers to be placed in the 
right-of-way with an encroachment permit for a maximum of five days. The five-day limit would discourage long-
term placement of temporary storage containers in the public right-of-way.
Next Steps
Following this meeting, City staff will incorporate feedback from the Commission and public comment and present an 
ordinance for Municipal Code amendments and a resolution for Comprehensive Fee Schedule modifications to City Council 
in late Fall 2020.
BUDGET IMPACT:
The recommended encroachment permit fee for storage containers will not have a significant impact on the Department’s 
budgeted revenue. The permit fee covers staff time and does not generate revenue.

ENCROACHMENT PERMIT PROCEDURES 
1. A Public Works Encroachment Permit is required for any work within the City right-of-way (street/sidewalk
area), public easements, or utility easements (see Subdivision Map or Title Report for easement locations).
Typical examples of work requiring this permit are sewer lateral connections to the City’s sewer main,
installing a City-approved sewer cleanout, removing and replacing driveway approaches and sidewalks,
installing a sidewalk underdrain, or installing a dock in the lagoon (for dock installation or repairs, applicant
must also fill out Dock Application Form).
2. The property owner and/or contractor performing the work are required to fill out and submit an encroachment
permit application (attached). The application shall include a diagram, with dimensions, showing the
proposed work and traffic control plan if applicable.  If a contractor is performing the work, the contractor
is required to have a current City of San Mateo business license on file with the Finance Department. Any
permit application or rider that includes utility work or traffic control plans shall be submitted for
review a minimum of ten (10) business days prior to the scheduled start date. Please note the typical
review time may be extended if the applicant is asked to revise and resubmit plans.
3.
If Public Works has no objections to the proposed work, an encroachment permit will be issued subject to
specific and general conditions (typical City of San Mateo General Conditions attached), and subject to the
applicant providing the following information:
a.
Commercial general liability (or comprehensive) and property damage insurance indicating the
City of San Mateo as an additional insured (insurance standard attached).
b.
Payment of the encroachment permit fee.  The minimum fee based on the 2019-20 Fee Schedule 
is $896.00 (a portion of this fee will be applied to the Stormwater Pollution Prevention Program
– STOPPP).
c.
Payment of the security deposit.  The deposit per the Permit Computation Worksheet is based on
the value of the new improvements plus 50% of the new improvements value for labor and
materials.
d.
Approved Traffic Control Plan (TCP). TCP requirements attached.
4. Once the encroachment permit is issued, the permittee must notify the Public Works Inspector 72 hours prior
to the initial start of work or any inspection (see general condition #5).
5. Encroachment permit security deposits are not refunded until the Public Works Inspector has reviewed the
work, and signed off the permit as being complete.  Deposits paid by check, or credit card generally take 2-
3 weeks after work has been completed to be refunded, and are processed through the Finance Department.
Deposits paid by performance, labor, and materials bonds or letters of credit are released by the Public Works
Department in writing and returned to the applicant.
6. Encroachment permit fees are non-refundable.
If you have any questions, or need additional information regarding the Public Works 
Encroachment Permit process, please email pwencroachment@cityofsanmateo.org

SUBJECT:
Disposable Food Service Ware Policy
RECOMMENDATION:
Review policy options to address disposable food service ware and provide a policy recommendation to City Council.
BACKGROUND:
The County of San Mateo has led regional efforts to address waste and litter from food facilities. Specifically, the County 
led the development of the Polystyrene (Styrofoam) Ban Ordinance and Reusable Bag Ordinance that were eventually 
adopted by all cities in the County. The San Mateo City Council adopted the Polystyrene Ban on May 6, 2013 and the City’s 
Reusable Bag Ordinance began implementation on June 6, 2013. Both efforts helped combat negative environmental 
impacts as Styrofoam and plastic bags often ended up as litter in our streets and polluted our waterways impacting marine 
wildlife and human health. Instead of Styrofoam, plastic is now typically used in food facilities as disposable food service 
ware and has become a pervasive pollutant. 
While disposable food service ware is convenient to customers, plastic disposables have lasting impacts on the 
environment. Plastic disposable food service ware is often used just for a few minutes before becoming waste that takes 
hundreds, if not thousands, of years to break down. Recycling is a complex process and unfortunately many plastic food 
service ware items are not recycled due to contamination. Furthermore, the plastic recycling market drastically changed 
over the past year; in 2019, China began to restrict plastics they will accept. Similarly, other recycling markets in India, 
Vietnam, and Indonesia are following suit. Now many plastics are piling up at waste facilities, landfills and are even being 
incinerated. This highlights the importance of reusables for dine-in restaurants but with the pandemic limiting dine-in 
operations, businesses rely on takeout and delivery options with disposables.
On February 25, 2020, the County of San Mateo adopted a Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance that addresses this 
pollution and environmental challenge. The County’s Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance (County’s Ordinance) was 
brought up at the City Council Goal Setting Session earlier this year and the Council prioritized exploration of the County’s 
Ordinance for FY 2020-21. In addition to preventing pollution from entering our waterways, an ordinance would help the 
City in achieving 100% trash reduction, a requirement of the Municipal Regional Stormwater Permit. Meeting this trash 
reduction requirement was also identified as a Council priority. This report describes policy approaches to address 
disposable food service ware, including the County’s Ordinance. 
Policy Approaches 
Jurisdictions in California have taken varied approaches to address disposable food service ware. The County of San Mateo 
developed a Food Ware Policy Matrix in October 2019 (Attachment 1) to summarize the different approaches. Some 
jurisdictions address all types of disposable food service ware while other jurisdictions regulate specific items, most 
commonly beverage straws and other small accessories. The following provides an overview of the County’s Ordinance

that impacts all disposable food service ware and a policy approach that addresses small accessories.
County of San Mateo: Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance 
The County’s Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance was adopted on February 25, 2020 and applies to food facilities 
that provide prepared food in unincorporated San Mateo County and in any city that adopts the County’s Ordinance. 
Attachment 2 provides a graphic summary of the County’s Ordinance. Food facilities are defined as an entity that has a 
valid health permit for distributing food/beverages to the public. This includes restaurants, mobile food trucks, farmers’ 
markets, and private schools. Disposable food service ware includes containers, cups, lids, utensils and straws. 
The provisions of the County’s Ordinance are summarized as follows: 

“Non-plastic and Compostable” defined:  The County requires food facilities use non-plastic, compostable 
products instead of plastic products. Non-plastic compostable products are defined as, but not limited to, natural 
fiber-based materials such as paper, sugarcane, wheat stalk/stem, bamboo, wood, etc. Traditional plastics 
(petroleum-based) and compostable plastics (bio plastics or polyactic acid [PLA]) are not allowed.

Food Service Ware Containers: Food facilities shall use non-plastic compostable plates, bowls, cups, food trays, 
clamshells, boxes, deli containers and other containers. These items may be lined with, but not entirely of 
compostable plastic, and must be certified by the Biodegradable Products Institute (BPI). 

Small Accessories:  Food facilities shall use non-plastic compostable straws, stirrers, utensils and toothpicks.  

“Upon Request” Only: Typically, food facilities provide small accessories without the customer asking. The 
County’s Ordinance requires small accessories such as straws, utensils, condiment packets, etc. be provided only 
upon request or at a self-serve area. Take-out delivery services that utilize digital ordering platforms would have to 
provide clear options that allow customers to request small accessories.
The County’s Ordinance is scheduled to begin enforcement in unincorporated County in March 2021, one year after the 
adoption of the Ordinance. This delay in enforcement allows time for the County to educate and inform food facilities of 
the required changes and allows food facilities to use up their existing inventory of disposable food service ware that does 
not meet the requirements of the new Ordinance. 
Following the County’s Ordinance adoption, South San Francisco, Burlingame and Atherton adopted the County’s 
Ordinance as a model. Half Moon Bay and Millbrae are in the process of adopting the County’s Ordinance. Enforcement in 
these cities begins one year after the ordinance adoption date. The County will provide education and enforcement in 
cities that adopt the County’s Ordinance, alleviating the City staff time required to implement the Ordinance. Due to 
COVID-19, the County is considering delaying the enforcement of the Ordinance. 
Small Accessories and “Upon Request” Regulations
Small accessories include straws, stirrers, utensils and lid plugs. Instead of addressing all disposable food service ware, 
some cities opted for regulations that focus on small accessories and require that small accessories be compostable. The 
cities of Pacifica, Palo Alto, Malibu and Santa Barbara adopted ordinances banning plastic straws, utensils and lid plugs and 
requiring these small accessories be compostable.
As of January 1, 2019, AB 1884 requires that all restaurants in California only provide plastic single-use straws upon 
request. Similar to this approach, jurisdictions have adopted ordinances that expand on California’s “upon request” straw 
regulations to utensils, stirrers and other small accessories. The cities of Palo Alto, Malibu and Santa Barbara included 
policy language that requires small accessories are only provided upon request. The “upon request” regulations can also 
be expanded to address the distribution of condiment packets and napkins. This policy approach can be coupled with 
other disposable food service ware regulations or adopted as a standalone policy. 
Food Facilities Outreach
The City of San Mateo has 428 food facilities that would be impacted by a potential disposable food service ware

ordinance. In an effort to get a sense of the barriers and challenges with the County’s Ordinance approach, staff conducted 
preliminary research by putting out an online survey. The survey was open for 28 days but only 13 food facilities 
responded. In addition to the survey, staff conducted in-depth phone and email discussions with 10 food facilities. This is a 
very limited sample size which helps provide some preliminary findings but makes it challenging to understand the full 
impact of a potential ordinance. More robust outreach and educational campaigns would be necessary prior to policy 
adoption.  
The results of the survey and staff’s direct outreach were varied and due to the small sample size reached, it is challenging 
to assess trends. Of the 23 food facilities reached, food facilities reported using a variety of plastic, compostable plastic 
and fiber-based compostable plastic products. None of the food facilities reported using all non-plastic compostable 
products that comply with the County’s Ordinance. Most restaurants did report using some compostable products. 
Depending on the type of restaurant, some businesses used very few types of disposables (e.g., only cups and straws) 
while other restaurants use all types of disposables. Overall, businesses understood the intent of the ordinance and are 
interested in “going green.” The online survey and staff’s direct outreach identified three major challenges to complying 
with the County’s Ordinance including cost, quality/durability, and availability of compostable products. 
One major communication challenge was having discussions with food facilities and distinguishing between non-plastic 
and fiber-based compostable products and compostable plastic products. This distinction is important for the County’s 
Ordinance as many products that are marked “biodegradable” are made from compostable plastics; while these items are 
compostable, they would be out of compliance with the County’s Ordinance. The County’s Ordinance does not allow 
compostable plastic because it is often missorted and placed in the recycling bin. Furthermore, since one of the goals of 
the Ordinance is to reduce the impact of potential pollution, fiber-based compostable disposables pose less of a threat 
than compostable plastic to the marine environment.
Overwhelmingly, food facilities identified their primary concern with complying with the County’s Ordinance is cost. A Cost 
Assessment prepared by the County of San Mateo (Attachment 3) shows that the non-plastic compostable products tend 
to cost more than the plastic alternative. If the City were to adopt the County’s Ordinance, the cost of compliance will vary 
depending on the types of disposables the facility already uses. Restaurants are especially worried since the pandemic has 
forced restaurants to rely on takeout operations and thus, they are forced to use disposables. Requiring more costly 
disposable products could negatively impact businesses that are already struggling. 
Another concern was the quality and durability of compostable alternatives for carrying their food items. Several 
restaurants pointed to the fact that the compostable option was not viable for their hot food item and that customers 
complain if containers leak. For many businesses, plastic containers are the preferred product. Additionally, several 
business owners mentioned concern regarding the availability of compostable products from their vendors.
Policy Discussion
COVID-19 immediately negatively impacted most restaurants as many restaurants saw a sharp decline in patronage and 
great loss in revenues. Food facilities had to close all indoor operations for six months and only now are able to offer 
limited indoor dining. While some food facilities have been able to provide outdoor dining, many rely on takeout and 
delivery options for their customers and are purchasing more disposables than ever. Restaurants are operating on very 
thin margins and any increase in cost will be more impactful now than in normal circumstances. It is important to consider 
these policy options in the context of the pandemic. It is likely that the recovery from the economic impact of COVID-19 on 
restaurant businesses will continue for an extended period, well beyond the end of the pandemic. 
The County’s model ordinance is the most comprehensive approach that would have the greatest environmental impact. 
In addition, the County is offering to provide education and enforcement support to any cities that adopt their model 
ordinance. However, a key consideration is the additional cost to the restaurants to provide the compostable products and 
whether that is something they will be able to absorb given the financial challenges that they face. 
Adopting a policy that regulates small accessories would have a more limited environmental impact but also would not 
have as large of a financial impact on businesses. This policy option could serve as a helpful stepping stone to transition

help save food facilities money. This approach might be especially impactful under current circumstances when many are 
ordering takeout and delivery to eat at home where they do not need utensils or condiment packets. Regulations requiring 
compostable small accessories and small accessories “upon request” are two approaches that could be combined or 
considered separately. The City would be responsible for outreach and implementation of these policy approaches 
because the County’s support is limited to cities that adopt the County’s Ordinance as a model. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Adoption and implementation of policy to address disposable food service ware would require dedicated staff time for 
outreach plus some direct expenses for outreach materials. Staff time and budget would depend on the policy approach 
adopted.
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
In accordance with Public Resources Code Section 21065, this discussion and recommendation is not a project subject to 
CEQA, because the City is not taking action at this time. In accordance with CEQA Guidelines Section 15308, adoption of a 
potential ordinance regulating disposable food service ware would be categorically exempt as it is an action by a 
regulatory agency for the protection of the environment.

Who Will Be Affected?
This Ordinance will impact food facilities that operate within the unincorporated areas of San Mateo County that provide prepared  
food to the public. 
Food Facility: An entity that has a valid health permit with the County of San Mateo (County) for distributing food/beverages to the public.  
Examples include restaurants, mobile food trucks, temporary food facilities (e.g., street fairs, etc.), farmers’ markets, catering  
operations, private schools, and other operations. 
Prepared Food: Food/beverage prepared on-site at the food facility using any cooking or food preparation technique (e.g., mixing,  
heating, blending, chopping, grilling, etc.).
Cities within San Mateo County have the option of adopting the County’s Ordinance, in which case, food facilities within the city  
will need to comply.
Affected Items
Disposable food service ware refers to one-time/limited-number-of-use items associated with food. This includes, but is not limited to:	
	
Bowls	
Napkins

Summary of Requirements
1. Accessories
Accessories such as straws, stirrers, cup spill plugs, condiment packets, utensils, napkins, etc. shall be provided only: 
	
•	 When requested by the consumer;
	
• 	When the consumer accepts an offer by the food facility; or
	
• 	At a self-serve area and/or a dispenser. 
Additionally, accessories shall be distributed unbundled as separate individual units. Take-out food delivery services that allow customers  
to order digitally (i.e., online) shall provide clear options for customers to ask for accessories.
2. polystyrene 
Polystyrene disposable food service ware (all #6 plastics, Styrofoam) will continue to be banned.

3. Not Allowed
	
Plastics, either traditional (petroleum-based) or compostable (a.k.a. bioplastics or PLA), are not allowed for:
	
Accessories: Straws, stirrers, utensils, and cocktail/toothpicks (and packaging that these individual items are wrapped in, if any)
	
Larger Items: Plates, bowls, cups, food trays, clamshells, boxes, deli containers, and other containers. These items can be lined 
with compostable plastic, but they must be certified by Biodegradable Products Institute or another approved 3rd party. 
4. Acceptable materials 
	
Include non-plastic, compostable, natural fiber-based materials such as paper, sugarcane,  
wheat stalk/stem, bamboo, wood, hay, etc.
	
Larger Items: Plates, bowls, cups, food trays, clamshells, boxes, deli containers, and other 
containers must also be certified by Biodegradable Products Institute (BPI) or another approved 
3rd party to ensure they breakdown into compost and are free of harmful fluorinated chemicals. 
	
The County will have available a guide with information on approved disposable food service ware.
Exemptions
	
•	 Disposable food service ware made from aluminum.
	
• 	Disposable plastic straws may be provided only upon request to consumers with medical needs. Healthcare facilities may 		
		
distribute disposable plastic straws without a request from patients.
	
• 	Drive-through areas of food facilities may distribute straws and cup sleeves without a request from the consumer.
	
• 	If no reasonably feasible disposable food service ware alternative exists, item may be exempt.
Enforcement
The County’s Office of Sustainability will be enforcing the Ordinance within unincorporated areas of San Mateo County and any 
additional jurisdictions that adopt the County Ordinance.
