The Field Training Officer Program is intended to provide a standardized program to facilitate the officer’s transition from 
the academic setting to the actual performance of general law enforcement duties of the San Mateo Police Department.
It is the policy of this department to assign all new police officers to a structured Field Training Officer Program that is 
designed to prepare the new officer to perform in a patrol assignment, and possessing all skills needed to operate in a 
safe, productive, and professional manner.
Entry level officers shall be required to successfully complete the Field Training Program, consisting of a minimum of 10 
weeks according to the California Code of Regulations (11 CCR 1004).  
The standard San Mateo Police Department Field Training Program is minimum of 18 weeks, 8 weeks longer than 
required by the State of California. 
Field Training is completed in 4 phases, each about 1 month long. Officers rotate through at least 4 different Field Training
Officers during their program.

POST Continuing Professional Training Requirement: 
After finishing the FTO Program, the training never stops. Per the State of California 
POST Continuing Professional Training bi-annual cycle, every San Mateo Police 
Department Officer, San Mateo Police Department Public Safety Dispatcher and San 
Mateo Police Department Public Safety Dispatch Supervisor shall completetwenty-
four or more hoursof Peace Officer Standards and Training every two years.  
The San Mateo Police Department exceeds these minimums, providing sixty hours 
of training to every officer every two years, in addition to any individualized 
training our officers receive. Perishable skills are part of this training schedule 
(arrest and control, firearms, driving, and interpersonal communication), and 
additional training is added to keep SMPD up-to-date. 
•
POST Requirement –at least 24 hours of Professional Training, every 2 years
•
SMPD – Quarterly required training for the entire department, 60 hours every 2 years

WHAT HAPPENS WITH A COMPLAINT?
Most complaints are either a Procedural Issue or a Performance Issue
•
Policy dictates that the Sergeant receiving the complaint attempt to resolve it at that level. Most 
complaints can be handled quickly –
•
The Sergeant offers a satisfactory explanation to the complainant – and no further action is 
needed
•
The Sergeant identifies a minor issue with the officer’s performance that can be handled 
through training or counseling (if satisfactory to the complainant)
•
Informal discussion with the officer
•
Documented counseling or Improvement plan – this is a supervisory tool - not 
“discipline”
•
Allegations of a crime, significant policy violations, or other conduct unbecoming of an officer are 
referred to the management level for final disposition. Management will later recontact the 
complainant to discuss a resolution or to advise them that appropriate action was or will be taken. 
•
The complainant can only be advised that we are taking “appropriate action” – the action taken is 
confidential as a personnel matter. 
Do all complaints go to internal 
affairs (IA)?
Not all complaints become IA 
investigations, and not all IA 
investigations are generated from 
complaints. 
IAs can be generated from a 
complaint which indicates a policy 
or law violation, or may be 
generated internally when a policy 
or law violation is alleged .

WHAT INITIATES AN 
INTERNAL AFFAIRS INVESTIGATION?
Misconduct -
SMPD initiates an Internal Affairs Investigation (IA) when the conduct of an officer is not in accordance with 
our standards – this could be a criminal, moral, or ethical issue, involving violations of department policy. 
Progressive Discipline -
An IA can also be a byproduct of progressive discipline, where training, counseling, documented direction, 
and other corrective action have been unable to correct a repeating issue, and the next step is formal 
disciplinary action.
What guides an IA Investigation?
California Penal Code  148.6 –
-
Requires agencies to notify complainants of their right to report alleged misconduct, agencies’ obligation to 
investigate,  and of the criminal consequences of filing false complaints
California Penal Code  832.5 –
-
Requires that police agencies have a defined and transparent process to investigate complaints
California Government Code 3300 et seq. - Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights Act (POBOR)
- Provides guidelines and structure to investigations of police officers that could result in discipline

Dear Chief Barberini and Mr. Corbett, 
 
We are a group of elected officials, nonprofit leaders, and community advocates who have been pushing for 
reforms to policing in our city and our region, and we are reaching out to connect with you both, in hopes that 
we can turn the recent conversations in San Mateo into action.  
 
While we have appreciated the willingness of the city to hold listening and study sessions, we have not felt
