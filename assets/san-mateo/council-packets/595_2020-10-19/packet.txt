SUBJECT:
Fiscal Sustainability Plan - Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive and file the update on the City’s Fiscal Sustainability Plan.
BACKGROUND:
On May 11, 2020, the City Council approved Phase I of the Fiscal Sustainability Plan to address the structural deficit 
exacerbated by COVID-19, which identified a combination of expenditure reductions and a revenue enhancement totaling 
$7 million: 

$3 million in employee concessions; 

$1 million in permanent/strategic position elimination; 

$1 million in additional TOT revenue as potential result of a November 2020 ballot measure that raises the City's 
TOT rate from 12% to 14%; and 

$2 million of support for the City's Capital Improvement Plan from other operating funds that has been historically 
been provided by the City's General Fund. 
The purpose of this administrative report and accompanying presentation is to provide an update to the Council on the 
following: 
1)
2019-20 General Fund results (unaudited); 
2)
2020-21 General Fund year to date results; and 
3)
CalPERS investment return for 2019-20, and the impact to the City.
2019-20 General Fund Results | 2020-21 General Fund Year-To-Date Results
General Fund Revenues
As presented in the adopted 2020-21 budget document, General Fund total revenues for the fiscal year ended June 30, 
2020 were projected to be $126.2 million. For context, the revenue projection was $7.8 million less than the adopted 
2019-20 budget of $134.0 million in General Fund total revenues, illustrating the estimated impact of COVID on the final 
quarter of the fiscal year. As of the time of this report, 2019-20 General Fund year-end revenues are estimated to be 
$133.0 million. Notable variances and are discussed below, as well as how the specific revenue type is performing in fiscal 
year 2020-21.

Property Transfer Tax
Fiscal year 2019-20 property transfer tax revenues totaled $9.1 million; $1.1 million, or 14%, greater than the prior 
estimate of $8.0 million. The previous assumption was that COVID-19 would significantly impact the real estate market, 
which would generate less property transfer tax revenue. However, despite the challenge of the COVID-19 health crisis, 
the real estate industry has adapted, and sales transactions continue to drive the City’s property transfer tax. One of the 
more significant drivers of the favorable variance was the sale of a high-value development in June 2020 that generated 
over $0.5 million in property transfer tax revenues.
The adopted 2020-21 budget projected $6.0 million in property transfer tax revenues. As of the end of September 2020, 
the City has received $1.4 million in property transfer tax from 183 transactions; an average of $7,700 in property transfer 
tax per transaction. For reference, the table below compares sales metrics from the real estate market for single family 
residential properties from March 2019 through August 2020.
Transient Occupancy Tax
Through January 2020, the City had received $3.2 million in TOT Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) revenues. As a result of 
the travel ban and resultant reduction in occupancy rate, staff estimated that TOT would end the year at $3.5 million. 
However, 2019-20 TOT totaled $4.3 million; $0.8 million, or 21%, more than anticipated. Much of the favorable outcome is 
directly attributable to $0.5 million in TOT received for the reporting period of February 2020. COVID-19 had a significant 
impact on TOT for the remainder of the fiscal year, and this trend has continued through the first quarter of 2020-21. As 
such, TOT revenues since the onset of COVID-19 have been about 10% of what had been received in comparison to the 
same period in the prior year.  Staff will continue to monitor TOT revenues closely.

Golf Fees
COVID-19 and the county health officer’s order required closure of many City facilities, including the Poplar Creek Golf 
Course on March 17th. Previous estimates for 2019-20 reflected an expectation that the golf course would be closed at 
least through the end of the fiscal year. However, the golf course was able to open on May 6th, as subsequent county 
health officer orders relaxed restrictions to permit golf activities with appropriate modifications. There were 13,128 
rounds played from May 6 to June 30, which was a 12.6% increase over the number of rounds played in the same time 
period in the prior year. As such, 2019-20 revenue from golf fees were $2.2 million; $0.2 million, or 13%, more than 
anticipated. This trend has continued through the first couple of months in 2020-21, with 15,792 rounds of golf played 
through the end of August, a 9.8% increase compared to the same time period in the prior year. This is reflected in the 
August year to date revenues of $618,000, which is $89,000, or 17%, more than the same time period in the prior fiscal 
year.
Coronavirus Relief Funds
The adopted 2020-21 budget did not assume any relief funds from the state or federal government in response to COVID-
19. However, as a part of the Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security Act (CARES Act), the federal government 
allocated $500 million to the State of California to provide relief to local municipalities in late March 2020. As a part of the 
process that led to the state’s budget getting adopted, the legislature also approved allocation of the CARES Act funds to 
local municipalities. As such, the City of San Mateo’s allocation of CARES Act funding is $1,272,829 and will be accounted 
for in the current fiscal year.  These funds will offset some of the cost that the City incurred in its response to COVID-19.
It appears unlikely that any additional relief for local government will be provided soon. The House of Representatives 
recently passed a $2.2 trillion coronavirus relief bill on October 1, 2020, largely along party lines. However, the bill is 
unlikely to pass in the Senate, as the Republican majority is unwilling to consider a proposal with a price tag more than 
$2.0 trillion. The bill would revive the provision that provided $600 a week in enhanced federal unemployment benefits, 
another round of $1,200 stimulus checks, $225 billion for schools, and $57 billion for childcare, and $120 billion to support 
struggling restaurants. However, the bill does not appear to have any provision to provide additional relief for local 
municipalities.

Interest and Miscellaneous
In 2019-20, the City accounted for $5.3 million in interest and miscellaneous revenues; $3.3 million, or 163%, above the 
previous estimate. The most significant contributor to the variance was from the fair market value adjustment of the City’s 
investment portfolio of $1.3 million. The adjustment is done at the end of each fiscal year in compliance with accounting 
standards and is difficult to predict as a part of the budget. The current federal funds target rate is between 0 and 25 basis 
points (0.25%), a historic low. As such, the year-end adjustment indicates that the City’s investment portfolio is worth $1.3 
million more based on the interest rate that the City purchased its various investments at in comparison to where the 
interest rate currently is for the same investment type. The adjustment does not have any impact on the City’s cash 
position, and the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) has indicated that the federal funds target rate will remain low 
for a number of years while inflation continues to run below its 2% target due to weaker demand and lower oil prices. The 
City’s investment portfolio has an average maturity of just under one year. As such, it is highly likely that the fair market 
value adjustment at the end of 2020-21 will revert all of the additional value that it had recognized at year end in 2019-20. 
Of the remaining $2.0 million in favorable variance, $0.8 million is attributable to interest earnings, $0.5 million from gain 
on investments, and $0.5 million from library donations. In recognition of the factors noted above, the $1.3 million fair 
market value adjustment has been removed from the bottom line of the variance analysis to instead focus on favorable 
revenue variance that does not result from compliance with accounting standards, but rather, economic activity.
General Fund Expenditures
Total General Fund expenditures are projected to end fiscal year 2019-20 at $135.4 million; $2.4 million, or 1.8%, less than 
expected. $0.5 million of the difference is from employee costs, reflecting the immediate relief of the hiring freeze. Of the 
$0.8 favorable variance in operating costs, $0.1 million was due to the study for a potential Property Based Improvement 
District (PBID) that was also deferred in the current fiscal year, $0.1 was related to cost savings from professional coaching 
services agreements, $0.2 million was related comprehensive development fee review services (for which the 
encumbrance was rolled over to 2020-21), and $0.2 million as a result of the cessation of the red light camera program.
The remaining favorable variance in transfers out and Measure S expenditures of $0.3 million and $0.9 million, 
respectively, were offset by $1.4 million in CIP carryovers to the subsequent fiscal year, which represents contractual and 
CIP obligations for multi-year projects.
2019-20 General Fund Summary 
General Fund revised estimated actuals are $133.1 million in total revenues and $135.4 million in total expenditures, 
resulting in a net loss of $2.3 million. While this is $9.2 million better than the anticipated net loss of $11.5 million, the 
revised estimate still results in a draw down on unassigned fund balance, and that is largely representative of only one 
quarter being impacted by COVID-19.  The above results will reduce total fund balance from $88.2 million to $85.9 million, 
which is comprised of $31.2 million in the 25% operating reserve, $4.8 million in the housing set aside, and $48.5 million in 
unassigned fund balance.
CalPERS 2019-20 Investment Returns
The California Public Employees Retirement System (CalPERS) earned 4.7% on its investment portfolio in fiscal year 2019-
20. CalPERS reported nearly a $70 billion, or 17%, loss in market value in the weeks that followed the immediate financial 
market reaction to COVID-19. While CalPERS managed to recover a significant portion back by the end of the fiscal year, 
the investment return is well below the expected 7% rate of return. As such, the City’s annual pension contributions will 
increase beginning in fiscal year 2022-23 by $1.5 million. CalPERS uses a ramping method to phase in the impact of market 
gains and losses over a five-year period, thus the increase in 2022-23 will be 20% of the $1.5 million, or $0.3 million. In 
year two of the ramping period, 40% of the new amortization base will be payable, or $0.6 million, and so on. The General 
Fund will be responsible for $1.25 million of the $1.5 million in additional annual pension contributions.

SUBJECT:
Short-Term Rental Regulation Policy Options
RECOMMENDATION:
Review proposed short-term rental regulation policy options and provide guidance on what regulations to include in an 
ordinance. 
BACKGROUND:
Over the past several years, cities have experienced a growth of short-term rentals (STRs) in residential communities. STRs 
are defined as rooms, dwelling units, or residential properties that are rented for short periods of time and are advertised 
on sites like Airbnb and VRBO. Although the City of San Mateo works with a consultant, HdL Companies, to identify short-
term rentals in the City, collect transient occupancy tax (TOT), and ensure STR operators obtain a Business Tax Certificate, 
the City has not adopted any comprehensive regulations to proactively regulate the usage of STRs. 
Although STRs can provide a source of income to property owners and optimize utilization of unoccupied rooms, units, or 
properties, they can also have negative impacts on the character of a neighborhood and overall housing supply. STRs may 
potentially increase neighborhood traffic and parking demand, violate noise regulations, and disrupt the quality of life for 
neighbors adjacent to an STR property. STRs may also result in the removal of housing units from the market, which will 
reduce the overall supply of long-term housing options in the City. Since the emergence of STRs in the City of San Mateo, 
the City has received complaints regarding STRs impacts related to parking, loud parties, and marijuana and tobacco 
smoke. Due to these issues, staff recommends that the City Council consider adopting an ordinance to regulate STRs and 
reduce negative impacts. 
Summary of Survey Responses: 
Prior to proposing regulations for Council consideration, staff conducted community engagement to gather residents’ 
perspectives on what type of regulations Council should consider to address STR issues within the City. Staff developed an 
STR survey on the “Speak Up San Mateo!” platform and invited a range of community members, including residents and 
short-term rental operators, to share their opinion on STRs in the City. The input gathered by the survey will be shared in 
greater detail during this City Council meeting, and responses are summarized below.
Many responders from the Short-Term Rental Regulations Survey identified themselves as a resident of the City (78%), 
whereas about 5% of responders identified as only a STR operator and 17% identified as both a STR operator and resident 
of the City. Although most of the resident responders (78%) believe the City should have regulations guiding the 
operations of STRs, 71% of responders who are STR operators do not believe the City should have regulations guiding STR 
operations. 
Within the survey, responders were asked to rate potential STR regulations – prohibition of special events, occupancy

Neighboring Jurisdiction and Short-Term Rental Regulations: 
Staff reviewed various short-term rental regulations that are in place in other neighboring cities to better understand the 
various methods and regulations that could be considered for the City of San Mateo. These regulations are summarized in 
the matrix in Attachment 1. Of the 5 neighboring jurisdictions staff reviewed: 

All jurisdictions prohibit special events, such as weddings and corporate events, from taking place on the property. 

4 out of the 5 cities have occupancy limits.

4 out of the 5 cities have parking requirements.

All cities require a local contact person to respond to and resolve complaints.

All cities have a limit on how many calendar days an unhosted STR operator can rent out their property.
More details on the research conducted on the 5 neighboring jurisdictions are included in Attachment 1.
Recommended Regulations for City of San Mateo: 
Based on the particular issues that San Mateo has experienced, staff recommends the following policy options for 
consideration: 

Prohibition of Special Events: The City could prohibit special events, such as weddings and commercial events, 
from taking place on a STR property to limit the noise, trash, and traffic impacts that stem from STRs. 

Occupancy Limits: The ordinance could limit the total number of people allowed to stay in a short-term rental at 
one time. Many jurisdictions’ approach to limiting occupants on STR properties vary. For example, the City of 
South San Francisco limits the number of transient occupants based on whether the STR property is hosted or un-
hosted. The City of Millbrae occupancy limits vary based on day hours and night hours. A recommended approach 
for the City of San Mateo could be to require STRs operators to not exceed the lesser of (1) 3 occupants per 
bedroom or (2) 12 occupants in total. Establishing a maximum number of occupants may help prevent special 
events and other nuisances, such as noise, traffic, and trash, from impacting neighbors. 

Parking Requirements: The ordinance can require any onsite parking, such as garages and driveways, be made 
available to short-term renters. This requirement may minimize parking conflicts in neighborhoods. For example, 
City of San Bruno, City of Redwood City, and City of Millbrae all require STR operators to make onsite parking 
spaces available to their renters. If onsite parking is not available for STR renters, the City could allow STR 
operators to identify one off-street parking that could be made available to their renters.

Local Contact Person Requirement: All the cities staff reviewed require a local contact person. The City could also 
require a local contact person, regardless if the STR property is hosted or unhosted, to resolve complaints. The 
local contact person can be required to be available 24 hours per day, 7 days per week during the term of any STR 
stay, respond within 60 minutes to complaints, and must take remedial action to resolve complaints.

Unhosted Rental Limits: As a way to preserve long-term housing supply, the City could limit the number of 
unhosted short-term rental days per calendar year. Unhosted properties are those in which the host in not present 
or onsite. All the jurisdictions staff reviewed limit the number of rental days for unhosted STRs. Staff recommends 
limiting the number of rental days per calendar year to 120 nights for unhosted units. Staff recommends not 
placing a limit on hosted units since the primary resident in onsite during the STR stay.
Staff is currently exploring enforcement options but is interested in receiving guidance from the City Council on the 
proposed regulations staff wishes Council to consider in an STR ordinance.
