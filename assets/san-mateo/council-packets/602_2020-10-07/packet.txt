Proposed Revisions to Heritage Tree Ordinance (Part 2) 
Managing Arborist, Matthew Fried, presented on the proposed revisions to the Heritage Tree Ordinance. 
Commissioners posed questions and offered comment. Commissioners expressed concerned to the 
recommended disclosure of Heritage Trees on adjacent properties within 30 feet of construction activity.  
They cited possible issues with the correct distinction of a Heritage Tree without having direct access to the tree 
as well as the ability to know with certainty whether the tree is 30 feet away from construction activity. Multiple 
commissioners recommended broadening the verbiage regarding this recommendation in the ordinance. 
Matthew commented that this recommendation is being brought forward as a way to protect not only the trees 
but also to inform builders of possible violations they may face in advance of a project as opposed to after the 
damage has already taken place, which is what is most commonly seen at this time.

SUBJECT:
Heritage Tree Removal Permit Appeal Hearing for 601 W. Hillsdale Blvd
RECOMMENDATION:
Hear an appeal of City staff's denial of a permit to remove one heritage magnolia tree at 601 W. Hillsdale Blvd and make a 
determination to uphold or overturn denial of the permit.
BACKGROUND:
On June 23, 2020, the Parks Division received a Tree Work Application from Ms. Elaine Lau, applying to remove a heritage 
magnolia tree from the front of her rental property at 601 W. Hillsdale Boulevard. The reason for removal stated on her 
application was “Tree roots system is growing under driveway. The owner wants to redo the driveway and pathway to 
house.” 
When Managing Arborist Matthew Fried inspected the tree in early July the driveway and pathway had already been 
removed and he did not observe any significant roots that could impact replacement of either the driveway or the 
pathway. He believed the offending roots had already been removed by the paving contractor.  In an email dated July 15, 
Mr. Fried denied Ms. Lau’s application to remove the tree.(Attachment 2) The issues that had been concerning Ms. Lau 
(hardscape disruption) had been mitigated. Further, the replacement driveway and pathway were constructed from 
interlocking pavers. Interlocking pavers are ideal for sites that may potentially experience displacement by tree roots. If a 
tree root begins to displace a paver(s) in the future, that paver can be temporarily lifted and the offending root trimmed 
away without harm to the tree. 
On July 23, Ms. Lau requested that the Director of Parks and Recreation reconsider Mr. Fried’s decision to deny the tree 
removal permit. (Attachment 3) In her request for reconsideration, Ms. Lau raised some additional concerns: (1) past 
interference with her sewer lateral; (2) potential future interference with the closest fire hydrant; and 3) minor sidewalk 
cracking. Staff’s response to Ms. Lau’s concerns is as follows:  
(1) Sewer lateral damage. Older sewer laterals were constructed of natural materials (i.e. terra cotta) and are 
subject to leaks due to natural settling and loose-fitting connections. Once a sewer lateral has a leak, any nearby 
vegetation can exploit this nutrient-rich water supply, enter the pipes, and clog or break it. Fortunately, modern 
sewer laterals are constructed of PVC (and similar materials) and have superior tensile and compressive strength, 
and connections that are sealed tightly with adhesive. Leaks from modern systems are rare. When they do occur 
they are the result of poor workmanship. The City views root intrusions of an updated sewer lateral as an 
‘infrastructure’ problem, not a ‘tree’ problem. Ms. Lau’s sewer lateral was replaced in 2014 and thus should not be 
susceptible to future root intrusion. 
(2) Potential fire hydrant disruption. The fire hydrant, about 20 feet from the tree, is connected to the main water

supply that runs beneath West Hillsdale Drive.  Staff has yet to encounter a conflict between a tree root and a fire 
hydrant supply line. If the City were to remove trees based on proximity to fire hydrants there would be far fewer 
acceptable places to plant trees. If a tree were ever to conflict with a fire hydrant or water main, the City would 
work with Cal Water and/or the Fire Department to mitigate the problem, removing the tree if necessary. 
(3) Minor Sidewalk Cracking:  While there is some minor sidewalk cracking out in front of Ms. Lau’s property, it is 
impossible to determine the original source. 
In a letter dated August 19, the Director upheld Mr. Fried’s decision to deny the permit.(Attachment 4) Subsequently on 
August 29, Ms. Lau requested an appeal hearing before the Park and Recreation Commission.(Attachment 5)
In summary, staff does not believe this tree meets the findings for approval of a heritage tree permit. While staff agrees  
that previous driveway damage was likely caused by the roots,  the roots that caused the displacement have already been 
removed and potential future root intrusion can be reasonably managed at little cost.

This hearing is not a project subject to CEQA, because it can be seen with certainty that it will not cause a physical change 
in the environment. (Public Resources Code Section 21065.)
