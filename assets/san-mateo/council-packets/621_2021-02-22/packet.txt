November 23, 2020
Meeting Minutes
1.
CALL TO ORDER 
 The meeting was called to order at 5:39 p.m. by President Lisa Diaz Nash. 
2.
PLEDGE OF ALLEGIANCE & ROLL CALL
Lisa Diaz Nash led those in attendance with the Pledge of Allegiance.  Executive Assistant Dena Gomez 
conducted roll call.  Board Members Lisa Diaz Nash, Elizabeth De Winter, Don Hill and Dr. Lester Chen were 
present.  Kathy Shields was absent because she resigned from the Board due to no longer being a resident 
of San Mateo.
3.
CONSENT CALENDAR
A request was made by Ms. Diaz Nash to pull the Minutes and the Financial Reports Report for further 
discussion.  A motion was made by Mr. Hill to adopt the remaining items on the consent calendar, 
seconded by Dr. Chen, and the motion passed 4-0.

request in more detail.  A motion was made by Dr. Chen to adopt the Minutes as corrected and the Financial 
Reports on the consent calendar, seconded by Ms. De Winter, and the motion passed 4-0.

4.   PUBLIC COMMENT
       None.
5.   OLD BUSINESS
Patron Computer Time Limits During Limited Reopening:  City Librarian James Moore advised the Board 
that San Mateo County will probably be in the purple tier soon, and after speaking with City Manager Drew 
Corbett, a decision has been made to postpone the limited reopening that was scheduled for December 1, 
2020.  Preparations will continue for when it is possible to reopen; however, computer service will not be 
initially available.  Mr. Moore shared that after consulting with the management team, they are in still in 
favor of starting out with a one-hour limit for computer use.  It was agreed to schedule the San Mateo 
Public Library Computer, Internet, and Wireless Access Policy as an agenda topic at a future meeting so the 
policy can be updated with the one-hour time limit on computer usage.
6.   NEW BUSINESS
A.
Fiscal Year 2021-2022 Fee Schedule:  Senior Management Analyst Nicol White presented the 
recommended revisions of the Library’s Fee Schedule for Fiscal Year 2021-2022 for the Library Board’s 
review.  A motion to support the recommended revisions of the Library’s Fee Schedule for Fiscal Year 
2021-2022 was made by Ms. De Winter, and seconded by Ms. Diaz Nash.  The motion passed 4-0.

B.
Library Code of Conduct Policy:  In preparation for the second phase of the limited reopening of the 
Main Library that will allow a limited number of patrons in the lobby to pick up their materials on hold, 
Mr. Moore presented the following proposed revisions to the addendum: to not allow donations 
initially during lobby service; patrons should inform staff if they have a medical condition that prevents 
them from safely wearing a face covering or who are under the mandated age for wearing masks (12+ 
years old), and staff will arrange to safely deliver their materials to them in front of the Library; and 
patrons must sanitize their hands using hand sanitizer provided at Library entrance.  Those who cannot 
use it can request to have their items brought out to them by staff.  A motion to approve the revisions 
to the addendum as submitted by Mr. Moore was made by Mr. Hill, and seconded by Dr. Chen.  The 
motion passed 4-0.

C.
Chromebook Café Pilot Program:  Supervising Librarian Rita Morin provided an overview of the 
Chromebook Café Pilot Program that was offered at the Main Library from September 1 through 
October 1, and again October 14 through November 5.  Members of the public could drop in to use a 
Chromebook in front of the Main Library for 30 minute intervals, and longer if no one was waiting to 
use one.  Ms. Morin shared that it is not recommended at this time to continue offering this service 
due to the lack of sufficient interest.  There was only a total of 14 Chromebook Café users during this 
pilot program.  Ms. Morin presented possible future alternative programs, including providing internet 
access off site by working with community partners such as the King Center, local parks or 
marketplaces with ample parking lots to accommodate setup.  Since staff are not able to provide 
internet or device instruction due to the current required social distancing mandates, these services 
will be considered at a future date.
             
7.   REPORTS AND ANNOUNCEMENTS
Board members shared the following:

Ms. De Winter attended the Zero Waste Chef virtual presentation presented by the Library.

Mr. Hill reported that the curbside service at the Hillsdale Library was very efficient when he went 
there to pick up his materials on hold.

Dr. Chen asked Mr. Moore what he learned about the possibility of renting out space in the 
parking lot of the Marina Library to the Fish Market.  Mr. Moore had not yet spoken with the City 
Manager about it.  [Update-Subsequently, Mr. Moore informed Dr. Chen that after speaking with 
the City Manager, both agreed that the potential benefits of doing this were not justified by the 
potential liabilities and payoffs.]  In addition, Dr. Chen asked about the Second Harvest Food for 
Fines program this year.  Mr. Moore shared that he had not received information on this yet but 
will reach out to Second Harvest and update the Board on what he learns.  Lastly, Dr. Chen 
requested that we place a formal resolution on the agenda for a future meeting to acknowledge 
the two recent benefactors that donated substantial sums to the Library.

Ms. Diaz Nash briefly attended the Foundation Board meeting where a lot of creative ideas were 
discussed, and she looks forward to what is planned and how the Library Board members can 
assist.  Also, Ms. Diaz Nash requested if Library staff can come back with a recommendation on 
how to recognize and celebrate the two recent benefactors, and added that it will also be an 
opportunity to reach out to community members and remind them that donations to the Library 
are a wonderful way to give back to the community.

Lastly, Ms. Diaz Nash asked about the recruitment process to replace Library Board Member Kathy 
Shields, and if it is possible to formally recognize Ms. Shields’ service on the Library Board.  Mr. 
Moore advised that it would be up to the Library Board members to agree on a way to formally 
recognize Ms. Shields’ service.  Mr. Moore also advised that he will keep the Board informed on 
the recruitment process.
Lynne Esselstein, Executive Director of the Foundation shared the following:

It has been a busy time with the Fall Appeal fundraising campaign.  So far, the revenue generated 
is close to what was raised last year.  Bookmarks for the Fall Appeal are included with materials at 
Curbside Pickup Service, and Library Board members are encouraged to forward emails from 
Constant Contact about the Fall Appeal to their friends and contacts.

Gives Day is December 3, 2020 and will be completely virtual since the Library will not be open.

A Save the Date notice will be sent out for the December Foundation Board meeting.  At this 
meeting, in addition to a holiday toast, ideas will be generated for the Spring Fundraiser that will 
be held in lieu of the Gala.

Ms. Esselstein believes there is a real need for the Cyber Café service, and that it would be great if 
Peninsula Library System can publish printing schedules for community members that list all the 
libraries in the PLS consortium.

Thank you to all the Library staff for the work that they do and Happy Thanksgiving!
Mr. Moore highlighted the following Library programs that were presented and a success story:

Teen Librarian Carla Avitabile presented a practice ACT test and three strategic support classes at 
Aragon High School including how to sign up for an E card, how to use Overdrive and Hoopla, and 
how to request items during the closure.

The 4th Annual Halloween Tales of Horror program was presented where established horror 
authors sent videos of themselves reading from their scary works.  This was presented on the 
Library’s YouTube channel.  There were approximately 170 views.

Medicare Open Enrollment Program was presented in collaboration with HICAP.

Zero Waste Chef Program was presented in coordination with the City of San Mateo’s 
Sustainability Office.  There were approximately 109 attendees. 

The BioTech Fall Series kicked off with a program on stress and inflammation with Dr. Aditi 
Bhargava.  There were approximately 70 attendees.

Supervising Children’s Librarian Adriana Valencia reported that the Drive-thru Halloween program 
that was presented in partnership with Parks and Recreation was a huge success.

The Children’s Division presented a bilingual early literacy program for Head Start families.

Children’s Librarian Chelsea Bell Stahl presented a Pride Storytime for Gay Pride Month.

Information Services Librarian Eric Groth helped a patron gather some census figures regarding the 
number of seniors in the individual neighborhoods of San Mateo.

Ms. Diaz Nash adjourned the meeting at 7:23 p.m.

Q1: The branch renovations project will begin in the first few months of 2021. 
Access to databases was enhanced by creating an A-Z listing of all library 
subscriptions. One subscription, Learning Express, was cancelled due to low 
usage. In July, curbside service became available for all new holds after the 
backlog from the library closure had been cleared. Curbside service was 
expanded to the branches in August. 96 appointment slots are available daily at 
the Main Library, and 144 slots are available weekly at both the Hillsdale and 
Marina branches. Centralized phone service is available Monday through 
Saturday from 10 am-4 pm. Since in-person conferences are unlikely to occur, 
each merit staff was allotted $250 for the fiscal year to spend on training of their 
choice.

Day in the District Overview
With the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic, “Day in the District” will be conducted via Zoom or other video 
conferencing. While the method of engaging with legislators is different, the basics remain the same.
1.
Organize a group of library supporters – it is more effective if you include library trustees, 
commissioners, Friends groups, Foundation members, and library users to meet with your 
legislator in late February and early March. 

If your legislator serves more than one public library system, reach out to those 
libraries and attempt to schedule one meeting for all libraries in the legislator’s 
district.

Designate one person who will contact the legislator’s office and setup the meeting. 

When you have finalized a date, determine who will provide the link for Zoom or 
another video conferencing platform. If you are to provide the link, do so within a 
day or two of finalizing the date and time of the meeting. 
2.
Prior to meeting with your legislator, schedule a separate one with those participating to 
ensure you stay on message. 

Highlight some of the transformational programs and service provided to your 
community.

Use the 2021 CLA Legislative Priorities as a template for your conversation, but also 
include any issues of local concern.
3.
During the meeting, keep the tone friendly and positive. 

If the legislator asks for specific information, please follow-up as soon as possible. 
4.
Send a thank you note with a business card a week to 10 days after the meeting.
Why is “Day in the District” Important?
“Day in the District” is an effective way to build relationships with legislators. Developing relationships 
with new legislators and bolstering your relationship with continuing legislators is incredibly important 
for the success of CLA’s advocacy platform.
You – as well as other library staff from all levels of the organization, Friends groups, foundation 
members, trustees, commissioners, literacy advocates and other supporters – are encouraged to 
schedule appointments with your legislators during late February and early March. 
These meetings are friendly meet and greets where you and your colleagues highlight some of the 
wonderful programs and services being offered by your library and to address specific legislative issues 
important to libraries across the state. 
Legislators have the potential to remain in office for 12 years. Since many of them will sit on policy or 
budget-related committees that have jurisdiction over library issues and funding, it is critical to build 
relationships for years to come. The new legislative session began on January 11, 2021 and runs through 
September 10, 2021. While many legislators come from local government, they may be unaware of the

2021 BUDGET PRIORITIES 
 
In October, CLA sent correspondence to Governor Newsom, 
requesting his inclusion of the following items in his 2021-22 State 
Budget.  As such, CLA is proudly supporting Governor Newsom’s 
January Budget which contains the following proposed augmentations. 
 
LUNCH AT THE LIBRARY: The Governor is proposing $800,000 in “ongoing” funding for 
this program which provides 300,000 meals annually to more than 32,000 children during 
the summer months at the public library.  CLA is grateful to Governor Newsom for 
proposing that the money be “ongoing,” as it will allow public libraries to successfully plan 
year-to-year.   
 
ACTION: Last year, the State Budget included $1 million for Lunch at the Library until the 
state revenues plummeted because of the pandemic.  The proposed funding was removed 
in the May Revision and final version of the Budget.  Because of this action, there will be 
zero state funding for “Lunch at the Library” in the Summer of 2021 unless the legislature 
acts to include the $800,000 proposed (see above) in an “immediate action” Budget trailer 
bill.  Without this action, the proposed $800,000 would be applied to the Summer of 2022 
only, and the program would essentially skip a year, leaving more than 32,000 children 
without meals in 2021.  Please ask your legislator to press for inclusion of the 
$800,000 in an “immediate action” Budget trailer bill.   
 
ZIP BOOKS: The Governor is proposing $1 million in “ongoing” funding to “support the 
online purchase and delivery of library books through the Zip Books program.”  Zip Books 
is an innovative way of providing books and materials to patrons who, for various reasons, 
are unable to physically get to a library to obtain their desired item.  During the pandemic, 
this service has been incredibly popular with home-bound seniors, the disabled and 
immunocompromised community, and mothers with small children.  Through their 
participating public library, a patron orders a book that is not currently part of the existing 
collection and has the book delivered to his or her residence through a partnership with 
Amazon.  When the patron is finished with the material, the book is returned to the local 
library where it becomes part of the collection or a neighboring library’s collection.   
 
ACTION: This funding is also proposed to be “ongoing” in nature, which will provide 
greater planning opportunities for public libraries.  If your legislator is a member of the 
Assembly or Senate Budget Committees or legislative Leadership, please request their 
support of this item.

2021 LEGISLATION PRIORITIES 
 
ACA 1-Aguiar-Curry: Local government financing  
CLA Position: Support 
 
CLA is in strong support of ACA 1 by Assemblymember Cecilia Aguiar-Curry and her joint authors, 
Assemblymembers Lorena Gonzalez and David Chiu, which seeks to lower the local vote threshold 
for construction bonds and special taxes from the current two-thirds vote threshold to 55%. The 
measure would apply to various local government projects such as roads, water, housing, public 
libraries and broadband.  In 2019, Assemblymember Aguiar-Curry had a nearly identical measure 
(also called “ACA 1”) which was supported by a large coalition of cities, counties, special districts, 
labor unions, fire chiefs, housing groups, water and park districts, CLA, etc.  Unfortunately, the bill 
died in 2020 because the author and supporters were unable to secure enough votes for the high-bar 
needed to get the bill out of the first house – a challenging two-thirds vote of the Assembly.   
 
Action: Assemblymember Aguiar-Curry has argued that school districts are required by statute to 
secure only a 55% vote for the passage of local school bonds, and ACA 1 would bring some parity to 
the system by allowing local government entities to utilize the 55% vote for local construction bonds 
(and special taxes).  The estimated backlog of library construction projects in the state is in excess of 
$4 billion.  This bill would begin to alleviate some of that backlog for library construction.  Please ask 
your Assemblymember to support the new 2021 version of ACA 1 when it is heard in policy 
committee and on the Assembly Floor.   
 
Note: The following legislators are co-authors of the measure.  If you are represented by one of these 
legislators, please thank them for their support of this important bill:  Joint authors:  Assy. Lorena Gonzalez and 
David Chiu.  Principal coauthor:  Senator Scott Wiener.  Co-authors:  Assy. Mark Berman, Autumn Burke, Ash 
Kalra, Mark Levine, Dr. Bill Quirk, Robert Rivas, Blanca Rubio, Mark Stone, Phil Ting, and Buffy Wicks. 
 
Closing the Digital Divide 
 
The California Broadband Council, represented by the leaders of the various state agencies including 
the State Library, recently released its “Broadband For All Action Plan.”  The plan identifies a 
statewide need of more than $6.8 billion to provide broadband services to unserved and underserved 
communities.  At CLA’s request, and with assistance from the State Library, the plan was amended to 
include several beneficial additions highlighting the role of libraries as “anchor institutions,” digital 
device and hotspot lenders, and digital literacy training centers.  The Plan cites areas of eligibility for 
future funding for libraries.  Several legislators will be moving forward a bi-partisan package of bills 
this year to capitalize on the work of the Broadband Council, such as AB 14-Aguiar-Curry, AB 34-
Muratsuchi, and SB 4-Lena Gonzalez.  These measures seek to provide more opportunities for 
cities, counties, special districts, schools, libraries, and community-based organizations to receive 
funding, or create new municipal bond Internet projects, separate from the traditional internet service 
provider model.  CLA supports efforts to close the digital divide and will be actively working with the 
three authors on their legislative efforts moving forward.
