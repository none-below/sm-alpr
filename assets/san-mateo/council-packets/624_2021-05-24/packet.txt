5.   OLD BUSINESS
A.  Follow Up Report on Age-Friendly Cities Meeting:  City Librarian James Moore shared an update 
from a meeting that he had on April 5 with Monika Lee and Vince Siminitus of the Age-Friendly 
Community Task Force.  This task force of the San Mateo Senior Commission is interested in learning 
more about the programs and services the Library (SMPL) is already providing such as e-books, 
streaming, volunteer opportunities, materials delivery, and training seniors on how to use SMPL 
resources; and how to better share this information in the Daily Journal and in the newsletter the 
task force recently launched.  Mr. Moore received suggestions from Ms. Lee and Mr. Siminitus on 
future Library programs and services including more programs on history, and in particular local 
history, possible live video of programs for homebound patrons, and programs or activities that 
facilitate the sharing of experience and wisdom of community members.  
Mr. Moore said that working with the Age-Friendly Community Task Force and the commitment to 
SMPL being a welcoming destination for seniors and community members of all ages will be a 
priority and a goal on the Library’s Work Plan for Fiscal Year 2021-2022.  One of the goals that will be 
included in this work plan will be to work with Public Works to create safe walking routes to the 
Library.  Board members offered feedback and suggestions including using the Library’s community 
outreach van for expanding services.  Mr. Moore shared that the lack of adequate staffing to 
support additional outreach services has been an issue, but that he recently received approval to fill 
the Supervising Librarian position to oversee the Marina and Hillsdale branches.  In addition to 
supervising staff at the branches, this position will also oversee the Library’s community outreach 
services.

B.  Library Reopening Update:  Mr. Moore shared an update on the Library’s reopening plan.  On 
May 3rd, all three locations will be open to the public for browsing and limited computer use.  
Patrons can continue to pick up holds without an appointment.  There will not be a limit on the 
number of patrons allowed into the building, but the hours will be limited to 4 hours a day on the 
days that the location is open.  The Main Library will be open six days per week, and Marina and 
Hillsdale branches will each be open two days per week.  During this phase of reopening, the 
following spaces will not be open: small study rooms on all three floors, Jobseekers area on 2nd floor, 
Business area on 2nd floor, Computer Lab, Community Meeting Rooms, except for limited library 
programs, and the Café Terrace.  Since café service has not resumed yet, no food or drinks in open 
containers will be permitted inside the library.  This is in accordance with the Library’s current Code 
of Conduct policy which states that consuming food and beverages purchased outside the Library is 
prohibited.  Mr. Moore said the Library will continue to follow guidance from public health officials 
for the next phase of reopening SMPL, and details will be posted on the Library’s website, in the 
Library’s e-newsletter and posted at the entrance of each location.  Governor Gavin Newsom has 
announced that California will fully reopen the economy on June 15th if two criteria are met: if 
vaccine supply is sufficient for Californians 16 and older who wish to be inoculated, and if 
hospitalization rates are stable and low.  
6.   NEW BUSINESS
       A.  Appropriate Context for Short Term Changes to Library Services and Operations:  Mr. Moore 
clarified how and when City and Library leadership can make temporary adaptive changes to 
services in response to changes in the operational environment in the event of an emergency, such 
as the COVID-19 pandemic.  In a state of emergency, policies do not need to be changed and 
approval does not need to be obtained from the Library Board of Trustees to make temporary 
operational changes.  Per the City’s Municipal Code, in such instances the City Manager is the 
director of emergency services.  In the event of a proclamation of an emergency, the City Manager 
(with City Council oversight) is empowered to make and issue rules and regulations on matters 
reasonably related to the protection of life and property as affected by such emergency.  
       B.  Overview of How Library Staff Determines the Need for Assistance from SMPD in Handling Patron 
Issues:  Mr. Moore provided a summary of the steps that staff take before requesting assistance 
from law enforcement when addressing emergency situations involving patron behavior.  If a patron 
is breaking a rule of conduct, staff will share the specific rules being broken with the patron and ask 
them to stop the prohibited conduct.  If the patron does not comply, staff will again ask the patron 
to stop the prohibited conduct and tell the patron they will have to leave if they do not comply.  If 
the patron is still non-compliant, a manager will then speak to the patron.  If the patron continues 
being disruptive, the manager will call the non-emergency phone number for the police department 
(SMPD), or 9-1-1 if the behavior is highly disruptive or threatening to staff or the public.  Library staff 
are careful to provide context so as not to unnecessarily escalate the situation.  Most of the cases 
where SMPD has been called for assistance involved people dealing with mental illness, substance 
abuse or homelessness issues.  SMPD has two officers stationed downtown who focus on addressing 
issues related to homelessness.  Officers have shown professionalism, restraint, patience and 
compassion when responding to calls for assistance, and have often already been acquainted with 
the person.  Mr. Moore advised that there are approximately four incidents per year where staff 
must request assistance from SMPD.

Policy for Release of Information 
Concerning Library Patrons 
                                       
 
In accordance with the California Public Records Act (Government Code Section 6254), 
library registration and circulation records are confidential.  It is the policy of the San 
Mateo Public Library to protect the privacy rights of all individuals by only disclosing 
such records to persons acting within the scope of their duties in the administration of 
the library, pursuant to written authorization by the individual to whom the records 
pertain or by order of the Superior Court. 
 
The San Mateo Public Library considers registration records to include verification of the 
fact that an individual is a registered borrower and any information required to become 
an eligible borrower such as name, contact information, etc.  Circulation records include 
any information which identifies the books and/or other materials borrowed by 
particular patrons.  
 
The confidentiality of library circulation records does not extend to statistical reports of 
registration and circulation or to records of fines collected by the library.  Information 
regarding delinquent accounts of library patrons may also be provided to collection 
agencies retained by the library to collect on delinquent accounts. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Revised, 3/27/10

Policy for Maintaining Library Policies 
 
 
The San Mateo Public Library is a community resource that requires clear, consistently 
applied policies as may be necessary or appropriate to achieve its mission of providing 
library services, programs and materials to the public; and to ensure the equitable 
treatment for all members of the community in the delivery of its services.  
 
One of the key roles of the Library Board of Trustees per the City’s Charter (Article VI, 
Section 6.01) is to develop and maintain the Library’s policies.  To fulfill this 
responsibility, the Library Board will: 
 
• Develop and review proposed new policies or policy changes as recommended 
by the City Librarian, Library Board of Trustees or the public to address 
important policy issues 
 
• Review all policies at least every five years to ensure they are still relevant; 
update when necessary and eliminate obsolete policies 
 
• Work with the City Librarian and library staff to maintain a current listing of 
library policies available to the public 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Adopted by Library Board, 3-27-10

Supporting Patron Record Privacy Policy Review Information:
According the League of California Cities, this is a summary of most current the Public Records 
Act as relates to the San Mateo Public Library’s (SMPL) “Policy for Release of Information 
Concerning Library Patrons”: 
 
 Library Patron Use Records 
 
All patron use records of any library that is supported in whole or in part by public funds are 
confidential and may not be disclosed except to persons acting within the scope of their duties 
within library administration, upon written authorization from the person whose records are 
sought, or by court order. The term “patron use records” includes written or electronic records 
that identify the patron, the patron’s borrowing information, or use of library resources, 
including database search records and any other personally identifiable information requests or 
inquiries. This exemption does not extend to statistical reports of patron use or records of fines 
collected by the library. Cal. Gov. Code section 6267.
 
Library Circulation Records 
 
Library circulation records that are kept to identify the borrowers, and library and museum 
materials presented solely for reference or exhibition purposes, are exempt from disclosure. 
Further, all registration and circulation records of any library that is (in whole or in part) 
supported by public funds are confidential. The confidentiality of library circulation records 
does not extend to records of fines imposed on borrowers. Cal. Gov. Code section 6254(j)
A noteworthy difference in language between this summary and that of SMPL’s current policy can be 
found at the end of the last sentence in first paragraph of the current policy. It reads: 
“It is the policy of the San Mateo Public Library to protect the privacy rights of all individuals by 
only disclosing such records to persons acting within the scope of their duties in the 
administration of the library, pursuant to written authorization by the individual to whom the 
records pertain or by order of the Superior Court.”
The most up-to-date version of the Public Records Act presented above, says at the end of the 
first sentence:
 “All patron use records of any library that is supported in whole or in part by public funds are 
confidential and may not be disclosed except to persons acting within the scope of their duties 
within library administration, upon written authorization from the person whose records are 
sought, or by court order.”
This appears to be the only meaningful difference in language between the assisting policy and 
current law.
