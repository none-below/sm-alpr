SUBJECT: Evaluation of Downtown Uses
RECOMMENDATION:
Provide direction to staff to explore potential near-term adjustments to the locational requirements and range of uses 
allowed downtown, resultant from stakeholder outreach, the pandemic, and an increasing number of ground floor 
vacancies.
BACKGROUND:
In 2009, the City of San Mateo adopted the Downtown Area Plan (Plan) after an extensive outreach and public input effort. 
The Plan created design standards, allowed a wide range of uses, and established a retail core within the downtown.  The 
purpose of this retail core was to make certain it allowed and promoted pedestrian-oriented uses to create and maintain 
the vibrancy of downtown San Mateo. This Plan's vision states: “Downtown San Mateo provides for a pedestrian-friendly 
environment lending to its charm as a traditional center of the community in the next years. The blend of historic buildings 
and new development will provide for a mix of retail, entertainment, and housing opportunities...” At its October 19, 2020 
meeting, the City Council indicated that it would like to hold a study session to explore potential near-term adjustments to 
the locational requirements and range of uses allowed downtown in light of feedback from property owners, the 
pandemic, and increasing number of ground floor vacancies. Staff was directed to reach out to stakeholders to gather 
input on the currently allowed uses and locational requirements downtown and return to Council to discuss whether 
further potential short-term adjustments are needed to facilitate the filling of vacant tenant spaces in the near term and 
maintain San Mateo’s downtown tax base. That the Plan will be comprehensively reviewed and updated in response to 
General Plan 2040 completion was acknowledged, and a desire to look at long-term changes was not indicated at the 
meeting. 
As this conversation unfolds, it is important to keep in mind not only current conditions downtown, but also the goals of 
the Plan. Goal Two of the Downtown Area Plan is to: “Enhance the Vitality and Activity of Downtown by Incorporating an 
Overall Good Mix and Diversity of Uses.” The Plan encourages a wide range of uses, including retail, entertainment, and 
service uses, to be maintained and improved. One way to do this is to monitor the success of downtown’s range of uses 
and make adjustments as needed to help ensure the range of uses and services corresponds with current demands. 
Successful implementation of Goal Two is important in that it supports several other Plan goals by facilitating the 
successful occupation of ground floor spaces by a variety of uses that are in current demand. This in turn contributes to 
the realization of other goals related to economic vitality, place-making, facilitating pedestrian activity, and ensuring 
downtown users have access to a wide range of goods and services.     
This report includes a map (Attachment 1) that shows the general downtown area, which consists of approximately 70 
blocks as well as the downtown retail core. Secondly, this report includes a table (Attachment 2) that summarizes the 
permitted and conditionally permitted uses in the downtown, as well as special requirements in the downtown core, 
known as the “Required Retail Frontage” requirements.

OUTREACH
In preparation for this study session staff sought input from several stakeholders, which included the Downtown San 
Mateo Association (DSMA), property owners, commercial brokers, merchants, and developers on the current conditions 
related to uses in the downtown. The initial input we received is summarized as follows:

Reduce square footage, frontage, and dimensional requirements for retail;

Allow for wider range of uses;

Have flexibility in use and design;

Avoid an over-concentration of uses that can become problematic (e.g. nightclubs, bars, adult, etc.);

Look to other cities for examples: Los Altos, Palo Alto and Redwood City;

Potential uses: food-halls, tutoring centers, limited professional offices, small fitness studios (e.g. yoga studio);

Allow oversight and approval at staff/Planning Commission level; and

Do not respond only to COVID without considering lasting impacts.
DISCUSSION: 
The retail industry has been evolving over the last 5-10 years. More retailers have shifted to having a greater online 
presence rather than focusing on traditional brick and mortar retail stores. Consequently, traditional retail, whether in 
shopping malls or on “Main Street” downtowns has suffered with an increase in vacancies. For many years, transitional 
uses were replaced by restaurants and drinking establishments. The pandemic has sped up vacancies of not just retail 
spaces, but also restaurant and drinking establishments alike. In the meantime, cities have seen in increase in demand for 
services such as small fitness studios, tutoring centers, professional offices like dental offices, retail/shipping outlets like 
Amazon, food halls, and other uses that have become more popular in recent years. A desire for these types of uses in San 
Mateo has been expressed by downtown stakeholders. Also expressed by stakeholders was the desire to have greater 
flexibility regarding storefront design and locational requirements, and have fewer layers of review for permit issuance. 
The Plan currently has dimensional and locational standards that limit what types of uses can be located where and how 
tenant spaces are configured, and this has been a constraint on filling/developing vacant ground floor spaces (see 
Attachment 2 for more details). This is consistent with customer inquiries received by Planning staff. Stakeholders echoed 
Council’s sentiment that the downtown atmosphere should be maintained and that sales tax-generating uses shall 
continue to be promoted. We also heard that the City should be cautious in reacting too quickly to the pandemic in this 
regard, that the longer term should be kept in mind, and that overconcentration of certain land use categories should be 
limited. 
As a comparison, staff, as well as stakeholders, felt it would be prudent to analyze how other nearby cities are responding 
to the shifting retail trends for ground floor uses, as well as the need to address abrupt vacancies in their respective 
downtowns.  Other cities have also started to look at their downtowns a bit differently with respect to ground floor uses. 
The cities that staff looked at were Burlingame, Palo Alto, Redwood City and South San Francisco. A comparative table 
showing permitted uses in their respective downtown cores is included in Attachment 3. In late 2020, responding to the 
COVID-19 pandemic, Palo Alto considered a proposal that would modify the city's definitions for retail and retail-like uses 
to allow medical offices downtown, educational uses, accounting firms, banks, law firms and real estate offices.  While 
Palo Alto did not immediately move forward with any changes, the City will be studying the issue in greater detail in the 
coming months. In 2017, Redwood City also updated its Downtown Precise Plan to broaden the definition of retail, while 
placing more restrictive measures on ground-floor downtown office uses. South San Francisco revised its Downtown 
Station Area Plan a few years ago to also allow office uses on the second floor and above. South San Francisco has also 
started to allow personal service uses on the ground floor. These include yoga and Pilates studios, and children's 
activity/tutoring centers in their downtown. South San Francisco will be reevaluating downtown uses and design standards 
as part of a zoning code overhaul after they complete their General Plan update. Note, the types of new uses allowed in 
nearby cities is reflective of stakeholder input and customer inquiries in San Mateo. It is too early to tell whether these 
changes have had benefits because of the business closures and sheltering in place prompted by the pandemic.
NEXT STEPS: 
In order to realize the vision of the Plan and achieve its goals, the City needs to continue to be responsive to changing

SUBJECT:
Cyber-Security Report 
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an informational report on the City’s cyber-security protection measures as recommended by the San Mateo 
County Grand Jury report on ransomware.
BACKGROUND:
On October 7, 2020, the San Mateo County Grand Jury released a report entitled “Ransomware: It Is Not Enough To Think 
You Are Protected” which advised local government agencies to ensure adequate measures are being taken to mitigate 
cybersecurity risks and provide recovery options. The report included findings emphasizing the threat and impacts of 
ransomware and the importance of strong cyber-defenses. The report recommended the City direct the Information 
Technology Department (IT) to assess cyber-security strategies, address any shortcomings, and make a report to the 
Council on this assessment. On November 16, 2020, Council approved a response letter to the Grand Jury, signed by Mayor 
Joe Goethals. In this response letter, the City agreed with the Grand Jury’s findings and committed to addressing their 
recommendations. 
This informational Study Session is being held to comply with the Grand Jury’s recommendations. Another recommended 
action that the City has taken was to begin an engagement with cyber-defense resources from the Department of 
Homeland Security Cybersecurity & Infrastructure Security Agency (DHS-CISA). Since November 2020, IT has received DHS-
CISA cyber hygiene assessments, which are rudimentary. IT will look for deeper engagement as additional services are 
made available from DHS-CISA. Finally, over the coming year, IT is taking additional cybersecurity measures and enhancing 
our cybersecurity planning efforts. 
Specific concerns detailed in the Grand Jury report included the following:
1.
System Security. This includes firewalls, anti-malware/antivirus software, use of network segmentation or subnets, 
strong password policies, and updating/patching regularly.
2.
Backup & Recovery. In the event of an attack, can you shut down your system quickly? What is being backed up, 
how it is being backed up, when are backups run, and where are the backups being stored? Have backups been 
tested? Can you fully restore a server from a backup?
3.
Prevention. Turning on email filtering, setting up message rules to warn users, providing employee training on 
phishing and providing a reporting system to flag suspect content.
These items are indeed important aspects of a strong cybersecurity program, and the City has all of them in place. 
However, there are many additional measures to be detailed. More broadly, cybersecurity should be a comprehensive 
program of effective policies and governance, strong technical defenses, and appropriate action by people.

First, let us define some important terms for reference:

Cybersecurity - technologies, processes, and practices designed to protect networks, devices, programs, and data 
from attack, damage, or unauthorized access.

Cyberattacks - efforts aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information, extorting money, or 
interrupting business operations. 

Phishing – email claiming to be from a legitimate source meant to induce individuals to provide sensitive 
information or as a vector for malware.

Malware – “malicious software” designed to damage or disrupt computer systems, capture data, and/or extort 
payment (e.g. ransomware).

Ransomware – a type of malware on a host machine that prevents access to data until a ransom is paid.
There are many malware types in addition to ransomware, and many types of cyberattacks in addition to phishing. This 
report will not detail all of these but instead focus on our approach to cybersecurity. This approach is based on guidance 
provided by the Center for Internet Security (CIS). CIS is a nonprofit organization of cybersecurity professionals whose 
mission is to "identify, develop, validate, promote, and sustain best practice solutions for cyber defense and build and lead 
communities to enable an environment of trust in cyberspace". They provide a set of ‘CIS ControlsTM’ which are “a 
prioritized set of actions that collectively form a defense-in-depth set of best practices that mitigate the most common 
attacks against systems and networks”.  CIS Controls are based on the US Government National Institute of Standards and 
Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework. CIS Controls provide a friendlier and more easily actionable guide based on 
the NIST standard.  The NIST Cybersecurity Framework was initiated by Presidential Executive Order (EO 13636) in 
February 2013, which directed NIST to work with stakeholders to develop the voluntary framework in collaboration 
between industry and government. It is utilized by government agencies at all levels, as well as private organizations.
There are 20 CIS Controls organized into three groups: Basic, Foundational, and Organizational. Each Control has numerous 
Sub-Controls which are comprehensive and detailed. There are also three ‘Implementation Groups’ based on the size and 
complexity of organizations which are used to determine which Sub-Controls should apply. The City of San Mateo fits into 
Implementation Group 2 (IG2). The remainder of this report will briefly list all CIS Controls and highlight examples of Sub-
Controls within that list which IT has in place as appropriate to IG2.  In some cases, limiting factors to our application of 
Controls will also be highlighted.  This is not an exhaustive description of our application of all Controls and Sub-Controls, 
and certain specifics and details are omitted from this public document for security purposes.
The following CIS Controls are referred to as Basic.
CIS Control 1:  Inventory and Control of Hardware Assets. IT maintains a detailed hardware inventory and utilizes a 
centralized remote management tool for City Personal Computers (PCs).  Efforts are made to ensure unauthorized 
or unmanaged devices are not given access to the network. Per policy, departments are expected to purchase 
technology through IT, or at a minimum consult with IT prior to any technology hardware purchases. 
CIS Control 2:  Inventory and Control of Software Assets. IT maintains an applications list and documentation 
library for all known software and can run reports of software installed on City computers. Per policy, departments 
are to purchase software through IT, or at a minimum consult with IT prior to any software purchases. IT seeks to 
avoid software that is unsupported by the vendor, or that does not have regular security updates. However, this is 
an area where the use of certain necessary software for City functions makes us unable to fully address security 
concerns.
CIS Control 3:  Continuous Vulnerability Management. IT utilizes anti-malware/antivirus scanning tools and 
performs anti-malware/antivirus scanning of City computers. IT deploys operating system and other software 
patches to all PCs in a timely manner through a centralized remote management tool.
CIS Control 4: Controlled use of Administration Privileges. IT controls IT administrative accounts, changes default 
passwords on systems, and follows other best practices including the use of strong passwords and multi-factor 
authentication. IT limits access to elevated administrative privileges and scripting tools. Of note, this is a primary 
method for attackers to spread inside a target.
CIS Control 5:  Secure Configuration for Hardware and Software on Mobile Devices, Laptops, Workstations, and

CIS Control 6: Maintenance, Monitoring and Analysis of Audit Logs. This Control includes the activation of system

purchased and is implementing Managed Detection and Response (MDR) services, further described later in this 
report.
The following CIS Controls are referred to as Foundational.
CIS Control 7: Email and Web Browser Protections. IT uses email filtering and encourages end users to only utilize 
supported browsers and email clients such as Microsoft Outlook for email and Microsoft Edge for Internet 
browsing. IT also applies default settings to supported clients and disables unnecessary or unauthorized browser 
plug-ins and client add-ons. However, due to certain expressed business needs, unsupported browsers are used by 
some staff.
CIS Control 8: Malware Defenses. IT utilizes anti-malware/antivirus software and ensures anti-malware/antivirus 
software and signatures are updated on City PCs. IT performs regular anti-malware/antivirus scanning and 
configures anti-malware scanning of removable media.
CIS Control 9: Limitation and Control of Network Ports, Protocols, and Services. IT strives to ensure only approved 
ports, protocols, and services are running. However, the use of certain necessary software for City functions makes 
us unable to fully leverage our IT capabilities in this area. 
CIS Control 10: Data Recovery Capabilities. IT uses modern backup systems and performs daily automated 
backups. Our backup strategy follows the 3-2-1 rule of 3 backups in at least 2 locations, 1 of which is in the cloud. 
Recovery procedures are tested. 
CIS Control 11: Secure Configuration for Network Devices, such as Firewalls, Routers, and Switches. IT maintains 
standard security configurations and installs the latest stable version of security related updates on all network 
devices. 
CIS Control 12: Boundary Defense. IT deploys modern, application-aware firewalls.  However, this is an area where 
the use of certain necessary software for City functions makes us unable to fully leverage our IT capabilities.  When 
software application replacement opportunities arise, IT encourages departments to consider cybersecurity and 
other IT standards, including those related to boundary defenses, as requirements for replacement systems.
CIS Control 13: Data Protection. IT maintains an inventory of software applications containing sensitive 
information and utilizes mobile device management (MDM) for City issued iPads used for City business. IT seeks to 
allow access only to authorized cloud storage and email providers by City staff for City business. IT also provides 
cloud hosting for everyday work files, and cloud-based applications for several business systems. IT discourages 
the use of non-supported and unauthorized cloud storage, cloud applications, and email providers. 
CIS Control 14: Controlled access based on need to know. IT segments the City network using network 
segmentation/subnets. This is not entirely based on ‘need to know’ assessments and more work could be done to 
determine which persons, computers, and applications have a need and a right to access critical assets based on 
classification. IT has encouraged the use of authorized cloud storage for work files, but a file server remains on-
premises with file permissions that are complex and difficult to control.
CIS Control 15: Wireless access control. IT maintains an inventory of wireless access control points (WAPs) and 
utilizes tools to manage them, disallowing peer to peer wireless client communications. IT also segregates wireless 
networks on common hardware and utilizes separate wireless networks for untrusted devices. 
CIS Control 16: Account Monitoring and Control. IT maintains an inventory of user accounts, has a process for 
revoking access, and requires multi-factor authentication (MFA) for Office 365 access. Contractors/consultants 
accounts have expiration dates. 
The following CIS Controls are referred to as Organizational. These Controls are less focused on technical controls and 
more on people and processes.

CIS Control 17: Implement a Security Awareness and Training Program. IT has a security awareness training 
program, but it is voluntary. CIS recommends this program be compulsory. Security awareness content is updated 
periodically but could be expanded to more subjects and should be based on an employee skills gap analysis. 
Security awareness training is limited in large part due to organizational ability to devote more time.
CIS Control 18: Application software security.  IT has implemented the best practice of separating production and 
non-production systems for systems managed by City IT. This Control also includes verifying newly acquired 
software is supported and replacing software that is outdated and unsupported. Where possible, IT works with the 
organization to avoid acquiring or keeping in place insecure, outdated applications without proper vendor support.  
However, these efforts are limited due to the selection of certain software for necessary City functions.
CIS Control 19: Incident response and management. This Control includes developing and implementing an 
incident response plan with defined roles, training, communications, and management oversight. Our incident 
response plan is formalized and undergoing additional revision.
CIS Control 20: Penetration tests and red team exercises.  IT has not had penetration tests or simulations of 
attacks on our defenses in the past but expect these to be part of our ongoing security efforts in the future. 
In total, these 20 CIS Controls form a strong framework for protecting the City’s digital assets. These Controls are helpful 
when considering where to apply limited technology resources, what policies to implement, and where organizational 
change can reasonably occur to make work practices more cyber-secure. Therefore, some attention to is paid to each 
Control and Sub-Control detailed by CIS. 
Recently, IT has addressed a significant technological gap by beginning the implementation of Managed Detection and 
Response (MDR). MDR is an outsourced service that provides threat hunting and immediate response to any threats 
discovered. It is a good option for organizations such as ours that cannot realistically maintain the resources and expertise 
to collect, manage, and analyze data generated by applications and network hardware that can expose these threats. MDR 
services also provide a human element in the form of access to expert security researchers and engineers who monitor 
networks, analyze incidents, and respond to security cases.
While there are many measures the IT Department can and does take, there are no magic bullets for cybersecurity IT can 
deploy to protect the City. Instead, cybersecurity is an ongoing program that includes effective policies and governance, 
strong technical defenses, and appropriate action by people. It must be a collaborative, collective effort that includes 
ongoing attention, adherence to policies, and cyber-aware end users. Users armed with current and accurate information, 
who follow best practices, and adhere to appropriate policies are our first and best defense to cyber threats.
