SUBJECT:
Campaign Contribution Limits Review
RECOMMENDATION:
Review and provide direction to staff on whether the City's campaign contribution limits and campaign rules should be 
amended or repealed, or if no changes should be made. 
BACKGROUND:
In 2004, the City Council added Chapter 2.80 to the San Mateo Municipal Code entitled “Contributions and Loans to City 
Candidate Campaigns.” This chapter (Attachment 1) established limits on the amount of contributions that may be made 
to City Council candidate campaigns to $250 by an individual and $500 by an organization, as well as limiting the amount a 
candidate can loan to their own campaign to $15,000. In 2017, the City Council amended the limits by increasing the 
amount an individual can give to $500 and an organization to $1,000. And in 2012, section 2.80.070 was amended to read: 
“Penalties. Any person who violates the provisions of this Chapter is guilty of a public offense.” 
Section 2.80.080 requires that the City Council conduct a post-election review of the requirements of this chapter no later 
than three (3) months following the certification of the results of the election for the purpose of allowing the City Council 
to consider the ongoing appropriateness of the provisions of this chapter, including, but not limited to the amount of the 
contribution loan limits established in Sections 2.80.030 and 2.80.040. The City Council certified the final results of the 
November 3, 2020 election with Resolution No. 129 (2020) on December 7, 2020.  See comparison of campaign funding 
between 2017 and 2020 below:

Issues Raised During Recent Elections
In the last two elections, issues have been raised by members of the public relating to nonmonetary (or “in kind”) 
contributions and contributions by individuals and organizations subject to their control.  While these concerns led to 
complaints being filed in both cases, no violations of the code were found. The Council may wish to consider code 
amendments to address these perceived “loopholes.”
Nonmonetary Contributions
State law has for many years required the reporting of contributions made to local candidates. Until this year, state law did 
not impose any contribution limits for municipal elections. With some limited exceptions, the state law on the disclosure 
of contributions requires the reporting of nonmonetary contributions to a candidate. The candidate is required to 
ascertain and disclose the fair market value of the contributed goods or services. Many cities have enacted campaign 
contribution limits that apply the limits to nonmonetary contributions. For example, if a city has enacted a $250 limit on 
contributions, a person would not be able to donate office supplies worth more than $250.  
When the City’s ordinance was enacted, the Council chose to exclude the donation of goods from contributions subject to 
the City’s contribution cap. San Mateo has enacted a $500 limit on donations made by individuals. However, under the 
current code a person may donate $600 in office supplies even though the same person would not be able to make a $600 
contribution. The council may wish to discuss whether to amend the existing code to address this situation.
Aggregation of Contributions by Different Business Entities
It is not uncommon for individuals to make contributions in their individual capacities and also make contributions in the 
name of business entities they own or control. The City’s code imposes a different limit on individuals than it does on 
“organizations” and defines organizations as including business entities. The term business entity is defined as including 
sole proprietorships. So, one individual who owns two companies may make a $500 contribution as an individual and two 
$1,000 contributions on behalf of the two entities they own. The Council may wish to consider amending the code to limit 
the amount an individual who controls business entities may make.   
AB 571- Statewide Limits on Campaign Contributions in Municipal Elections
In 2019, the Legislature enacted AB 571 to establish campaign contribution limits applicable to municipal elections. The bill 
established a limit on contributions that is adjusted over time. The current limit is $4,900. The limits imposed by AB 571 
serve as a default system of controls that applies if the local jurisdiction does not enact limits. A city is free to adopt limits 
that are either more restrictive or more generous than those established in the bill. If a city does not enact a campaign 
contributions limit, the limits provided by state law are enforced by the Fair Political Practices Commission. If a city enacts 
its own limits, responsibility for enforcement remains with the city.

Options
[1]  Repeal the Existing City Campaign Contributions Limits  
Recognizing that state law now limits campaign contributions in municipal elections, the Council could choose to simply 
repeal the current code provisions in their entirety. One advantage of this approach is that there would be one consistent 
system of campaign finance rules applicable to state and local elections. The one set of definitions of the key terms of the 
law applicable throughout the state which might help avoid confusion and make it easier for campaign candidates and 
treasurers to comply. The other advantage with this option is that enforcement would be performed by the FPPC freeing 
up city attorney resources.  
On the other hand, the limits are significantly higher under the state law and concerns might be raised about the 
perception that wealthier donors might garner greater influence through large donations to council candidates.
[2]  Adjust Limits on Nonmonetary Contributions
The current ordinance does not limit the value of nonmonetary contributions of goods to a candidate’s campaign. The 
Council could continue to exclude nonmonetary contributions from the contribution limit, or it may amend the code to 
count the value of nonmonetary contributions toward the limits.  
Even if the Council were to decide not to limit nonmonetary contributions, staff would recommend that the provisions of 
the code addressing nonmonetary contributions be clarified. The issue that arose during the last election involved the 
donation of campaign mailers purchased by the donating organization. While the flyers might reasonably be considered to 
be “goods,” they might also be considered a combination of goods (the paper they were printed on) and services (the 
services provided by a vendor to prepare the flyers.) If the Council desires to continue to allow nonmonetary 
contributions, it should consider and provide direction on whether the exception applies to only goods (like paper) or 
whether it also intends for the exception to apply to goods and services (i.e. the paper and services provided to produce 
campaign flyers.)  
[3]  Contributions by Business Entities Owned by an Individual Contributor
The Council may choose to continue to allow individual contributors to make additional contributions in the name of 
business entities they own. Alternatively, the Council may choose to limit the contributions of such individuals to the 
individual gift limit.
[4]  The Amount of the Contribution Limits
The current code limits contributions by individuals to $500 and contributions made by organizations to $1,000. These 
limits were last adjusted in 2017.  The Council may choose to leave the limits where the are or direct that the limits be 
adjusted.

7.04 Verified Statement of Candidates.
(a) No earlier than the first day, and no later than the last day, specified or provided under 
state law for the filing of nomination papers by any candidate for elective municipal office 
in general law cities, as such law shall be in effect on the first day of January next 
preceding the municipal election, each candidate for an elective office shall file with the 
city clerk a statement containing the following information in the order herein set forth:
1. His/her name;
2. The office for which he/she is a candidate;
3. His/her present residence and occupation;
4. The various kinds of business or employment he/she has been engaged in during the 
past five years and where, also the positions of importance and trust which he/she may 
have held in connection therewith;
5. The civic, improvement or other organizations which he/she has been a member of 
within the past five years and the positions of honor or trust, which he/she may have held 
therein;
6. The public offices he/she ever held, if any, as principal, deputy or employee;
7. The experience, training or education he/she has received which, in his/her opinion, 
would qualify him to fill the office for which he/she is a candidate;
8. The length of time he/she has been a resident in the city;
9. The principal public improvements or betterments which he/she would urge the 
accomplishment of if elected;
10. The names of not more than 15 residents who know something of his/her character 
and abilities;
11. Any other information which, in his/her opinion, would enable the electors to determine 
his/her qualifications for said office.
(b) Said statement shall be verified, and be accompanied by a photograph of the 
candidate taken within the past two years.
(c) The city clerk shall cause the publication of the statements of each candidate so filed, 
with the candidate's photo engraving annexed thereto, in the official city newspaper by two 
insertions therein prior to the day of election. No response to any one of the various 
requirements above-mentioned shall exceed 100 words in length.

SUBJECT:
Billboards on City-owned Property – Consider Options
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive report from staff and provide direction on whether to pursue actions to authorize the placement of billboards on 
City-owned land.
BACKGROUND:
Introduction
At the 2020 goal setting session, the City Council directed staff to explore and report back on several non-tax revenue 
opportunities.  One of these opportunities involved a program for operating electronic billboards that has been employed 
by the City of San Carlos.  In November, the City Council held a study session at which it received a report on the revenue 
opportunities and provided direction to staff.  At the study session, the city attorney advised the Council about a potential 
legal concern with the billboard program.  The city attorney noted that this legal issue had been raised in a federal lawsuit 
in Texas in which a billboard company sued a Texas city that had adopted a program similar to the one adopted in San 
Carlos.  The Council directed staff to monitor the Texas case and to research city billboards in Belmont and Millbrae and 
report back to the Council.
Kenjoh Outdoor Advertising v. Castle Hills, Texas
The City of Castle Hills enacted a sign ordinance similar to the one adopted by the City of San Carlos. Under the ordinance, 
billboards were prohibited within the community unless they were located on property owned or leased by the city. 
Kenjoh was in the business of outdoor advertising and had secured two privately-owned sites for the placement of 
billboards.  When Kenjoh sought approval for the installation of the billboards, the City denied the application because 
Kenjoh’s sites were not owned or controlled by the City. Kenjoh sued.
In its lawsuit, Kenjoh argued that the City’s ordinance violated Kenjoh’s First Amendment rights.  It appears that Castle Hills 
was concerned about its prospects of prevailing in the case.  The case was filed on September 25, 2020, and by January 4, 
2021, had been settled and dismissed.  Under the settlement, Kenjoh was permitted to install two billboards on the sites it 
had acquired.  The City also amended its sign ordinance to prohibit the installation of any new billboards anywhere else in 
the city.  Kenjoh’s first amendment claim was resolved through the settlement so there is no indication how the court 
would have ruled.
Programs in Other Cities
At the November study session, Councilwoman Papan noted that she believed that, in addition to San Carlos, Millbrae and

Belmont had also amended their sign codes to allow billboards on city property. The council directed staff to research 
these programs.  
Millbrae
Staff was able to confirm that Millbrae did amend its sign code to allow the placement of billboards on property owned or 
leased by the city.  The Millbrae program is modeled on the San Carlos program and, like San Carlos, billboards are 
prohibited on property in which the city has no interest.  It appears that the First Amendment concerns were not raised 
and considered during Millbrae’s deliberations on the program.
Belmont
The City of Belmont has added an electronic billboard, but it was not installed pursuant to an ordinance of the type 
adopted in San Carlos.  At the November study session, the city attorney explained to the City Council that state law 
authorizes cities to enter into relocation agreements to provide for the relocation of existing billboards.  In Belmont, an 
outdoor advertising company owned several old and unsightly billboards in the community.  The city and the company 
entered into a relocation agreement under which the company was permitted to erect a modern, electronic billboard on 
freeway facing property owned by the city.  In exchange, the company agreed to remove several older billboards.  Belmont 
leases the property it owns to the company and realizes a revenue stream through the arrangement.
Options
It appears that the Council has options that involve different levels of risk and the potential for generating additional 
revenue through billboards. 
Do Nothing
The City’s current sign ordinance prohibits billboards and would have to be amended to allow for them.  Billboard 
prohibitions have been upheld by the courts so maintaining this prohibition would be the safest course of action from the 
perspective of minimizing legal risk.  Of course, if the Council were to adopt this option it would forgo any additional 
revenue.
Adopt the San Carlos Model
The City Council could direct staff to amend the City’s sign ordinance to reflect the approach that San Carlos and Millbrae 
have taken.  This approach would allow for the most significant increase in revenue as there is the potential for numerous 
billboards.  At the November study session, the city manager reported that based upon his research, each billboard could 
generate between $200,000 and $300,000 per year.  This approach has the most legal risk.  While there is no reported 
case on point, there are strong arguments that can be made that changing city law to prohibit billboards unless they are 
installed on city land is unlawful. In Castle Hills, the city elected to allow the plaintiffs to install their signs on private land 
rather than defend the city’s program.
A Relocation Agreement
The only billboard remaining in San Mateo is a small, older sign that is grandfathered under state law.  Under the same 
state statute, the City could enter into a relocation agreement to authorize the relocation of the sign to a city-owned site.  
City staff has identified the golf course property as a potential site for relocation. Through this relocation agreement the 
City could negotiate lease payments from the sign company that would provide an additional source of revenue.  It is likely 
that the sign company would want to upgrade its sign to a large, modern electronic sign.  This approach would appear to 
present little legal risk as it is expressly authorized by state law.
