Harumo Sato
Mountain View, CA
Previous collaborations include: Department of 
General Services in Washington, DC, Target, US 
Soccer Federation, and Pow Wow San Jose
After moving into the US from Japan in 2011, I started to live in 
the Bay Area in 2016 and recently started a family here after 
giving birth to my son in San Mateo county. I quite often visit San 
Mateo Library since it has a great selection of Japanese children 
books. Now, as a new family of three, seeing many friends with 
kids moving out to different states due to the high priced rent 
and property, it is true that I have such fear sometimes if we 
could afford to live in the Bay in the future. The housing issue is 
real for all of us, and I’d really like to support and advocate this 
affordable housing movement as an artist, a resident, and a new 
mother. I have worked with SV@Home in San Jose multiple 
times for their visual needs as well.
“
”
San Jose
Menlo Park

Harumo Sato
I believe the artwork for Kiku Crossing Garage Art must:
•
visually reference Japanese American, Ohlone, and Mexican to attribute their history deeply related with San Mateo
•
celebrate the lively neighborhood with great public transportation access
•
reference local wild animals and vegetation to admire the local ecology
•
celebrate inclusiveness through portraying a variety of colors (not limited to skin tone)
•
provide visual pleasure from every possible angle, thus welcoming any and all visitors to the Crossing.
Therefore I propose a series of designs inspired by the iconic Ohlone basket patterns, Mexican and Japanese 
textile design with full of high-energy colors, patterns, and movement.

Laurie Satizabal
Redwood City, CA
Graduate of College of San Mateo (AA in 
Studio Art), Graduate of San Jose State 
University (BFA in Pictorial Art), former graphic 
design  intern of the County of San Mateo, 
current volunteer with the United Nations
I am interested in this project because it represents communities 
from diverse backgrounds. As an immigrant from Panama I know 
the importance of feeling seen and recognized in the place I’ve 
decided to call home. I want to make people feel welcomed, 
accepted and acknowledged and if I can do that through art, I’m 
fulfilled. Specifically for this project I am drawn to the themes of 
local ecology because in my own work I illustrate fauna and flora 
often. As someone who knows first-hand what it's like to settle in 
a new and unfamiliar place, the topics of "immigration stories" 
and "mobility" spark my interest as well.
“
”

--- page 26 ---
Laurie Satizabal
I wanted to create a series of illustrations that depicts and blends San Mateo’s past with how we use this area presently. 
By combining these elements I will address topics such as how the past is a bedrock of the present and how do we 
want to see this community in the future. My main focus in this project is to create a sense of community, union and the 
acceptance of diversity to those around us. I want my illustration to create a safe feeling of belong and recognition in 
those who see it. I acknowledge the history of this beautiful area we live in and want to focus on bringing forth that 
resilience and inspiration. 
I will focus on bringing and explore elements of the local ecology, mobility, transportation and immigration 
stories around the county with a whimsical, illustrative style.

Claudio Talavera Ballon
San Francisco, CA
San Francisco-based visual artist with over 20 
years of experience working primarily as an oil 
painter and muralist, as well as an instructor 
and designer with work in Redwood City, 
Dublin, San Francisco, and Oaxaca.
I continually explore the themes of migration, convergence, and 
social justice. My artwork seeks to honor the experiences of 
everyday people who struggle for a better life, including those 
who come from far away, like myself, an immigrant to the United 
States. Most recently, I painted a mural entitled Kiku Dreaming, a 
large scale mural that focuses on the historic local 
chrysanthemum industry with its pioneer Japanese American 
growers in Redwood City, many of whom were later forced into 
internment camps.
“
”
Redwood City
San Francisco
Redwood City

Claudio Talavera Ballon
For this project, I constructed images where these themes converge, strengthening the sense of community identity, 
connection, and a deeper sense of place while adding color, vibrancy, and character to the cityscape. Toward this end, I 
have created four models that emphasize the relationships in the natural world and the relationship between humans 
and nature.
By inserting the natural ecosystem over the human with these designs, we remind ourselves of the challenge of 
conservation and restoration of the natural habitats that enrich the quality of life we have in the Bay Area.
