DRAFT ORDINANCE
Amending Section 17.08.020 of the San Mateo Municipal Code to Allow Temporary Signs to Identify a Place of 
Public Interest in the Public Right-of-Way with an Encroachment Permit
WHEREAS, Section 17.08.020 of the San Mateo Municipal Code addresses objects in the public right-of- 
way; and
WHEREAS, The City wishes to add a provision allowing the City to install signage marking points of public 
interest; and
WHEREAS, The City also wishes to update Section 17.08.020 to refer to the Public Works Director and 
reference City Council adopted policies regarding outdoor dining in the public right-of-way;
NOW, THEREFORE, THE COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF SAN MATEO ORDAINS AS FOLLOWS:
Section 1. Section 17.08.020 of the San Mateo Municipal Code is amended as follows:
17.08.020 Placing Certain Objects in the Public Right-of-Way Prohibited
No person shall place or cause to be placed, anywhere upon any public way, right-of-way, street, or 
sidewalk, and no person owning, occupying, or having the control of any premises shall suffer to remain in front 
thereof, upon any sidewalk, in the public right-of-way, of the half of the street or way next to such premises any 
appliances, furniture, bench, inoperable vehicles, boats, machinery, foods, merchandise, implement box, debris 
box, mailbox, or other object, which shall be an obstruction for more than one hour at a time.
The foregoing shall not apply to:
(a) Goods or merchandise in actual course of receipt, delivery, or removal;
(b) Ornamental trees and planter boxes placed along the outer line of the sidewalk, behind the curbs 
under permit or direction of the Public Works Director or designee;
(c) Barriers for the protection of permitted ornamental trees;
(d) Poles erected under franchises obtained from the City or other authority;
(e) Hydrants or other objects erected by permission or direction of the City;
(f) Clocks or fountains erected for the accommodation of the public under permit or direction of the 
Public Works Director or designee;
(g) Debris boxes and materials used in the construction, repair, or demolition of any building; if such 
debris boxes and materials are permitted by the Public Works Director or designee under permit issued in the 
manner, and meeting the requirements and conditions of Sections 17.08.070, 17.08.080, and 17.08.090, 
14 of 134

--- page 15 ---
including the posting of bonds and insurance meeting the City's minimum requirements. Such permit shall 
require the placement of necessary barricades and sufficient nighttime warning lights and/or reflectors;
(h) Garbage and rubbish containers conforming to the specifications of the Public Works Director or 
designee may be placed on sidewalks or gutters in direct proximity to the curb on scheduled garbage collection 
days between the hours of 5:00 a.m. and 8:00 p.m. and during "Annual Cleanup" weeks in accordance with 
published annual cleanup regulations;
(i) Bus benches and passenger waiting shelters erected by a public transit agency that conform to State 
building standards and do not cause a pedestrian or vehicular hazard
(j) Restaurant seating and associated means of delineating the physical extent of the seating area 
conforming to either the standards set forth in Section 27.87.030 or as adopted by City Council resolution and

(k) Sidewalk vendors; and
(l) Storage containers placed in the public right-of-way for no longer than seven days with the option of

designee pursuant to Municipal Code Sections 17.08.070, 17.08.080, and 17.08.090.
(m) Signs placed by a public officer or employee acting in their official capacity to identify a place of 
public interest.
Section 2. Environmental Determination. In accordance with Public Resources Code Section 21065, adoption of 
this Ordinance is not a project subject to CEQA in that it can be seen with certainty that there is no possibility 
that the amendments will cause a significant impact to the environment, because the amendments authorize 
City signage regarding points of public interest in the public right-of-way, update terms, and reference an 
existing City Council policy regarding outdoor dining in the public right-of-way.
Section 3. Severability. In the event any section, clause or provision of this ordinance shall be determined 
invalid or unconstitutional, such section, clause or provision shall be deemed severable and all other sections or 
portions hereof shall remain in full force and effect.
Section 4. Publication. This Ordinance shall be published in summary in a newspaper of general circulation, 
posted in the City Clerk's Office, and posted on the City's website, all in accord with Section 2.15 of the City 
Charter.
Section 5. Legislative History and Effective Date. This ordinance was introduced on Clerk to complete., and 
adopted on Clerk to complete., and shall be effective 30 days after its adoption.

Adding Chapter 10.20 “Fireworks” to Title 10 of the San Mateo Municipal Code
WHEREAS, Staff has identified Municipal Code provisions related to enforcement of fireworks violations 
that require clarification; and
WHEREAS, Staff recommends adding a section to the Municipal Code to clearly indicate that the Police

NOW, THEREFORE, THE COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF SAN MATEO ORDAINS AS FOLLOWS:
Section 1. Chapter 10.20 of the San Mateo Municipal Code is added to Title 10 as follows: 
CHAPTER 10.20 FIREWORKS
10.20.010 Definitions
(a) As used in this Chapter “fireworks” means and includes:
(1) Any combustible or explosive composition or any substance or combination of substances or 
articles prepared for the purpose of producing a visible or an audible effect by combustion, explosion, 
deflagration or detonation, whether manufactured, homemade or improvised;
(2) Fireworks classified by the State Fire Marshal as “dangerous fireworks” and as “safe and sane 
fireworks” pursuant to Health and Safety Code Sections 12561 and 12562;
(3) Any pyrotechnic devices for which the State Fire Marshal requires a license to manufacture, 
sell, transport or operate; and
(4) Firecrackers, torpedoes, skyrockets, roman candles, cherry bombs, sparklers, chasers, snakes 
or other fireworks of like or similar construction and any fireworks containing any explosive or 
flammable compound or substance.
10.020.020 Sale and Use of Fireworks Prohibited
(a) It is unlawful to sell or offer for sale fireworks of any kind or nature in the city. 
(b) It is unlawful to fire, discharge, burn or use fireworks of any kind or nature within the city except that 
a public display of fireworks may be permitted by a permit issued by the fire chief subject to such conditions as 
he/she may impose to ensure that such public display of fireworks is conducted in such a manner as to avoid the 
ignition of fires and injury to persons and property.  Public displays are not permitted in residential 
neighborhoods under any conditions.
(c) Should the provisions of this chapter conflict with the provisions of any other ordinance or city code 
section of the city, the provisions of this section shall prevail.

RECOMMENDATION:
Award a contract to JMB Construction for construction of the Pacific Boulevard Force Main in an amount not to exceed 
$1,892,650; establish a contingency fund in an amount of $283,000; and authorize the Public Works Director to execute 
the agreement in substantially the form presented and issue change orders within the contingency amount.
BACKGROUND:
The Clean Water Program (CWP) is improving the City’s sewer collection system to prevent sanitary sewer overflows and 
unauthorized discharges of sewage. The Pacific Boulevard Force Main project is part of the CWP’s efforts to proactively 
rehabilitate aging sewer pipes and increase wastewater system capacity. The new force main will increase overall pumping 
capacity from the recently improved 41st Avenue Pump Station. This work will ensure proper operation of the sewer 
collection system and address failures that can lead to sanitary sewer overflows. 
The project includes the installation of a new 14-inch force main using horizontal directional drilling as the primary 
construction method. This method reduces the impacts to the community and the amount of surface pavement 
restoration compared to open trench construction. The force main will be installed along Pacific Boulevard, from the 41st 
Avenue Pump Station to the intersection of Pacific Boulevard and Delaware Street. Notice to proceed with construction is 
anticipated to be given in April 2021. 
In accordance with Municipal Code section 3.60.030, a competitive public process was used to obtain bids for this service. 
A Notice Inviting Sealed Proposals and Bidding Documents was issued to advertise the project on February 1, 2021. Six (6) 
bids were received and opened on March 11, 2021 with the following results:

SUBJECT:
Private Development and Right-of-Way Support Services Agreement – Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve Amendment No. 3 to the agreement with CSG Consultants, Inc. for professional consultant services for private 
development and right-of-way support services in the amount of $150,000, for a new agreement total of $350,000; extend 
the term of the agreement to May 2, 2022; and authorize the Public Works Director to execute the amendment in 
substantially the form presented.
BACKGROUND:
On January 6, 2019 the Public Works Director approved an agreement with CSG Consultants, Inc. (CSG) for private 
development and right-of-way support services in the amount not to exceed $48,000 to address a significant increase in 
the number of permits and planning applications that required Public Works staff review. CSG was selected through a 
competitive pricing process after the City solicited and received proposals from 11 different engineering firms. CSG’s scope 
of work primarily includes reviewing building permits, planning applications, and right-of-way related work. On July 15, 
2019, the City Manager approved Amendment No.1, increasing the agreement amount to $90,000. On March 2, 2020, the 
City Council approved Amendment No. 2, increasing the agreement amount to $200,000.  Amendment No. 3 would 
increase CSG’s total contract amount to $350,000 and extend the term of the agreement to May 2, 2022.
The annual number of building permit and planning applications submitted to the City has increased during recent years.  
As examples, City staff reviewed 74 planning applications during fiscal year 2018-19, 104 planning applications during fiscal 
year 2019-20, and 125 planning applications are projected for the end of fiscal year 20-21, which is a 67% increase in two 
years. Accordingly, staff continue to need outside consulting services to assist with the review and processing of 
applications within the required timeframes.
Costs associated with staff time and consultant services required to review and process applications and permits are paid 
by fees collected from the building permit applicants and planning application deposits. Accordingly, all costs associated 
with CSG’s review of the applications is recovered by the City. To ensure the City is providing cost-effective services for 
residents and developers, staff reviews CSG’s hourly charges to project accounts to verify plan reviews are completed in an 
efficient manner. Based on the historic level of effort and expenditures to date, the modified contract amount is estimated 
to be sufficient for approximately one year of support from CSG staff. 
Staff recommends approval of Amendment No. 3 to the agreement with CSG to meet the City’s level of service 
requirements for building permits, planning applications, and right-of-way permit reviews.

SUBJECT:
Wastewater Treatment Plant Centrifuges – Contract Change Order and Amendment
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve Amendment No. 1 and Change Order No. 2 to the contract with GEA Mechanical US, Inc. - WESTFALIA (GEA) in 
the amount of $24,000 for a new contract total of $186,136.55 for repair and maintenance of centrifuges at the 
Wastewater Treatment Plant and authorize the Public Works Director to execute the amendment and change order in 
substantially the form presented. 
BACKGROUND:
The centrifuges are vital pieces of equipment in the Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) process. The centrifuges remove 
the remaining water from digested solids to produce a compatible material ("cake") that can then be hauled away to be 
used for either landfill alternate daily cover or land application. These units are the last step in the solids handling process 
and are crucial to maintaining compliance in operations. If all the centrifuges become inoperable, digested sludge would 
not be able to be processed. If a backup option is not set up in time, an overflow situation could occur and/or additional 
cost incurred to haul off this non-contract liquid sludge. 
Purchasing parts in a timely manner, as well as having factory-trained technicians available, is recommended to minimize 
downtime in the event of a failure of an operating unit. Two of the WWTP’s centrifuges required an immediate major 
service, which have been completed. Due to the precise nature of centrifuge operation, GEA factory-trained technicians 
completed these repairs. The cost of the repairs previously performed surpassed the budgeted amount due to the 
extensive work needed to repair the centrifuges. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
The cost of the centrifuge repairs was not budgeted, but sufficient funds are available in the WWTP Maintenance budget 
to cover the expenses. 
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This centrifuge repair work is categorically exempt from CEQA as an “existing facility,” because it consists of the operation, 
repair, and maintenance or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or 
topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15301)

SUBJECT:
Central Park Tennis Court Garage Repair – Budget Appropriation and Contract Change Order
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to appropriate $25,000 of available fund balance from the Parking In-lieu Fund; approve Contract 
Change Order No. 3 to the contract with Newton Construction and Management, Inc. in the amount of $539,436.51 for a 
new contract total of $1,569,633.95; establish a contingency reserve in the amount of $30,000; and authorize the Public 
Works Director to execute the contract change order in substantially the form presented and issue change orders in 
accordance with the contract documents and within the contingency amount.
BACKGROUND:
The Central Park Tennis Court Garage has served the downtown community for over sixty years. It is a concrete structure 
that has 132 underground parking spaces on a single level and six tennis courts on the roof level. A structural assessment 
was performed in 2019 that identified necessary repairs to the components of the garage structure. The recommended 
repairs were intended to extend the useful life of the garage for ten years by installing a waterproof membrane over the 
top surface of the concrete deck underneath the tennis court surface. The waterproofing membrane would protect the 
steel reinforcement that had been damaged by the intrusion of rainwater. However, because no original drawings of the 
garage structure were available, the assessment was limited to observation of the conditions that were open and visible at 
the time of inspection.
In August 2020, Council awarded a contract in the amount of $747,831.76 to Newton Construction and Management, Inc. 
for repairs to the garage. Once the demolition process to remove the existing tennis court surface began, a number of 
unknown conditions were exposed that required additional effort. In November 2020, Council approved Change Order No. 
2 in the amount of $279,903.18 for additional demolition and structural repairs. 
Cracking observed in the concrete deck necessitated additional analysis by the project’s structural engineer. An X-ray scan 
of the existing steel reinforcement at the locations of the cracks was conducted and a slope survey was completed to 
confirm positive drainage, allowing the waterproof membrane manufacturer to provide a 10-year warranty on their 
product. 
Results of the X-ray scan and slope survey provided additional information to the structural engineer. Based on these 
findings, the structural engineer and waterproofing system manufacturer provided recommendations for additional 
concrete slab and steel reinforcement repairs and surface preparation. The recommended level of repairs are based on 
increasing the useful life of the structure for ten years. Change Order No. 3 in the amount of $539,436.51 includes the 
additional scope of work needed to restore the operations at the parking and tennis structure. Work is projected to be 
finished at the end of May 2021 and the courts re-opened for play in mid-June, weather permitting.

SUBJECT:
Electrical Vehicle Charging Installation – Grant Acceptance
RECOMMENDATION:
Accept a grant of up to $134,000 from the California Electric Vehicle Infrastructure Project to rebate a portion of the costs 
of electric vehicle charger installation at the Main Street and 2nd and El Camino public parking garages.
BACKGROUND:
The California Electric Vehicle Infrastructure Project (CALeVIP) provides rebates for electric vehicle (EV) charger 
installations. CALeVIP is funded by the California Energy Commission and implemented by the Center for Sustainable 
Energy in partnership with Peninsula Clean Energy for the San Mateo County region. Up to $4 million will be distributed for 
Level 2 EV charging infrastructure in San Mateo County.
Staff applied for CALeVIP rebate funding for installation of EV chargers at the Main Street Garage and the Second and El 
Camino Garage. The City is potentially eligible for an award of up to $134,000 depending on the number of chargers the 
City installs at each site. Staff applied for the maximum rebate amount, anticipating that further analysis and review would 
determine the actual number of chargers installed. CALeVIP funding can be used to offset the cost of EV supply equipment, 
transformer, electric panels, installation (labor and materials), project signage, network agreements with network 
providers and more.
CALeVIP has reserved a maximum rebate award of $80,000 for the City of San Mateo’s Main Street Garage application. 
CALeVIP did not award funding for the Second and El Camino Garage (maximum award of $54,000), but it is possible that 
the City will receive additional funding for this location at a later date. CALeVIP funding is awarded on a first-come, first-
served basis and additional funding reservations will be released throughout the program cycle. Budget appropriation for 
the CALeVIP rebate funding will be included as part of the 2021-22 CIP budget. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Matching funding ($75,000) for the design and installation of the electric vehicle chargers has been proposed as part of the 
2021-22 CIP budget and CALeVIP rebate-related budget appropriations will also be included in the proposed 2021-22 CIP 
budget. In the event that the CALeVIP funding does not come to fruition, staff will explore other sources of rebate and 
grant funding and/or modify the project scope.

SUBJECT:
Police Department Budgeted Positions – Reallocations
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to reallocate one authorized Police Officer position to a Police Sergeant position and reallocate one 
authorized Police Sergeant position to a Police Lieutenant position.
BACKGROUND:
The San Mateo Police Activities League (PAL) is a collaborative effort between the San Mateo Police Department and the 
San Mateo Parks and Recreation Department to keep kids on track, in school, and away from negative and troubling 
behaviors.  PAL is a 501(c)(3) non-profit organization, with oversight guided by a volunteer board of directors. PAL employs 
several staff members directly, and the Police and Parks and Recreation Departments each provide one full-time City 
employee dedicated to assisting with PAL operations. These positions are funded by the City.  The Police Department 
provides a full-time Police Officer responsible for running PAL’s day-to-day operations.  Supervision is provided via a Police 
Sergeant assigned to the Police Department’s Youth Services Unit (YSU), and management oversight is provided by the 
Lieutenant of the Investigations Division (Attachment 2). 
CHALLENGES WITH CURRENT STRUCTURE
Both the PAL Officer position and the YSU Police Sergeant position have historically been difficult to recruit.  Traditionally 
these positions have been filled as short-term 2-3 year specialty assignments.  The roles require skills and experience that 
are a deviation from what is considered “typical police work,” and often PAL loses a great deal of momentum, institutional 
knowledge, and experience when rotations occur. The current organizational structure is challenging in that investigations 
and youth services are not naturally complementary, and the demands on each unit are large.  At times it is difficult to 
maintain a clearly identified command and reporting structure given the variety of stakeholders. 
PAL’s current organizational structure (Attachment 3) does not provide the infrastructure and leadership of a dedicated 
unit, with many of the management duties being auxiliary to a competing primary assignment. While there is a Lieutenant 
assigned to providing management oversight of PAL, these are ancillary duties as the Lieutenant’s primary focus is to run 
the Police Department’s Bureau of Investigations. The result is that many of the management duties fall to the YSU 
Sergeant, and many of the supervisorial duties fall to the PAL Officer, creating a situation where the chain of command is 
ambiguous. In functioning as the executive director, the PAL Officer must perform duties that are consistent with a 
Sergeant or Lieutenant position. These factors make it difficult to grow and expand on the valuable community service 
programs provided by PAL and YSU. 
PROPOSED REORGANIZATION
Staff proposes reorganizing PAL and the Youth Services Unit to provide stability and greater service to the community 
(Attachment 4). In addition to more clearly delineating roles and responsibilities, the reorganization combines PAL and YSU

functions into a single unit overseen by a new Police Lieutenant position that would be created by reallocating the existing 
YSU Police Sergeant Position. Elevating this position from a Sergeant to a Lieutenant is necessary to reflect the increased 
management functions that are expected and necessary to effectively run this unit. The Lieutenant would provide overall 
leadership, direction, and management of all PAL operations as well as the Juvenile Diversion, Youth Service/School 
Resource Officer, and Police Explorer Programs. The Lieutenant will be the clear management conduit between the PAL 
Board, Police Department, Parks and Recreation Department, and schools.  
The restructuring also includes reallocating the PAL Officer position to a Police Sergeant, with responsibilities including 
serving as the face of PAL for events, programs, and fundraising; community outreach to identify kids and families in need 
of PAL support; and added supervisorial oversight of the employees assigned to the division, including youth service 
officers and staff assigned to the juvenile diversion program. 
BENEFITS
Organizing the PAL and Youth Services functions into their own unit within the Police Department with dedicated 
leadership will increase effectiveness and underscore the importance our agency places on community-oriented police 
services. Police work is much more than “enforcing the law,” and this change will create more opportunities for police 
officers to have positive interactions with the community. This will build trust and mutual respect, and ultimately make our 
staff better equipped to address social problems in our community, which is critical to public safety. The reorganization 
will allow us to bolster our community-oriented services and programs, as well as provide career development 
opportunities for community-focused employees. It is envisioned that both the new Lieutenant and Sergeant positions 
would not be short-term rotational assignments; rather, it would be expected that one provides a 5-7 year commitment 
when assuming a role in this unit, in order to provide the much needed sustainability and continuity. 
The proposed reorganization and funding plan has been presented to PAL’s board of directors and has received its 
unanimous support.  Additionally, the positional reallocations have been discussed with the impacted bargaining groups, 
and they are supportive of the changes.  Per San Mateo Municipal Code Section 2.57.030(d), approval of position 
reallocations must by approved by City Council Resolution.
BUDGET IMPACT:
The current PAL Officer position and Youth Services Sergeant position are funded in the Police Department’s General Fund 
Budget.  Reallocating these positions to a Sergeant and Lieutenant will result in an additional annual cost of approximately 
$52,000 for fiscal year 2021-22. These additional annual costs in excess of the City’s current funding commitment will be 
fully reimbursed by PAL on an ongoing basis, resulting in no net financial impact to the City.

RECOMMENDATION:

crossing optimization project and grade separation design guidelines project.
BACKGROUND:
The Peninsula Corridor Joint Powers Board (Caltrain) has recently submitted Transportation and Infrastructure Project 
Funding Requests for two projects, the crossing optimization project and the grade separation design guidelines project. 
Caltrain has requested letters of support from the City of San Mateo for these two projects. 
The crossing optimization project will implement wireless grade crossing technology at the 41 Caltrain owned at-grade 
crossings (where streets and railroad tracks intersect) along the rail corridor. As Caltrain nears completion of its Peninsula 
Corridor Electrification Project, the installation of wireless technology is critical to optimize system efficiency. 
Implementing an optimized crossing system would reduce gate down time, which in turn creates several benefits including 
increased traffic flow and vehicle throughput as well as reduced congestion and minimized vehicle idling. Caltrain is 
requesting $1.89 million in funding for 12 crossings in Congresswoman Speier's district, 9 of which are in San Mateo.

corridor between San Francisco and San Jose and an additional 28 at-grade crossings located along the Union Pacific 
Railroad owned corridor running south from San Jose to Gilroy. The requested funding, $5 million, would augment and 
expand the effort to undertake a significant corridor-wide study by providing resources to develop technical design 
guidelines for future grade separation projects. These design guidelines will leverage the planned strategy work and will 
help ensure that projects are delivered in an efficient and cost-effective way while also meeting community expectations.

SUBJECT:
Budget Update and Appropriation and American Rescue Plan Funds Discussion 
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to appropriate $1.9 million in available fund balance of the General Fund; receive the fiscal year 2020-
21 budget update and the 2021-22 budget preview; and preliminary considerations for disposition of American Rescue 
Plan funds. 
BACKGROUND:
This report serves to provide the City Council with the recurring and periodic update of the City's financial condition, as 
committed to by Finance Department staff in response to the economic uncertainty and financial impact of COVID-19.
Fiscal Year 2020-21 Budget Update 
During the mid-year budget update that was presented to the City Council on January 30, 2021, General Fund revenues 
were estimated to be $126.8 million, largely reflecting the impact of COVID-19 on transient occupancy tax, sales tax, 
property transfer tax, and recreation service fees. Since the mid-year budget update, the primary change in year-end 
estimates for General Fund revenues is the City's allocation of $350 billion in support for state and local governments as a 
part of the $1.9 trillion package from the American Rescue Plan (ARP), which was signed into law by President Biden on 
March 11, 2021. Based on current estimates, the City of San Mateo is slated to receive $18.64 million in relief funds from 
the American Rescue Plan. The City will receive half by the end of the current fiscal year, and the remaining half next fiscal 
year. 
The City received an update the County of San Mateo Controller’s Office that the property tax in lieu of vehicle license fees 
(VLF) county-wide shortfall that was previously estimated at $69.5 million has increased to $96.2 million. The City’s VLF 
amount due is $13.2 million but will only be receiving $7.5 million as a result of the VLF shortfall. The adopted 2020-21 
budget assumed a VLF shortfall in its revenue estimate of $10.0 million, but the year-end estimate is now $2.5 million less 
than the adopted budget. As was the case with the VLF shortfall in the prior fiscal year, the County of San Mateo will file a 
claim for the county-wide shortfall of $96.2 million in fall 2021 for the State of California to consider as a part of its fiscal 
year 2022-23 budget. The County Controller’s Office will provide additional detail at the next meeting of the San Mateo 
County Finance Officers’ Group (SAMFOG) meeting on April 20. Finance staff will continue to keep the City Council 
apprised of any subsequent developments on VLF shortfalls, which is an trend that will continue in significantly impact the

Ordinance Introduction – “Protected Trees” Ordinance and Administrative Guidelines Approval
RECOMMENDATION:
Introduce an Ordinance to amend the San Mateo Municipal Code to add a new Chapter 13.40 “Protected Trees” to replace 
the existing Chapters 13.35 “Street Trees” and Chapter 13.52 “Heritage Trees” and approve the “Protected Trees 
Ordinance Administrative Guidelines”. 
BACKGROUND:
The City of San Mateo’s Heritage Tree Ordinance (SMMC Chapter 13.52) was first adopted in 1968 to ensure a significant 
and thriving population of large healthy trees in San Mateo for public enjoyment and environmental sustainability, while 
honoring the reasonable economic enjoyment of private property. Since first adopted, the Heritage Tree Ordinance has 
been revised several times, most recently in 2007, to improve its relevance to the times and to enhance its efficacy toward 
achieving its intended purpose. Over the past six years, Parks staff has observed some elements of the ordinance that: 
• Are difficult to interpret and confusing to the public 
• Create tree preservation loopholes 
• Are difficult to administer and enforce 
• Are not in accordance with current industry best practices 
With the aim of enhancing clarity, increasing and maintaining canopy, and increasing effectiveness of ordinance 
implementation, staff has prepared Chapter 13.40 “Protected Trees” ordinance to replace the existing Chapter 13.35 
“Street Trees” and Chapter 13.52 “Heritage Trees”. The proposed updates are informed by staff and resident experiences, 
Park and Recreation Commission comments from four public meetings, and by a study of ordinances recently adopted in 
neighboring cities. Considerable input was provided by arborist staff in the Community Development Department, the City 
Attorney’s office, and several outside consulting arborists. Staff presented proposed changes to the public at four Park and 
Recreation Commission meetings between July 2020 and February 2021. At these meetings, staff presented on aspects of 
the ordinance that could use refinement and raised for discussion proposed changes to better meet its intent and purpose. 
The presentations were announced to the public on the City’s website and Nextdoor.com. 
At the final presentation to the Park and Recreation Commission on February 3, 2021, the Commission was uncomfortable 
with a proposed revision to hold property owners financially responsible for the emergency removal of privately-owned 
street trees. In response to Commission concerns, staff redacted this provision, and the City will remain responsible for 
trees removed by the City to remedy immediate hazards. The commission voted 3 to 1 in favor of recommending that City 
Council approve the adoption of SMMC Section 13.40 “Protected Trees Ordinance” and the accompanying Administrative 
Guidelines.

Article I. General Provisions
13.40.010 Purpose and Intent
This Chapter is adopted because the City has been forested with a variety of healthy and valuable trees 
which must be protected, preserved, and replenished for the health and welfare of residents and in order to 
preserve scenic beauty, maintain property values, prevent erosion, protect against flood hazards and landslides,

for the protection of trees within the City in order to retain as many trees as possible consistent with these 
purposes and the reasonable economic enjoyment of private property.

Article IV. Violations and Remedies
13.40.140 Violations.
(a) It is unlawful for any person or entity to engage in, or cause, Major Pruning or Removal of a 
Protected Tree in the City without obtaining a permit to do so. This applies to any owner, possessor or manager 
of property who engages in Major Pruning or removes or allows or instructs another to engage in Major Pruning 
or remove a Protected Tree and to any other person, tree removal company or other entity that actually 
removes or participates in the Major Pruning or Removal of the Protected Tree. 
(b) It is unlawful for any person or entity to:
(1) cause Damage to a Protected Tree compromising its health or structural integrity according 
to accepted industry standards;
(2) to cause Damage that is severely detrimental to its overall aesthetics;
(3) to remove fencing or other protective barrier surrounding a Protected Tree that was 
required as a condition of development on the property; 
(4) to violate any provisions of this Chapter or the terms of any permit or Tree Protection Plan 
issued in accordance with this Chapter; or
(5) knowingly or negligently providing false or misleading information in response to any 
disclosure requirements of this Chapter or in application for any permit issued under this Chapter.
(c) Citizen complaints regarding a potential violation must be received by the Managing Arborist within 
one year of the alleged violation.
(d) When a tree has been removed from the site and its diameter is no longer visible, the Managing 
Arborist may rely on a stump measurement and allow use of photographs, neighboring trees, and or any other

Permits and decision-making criteria for tree removal 
Applicants who submitted a tree permit application before July 1, 2021, will have their 
applications reviewed under the prior ordinance (13.35 or 13.52). The review process includes, 
but is not limited to tree replacement requirements, penalties, and the appeal process. 
Applications received on July 1, 2021 or later are subject to all provisions of the Protected Tree 
Ordinance 13.40. 
The determination in granting or denying a permit is based on the criteria listed in Municipal 
Code Section 13.40.100(d), further articulated below: 
1. Tree Condition.  
The tree is dead, or in a poor state of health that cannot be abated through reasonable 
arboricultural treatments. 
 
2. Danger to People or Property.  
Tree risk assessment is a systematic process used to identify, analyze, and evaluate tree 
risk. Risk is assessed by categorizing the likelihood (probability) of an occurrence 
(failure), the likelihood of impacting a target, and the severity of consequences should 
failure occur to determine a risk rating.  The International Society of Arboriculture (ISA) 
publishes best management practices for assessing tree risk. An arborist’s report from an 
ISA Qualified Tree Risk Assessor is recommend and may be required for tree permits 
requested based on risk. The report should provide evidence that the tree risk rating 
cannot be mitigated to a low residual risk rating through pruning, cabling, or other means. 
 
3. Interference with existing structures or utility services.  
The City expects property owners to make reasonable efforts to mitigate conflicts with 
protected trees. Example mitigation activities for structures and utility services: 
• Root pruning and installation of a root barrier to prevent damage from roots. 
• Relocation of low-voltage wires 
• Replacement of sewer lateral system to eliminate root intrusion due to outdated, 
leaky plumbing. 
If it can be demonstrated that mitigation options are infeasible or inappropriate, a permit 
may be issued. 
 
4. Necessity to remove the tree or trees in order to allow reasonable economic 
enjoyment of the property.  
This criterion applies primarily to the construction of dwellings or amenities. The City 
expects residents to consider alternative designs that could preserve the tree. If it can be 
demonstrated that alternative designs are infeasible or inappropriate, a permit may be 
issued. 
 
5. Effect of the removal on erosion and soil retention.

13.52.140 Remedies 
Protected Trees removed without a valid permit are to be replaced with one 48-inch box tree. If 
the value of the tree removed is greater than that of a 48-inch box tree, The Managing Arborist 
may impose enhanced replant conditions not to exceed the value of the tree(s).  
Equivalencies 
If the Managing Arborist determines that replanting of the assigned replacement trees is 
infeasible, s/he may allow a combination of planting or monetary equivalencies. The monetary 
equivalencies are based on the cost to purchase and install trees of commonly available sizes. 
ONE 48 inch box  =  TWO 36 inch boxes  =  FOUR 24 inch boxes  =  $2,500
