SUBJECT:
Bicycle and Pedestrian Projects FY 20-21 Q3 Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission, acting as the City of San Mateo's Bicycle and Pedestrian Advisory 
Committee, receive the 2020-21 3rd Quarter update on bicycle and pedestrian projects.
BACKGROUND:
Staff will present brief updates on the pedestrian and bicycle infrastructure projects and grant efforts.
Infrastructure Projects

North San Mateo Drive Complete Streets: This project consists of the implementation of a road diet converting 
the existing four-lane roadway to two-lanes with a center turn lane and bicycle lanes from Poplar Avenue to 
Peninsula Avenue. Additionally, the project will install bike lanes between Poplar Avenue and Catalpa Street 
and includes one mile of bicycle lanes and a bicycle boulevard, green infrastructure, pedestrian countdown 
signals, and curb extensions. 
o
Q3 Update: The project is substantially complete. Striping and activation of traffic signals have been 
completed, and the City is reviewing final punch list items.

28th Avenue Bicycle Boulevard: The bicycle boulevard will be implemented in two segments on 28th Avenue. 
The first, planned for installation between Mason Lane and El Camino Real, includes a curb extension at 
Hacienda Street, a speed table (elevated crosswalk), pavement markings, and wayfinding signage. 
o
Q3 Update: Golden Bay Construction has completed installation of all new curb extensions on the 
corners of 28th and Garfield as well as at the corners of 28th and Hacienda. Some paving restoration 
work will occur in early May, along with the installation of the new raised crosswalk at 28th and 
Garfield.  There will be an overnight roadway closure for installation of the raised crosswalk.  Striping, 
including the installation of pavement markings, will be installed mid-May. The project is expected to 
be completed in June 2021. 
o
A second portion of the bicycle boulevard was implemented east of El Camino Real, between West 
Kyne Street and Saratoga Drive. This portion of 28th Avenue was included in the City’s Safe Streets 
program, and the permanent bicycle boulevard implementation including pavement markings and 
wayfinding signage was installed in February 2021. 

Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) Program: The City receives federal funding from the U.S. 
Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) for the CDBG program and allocates the funds based 
on community priorities.

Q3 Update: The projects identified for implementation with these funds are the projects identified as 
the 2nd and 4th highest priority projects in the 2020 Bicycle Master Plan. These projects include a

Planning Projects

Transit-Oriented Development Pedestrian Access Plan: The City was awarded Transportation Development Act 
(TDA) Article 3 funds for this project, which will evaluate the full pedestrian path of travel to the City’s high-quality 
transit stops. The Plan will identify necessary improvements to create a safe and comfortable pedestrian realm 
within one-half mile of those locations. 
o
Q3 Update: City Council approved an agreement with Fehr & Peers in April 2021 to assist staff in the 
completion of this project. Staff has issued the Notice to Proceed to the consultant team and will begin 
work on the project shortly.
Grant Efforts

Caltrans Active Transportation Program (ATP): Staff applied for the ATP Cycle 5 call for projects for the Delaware 
Street Safe Routes to School bicycle project. This project will design and implement the Delaware Street Class IV 
separated bike lane and Class III bicycle boulevard as planned in the 2020 Bicycle Master Plan. This project 
provides necessary safety improvements in an area with a high level of traffic stress and history of collisions. This 
project is primarily focused on improving safety and connectivity to two public schools accessed from Delaware 
Street, and connecting neighborhoods directly to schools they attend. Further, this project provides strong 
connectivity to the newly relocated Hillsdale Caltrain station from the north and south. Staff received notification 
that the Metropolitan Transportation Commission (MTC) has recommended the project for full funding in the 
Regional ATP Program. Following approval by the California Transportation Commission, anticipated in late June 
2021, the City would be awarded $1.66 million for the design and construction of this project.

MTC Safe and Seamless Mobility Quick-Strike Program: MTC announced a one-time regional competitive call for 
bike/pedestrian and transit connection projects that can be quickly implemented (‘quick-build’). Each County 
Transportation Authority (CTA) was asked to submit a Letter of Interest nominating local projects, and the 
City/County Association of Governments (C/CAG) prepared the competitive process for San Mateo County. City 
staff submitted a letter nominating the Delaware Street project as a quick-build project and was selected by C/CAG 
to be included in the first tier of projects to be recommended to MTC. However, given the project was later 
recommended for regional ATP funding, the quick-strike funding is no longer necessary or applicable for this work. 

Caltrans Sustainable Communities Grant: Caltrans recently opened the FY 2021-22 grant program for the 
Sustainable Transportation Planning Grants. Staff submitted an application for the San Mateo Complete Streets 
Plan, which will serve to bring together the City’s various active transportation plans and Green Infrastructure Plan 
to fully envision a multimodal transportation network for the City. Caltrans has indicated grant award 
announcements will be made in June 2021.

SUBJECT:
2020 Climate Action Plan - Annual Progress Report
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on the implementation of the 2020 Climate Action Plan.
BACKGROUND:
In April 2015, City Council adopted the City’s first Climate Action Plan (CAP). This plan served as the City’s comprehensive 
strategy to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and staff provided annual updates to the Sustainability Commission 
and City Council on CAP implementation. Since 2019, staff engaged the Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission and 
the public in the development of an updated 2020 CAP that establishes new greenhouse gas emission targets for 2030 and 
2050 and identifies creative and innovative new strategies to meet these targets. The City Council adopted the 2020 CAP 
at the April 6, 2020 City Council meeting. This report summarizes the progress made on the implementation of the 2020 
CAP.
The impacts of the COVID-19 lockdown caused a record drop in carbon emissions, but despite the sharp drop in the early 
part of 2020, data shows that global emissions picked up in the second half of the year. Emissions from transportation 
accounted for the largest decline with a drop in car trips and aviation. Compared to 2019, the United States’ emissions 
decreased by 12% and the European Union’s emissions by 11%. However, China’s emissions dropped only 1.7% compared 
to 2019, likely reflective of the more limited duration of their lockdown and their ability to recover more quickly. As global 
economies continue to recover, carbon emissions will also rebound and the City’s CAP efforts continue to be a priority to 
support sustainable and long-term emission reductions in our community.
2020 Climate Action Plan Implementation
The 2020 CAP contains implementation measures in the following categories: building electrification, renewable energy, 
energy efficiency, municipal energy efficiency, clean transportation fuels, sustainable transportation, solid waste, water 
and wastewater, and off-road equipment. Attachment 1 to this report provides both qualitative and quantitative data to 
show how progress has been made on each of the CAP measures and Attachment 2 summarizes available metrics for each 
CAP measure. 
Some key highlights of implementation on the 2020 CAP are as follows:

Building Electrification Reach Codes: The City Council adopted a reach code requiring residential buildings and 
office buildings be constructed all-electric. The reach code also requires that new residential buildings with 100% 
affordable units be all-electric or exceed the State Energy Code. This reach code is effective January 1, 2021 
through December 31, 2022 (the end of the triennial code cycle) and replaces the City’s previously adopted 
building electrification reach codes that allowed two pathways for compliance.


Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance: The City Council adopted the Disposable Food Service Ware Ordinance 
that will require food facilities and restaurants to use compostable products instead of plastic disposables. This 
ordinance goes into effect October 1, 2022. Staff also launched the “Quit the Cutlery” campaign in which 
restaurants pledge to only distribute cutlery, straws, condiment packets, etc. by request of the customer. This 
voluntary effort helps reduce waste as many customers are picking up food or getting delivery to eat at home. 

Sustainable Solutions Turnkey (SST): In 2018, the City entered into a contract with PG&E for the Sustainable 
Solutions Turnkey program to for energy efficiency upgrades at City facilities. The $3.2 million project includes 
upgrades at most facilities including City Hall, libraries, fire stations, and recreation facilities. All on-site work is 
now complete, and the City has begun to pay back one of the two on-bill financing loan packages. The second on-
bill financing package will be finalized soon marking the end of the project work.

Electric Vehicle Infrastructure: Staff leveraged Peninsula Clean Energy’s electric vehicle ready technical assistance 
and applied to the California Electric Vehicle Infrastructure Project (CALeVIP) to install electric vehicle chargers at 
the Main Street Garage and Second and El Camino Garage. The City was awarded funding for the Main Street 
Garage and is currently negotiating a contract with an installer. Funding for the Second and El Camino Garage is 
pending for a future funding cycle, staff hope to install chargers at this site next year. 
As part of the 2020 CAP update, the City completed a 2018 GHG emission inventory. This inventory showed that the City 
has reduced emissions 18% below 2005 levels and well on track to meet and exceed the 2020 GHG target of 15% below 
2005 levels. This progress report includes qualitative and quantitative data points to show the City’s progress on the 
various measures in the CAP. A formal GHG inventory analysis is a data intensive effort and is completed roughly every five 
years.

County’s Bay Front Cleanup initiative where San Mateo residents picked up 362.35 lbs of trash
