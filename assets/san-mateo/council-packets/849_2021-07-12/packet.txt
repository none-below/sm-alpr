RECOMMENDATION:
Review and provide feedback on Project 1 and Project 2 of the Age Friendly City Initiative and discuss the proposed Age 
Friendly Action Plan.
BACKGROUND:
The City of San Mateo has been designated as an Age-Friendly City by the World Health Organization for its commitment 
to being a welcoming community for people of all ages. 
The World Health Organization established the Global Network of Age-Friendly Cities and Communities to connect 
organizations worldwide with the common vision of making their community a great place to grow old in as a response to 
global population aging. It focuses on action at the local level that fosters the full participation of older people in 
community life and promotes healthy and active aging. 
San Mateo received this distinction with the help of an Age-Friendly Community Task Force comprised of primarily older 
adults in the community. The task force, in collaboration with the Senior Commission, is committed to conducting a 
baseline assessment on the needs of older adults, establishing a 3-year action plan to address the identified needs, and 
developing tools to measure progress.

Age-Friendly Cities – San Mateo 
Safe Pedestrian Walking Routes for Seniors - and Everyone 
 
 
Executive Summary 
 
 
Goal:  Identify major concentrations of seniors in the City and the destinations they would most 
likely walk to if conditions were safe.  Prioritize repair of sidewalks and minimize safety hazards 
on specific routes between them to reduce liability risk and encourage walking by seniors for 
health and socialization.  
 
Process for Identifying Major Concentrations of Seniors: Identify  properties with significant 
senior populations and evaluate census data by neighborhood and age groups.  Thirty-two (32) 
properties with 4,840 units satisfied threshold criteria.  Estimated numbers of senior residents in 
each property were obtained from property managers, residents or calculated from reference 
data.  They total approximately 3,200 seniors, about 16% of the total in the City.  There are many 
smaller apartment buildings and condos near these larger ones so the total number and 
percentage of San Mateo seniors in these areas of  concentration is  likely significantly larger. 
 
Process for Identifying Most Likely Destinations: Shopping, medical facilities, government 
offices, parks, places of worship, etc. within approximately 0.5 miles (1 mile round-trip) of each 
property were identified utilizing  Google Maps.  84 unique destinations were selected. 
 
Process for Identifying Walking Routes: Properties and destinations were plotted on Google 
Maps and the shortest connecting routes were drawn and distances captured. Where possible, El 
Camino Real was avoided or minimized.  Common routes were selected where multiple threshold 
properties and/or destinations were in the same vicinity. Close to 400 connecting blocks were 
mapped. 
 
Findings:  The properties and routes identified are in four “zones” anchored by: Downtown (NW), 
Hillsdale Mall (SW),  Norfolk (NE)  and 101 at Hillsdale Ave. (SE),  Per Google Maps, the mapped 
routes total 25 miles or 12.5 % of the City’s 400 miles of streets.  As noted above,  over 16% of the 
City’s seniors live along these routes. More than half (56%) of the seniors in the properties 
identified are in 14 buildings in four neighborhoods.  The data generated support and enable 
prioritizing repairs and improvements in the locations that  provide highest leverage in terms of 
benefiting seniors and demonstrating the City’s Age-Friendly commitment. These data can be 
integrated into and enhance the City’s existing Sidewalk Repair Plan which dates to 2008.

City Public Works Engagement: The project plan, data and findings  were reviewed with Matt 
Zucca of Public Works early on and near completion to assess project support and utility of such 
data. He acknowledged that these data were not previously available and are not currently part of 
the City’s criteria for prioritizing sidewalk repair or other pedestrian safety-related work. The 
current Sidewalk Repair Plan cycles through 15 sections of the city over a 15-year period  with 
priorities based primarily on tree counts.  He agreed our data would add value and committed to 
their inclusion when provided. He confirmed the data collected in both the tabular and Google 
Map formats used would enable integration into the City’s existing systems as is.  
 
Ancillary Issues Not Pursued: The original project plan anticipated  volunteers walking the 
designated routes to identify and categorize hazards and prioritize repairs based on conditions. 
This was not necessary as the City had already engaged a consultant for a project that included, 
within its larger overall scope,  evaluating all of the City’s streets and sidewalks from an ADA 
perspective. These data would be available in a time frame consistent with the completion of our 
data collection and analysis.  We also considered retrieving and analyzing pedestrian accident data 
by location and age group but were told these data were also already being analyzed by Public 
Works and would be integrated by them into the repair or improvement prioritization process.  
 
Recommendations to the City: 
 
1. Formally commit to utilizing these data in setting priorities for sidewalk repair and 
addressing other pedestrian hazards.  
2. Accelerate timing of work on these specific routes  by reprioritizing current schedules or 
increasing funding for this specific need. 
3. Notify Owners of properties on these routes of issues identified in the ADA Report to make 
corrections for which they are financially responsible.  
4. Set up a mechanism for and report progress on sidewalk repairs and work completed on 
these specific routes to the Senior Citizens Commission on at least an annual basis.  
5. Communicate the commitment and progress to the city population at large so interested 
citizens know which routes have been targeted to be more “Age-Friendly” and can 
preferentially use them.  
6. Include commitment to and progress made on this effort in the Age-Friendly Action Plan to 
be submitted to  AARP in 2022.

Major Concentrations of Seniors in the City of San Mateo 
by Property  
 
 
Properties identified and listed include:  
 
(1) Multi-residential condominium or rental apartments of 40 or more units limited to seniors  
(2) Smaller residential condominium or rental apartments limited to seniors that are within 
approximately 0.5 miles from other larger properties included and would share some of 
the same walking routes 
(3) Multi-residential  condominium or rental apartments not limited to seniors but which are 
estimated to have senior populations significantly larger than their representation in the 
city overall  
(4) Large multi-residential condominium or rental apartments not limited to seniors but which 
are estimated to have senior populations approximating their representation in the city 
overall 
 
The rationale for including these specific properties was to identify concentrated populations of 
senior housing and to estimate the total number of seniors living in those properties. That would 
enable a  calculation of the estimated number and percentage of San Mateo seniors that would 
benefit from a targeted sidewalk repair plan that prioritized correction of hazardous conditions on 
identified walking routes from these properties to typical destinations within approximately 0.5 
miles.  Such an effort would help fulfill a commitment made by the City in its Age-Friendly 
certification application 
 
The properties are listed by category above in order of highest to lowest number of units in each. 
 The number of units was obtained either on line or from the respective property manager. The 
 estimated number of senior residents was obtained either from the property manager or, when 
 not available, applying a factor derived from a representative property. In the typical case  
 a factor of 1.5 residents per unit was used for one and two-bedroom units.  For “Studios” and  
“Beds” a factor of 1.0 was used. Calculated numbers were rounded down to allow for some low  
number of vacancies.  
 
In the case of housing not limited to seniors, estimates were based on the best data available 
from property managers,  realtors with knowledge of the properties, current or 
former HOA Board Members and long-time residents.  
 
The accuracy of any individual property’s number of senior residents is not critical as the goal was 
simply to approximate the number of seniors in the aggregate across all included properties and 
compare that to the senior population at large.  Many property managers reported they could not 
provide the demographic data requested due to Fair Housing Law restrictions.  
 
It should be noted that what qualified as a “senior” in the senior-restricted properties varies as 
well, complicating the estimation process.

Additional Comments Supporting the Process for Identifying Senior Concentrations  and 
Population Estimates 
 
Having estimated the number of seniors in each property by the methods described previously, 
the next step involved calculating a total for all the identified properties. This yielded 3,224. This 
represents approximately 16% of the total number of seniors in the City (20,070 > 60 per 2010 
census data.  Had we calculate based on the 15,205 seniors  > 65 in the City, it would be 18%. It is 
likely somewhere in between.   
 
Subsequent plotting of these properties on a map showed that they fell in four “zones”. In three of 
those there are many other smaller condominium and apartment complexes that also house 
representative numbers of seniors.  This is not surprising given zoning and other considerations 
dictating multi-unit residential placement.  While their numbers are not included here, it gives 
reason for confidence that our estimates of senior populations in these areas are likely 
conservative. The consistency of census data of seniors by neighborhood with our targeted zones 
provides additional support for their validity. Those data follow in the next section. 
 
The actual number or percentage is not critical. What matters is the accuracy and validity of the 
zones where safe walking routes would benefit the greatest number of seniors relative to other 
parts of the City.

Census Data/Senior Concentration Validation  
 
As noted in the previous section, we estimated the number of senior residents in each of the 
identified properties and calculated a total of 3,224.  That number is likely conservative given that 
many smaller multi-residential condominium and apartment complexes with senior residents are 
in the same parts of the City as the larger ones captured. 
 
An alternative approach to identifying senior concentrations in the city is to look at census data by 
neighborhood.  2020 census data is not yet available so 2010 data was evaluated for this purpose.  
Since the purpose is to identify concentrations rather than absolute numbers, we expect 2010 data 
to be valid as senior concentration locations are not expected to have changed appreciably, if at all, 
over the past ten years.  That conclusion is based on: 
 
• The largest growth in the City’s population is in the new Bay Meadows neighborhood.  
There is no reason to believe that the senior population in that neighborhood is higher than 
in the general population as there are no major senior-only condo or apartment buildings 
in that development.  
• Even if there were higher than representative senior populations in this area, it would not 
affect the recommendations of the project because all of the sidewalks in this area would be 
in good condition,  given their newness, and would not need to be given priority attention 
for repair.  
• All senior population data on the 32 properties identified are based on 2020 counts and 
fixed building capacities so those data are constant and valid independent of census data.  
 
 
Issues: 
 
Senior definition: It must be emphasized that the goal of estimated senior counts either 
from calculated  building populations or extracted census data is to support an impact 
assessment of benefits to be gained. by focusing repairs on specific walking routes.  A 
difficulty encountered is that the definition of “seniors” varies in both data sets. Some 
properties are restricted to seniors 55 and up, others have different criteria. We accepted

Destinations within Walking Distance of Senior Concentrations 
 
 
Distance – A  walking distance of approximately 0.5 miles from the designated properties meeting 
threshold criteria was arbitrarily set.  This choice was considered reasonable as the large majority 
of seniors of varying physical conditions and abilities would be able to manage this at their own 
pace in 10 – 20 minutes, given that the residential properties and destinations involved, with few 
exceptions, are almost entirely on level ground.  The opportunity to rest or take care of business at 
endpoint destinations would enable an equivalent return trip that would constitute about a one-
mile roundtrip, an appealing recreational and fitness goal.   This would be doable even carrying a 
shopping bag. 
 
Destination types – Destinations identified were considered with senior-specific business and 
recreational needs in mind. They included medical facilities, food markets and pharmacies, 
government facilities such as post offices and libraries, senior centers and parks, places of 
worship,  shopping centers and banks, and train stations and other larger senior residential 
buildings where friends may live.  In some cases, one or more of these destinations slightly outside 
the 0.5 mile range (no more than 0.6 miles) was included because of its significance with the 
expectation that a significant number of seniors could manage that and the desire to encourage 
walking rather than driving to such destinations.    
 
The destinations selected were identified by  expanding Google maps around each residential 
property and checking distances to each of the targeted destination types appearing on the map.   
A total of 84 unique destinations were identified, with the large majority of them within 0.5 miles 
of multiple residential properties, especially those in the NW (downtown-anchored) grouping.

Mapping of Senior Concentrations to Target Destinations 
within approximately 0.5 miles 
 
 
 
 
Designated properties and target walking destinations within approximately 0.5 miles from each 
were plotted using the route mapping tool in Google Maps.  Considerations included 
 
1. Most direct route 
2. Avoidance of El Camino Real where the same destination can be reached within the approximately 
0.5 mile limit 
3. Avoidance of blocks where there are no sidewalks such as the block of Claremont between E. 5th 
Avenue and the Social Security Administration Office 
4. Using routes already plotted from other nearby properties and/or destinations

Summary:  
 
The project’s “Age-Friendly” intent is for the City to prioritize sidewalk repairs and other 
pedestrian improvements on the full set of walking routes from all senior population 
 concentrations identified. As a practical matter, the City should consider prioritizing within that 
set of routes  those where the highest leverage benefits can be achieved in terms of number of not 
only seniors impacted per mile of sidewalks repaired, but all pedestrian traffic. 
 
Short of specific studies designed to measure pedestrian traffic, one can extrapolate from exiting 
data the highest leverage walking routes.  Examining the fourteen properties in the four 
neighborhoods on page 14 is a reasonable starting point.   Not all seniors in these properties walk 
the routes ultimately identified, or currently walk at all, but that is true of seniors in 
neighborhoods of strictly single-family housing as well.  Additionally, it is reasonable to assume 
that visits to seniors by friends, family members, caregivers, service people, contractors, etc. result 
in higher walking traffic on these sidewalks and extend the benefits of improving sidewalk 
conditions preferentially in these target areas. These neighborhoods are home to major shopping 
districts and commercial businesses that draw large amounts of pedestrian traffic of all 
demographics and prioritization would therefore support the “…and for EVERYONE” project 
scope.  
 
25th Avenue is presumably targeted for repairs this year and the Central Business District in two 
years so acceleration may not have a major negative impact on other parts of the City if these were 
accelerated.

Current Programs and Services

Does your department have any current services, programs or projects that are

What information regarding seniors would be helpful for your department when
