Reducing the Impact Fee Burden on Affordable Housing – Policy Options Review

fee burden on affordable housing projects.  He requested direction for staff on exploring possible incentive 
programs for affordable housing. Council asked questions of staff. Public Comment – there were no speakers.

the 100% Affordable Projects. It is assumed because the City makes contributions to 100% affordable projects, 
that as part of the process all the tools would be examined and use of the ones that make the most sense for that 
particular 100% affordable project would be used.

PUBLIC HEARING
1.
Transition to District-Based Elections for Council Members – Public Hearing #2
City Manager Drew Corbett and City Attorney Prasanna Rasiah presented an overview on the history, public 
hearing and community outreach meetings undertaken to date to move to by-district election of council members. 
Rasiah outlined the options under discussion and the affiliated charter amendments that would be needed. Paul 
Mitchell, Redistricting Partners, and Chris Chaffee, consulting demographers, presented the rules around 
districting and summarized public input received to date. Council asked questions of staff and the consultant. 
Public Comment:  Rich Hedges stated he is in favor of six council seats and an at large mayor and advocated for a 
district encompassing Mariner’s Island. Karim Sarraf asked questions regarding the process and encouraged 
promoting diversity once districts are drafted. Lisa Nash supported maintaining five districts and rotating mayor. 
Scott Rafferty commended the City did an excellent job on the website, commended the demographer, and noted 
he was surprised by how serious the charter amendment discussion is. Jordan Grimes stated he is a strong 
proponent of district elections and encouraged focus on mapping the districts. George Derby advocated for a 
district for San Mateo Creek East end and asked how upcoming significant changes to neighborhoods are factored 
into the map drawing. 
Staff and the consultant responded to the public’s questions. 
Council discussion ensued on issues with the proposed options of: A) five districts with a rotational mayor; 
B) increased council seats and/or elective mayor effective November 2024; and C) increase in number of council 
seats and/or elective mayor in November 2024 and retain current at-large system for November 2022. Preferences 
for directly elected versus rotational mayor systems were expressed, as well as thoughts around increasing the 
number of council seats. After each council member shared their views, much deliberation occurred with direction 
given to stay at five districts with a rotational mayor as there is time to make more deliberative adjustments to the 
election system in the future; with more time to solicit additional data and citizen input without being under the 
constraint of the California Voting Rights Act timing requirements; concern with limiting voter confusion with too 
many changes at once; and the desire to minimize the potential for litigation. It was noted the third public hearing 
on this item is scheduled for Thursday, September 30, 2021, at 6:00 pm.

ADDING CHAPTER 11.29, “PEDESTRIAN MALLS,” TO TITLE 11, “VEHICLES AND TRAFFIC,” OF THE SAN MATEO

WHEREAS, the City Council of the City of San Mateo has adopted a resolution establishing two 
pedestrian malls on segments of B Street, between 1st and 2nd Avenues and between 2nd and 3rd Avenues, 
while still maintaining through east-west traffic on 1st, 2nd, and 3rd Avenues, pursuant to the Pedestrian Mall 
Act of 1960 (California Streets and Highways Code Section 11000 et seq); and
WHEREAS, the resolution will go into effect as of the effective date of this ordinance; and
WHEREAS, the City wishes to amend the Municipal Code to add a new chapter establishing rules and 
regulations prohibiting motorized vehicular traffic and governing the use of the pedestrian malls.
NOW, THEREFORE, THE COUNCIL OF THE CITY OF SAN MATEO ORDAINS AS FOLLOWS:
Section 1. Chapter 11.29 “Pedestrian Malls” of the San Mateo Municipal Code is added as follows:
11.29.010 Adoption and Purpose
This chapter shall be known and may be cited as the "Pedestrian Malls Ordinance." The purpose of this 
chapter is to establish rules and regulations pertaining to the operation of pedestrian malls.
11.29.020 Definitions
Pedestrian Malls means streets, or portions thereof, on which vehicle traffic is restricted in whole or in 
part and which is to be used exclusively or primarily for pedestrian travel.
Motorized Vehicular Traffic means vehicular traffic powered by motor, including without limitation, 
automobiles, trucks, buses, motorcycles, motor or e-scooters.
11.29.030 Boundaries
Pedestrian malls are established along the following boundaries, excluding the east-west traffic on the 
intersecting streets:
(a) B Street between 1st and 2nd Avenues.
(b) B Street between 2nd and 3rd Avenues.
11.29.040 Prohibition of Vehicular Traffic
All motorized vehicular traffic is prohibited on a pedestrian mall subject to the following exceptions:
(a) Emergency vehicles and equipment of all types;

RECOMMENDATION:
Approve Amendment No. 2 to the agreement with WRA, Inc. for additional scope of work in the amount of $99,360 for a 
new agreement total of $186,110, and authorize the Public Works Director to execute the amendment in substantially the 
form presented. 
BACKGROUND:
The Poplar at Golf Course Trash Capture Project is part of the City’s plan to achieve compliance with the San Francisco Bay 
Regional Water Quality Control Board’s (RWQCB) Municipal Regional Stormwater Permit’s trash capture requirements. 
The proposed location is within an existing storm drain channel and performing the work requires significant 
environmental documentation and permitting from regulatory agencies, including a Section 404 permit from the United 
States Army Corps of Engineers, a Section 401 permit from the RWQCB, and Lake or Streambed Alteration Agreement from 
the California Department of Fish and Wildlife. 
In August 2020 an agreement was executed with WRA, Inc. for these environmental services in the amount of $79,750. 
WRA's scope of work includes analysis and documentation under the California Environmental Quality Act, cultural 
resources assessment report, biological resources assessment report, aquatic resources delineation map and data sheets, 
San Francisco Bay Conservation and Development Commission jurisdiction analysis, and regulatory agency permit 
applications. Amendment No. 1 was issued for additional biological and environmental permitting work for an amount of 
$7,000. 
Amendment No. 2 is to perform a formal cultural resources evaluation that was not anticipated to be necessary as part of 
the original scope of work and to provide additional, ongoing services during and after construction.  The additional scope 
of work includes a mitigation and monitoring plan required by the RWQCB for the development of a compensatory 
mitigation area consisting of planted native vegetation within the Poplar Creek Golf Course.  The additional scope of 
services also includes nesting bird surveys, biological monitoring during construction, and three years of annual monitoring 
and reporting of the mitigation area.  Staff recommends approving Amendment No. 2 to the agreement as the additional 
scope of work is required to proceed with the project. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
Sufficient funds are available in the Poplar at Golf Course Trash Capture Project.

This Agreement is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative activity that will not

Adopt a Resolution finding that competitive bidding would be impracticable and unavailing; approve a contract to 
PlayCore, Wisconsin Inc, DBA Gametime for purchase of playground equipment for the Borel Park Development Project in 
the amount of $199,920.29; and authorize the Director of the Parks and Recreation Department to execute the contract in 
substantially the form presented.
BACKGROUND:
Chapter 3.60 of the San Mateo Municipal Code provides that, as a general rule, contracts valued at $100,000 or more are 
to be competitively bid.  San Mateo Municipal Code Section 3.60.050(b) provides that an exception to the City’s 
competitive bidding requirement may be made if the City Council adopts a resolution finding that competitive bidding 
would be impracticable or unavailing.  In this instance, competitive bidding would be impracticable or unavailing because 
staff recommends the purchase of a custom design, which is available from only one vendor.
The price is comparable to prices for similar playground equipment available from other vendors.  In addition, the Parks 
and Recreation Department is recommending that the City purchase the playground equipment for the Borel Park 
Development Project directly from the supplier rather than having the awarded construction contract company, Andreini 
Bros Inc purchase at a mark up cost to the City. During the design development phase, several playground designs from 
various vendors were presented to the public and Park and Recreation Commission for review and comment, and the 
design from Gametime appeared to be the one most preferred.    Because the play equipment elements are proprietary in 
nature, it is not possible or viable to bid this purchase as all other companies only offer their own designs. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There are sufficient funds in the Borel Park Development Project to cover the purchase of this equipment. 
ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINATION:
This agenda item is not a project subject to CEQA, because it is an organizational or administrative activity that will not 
result in direct or indirect physical changes in the environment. (CEQA Guidelines Section 15378(b)(5).)  In addition, the 
environmental impacts of the Borel Park Development Project were previously analyzed in a mitigated negative 
declaration.

SUBJECT:
4th and S. Railroad Avenue Parking Lot – Exclusive Negotiating Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Approve an Exclusive Negotiating Agreement with DTSM Housing, LLC (a limited liability company formed by Prometheus, 
Harvest Properties, and Alta Housing) to explore the development of affordable housing on the City-owned parking lot 
located at East 4th Avenue and South Railroad Avenue and authorize the City Manager to execute it in substantially the 
form presented.
BACKGROUND:
The City Council approved a resolution declaring the surface parking lot at East 4th Avenue and South Railroad Avenue, 
known as the Talbot’s parking lot, as “Surplus Land” at the January 4, 2021 City Council meeting. On January 7, 2021, staff 
released a Notice of Surplus Lands Availability to all housing developers that have registered with the State Department of 
Housing and Community Development (HCD). In response to this notice, the City received four proposals for the 
development of affordable housing on the site. After going through an evaluation process, on July 19, 2021, the City 
Council directed staff to negotiate an Exclusive Negotiating Agreement (ENA) with the Prometheus/Harvest 
Properties/Alta Housing team to explore the redevelopment of the Talbot’s parking lot site. 
The Prometheus/Harvest Properties/Alta Housing team proposes to develop the Talbot’s parking lot property in 
conjunction with the adjacent Talbot’s toy store property. The proposal is for a mixed-use development that includes 
118,000 square feet of commercial development on the Talbot’s toy store property, affordable housing on the City’s 
property, and shared parking underneath both sites. The project includes 52 to 54 affordable housing units ranging in size 
from studios up to 3-bedrooms. The housing units would be targeted to households earning between 30% to 80% of Area 
Median Income.  The proposal also includes 5,000 square feet of ground-floor space dedicated for the Self-Help for the 
Elderly non-profit organization.  The development team is negotiating to purchase the other two properties on the block, 
which would result in the project being adjusted if additional properties are included.
The development proposal requires that the City contribute the land for the affordable housing, in the form of a long-term 
ground lease. In addition, it is anticipated that the City will need to provide $2 million from our affordable housing funds to 
assist with financing the development. It is anticipated that the development team will also seek out other sources of 
funds, including State and County sources, as well as federal affordable housing tax credits, to finance the affordable 
housing component of the project.
The Prometheus/Harvest Properties/Alta Housing team formed a limited liability company, DTSM Housing, LLC, for the 
purpose of implementing their proposed project. The ENA with DTSM Housing, LLC commits the City to not explore other 
development opportunities for the property during the time that the development team is pursuing project entitlements 
and negotiating the deal terms for a future Disposition and Development Agreement (DDA). The ENA commits the

SUBJECT:
Classification Updates – Finance Department
RECOMMENDATION:
Adopt a Resolution to approve the 1) job specification and salary ranges for the classification of Deputy Director of 
Finance; 2) reallocation of the vacant Budget Manager position to Deputy Director of Finance; 3) reallocation of the 
Management Analyst II position to Senior Management Analyst; 4) abolishment of the classifications of Budget Manager 
and Treasury & Debt Manager; and 5) amendments to the 2021-22 Position Budget for the Finance Department.
BACKGROUND:
Per San Mateo Municipal Code Section 2.57.030(d), classification revisions become effective upon approval by the City 
Council.
The proposed reorganization for the Finance Department will provide the resources needed to 1) provide growth and 
employee retention opportunities within the department 2) provide for long-term service level sustainability, and 3) 
support the multi-year process of replacing the City’s enterprise resource planning system. 
Since December 2019, the Finance Department has experienced significant staff turnover. Of the 19.0 full time equivalent 
(FTE) employees that were included in the 2019-20 Position Budget, 3 employees have retired (one additional employee 
will retire in December), and 3.0 FTE found promotional opportunities outside of the City. The primary reorganizational 
request is to reallocate the vacant Budget Manager position to Deputy Director of Finance, abolish the classifications of 
the Treasury & Debt Manager and Budget Manager and instead, recruit for a Deputy Finance Director. Based on the City’s 
experience with recruitment for comparable internal and external job classifications, it will be difficult to attract and retain 
candidates with the desired experience and expertise under the current organizational structure. The Deputy Finance 
Director will oversee the Budget, Payroll, and Treasury divisions. 
With significant staff turnover in the department, the residual work has been absorbed by the remaining employees. It is 
through their hard work and dedication that the Finance Department’s service level has been maintained, but the 
additional workload is not sustainable in the long run. The other primary provision of the department reorganization is the 
reallocation of the Management Analyst II to Senior Management Analyst. This change will provide stability to the Payroll 
and Treasury Divisions, as the Management Analyst II splits their duties between the Payroll and Treasury Division, and 
both divisions need more dedicated resources for long-term success. The proposed reallocation will shift the payroll 
responsibilities to the Payroll Technician and Accounting Assistant, and the Senior Management Analyst will supervise the 
Treasury Division Accounting Assistants and focus on the City’s investment and debt portfolios.

BUDGET IMPACT:
The proposed organizational changes in the Finance Department will result in a net 1.0 FTE reduction to the overall 
department headcount and result in a net cost reduction of approximately $82,000 to the General Fund. While this net 
reduction will be realized in the long term, additional temporary staffing resources will be requested in the near future to 
support implementation of the new enterprise resource planning system.

position to Senior Management Analyst becomes effective after a City-only promotional exam is conducted to fill the

SUBJECT:
COVID-19 Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on operational impacts and other City efforts related to COVID-19 and confirm rules and regulations 
issued by the City Manager acting as the Director of Emergency Services in response to declared emergency.
BACKGROUND:
On March 4, 2020, the Governor proclaimed a state of emergency in response to the outbreak of respiratory illness due to 
a novel coronavirus now referred to as COVID-19. As a consequence of this proclamation the City Manager, acting as the 
City’s Director of Emergency Services, was authorized to make and issue rules and regulations on matters reasonably 
related to the protection of life and property as affected by such emergency. 
In accordance with Government Code Section 8558(c), the City Council declared a local health emergency on March 16, 
2020. Per Government Code Section 8630(c), the City Council is required to review the need for continuing the local 
emergency at least once every 60 days until the City Council terminates the declaration of local emergency.  The current 
local emergency proclamation is in effect through the month of October.  In addition to positioning the City for potential 
mutual aid and cost recovery opportunities, the emergency proclamation also provides the City Manager broader 
authority to be able to expeditiously address issues related to the emergency. With that said, actions taken by the City 
Manager outside of the general authority of the duties provided for in the City Charter are subject to confirmation by 
Council as soon as practicable. 
State and local emergency proclamations are still in effect, and as of June 15th, the State transitioned into its “Beyond the 
Blueprint” framework that removed many of the restrictions that had been in place under the “Blueprint for a Safer 
Economy” framework. However, with COVID-19 cases rising after the transition into the “Beyond the Blueprint” 
framework, the County of San Mateo has joined most other Bay Area counties in issuing new health orders requiring that 
all people, regardless of vaccination status, wear face coverings in all indoor public settings, with limited exceptions. This 
new health order has been in effect since August 3rd. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for receiving this informational report.

SUBJECT:
2020-21 Community Development Block Grant Project – Agreement
RECOMMENDATION:
Award an agreement to McGuire and Hester for the 2020-21 Community Development Block Grant Project in the amount 
of $1,668,271; establish a contingency reserve in the amount of $170,000; and authorize the Public Works Director to 
execute the agreement in substantially the form presented and issue change orders as allowed by the contract documents 
and within the amount of the contingency reserve.
BACKGROUND:
The City receives federal funding from the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development for the Community 
Development Block Grant (CDBG) program and allocates the funds based on community priorities. A portion of the funds 
are available for infrastructure improvements, including paving, bicycle, and pedestrian projects. This year, the 2020-21 
CDBG Project (Project) covers 2.3 miles of pavement rehabilitation, installation of curb ramps, Class II bicycle lanes and 
bicycle boulevards, traffic striping, roadway signage, and traffic signal video detection equipment in the North Central 
neighborhood. The bicycle improvements are from the adopted 2020 Bicycle Master Plan (the Plan) and include Humboldt 
Street bicycle lanes (2nd highest priority project in the Plan) and the Poplar Avenue/Delaware Street/Claremont Street 
bicycle boulevard and lanes (4th highest priority project).  
As detailed below, the proposed bicycle improvements would result in the loss of parking.  Staff recommends awarding 
the contract to McGuire and Hester so necessary pavement and concrete improvements can be implemented and ensure 
timely use of the grant funds. Based on feedback from the Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission (SIC) and the 
public, staff will concurrently implement the construction work while conducting additional parking analyses, evaluating 
potential additional parking programs, and conducting more community outreach. As the bicycle improvement portions of 
the Project will not occur until spring or summer of 2022 based on the estimated project schedule, staff will return to 
Council and SIC with the results of these efforts before proceeding with the actual construction work.  
Design and Parking Analysis
Staff evaluated the Project design options for implementing the Class II striped bicycle lanes on both Humboldt Street and 
Poplar Avenue, which are both narrow. Removal of approximately 214 on-street parking spaces on one side of the 
roadway on Humboldt Street generally from Peninsula Avenue to 5th Avenue and on Poplar Avenue from El Camino Real to 
Delaware Street is required to provide sufficient space within the existing roadway width to install the bicycle lanes.
As part of the design, staff engaged a third-party data collection firm, IDAX Data Solutions, to conduct parking analyses on 
Humboldt Street, Poplar Avenue, and the surrounding side streets to fully assess the proposed parking removal. IDAX has 
specific experience conducting parking occupancy studies in similar types of neighborhoods for other jurisdictions and in 
evaluating the impact of parking loss due to the installation of bicycle lanes. The data collection effort included 
determining the full amount of available parking spaces on-street on the Project roadways, and the existing parking usage 
128 of 173

--- page 129 ---
CITY OF SAN MATEO
on both a weekday and a weekend day (7a.m.-7p.m). The data collection effort also included 200 feet on all side streets 
that connect to the Project’s corridors. 
Based on the data collection, staff concluded that with the implementation of the Project, parking will at times be fully 
occupied along the corridor but that parking capacity is available on the side streets in the vicinity to accommodate the 
parking removal planned during most hours of the day. While capacity is expected to be available in the vicinity, 
sometimes parking may be at a distance greater than 200 feet from residents’ homes. 
It is important to note that alternate bicycle network alignments were also evaluated and staff concluded that other 
options would have resulted in approximately the same or greater loss in available parking when compared to the 
proposed alignment. In addition, the proposed alignment offered greater connectivity, improving the bicycle network 
altogether. 
Community Outreach
During the Project design effort, staff made a presentation to the SIC in May 2021 as part of an update on bicycle and 
pedestrian projects. At that time, it was estimated that the Project would result in the loss of 173 on-street parking spaces. 
Staff began outreach in the neighborhood in May with a virtual community meeting, communicated information about the 
meeting via mailed postcards to residents on Poplar Avenue and Humboldt Street, postings on Nextdoor, and direct 
outreach with the Home Association of North Central San Mateo. Staff developed a Project website, which currently 
contains Project information, maps, details on the planned parking removal, video of the community meeting, and 
frequently asked questions about the Project. 
In July, Public Works crews installed 100 signs on light poles and street signs along Poplar Avenue and Humboldt Street to 
directly reach residents, visitors, and others who currently park on roadways where parking will be removed. The signs 
contain a QR code that links to the Project website, which can be translated and read in multiple languages. Since the signs 
were posted, Public Works has seen more than 400 visitors to the Project website and have received comments from 11 
residents via phone or email. Most people contacting Public Works in response to the signs were opposed to the Project 
due to the loss of parking.
Staff also sent a mailer to approximately 3,000 households in a wide area surrounding the Project to inform residents of 
the proposed contract award at the October 4th City Council meeting.  During the five days after the mailer was delivered, 
there were approximately 90 unique visits to the Project webpage with fewer than half the visitors from San Mateo 
locations.  Staff received three resident inquiries because of the mailer expressing mixed opinions about the objectives of 
the Project with the primary concern about parking loss.
Sustainability and Infrastructure Commission Feedback
In September, staff presented on the Project to the SIC during the recurring updates on bicycle and pedestrian projects.  
The SIC supported the Project, but there were concerns expressed during the meeting about: (1) whether the parking 
occupancy studies performed to date adequately captured the Project’s impact to the community; (2) whether additional 
programs can be implemented to help alleviate the loss of parking; and (3) whether outreach conducted was sufficient to 
reach the impacted residents.  Additionally, some public comment expressed concerns about non-resident vehicles parking 
on City streets and using ride share to access the San Francisco Airport.  
Staff requested a proposal from IDAX to perform a more extensive parking occupancy study to confirm the initial results. 
Due to lack of availability, the consultant cannot start work until early November and the results will not be available until 
the end of the year. However, the parking analysis conducted by IDAX during the design followed practices used in other 
cities and in similar neighborhoods. 
Staff is also evaluating other potential enforcement and programmatic efforts that may assist with alleviating parking loss.  
San Mateo Municipal Code Section 11.40.120(b)(1) and Vehicle Code Section 22651(k) provide for the removal of a vehicle 
from a city street if parked or left standing for more than seventy-two (72) hours. Following implementation of this 
Project, additional parking enforcement may be requested to address non-resident parking and abandoned or commercial

impact from non-resident vehicles.  
Another option is the use of shared parking. Staff is in the process of identifying parking lots in the neighborhood that can 
be used overnight. Potentially viable options include surface lots at the nearby schools, churches, and City parks. 
Agreements for the use of these parking lots would need to be developed.
With respect to public engagement on this Project, there were 17 participants in the community meeting and 77 views of 
the recorded presentation as well as over 500 unique visits to the Project website. However, staff will also evaluate other 
methods of reaching the community to discuss the Project and evaluate potential community effects.  Additional measures 
could include working with local community organizations on additional outreach as well community “pop-up” events 
within the neighborhood.
Project Bidding
The 2020-21 CDBG Project covers 2.3 miles of street and includes pavement rehabilitation and installation of curb ramps, 
Class 2 bicycle lanes and bicycle boulevards, traffic striping, roadway signage, and traffic signal video detection equipment.

The Project will be funded from CDBG funds and Pavement Management Plan funds. CDBG funds are subject to minimum

deadline.
Recommendation
Based on feedback received from the public and from SIC, staff will conduct a more expansive parking study to reaffirm 
previous results.  Potential programs to help alleviate the loss of parking resulting from Project implementation (e.g., 
arrangements for night parking on private lots and/or an RPPP zone implementation) are also being evaluated.  To allow 
time for these efforts and to meet the minimum expenditure requirements on CDBG funding, staff recommends awarding 
the contract to McGuire and Hester so necessary pavement and concrete improvements can be implemented.  Staff will 
concurrently implement the construction work while assessing the findings of the additional parking study, evaluating 
potential additional parking programs, and conducting more community outreach. As the bicycle improvement portions of 
the Project would not occur until spring or summer of 2022 based on the estimated project schedule, staff would return to 
Council and SIC with the results of these efforts before proceeding with the actual construction work.

North Central, and the best suited for a bike lane, but it's currently very dangerous for bikers. As a result most bikers ride

driveways. My family lives two blocks from the proposed bike lane and relies heavily on biking to get around. Our route

completed. We should bias towards action, particularly when addressing issues of public safety, and not get caught in 
endless cycles of plans, studies, and feedback.

Nextdoor surrounding this project (1, 2) and there are many residents of North Central (and N Humboldt) who support

•

• 
Implementing a residential permit program (and enforcing it!) to disincentive storing cars on the street 
•

• 
Partnering with a company like 1-800-gotjunk to help clean out residents' garages and driveways
