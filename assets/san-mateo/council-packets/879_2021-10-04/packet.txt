SUBJECT:
City Council Strategic Plan – Mid-Cycle Updates
RECOMMENDATION:
Review mid-cycle status updates of City Council Strategic Plans for fiscal years 2020-21 and 2021-22 and review and 
approve possible revisions to the Strategic Plan for fiscal year 2021-22. 
BACKGROUND:
The City Council goal setting process includes a three-step deliberation resulting in the adoption of a Strategic Plan that 
allows for multi-year resource planning. The first step of the process was a City Council workshop, held on January 30, 
2021. At this workshop, the City Council reviewed the status of items on the adopted fiscal year 2020-21 Strategic Plan and 
developed a proposed Strategic Plan for fiscal year 2021-22. At the March 15th meeting, City Council reviewed the draft 
Strategic Plan to ensure accuracy. City Council formally adopted the fiscal year 2021-22 Strategic Plan at the April 5th 
meeting.
Staff is providing a mid-cycle review of the strategic plans from both fiscal years 2020-21 and 2021-22 and will present the 
updates to City Council. This report provides a high-level summary of the key changes that have occurred since the April 
Strategic Plan adoption.  Summaries of the current status of each objective are included in the Strategic Plan documents 
attached to this report. The highlighted lines indicate planned deliverables that are currently delayed, on-hold, or shifted. 
Additionally, staff is requesting City Council to discuss the unforeseen new work plan items impacting staff resources. 
City Council Strategic Plan Changes
Many of the work plan items remained on track and were successfully executed. However, a few deliverables were 
delayed, on-hold, or shifted from both Strategic Plan Fiscal Year 2020-21 and Fiscal Year 2021-22. Details of each of these 
items are highlighted in the attachments. 
Delayed, but ongoing: 

Minimize impacts to residents from train horn noise at City at-grade crossings (FY 2020-21)

Develop an anti-littering trash and cigarette butt outreach campaign (FY 2020-21 and FY 2021-22)

Implement Library Space Master Plan (FY 2020-21)

Implement the Central Park Master Plan (FY 2020-21)
On-hold:

Create a racial equity community art project (FY 2020-21 and FY 2021-22)

Council Requested:

Evaluate and Analyze Additional Renter Protection Laws.  On September 7, Councilmembers expressed interest in 
exploring whether the City could (1) apply just cause eviction requirements upon the commencement of a 
tenancy, (2) require a permit before an eviction in connection with a substantial remodel, and (3) create a rent 
registry.  Doing so would require a careful analysis of State law, including whether such regulations were 
preempted, as well as what other local jurisdictions were doing.  It would also likely require redirecting some City 
Attorney’s Office resources from other anticipated or ongoing efforts, such as electronic billboard lease 
negotiations, updates to the sign ordinance, Zoning code changes related to streamlining development processes 
and other cleanup measures, and assistance with the General Plan update.  
Staff recommends discussing whether to add these items to the Strategic Plan for fiscal year 2021-22 as new objectives.

BACKGROUND:
Due to the the City's pre-existing structural deficit and the severe and immediate financial impact of COVID-19, the 
General Fund long-term financial plan anticipates a $30 million draw down on the Rainy Day Reserve, from a peak of $54 
million in 2018-19 to trough of $24 million in 2026-27. In response to the financial impact of the pandemic, the City has 
implemented its Fiscal Sustainability Plan to reduce the cost of General Fund operations, which principally included 
strategic and permanent elimination of $1.0 million in vacant positions and $3.0 million in employee compensation 
concessions, the latter of which the City has made significant progress: 

Department Directors and Deputy Department Directors implemented compensation concessions; 

Agreements are in place with the San Mateo City Employees' Association and San Mateo Management Association 
that largely achieved the targeted concessions; and 

Tentative agreement with the Service Employees International Union (SEIU) Local 521 Maintenance Unit has been 
reached and the proposed memorandum of understanding is before the City Council for consideration on a 
separate agenda item that also largely achieved the targeted concessions.
However, as noted earlier, even with the Fiscal Sustainability Plan coming to fruition in full and incorporated into the 
General Fund Long-Term Financial Plan, the Rainy Day Reserve is projected to draw down by $30 million. The projected 
annual loss, including net reductions and the difference between resources (projected recurring revenues) and 
requirements (projected recurring expenditures) is approximately $7.0 million. As such, in order to improve the City's long-
term fiscal position, this study session will evaluate the impact of potential revenue enhancement options. 
Real Property Document Transfer Tax 
As a charter city, San Mateo has a real property document transfer tax (RPTT) rate of 0.5% in place, as codified by Section 
3.48 of the San Mateo Municipal Code and Ordinance No. 1977-9. RPTT is the City's most volatile and difficult to project 
tax revenue source, as it is susceptible economic ebb and flow. Prior to the Great Recession, the City accounted for $10.4 
million in RPTT in 2006-07. By 2008-09, RPTT dropped to $3.3 million, and did not recover to pre-Great Recession levels 
until 2013-14. The City accounted for an historic high of $13.9 million in RPTT in 2018-19, but dropped to $9.1 million in 
2019-20, the first year that accounted for the impact of COVID-19 (albeit only for the last quarter of the fiscal year). In 
2020-21, the General Fund accounted for $11.8 million in RPTT.

Transactions and Use Tax
San Mateo voters approved Measure S in November 2015, which extended Measure L, a 1/4 cent transactions and use tax, 
also known as a district or local add-on sales tax. With the passage of the Measure RR in November 2020 to support 
Caltrain operations, the current sales tax rate in the City of San Mateo is 9.625%. The State of California imposes a limit of 
2% above the statewide base sales tax rate, thus the City has 1/4 cent of capacity left before reaching the limit. If a 
revenue ballot measure for an additional 1/4 cent transactions and use tax were to be passed by San Mateo voters, the 
impact would equivalent to Measure S. Measure S is projected to generate $6.1 million in 2021-22, and as the local 
economy recovers from the pandemic-driven recession, increase to $7.5 million by 2026-27. 
Of the 379 transactions and use tax ballot tax measures from California cities and counties between 2002-2020 that were 
structured as general taxes that were new or increased the sales tax rate, 289 passed and 90 failed, a pass rate of over 
76%.
Utility Users Tax
Pursuant to Article XI §5 of the California Constitution, a utility users tax (UUT) may be imposed by a city on the 
consumption of utility services, including (but not limited to) electricity, gas, water, sewer, telephone (including cell phone 
and long distance), sanitation, and cable television. The tax would be levied by the City, collected by each utility as a part 
of its regular billing processes, and remitted to the City. As an approximation of the potential revenue impact of a UUT, 
Finance Department staff analyzed utility consumption metrics from the utility providers and applied a UUT rate of 5% for 
natural gas and electricity, and 4% for telecommunication and cable television services, which is the rate in place in the 
City of Redwood City. Based on the utility consumption by the San Mateo community between April 2020 through March 
2021, passage of a UUT ballot measure would generate approximately $5.9 million in UUT revenues per year. If the City 
exempted public agencies and PG&E customers enrolled in the California Alternate Rates for Energy (CARE), the additional 
UUT would be approximately $5.4 million.
The Council could also consider including language in a UUT ordinance to assess the UUT on streaming services. The City of 
Chicago, Illinois was the first major city in the United States to tax streaming services. According to an Urban-Brookings Tax 
Policy Center analysis, 33 states tax online entertainment through their sales tax. However, in California, services are 
exempt from sales tax. As such, some California cities are considering amending their UUT ordinances to include streaming 
services. For the City of San Mateo, the shift in consumer behavior to receive television entertainment from cable to 
streaming services has manifest via a 12% reduction in cable franchise fees from the peak in 2016-17 to 2020-21. Including

behavior. The projected potential revenue from UUT above does not reflect the UUT being subject to streaming services,

UUT also does not fare well at the ballot box in California. Of the 85 UUT ballot measures that were structured as a general

Business Tax
The City has over 10,000 active businesses, both including businesses that are located within and outside of the City. The 
City's current business tax rates, which are largely based on gross receipts, have been in place since 1977 via Ordinance 
No. 1977-20. Of the 10,000 active businesses, 75% pay a gross receipts-based business tax, and the remaining 25% pay a 
flat rate. In 2020-21, the General Fund accounted for $6.3 million in business tax revenues. 
For businesses making $30,000 or less in gross receipts per year, the current business tax is $31 per year for a new 
business, and $25 for a renewal. The gross receipts tax table has progressive tiers in $5,000 increments up to $100,000. 
Businesses that generate greater than $100,000 in gross receipts pay a base tax of $143.50 for the first $100,000 in gross 
receipts, plus $3.60 for each $5,000 or fraction thereof, thereafter. Renewing businesses pay a base tax of $115 for the 
first $100,000 in gross receipts, plus $2.85 for each $5,000 or fraction thereof, thereafter. These bookend gross receipt-
based business tax tiers represent the majority of businesses in San Mateo - small businesses (18.6%) and larger 
companies (20.8%).
If the City were to increase its business tax by rounding the current gross receipts-based tax rates up to the nearest $5 or 
$10 increment (i.e. for the first gross receipts business tax tier, the tax rate for a new business would increase from $31 to 
$35, and a renewing business’ tax rate would increase from $25 to $30), business tax revenues would increase by 
approximately $0.9 million.
Of the 29 business tax ballot measures from California cities that were for general revision purposes between 2002 – 2020, 
25 passed and 4 failed, a pass rate of over 86%.
Electronic Billboards on City Property
On March 1, 2021, the City Council held a study session to consider options for electronic billboards on City-owned 
property.  The first option would allow electronic billboards only on land owned or leased by the City, similar to nearby 
jurisdictions.  The second option would allow an existing billboard to be removed in exchange for the construction of an 
electronic billboard on City-owned land, as provided by State law (a relocation agreement and lease agreement would also 
be required).  The Council expressed interest in both options.  Staff explained that standards could be identified to 
determine where signs could be placed in order to protect safety as well as aesthetics, and that a code amendment would 
be pursued concurrently pursuant to State law to facilitate relocation agreements.
A local ordinance could allow electronic billboards only near major highways, e.g. 101 and 92, and primarily oriented for 
viewing from the adjacent highway.  They could also be restricted from locating on parcels adjacent to residential areas 
and allowed to overhang a public right of way through an encroachment permit.  Such billboards could be prohibited from 
being located within 1000 feet of one another, consistent with State law.  Notably, electronic billboards already cannot 
show messages in motion or change messages less than once in four seconds or allow for the brilliance of the sign to
