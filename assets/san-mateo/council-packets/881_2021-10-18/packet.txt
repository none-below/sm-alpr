SUBJECT:
Speed Reduction in School Zones 
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on development of policy for speed limit reduction in school zones and provide direction to staff.
BACKGROUND:
Council’s strategic plan prioritizes the implementation of bicycle and pedestrian projects that address safety. Evaluating 
school zone speed limit reductions to address quality-of-life and safety within neighborhoods near schools is part of this 
objective. Speed limit reductions in school zones can help to reduce the risk of severe or fatal conflicts between vehicles 
and active transportation users (e.g., cyclists, pedestrians) and can also help promote students and families walking or 
biking to school. 
The California Vehicle Code (CVC) governs how speed limits are established and grants local jurisdictions the authority to 
establish certain speed limits on qualified streets as follows: 

15 miles per hour (mph) school zones contiguous to the school and within 500 feet of the school boundary and

25 mph school zones between 500 feet and 1000 feet from the school boundary.
The CVC also defines the criteria that must be satisfied for these speed limits to be implemented. CVC 22358.4(b) permits 
15 mph school zones and extended 25 mph school zones on streets that satisfy the following conditions: 
1.
No more than two through traffic lanes,
2.
A posted speed limit of 30 mph or lower, 
3.
Within a residence district (as defined by the CVC), and
4.
Contiguous to a school.
Pursuant to the CVC, the enactment of 15 mph school zones and extended 25 mph school zones on qualified streets 
require Engineering and Traffic Studies (E&TS), an ordinance or resolution by the City Council, and installation of 
appropriate signage. If enacted, the 15 mph school zones and extended 25 mph school zones would be in effect when 
children are present. California Assembly Bill 43 (AB 43), which was signed into law recently, does not affect the ability nor 
amend the provisions for local jurisdictions to establish reduced school zone speed limits under CVC 22358.4(b). AB 43, as 
applied to local jurisdictions, primarily concerns residential and business district speed setting.
Analysis
To identify City streets that qualify for the reduced school zone speed limit per the CVC, staff looked at all schools within

the City and analyzed their contiguous streets to determine their eligibility for a reduced speed limit. Using data from the 
California Department of Education, staff identified a total of 34 public and private schools serving kindergarten through 
12th grade within the City. Staff evaluated the 61 streets contiguous to these schools and determined 11 streets did not

The CVC’s definition of “residence district” is different from the City’s General Plan’s “local street” classification and 
residential zoning designations and is based on the density of separate dwelling houses or business structures that use the 
street for primary ingress and egress.  A minimum density threshold must be met for a street to be considered within a 
residence district under the CVC. 
 
Based on the conditions specified in the CVC as discussed above, staff determined that 11 schools do not have any 
qualifying streets. The remaining 23 schools have a total of 38 streets that are eligible for a reduced school zone speed 
limit (Attachment 1). The eligible streets, which are comprised of 4 arterial streets, 9 collector streets, and 25 local streets, 
are adjacent to 15 public schools and 8 private schools in the City.
Evaluation of School Zone Speed Limit Policies in Other Cities
Since the passage of California Assembly Bill 321 in 2007, which added the aforementioned CVC subsection, numerous 
cities have implemented reduced school zone speed limits on qualified streets, including San Francisco, Sacramento, 
Sunnyvale, Mountain View, Burlingame, Menlo Park, Los Altos, Brisbane, and Berkeley.  In developing a reduced school 
zone speed limit policy, these jurisdictions considered safety and active transportation goals and the cost of 
implementation. Some cities, such as San Francisco and Mountain View, implemented a city-wide policy that encompassed 
both public and private schools. Including both public and private schools provides a consistent expectation for drivers 
near schools across the city. Cities that implemented a public school-only policy, such as Sunnyvale, took into 
consideration: a) that private schools often attract students from a larger area, which may result in less bicycle and 
pedestrian traffic compared to public schools; b) that private schools have a greater potential to relocate; and c) the cost 
of implementation.
Options for City Council Consideration 
In evaluating options to determine where to implement reduced school zone speed limits, staff has identified three local 
policy options for Council’s consideration. These options consider implementation at: (1) all schools within the City’s 
boundaries; (2) only public schools; or (3) all schools with 100 or more students. The threshold of 100 or more students is a 
cut-off point based on a natural gap in student population amongst the schools; this threshold includes all public schools in 
the policy. Council may select any of the options above, or any establish an alternative threshold. Attachment 2 contains 
figures showing the location of the school zones and their preliminary boundaries under each of these three options. 
Additionally, cost estimates are provided that include design, implementation, and post-implementation evaluation costs 
for each option. Cost estimates do not include community outreach. Once Council provides policy direction, staff will 
prepare a community engagement plan including cost estimates.

Next Steps
Moving forward, staff will refine the school zone speed limit boundaries, identify funding, and propose a resolution for 
Council adoption. Following a policy adoption, the implementation would include final identification of eligible roadways, 
signage design and installation, and coordination with San Mateo Police Department for education and enforcement of the 
new speed zones. 
Staff will develop an outreach strategy during implementation that targets the impacted schools and the neighborhoods 
surrounding them.  Staff recommends evaluating the travel speed and traffic volume after the implementation of 15 mph 
school zones to determine the effectiveness of the policy. Staff can develop further recommendations on safety 
improvements based on the evaluation, such as implementation of identified bicycle facilities in the 2020 Bicycle Master 
Plan, traffic calming measures, or enhanced signage. 
BUDGET IMPACT:
There is no budget impact for the Council’s consideration of these policy options. The budget impact for implementation 
of school speed limit zones will vary based on the Council’s policy direction to staff and may range from $180,000 to 
$245,000.

SUBJECT: Downtown Uses and Vacancies – Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive an update on allowable downtown uses and vacancies and provide direction to staff regarding any potential 
modifications to downtown use requirements or other development standards.
BACKGROUND:
At the October 19, 2020 meeting, the City Council expressed concern about the increasing number of ground floor 
vacancies in Downtown San Mateo related to the Covid-19 pandemic. There was also a concern raised related to the 
allowable commercial uses on the ground floor within the Central Business District (CBD) and if this range of uses was too 
restrictive to meet current market conditions. To better understand the issue, a study session was requested to discuss the 
site and development standards for commercial tenants and range of uses allowed in the CBD. In addition, Council 
requested that staff solicit feedback from downtown stakeholders to gather input on the City’s development standards 
and allowed uses for downtown and how that might be affecting the ability of interested tenants to occupy vacant spaces.  
On February 1, 2021, the City Council held a study session to receive additional information on allowable downtown uses 
and an overview of the stakeholder outreach that was conducted. Following the presentation, public input and discussion, 
the Council provided feedback that additional information was needed related to downtown vacancies and if the range of 
allowed ground floor uses in the CBD was excluding newer uses that were not considered when the Downtown Plan was 
updated in 2009. The Council also requested community feedback related to downtown activity and vibrancy.  
For reference, a map from the Downtown Plan that shows the general downtown area and the Downtown Retail Core, or 
CBD, is included as Attachment 1. The Downtown Land Use Plan, which includes the blocks that are subject to Required 
Retail Frontage, is included as Attachment 2.  In addition, a table that summarizes the permitted and conditionally 
permitted uses in the CBD, as well as the blocks subject to the Required Retail Frontage, is included as Attachment 3. 
This agenda report is intended to provide results from the community-wide survey that was conducted in March of this 
year, an update on downtown vacancies, and further information about the range of ground floor uses that are allowed in 
the downtown core, with a goal of getting input from Council on any short-term actions that may be necessary to support 
healthy vibrancy and activity in Downtown San Mateo.    
Downtown Community Survey
During the month of March, staff published an online survey to collect community input on downtown activity and 
vibrancy, including questions related to the street closure on B Street, the type of businesses that were desired downtown, 
and whether they visit multiple business types during their trips downtown.  A total of 975 individuals visited the survey 
page and 738 completed the survey.

When asked what they do when visiting downtown, 85% said they dine at restaurants, cafes and/or bars, 78% said they 
pick up food to-go and 57% said they visit for entertainment.  Over 50% of respondents also said that they visit downtown 
for grocery shopping, general services such as the Post Office, and retail shopping. When asked about what type of 
businesses that they would like to see more of downtown, 65% said that they would like to see more general retail stores, 
55% said more restaurants and cafes, and 43% said more entertainment-related businesses and more produce or grocery 
stores.
With regard to visiting multiple businesses during a trip to downtown for personal care services or for general services, 
51% of respondents noted that they would also pick up food to eat at home, 46% said that they would also dine at a 
restaurant or café, 42% said that they would shop at a grocery store, and 39% said that they would also shop at a retail 
store. When asked if they visit multiple businesses during a trip downtown for a fitness-related activity, 72% said they do 
not visit any other businesses, 21% said that they would also pick up food to eat at home and 18% said they would also 
shop at a grocery store. So, the survey results do seem to indicate that personal care service and general service 
businesses are more likely to drive customer traffic to other downtown uses than fitness-related businesses.
A summary of the survey results for all 16 questions is included as Attachment 4. While this community survey did receive 
a high number of responses, it should be noted that since it was a voluntary online survey, it does not meet the 
requirements of being statistically representative of the community as a whole.  
Downtown Vacancies
Business activity in Downtown San Mateo has been fairly resilient over the past 18 months despite the economic hardships 
that have resulted from the Covid-19 pandemic. To better assess the situation and get a more accurate understanding of 
current conditions related to ground floor retail businesses (including restaurant and food service businesses) and 
vacancies, staff canvassed the downtown core (the blocks between Baldwin and 5th Avenue, and El Camino Real and 
Railroad Avenue). This staff survey occurred during the first week of September 2021.
The results of this survey found that there were 32 vacant retail store fronts in the core area out of a total of 275 ground 
floor retail spaces. Of these vacancies (shown in the table below), staff found that 18 are actively looking for a tenant, six 
are being held vacant due to anticipated redevelopment and two are being held off the market due to the condition of the 
site or at the behest of the property owner. Additionally, there are six sites that have new tenants identified, but have not 
yet begun their tenant improvements. This means while these store fronts are currently still vacant, a new business should 
be in operation within the next 6-12 months, depending on the duration to complete tenant improvements.

Removing the properties that are actually leased to a new tenant but still appear to be vacant, the vacancy rate in the 
Downtown is 9.75%. As a point of comparison, according to commercial retail brokers, the typical vacancy rate in 
commercial districts during non-recession times is 7%. 
To gain further insight into these numbers and current trends, staff reached out to several retail brokers about the status 
of the retail vacancies in the Downtown. The brokers affirmed that Downtown San Mateo remains a competitive area to 
lease despite the effects of the pandemic and is doing better relative to neighboring jurisdictions. Several brokers 
responded that “non-food” uses have been difficult to lease across the board in the Bay Area. Storefronts that lack 
sufficient kitchen infrastructure, such as electric power capacity, grease traps, and gas access, have a tougher time finding 
a tenant and often remain vacant for a longer period of time. 
An example of this type of vacancy in the downtown is the former Saigon Day Spa, a 1,680 square-foot nail salon at 135

South B Street. The site has had difficulty finding a tenant because it cannot accommodate a food use due to the lack of 
kitchen infrastructure. The 1,680 square-foot space, which has a depth of approximately 100 feet, cannot be split into two 
units and is larger than the typical market demand for a single tenant space. The ideal size for a retail tenant is around 
1,000 square feet with a building depth of around 50 to 60 feet. 
Furthermore, San Mateo’s Downtown is located between Hillsdale Shopping Center and Downtown Burlingame, which 
makes it more difficult to tenant a “soft-goods retailer” due to the proximity between these two destination shopping 
centers. Many retailers who have multiple stores seek to have a minimum distance of at least three miles between their 
locations. The distance between Hillsdale Shopping Center and Downtown Burlingame is four miles. Thus, a retailer in 
Downtown Burlingame can locate at the Hillsdale Shopping Center, or vice versa, but would not likely also locate in 
Downtown San Mateo due to the proximity to an existing store.
Finally, many of the brokers talked about online shopping as a permanent change, with a continuing reduction in the size 
and number of brick and mortar retailers. With the ability to shop online for goods, soft-goods retailers that retain a brick 
and mortar storefront are reducing the size of their spaces and instead are expanding in their distribution locations to 
complete with online retailers like Amazon. However, while the longer-term online retail trend has been reducing the 
number of brick and mortar retail tenants, there are other trends that have been driving changes to commercial tenants 
and uses in downtowns. Restaurants and other food related establishments, personal services, fitness, financial 
institutions and boutique medical uses are all seeking locations within active downtown settings. Thus, because of 
Downtown San Mateo’s central location to customers, the ability of customers to complete multiple errands at one stop 
and its proximity to public transit access, demand for ground floor commercial space in the Downtown remains strong. 
Overall, Downtown San Mateo is going through a transition fueled both by longer-term economic trends and the near-
term economic shock related to the pandemic, as well as a high volume of proposed redevelopment sites. The relatively 
low vacancy numbers of properties being actively leased, coupled with the ongoing strong development activity, are signs 
of resiliency.  While Downtown is unlikely to see a resurgence of new soft goods retailer businesses moving forward, 
demand from other business sectors is supporting a healthy tenant occupancy of ground floor commercial spaces.  
While the overall vacancy picture points to a resilient Downtown, the Council may want to consider policy interventions 
directed at the 3% of spaces in the downtown core that are off-market or being held for future redevelopment. The City 
can look at ways to encourage these sites to be in active use, whether that includes helping to facilitate pop-up retail uses 
or potentially looking at financial penalties for properties that do not have an active lease in place beyond a certain 
timeframe, such as over a year. This may encourage landlords with long-standing vacant properties to be more flexible on 
lease rates or invest in improvements to make it more attractive to tenants in order to avoid these penalties. 
Allowable Ground Floor Uses
Within the Downtown area, the allowable ground floor uses are broken up into two groups – those allowed in a space on a 
block that is subject to Required Retail Frontage and those spaces located within the larger Downtown core (aka CBD) 
area. Within the Required Retail Frontage area, retail, personal services, restaurants, theaters, banks (with limitations) and 
health/fitness uses are permitted. Optometry and art gallery uses have also been determined to be permitted uses, 
subject to certain requirements, within the Required Retail Frontage area.  A wider range of uses are allowed within the 
larger Downtown Retail Core area.  The Allowable Uses in the Downtown Core, included as Attachment 3, provides a full 
breakdown of all permitted and special uses that are allowed.
With regard to allowing commercial uses that may not be explicitly listed but that are consistent with the intent of the 
Downtown Area Plan and would be compatible with the adjacent retail uses, the Zoning Code (Section 27.38.020.f) does 
allow for their consideration. Under this provision, the Zoning Administrator can determine that a use is allowed in the 
CBD when it can be found that it is similar in nature and operation to other business in the downtown core, compatible 
with existing and permitted uses, and/or desirable in serving the Downtown core area and the community at large. 
Allowing fitness uses (gyms and health studios), optometrists and art galleries in Required Retail Frontage space are past 
examples of when the Zoning Administrator made the determination that the use, subject to certain requirements, could 
be permitted. Staff continues to thoughtfully consider all business use requests, and when appropriate, review them with

To understand more recent business types and uses that have inquired about occupying a ground floor space in the 
Downtown core, the Planning and Development Review Technician staff were surveyed.  The highest number of inquiries 
were related to restaurant and food service-related uses, and personal service uses.  The most frequent requests for 
ground floor spaces that were not permitted were related to banks interested in a corner location and larger office uses.  
On a few occasions, boutique gyms and health studio type uses were not allowed due to their proposed floor plan, but in 
most cases, staff was able to work with the applicant to modify their plan to ensure the use met applicable frontage 
requirements and could be approved. Overall, there appears to be a lot of business types and uses that are interested in 
occupying ground floor space in the Downtown core, and in most cases, we are successful in finding a path for approval.
With regard to vacant spaces and how the City’s use and development regulations may be affecting occupancy, staff 
reviewed the list of current Downtown locations that have vacancies to better understand the issue. Some of the barriers 
to occupancy appear to be related to the physical space (i.e. it is too large for what the market is seeking, or it needs 
significant upgrades) or the landlord (i.e. the property is anticipated for redevelopment or is being purposely held off the 
market). It also appears that larger footprint spaces are more challenging to re-tenant than smaller ones. In general, all of 
the City’s development standards and retail frontage requirements create some degree of a barrier depending on the 
business that is looking at a space; however, this is counter-balanced by the City’s overarching policy goals related to 
maintaining the Downtown as a vibrant and active retail center and these limitations and requirements serve to support 
that goal. If the Council is interested in updating or changing some of these overarching goals, which provide the basis for 
the more specific development standards and retail frontage requirements, then a review of the Downtown Area Plan 
would be the most appropriate way to proceed. A comprehensive review of this Plan is scheduled to occur after the 
General Plan Update is completed.
However, one requirement that was identified as a potential barrier to reoccupying a vacant space, in particular a larger 
space, is the parking in-lieu fee that is charged when there is a change of use that increases parking demand. The most 
frequent example of when a parking in-lieu fee is charged is when a space with a retail or personal service use is changed 
to a restaurant use.  Under the current parking in-lieu requirements, which were adopted by the City Council on March 2, 
2020, a change in use that results in an increase in parking demand of more than 10 spaces is required to pay the fee of 
$50,000/space for each additional parking space required. This is the same fee that is required for new developments that 
are not providing all required parking on-site.  It should be noted that most changes of use in Downtown are in smaller 
properties that do not trigger the parking in-lieu fee requirement. But, if the Council is concerned that this fee is creating 
an unreasonable barrier to the re-occupancy of larger vacant spaces, staff could revisit the parking in-lieu policy and the 
fee requirements for changes of use in existing spaces.      
Next Steps
The City’s General Plan Update (GPU) is currently underway, with a target to adopt by the end of 2023, and the Downtown 
is included as a special study area, so there is an opportunity to include goals/policies/actions related to the Downtown in 
the GPU. In addition, once the GPU is completed, a comprehensive update to the Downtown Area Plan will be initiated to, 
among other things, ensure that the Plan is consistent with the updated General Plan. This update will be a good 
opportunity for the City Council and the community to consider more comprehensive changes and updates to Downtown 
uses, development standards and other requirements, and evaluate how they align with the vision for Downtown San 
Mateo moving into the future.   
With the ongoing GPU effort and the longer-range plan for updating the Downtown Plan in mind, the Council should 
consider the information presented with this agenda report and provide input to staff on how to proceed:
1.
No action related to Downtown is needed at this time; updates to Downtown goals, polices and use requirements 
should be completed as part of the GPU effort and when the Downtown Area Plan is updated (anticipated in the 
next 3-4 years); or
2.
Some shorter-term policy options related to Downtown are necessary at this time, such as:
a.
A review of the parking in-lieu fee assessed for changes of use; or
