SUBJECT:
Transition to District-Based Elections for Council Members - Public Hearing #3
RECOMMENDATION:
Receive a third presentation from staff and the City's consulting demographer regarding the process and criteria for 
establishing Council district boundaries for by-district elections, hold the first of two public hearings to receive public input 
regarding the initial draft maps of five districts, and provide specific direction for development of a revised set of draft 
maps.
BACKGROUND:
On August 23 and September 13, 2021, the City Council held the first and second of five public hearings required for the 
City’s transition to by-district elections. Public hearings #1 and #2 were held prior to any draft maps being drawn and 
covered the composition and number of districts, form of government under consideration, charter amendment 
considerations, and the public input process. At its September 13 hearing, the Council provided direction to begin drawing 
draft maps for a by-district election system comprised of five districts and a rotational mayor.
Based on public input and Council feedback, the City’s demographer has prepared three draft maps for review. This public 
hearing #3 on September 30 is to receive feedback on this first set of draft maps, and provide direction to the 
demographer on any revisions to be considered. An updated set of draft maps will then be discussed at public hearing #4 
on October 14, during which the Council will be asked for final direction on the preferred map and direction on election 
sequencing. On November 1, 2021, the Council will hold public hearing #5 to consider introduction of an ordinance 
approving the official district map. 
In accordance with Elections Code 10010, the City published on its website the draft maps submitted by its demographer 7 
days prior to this hearing, along with the potential sequence of elections.  Any updated draft maps will also be published 7 
days in advance of the next public hearing.
Map Drawing Criteria
In creating the district boundaries, the City must ensure compliance with the following state and federally mandated 
criteria:
1.
Each council district shall contain a nearly equal population; and
2.
Each council district shall be drawn in a manner that complies with the Federal Voting Rights Act, and the 
Constitutions of the United States and California (e.g., race cannot be the predominant factor).
In addition to the above, under Elections Code Section 21621(c), certain criteria must be used to establish the boundaries 
of the council districts including the following, in order of priority:

3.
Geographic contiguity
4.
To the extent practical, maintain neighborhoods and local communities of interest (such as cultural or historical 
bonds, common issues, neighborhood activities, or legislative/election concerns. Communities of interest do not 
include relationships with political parties, incumbents or political candidates.)
5.
Boundaries that are easily identifiable and understandable to residents (e.g., geographical and topographical 
features such as rivers and creeks, or other physical barriers such as railroads or major streets.)
6.
Geographic compactness is encouraged.
Considerations on Drawing the Draft Maps
The demographer has drawn and evaluated these draft maps to ensure compliance with the above criteria. The City is 
using the latest 2020 Census data provided by the Statewide Database with the state prison population reallocated, as 
required under the Fair Maps Act. These maps use the underlying Census tract geography to establish the boundaries of 
individual City Council districts. In drawing these maps, every care was taken to honor neighborhoods and communities of 
interest. However, please note that sometimes Census blocks do not perfectly align with known neighborhood boundaries 
or streets. Some census blocks follow natural geographic boundaries, such as a turn in a creek, which may be why some 
notches or boundary lines do not precisely follow neighborhood borders or roads. 
Furthermore, each City Council map plan must be below 10% total deviations between the largest to smallest district by 
population. Deviation is calculated by first determining the ideal population for each district. In San Mateo's case, the ideal 
population is 21,161 residents per district. The total deviation is determined by adding together the deviations of the 
largest and smallest districts from that ideal population size. For example, if your largest district is 5% larger than the ideal 
population and the smallest district is 3% smaller than the ideal population, then the total deviation is 8%. As achieving 
near population equality between districts is the first criteria that must be met, some known neighborhoods will be split 
between districts. 
Further explanation of the public input and analysis that went in to drafting each of these three draft maps are included as 
attachments to this report. Draft Map 1 is outlined in Attachments 1 and 2. Draft Map 2 is outlined in Attachments 3 and 4. 
Draft Map 3 is outlined in Attachments 5 and 6.
Part of this process is to encourage public input and Council discussion by providing several different distinct maps to 
consider. After taking public testimony, Council is asked to provide feedback on the preferred draft map(s) and any 
changes to be made. The demographer will take that direction and refine the proposed map(s), which will be considered 
during public hearing #4 on October 14. 
Future Hearings on Sequencing
The Council will consider introduction of an ordinance that approves the final district map during a regular meeting on 
November 1, 2021. Under State law, the City is required to include the potential sequencing of district elections when 
publishing draft maps. 
For the purposes of this legal requirement, the following option is presented. Elections for Districts A, B and C could be 
held in November 2022, and for Districts D and E, elections could be held in November 2024. Please note this potential 
sequencing is very preliminary, that in these draft maps the districts have been randomly assigned letters, and the 
sequencing may change before a district map is approved by the City Council. Further direction on sequencing will be 
requested during a future public hearing. 
Public Input
The City has been actively collecting public input on this process through a robust multilingual outreach campaign that 
includes print and digital materials, in-person workshops, pop-up activities, and these virtual public hearings. Input 
collected includes Communities of Interest (COI) form submissions on paper and online, hand-drawn and digital map 
submissions through the online tool DistrictR, as well as general public comments. The first of three in-person workshops

COI Testimony in Support
Draft Plan 1 has a total deviation of 7.24%, all districts are contiguous, and compact. This plan splits two 
neighborhoods total between the five districts. It also follows themes seen throughout the COI Forms 
and email Submissions, as well as staff input and geographic analysis, including:

A: Keeps the neighborhoods of Shoreline, North Shoreview, Shoreline Park, South Shoreview, 
Parkside, Mariners Islands, and Bridgepoint together. Common themes include environmental 
concerns and recreational amenities, shared waterways, entire district east of highway 101, 
impacts of traffic and safety.

B: Keeps the neighborhoods North Central and Central together. Common themes include 
expanding affordable housing, public safety, proximity to downtown, traffic impacts and 
speeding, diverse housing types, proximity to rail line and highway 101.


C: Keeps the neighborhoods of San Mateo Park, Baywood, and Baywood Knolls together.


D: Keeps the neighborhoods of Hillsdale, 25th Avenue and Laurelwood together. Common


E: Keeps the neighborhoods of Bay Meadows and San Mateo Village together, Aragon and 19th

Background
• May 24, 2021: City receives demand letter alleging current at-large 
Council election system violates California Voting Rights Act (CVRA)
• June 21, 2021: Council adopts resolution of intent to transition to 
district elections
• July 19, 2021: Council approves updated transition schedule, with 
goal to complete process by Nov. 15, 2021
• Aug. 23/Sept. 13, 2021: Public Hearings #1 and #2 held, Council 
decides to move to 5 districts with rotating mayor
• Sept. 23, 2021: First round of draft maps published
• Oct. 7, 2021: Updated draft maps to be published
• November 2022: First by-district City Council elections to be held
