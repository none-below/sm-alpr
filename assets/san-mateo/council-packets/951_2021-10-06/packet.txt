SUBJECT:
Non-Resident Surcharge Policy Pilot for Recreational Programs - Update
RECOMMENDATION:
Amend the Non-Resident Surcharge Policy Based on the Pilot Program Positive Outcomes.
BACKGROUND:
In January 2020 the department presented the Non-Resident Surcharge Policy to the Commission for feedback. 
(Attachment 1). Prior policy had been to charge an additional 25% non-resident surcharge for programs up to $100 and 
$25 for programs costing over $100.  Some programs were exempted from the surcharge such as adult sports leagues, 
summer camps, programs held at a private facility and programs held in conjunction with another public agency. The 
Commission recommended eliminating the surcharge and adding a resident priority registration period as part of a pilot 
program. The pilot began in the 2020 Fall season, however, since the January 2020 Commission meeting, the global 
pandemic has necessitated that we transition our programing where possible to virtual platforms whilst in person activities 
have been reduced greatly. In June 2021 we saw some relaxing of social distancing protocols in our county and following 
these we have also returned to a limited amount of in person activities. Whilst we are all aware of the reduction in 
programming the pandemic caused, the reduction in revenue was a huge blow to the department dropping from $4 
million to approximately $1 million for FY 20-21.  Whilst the Recreation division has been trying to rebuild this revenue 
base, it appears that the year ahead will be challenging with virtual programing remaining as part of our offering to 
patrons and in person programing looking to make a gradual return especially for selected indoor activities 
At this time, staff has taken the opportunity to update the Commission with a year-to-date analysis of registration statistics 
tabled in Attachment 2 as was the original intent at the January 2020 meeting. The table shows that whilst we are only at 
41% of pre pandemic registration numbers overall, registration percentage for non-residents has increased to 26% (21% in 
2019). On the resident front we are seeing that 30% of residents are taking advantage of the priority registration period, 
registering ahead of non-residents. Further the comparison study (Attachment 3) of neighboring cities shows that although 
most neighboring Peninsula cities apply a non-resident surcharge, there is no evidence or similar analyses to show that 
removal of a non-resident surcharge would be beneficial to their programming. 
Based on the presented analysis, staff recommends that the non-resident surcharge be removed based on the premise 
that we are trying to rebuild our revenue base and encouraging patrons from neighboring cities as well as those from 
further afield is a great way forward. The statistics show 1 in 3 of our residents take advantage of the early registration 
period securing spots in our most popular programs such as Ceramics and anecdotally no negative feedback has been 
received. Most importantly applying a surcharge at this point would be detrimental to the custom that we are trying to 
encourage.

SUBJECT:
Review of Non-Resident Surcharge Policy for Recreation Programs
RECOMMENDATION:
The department has no recommendation on this item. Rather, the department seeks input from the commission with the 
3 options below: 
1.
Leave the policy as is 
2.
Adjust the policy 
3.
Discontinue the Non-Resident Surcharge
BACKGROUND:
The department is asking for the Parks and Recreation Commission to review the department’s current Non-Resident 
Surcharge Policy that was issued and approved on April 9, 2011 (Att 1 - Current Non-Resident Surcharge Policy).
To summarize the current policy, a non-resident program fee is calculated at resident rate plus
25% but not to exceed $25 above the total resident rate. In addition, there is no non-resident surcharge applied to the 
following programs:
Admission fees; Adult Sports Leagues; Swimfit memberships; Beresford Kids Club; Summer    
Camps listed in the Summer Camp Booklet; programs located at a private facility or SMFCSD; programs co-sponsored with 
another agency or if registrations may be made through the vendor and the vendor rate is less than ours would be with a 
non-resident surcharge.
Historically, residents are charged a lower rate to reflect their payment of city taxes and reward them for residing in their 
home city. However, in 2019, when considering the impact of the policy, we identified that with the passing of time, in 
many cases the $25 cap renders that benefit irrelevant as many courses exceed $100 and can be as high as $300.
As a result, when considering the value of a non-resident surcharge and the fact that 30% of our registered patrons are 
nonresidents, we may also want to consider that eliminating the non-resident surcharge may be an enticement for those 
residents in adjoining cities to favor our activities over other local cities. Removing the surcharge may also play a role in 
our ability to contract with instructors as many of our contract instructors are engaged by our neighboring cities.  
Anecdotally, having nonresidents participate in our programming could serve as word of mouth promotion and drive 
revenue for other local business such as restaurants and shops which in turn benefits the city.
As we consider the value of a non-resident surcharge, we surveyed other cities on the peninsula (See Att 2 - Neighboring 
Cities Non-Resident Surcharge Data) and found that most do apply a nonresident surcharge. We may want to consider that 
if this policy was retained, that the $25 maximum surcharge should be waived so that the policy be uniformly applied. We 
also have a priority registration period for current registrants that we could potentially look into expanding.
